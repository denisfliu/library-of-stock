# Philosophy Category Supplement

## How Philosophy Differs

Philosophers develop **ideas and arguments** forming a coherent intellectual narrative. Unlike literature (discrete works with plots) or visual arts (discrete paintings), philosophy topics are about:

1. **Ideas/concepts** — the categorical imperative, conatus, the thing-in-itself, the veil of ignorance
2. **Arguments and positions** — why Kant says lying to a murderer is wrong, Spinoza's pantheism
3. **Works as vehicles** — the Ethics, Critique of Pure Reason — containers for ideas
4. **Debates and interlocutors** — Kant vs. Forster on race, Jacobi's critique of Spinoza
5. **Intellectual lineage** — who influenced whom, who responded to whom

## Sectioning for Philosophers

Organize by **idea clusters that may span multiple works**, not purely by work:

### Primary sections: Key Ideas/Concepts
Name after the idea, not the work:
- "The Categorical Imperative" (not just "Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals")
- "Conatus and the Affects" (not just "Ethics Part III")
- "The Thing-in-Itself and Transcendental Idealism"

Include which work(s) the idea comes from in the section description.

### Secondary sections: Major Works
Works frequently clued as answerlines or with specific structural clues:
- "Critique of Pure Reason" — if clues reference A/B editions, Transcendental Aesthetic, synthetic a priori
- "Ethics (Spinoza)" — if clues reference geometric structure, specific axioms

### Also include:
- **Debates/Critiques** — if clues frequently reference specific interlocutors
- **Reception and Legacy** — if clues reference how later thinkers used the philosopher
- **General / Biographical** — the usual giveaway identifiers

## Card Types for Philosophy

### 1. Concept -> Philosopher (most common)
- Front: `Philosopher: argued that lying to a murderer at your door is still immoral`
- Back: `Immanuel Kant`

### 2. Concept identification
- Front: `Concept: the principle that one should act only according to universalizable maxims`
- Back: `Categorical Imperative (Kant)`

### 3. Work -> Philosopher
- Front: `Work: written in geometric order with axioms and definitions, emulating Euclid's Elements`
- Back: `Ethics (Spinoza)`

### 4. Specific argument -> Work
- Front: `Work: proposes that standing armies should be abolished as preliminary articles for peace`
- Back: `Toward Perpetual Peace (Kant)`

### 5. Interlocutor/Debate cards
- Front: `Philosopher: Friedrich Jacobi accused this thinker of "pure materialism" and thus atheism`
- Back: `Baruch Spinoza`

### 6. Key term definition
- Front: `Concept: the doctrine that "every man strives to preserve his own being"`
- Back: `Conatus (Spinoza)`

## Indicator Choices

- `Philosopher:` — general facts, identifying the thinker from a described idea
- `Concept:` — identifying a named concept/principle/doctrine
- `Work:` — identifying a specific philosophical text
- `Debate:` — identifying a philosophical dispute (use sparingly)

## What Makes a Good Philosophy Card

- Front describes an idea or argument in enough detail for recognition
- Back is the concept name, work, or philosopher — whichever is most useful to test
- Avoid cards where the front just says "wrote [book title]" — that's an identifier, not learnable
- Include the "why" when possible — "argued lying is wrong *because* it violates universalizability" beats just "argued lying is wrong"
- **Interlocutors are high-value** — knowing Deleuze wrote on Spinoza or Constant criticized Kant's lying example wins quizbowl games

## Tags

Use recognized philosophical movements/schools:
- Rationalism, Empiricism, German Idealism, Existentialism, Pragmatism
- Utilitarianism, Phenomenology, Analytic Philosophy, Stoicism, Epicureanism
- Frankfurt School, Vienna Circle, etc.

NOT broad labels like "Western philosophy" or "Ethics."
