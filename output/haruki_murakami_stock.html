Haruki Murakami

Contemporary Japanese novelist. Best known for Norwegian Wood (Toru Watanabe's love triangle with Naoko and Midori, named after a Beatles song), The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle (Toru Okada's search for his missing cat Noboru Wataya, sitting in a well), Kafka on the Shore (runaway fifteen-year-old, the amnesiac Nakata, Johnnie Walker, Colonel Sanders), 1Q84 (Aomame and Tengo, two moons, Little People, Air Chrysalis), and A Wild Sheep Chase (a sheep with a star-shaped birthmark). Also wrote the short story collections The Elephant Vanishes, Men Without Women, and First Person Singular. Major translators include Alfred Birnbaum and Jay Rubin. Translated Raymond Carver and Raymond Chandler into Japanese.
Norwegian Wood

A novel in which Toru Watanabe recalls his relationships with the depressive Naoko and the vivacious Midori, triggered by hearing the title Beatles song. His roommate is the neurotic geography major nicknamed 'Storm Trooper.' His best friend Kizuki commits suicide on his 17th birthday. The piano teacher Reiko was accused of being a lesbian by a thirteen-year-old student. A female bookstore owner recounts stripping in front of her father's altar. The novel ends with Toru considering 'where are you now?' after declaring his love for Midori. In Germany, it was published as Naoko's Smile. Often described as the Japanese Catcher in the Rye. Expanded from the short story 'Firefly.' Wikipedia

The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle

A novel about Toru Okada's search for his missing cat Noboru Wataya. While sitting in a dried-out well (shown to him by teenager May Kasahara), Toru fends off the influence of Noboru Wataya to reunite with his wife Kumiko. Lieutenant Mamiya recollects his service in the Japanese army, including being left to die in a well and witnessing a spy (Yamamoto) being skinned alive by Mongolians (the agent Boris). May Kasahara addresses Toru by the name of the wind-up bird. A soldier is ordered to kill a man with a baseball bat. Sections named 'The Thieving Magpie' and 'Bird as Prophet.' The short story about the narrator cooking spaghetti to Rossini's The Thieving Magpie and being called by his wife about their missing cat became the first chapter. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Haruki Murakami is a contemporary Japanese novelist whose works frequently appear in quizbowl. His most commonly identified novels are Norwegian Wood (Toru Watanabe's love triangle with Naoko and Midori, named after a Beatles song), The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle (Toru Okada's search for his missing cat), and Kafka on the Shore.

Norwegian Wood features the geography major roommate nicknamed 'Storm Trooper,' the piano teacher Reiko (accused of lesbianism by a student), and was expanded from the short story 'Firefly.' It was published in Germany as Naoko's Smile and is often called the Japanese Catcher in the Rye.

The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle centers on Toru Okada sitting in a dried-out well (shown to him by teenager May Kasahara) to fend off the influence of Noboru Wataya. Lieutenant Mamiya recollects witnessing a spy being skinned alive by Mongolians. The novel has sections titled 'The Thieving Magpie' and 'Bird as Prophet.' A short story about the narrator cooking spaghetti to Rossini became the first chapter.

Kafka on the Shore features the fifteen-year-old Kafka running away from home to Miss Saeki's library, the amnesiac Nakata (who lost his memory after the Rice Bowl Hill Incident and searches for cats), Johnnie Walker harvesting cats' souls for a flute, and Colonel Sanders as an 'abstract concept.' The boy named Crow may represent Kafka's subconscious.

1Q84 features the assassin Aomame and math teacher Tengo in an alternate reality with two moons. Fuka-Eri writes about the 'Little People' in Air Chrysalis. Aomame listens to Janacek's Sinfonietta, reads In Search of Lost Time while hiding, and is magically impregnated.

A Wild Sheep Chase concludes the Rat Trilogy (with Hear the Wind Sing and Pinball, 1973). The protagonist searches for a sheep with a star-shaped birthmark, and his girlfriend has magical ears.

Hard-Boiled Wonderland and the End of the World alternates between chapters about Calcutecs vs. Semiotecs and a strange End of the World with unicorn skulls and INKlings.

Notable short stories include 'Barn Burning' (adapted into Lee Chang-dong's Burning), 'The Second Bakery Attack' (newlyweds robbing a McDonald's), and 'Drive My Car' (adapted into a 2021 film). His translators are Alfred Birnbaum and Jay Rubin. He translated Raymond Carver and Chandler into Japanese, and his memoir is titled What I Talk About When I Talk About Running.
