Wissenschaftslehre and 'The I Posits Itself'

Schopenhauer's philosophical system, the Wissenschaftslehre (doctrine or science of knowledge), is built on the foundational principle 'the I posits itself absolutely.' This is grounded in Tathandlung (fact/act), the notion of the I positing itself. He argued consciousness could not be grounded in the 'thing in itself' and should be founded on 'pure selfhood.' His system opposed dogmatism's leap from things to representations. The I discovers its limits via a 'check' (Anstoss), reconciling absolute freedom with objective necessity. Fichte was a mentor of Schelling and influenced Hegel's concept of recognition.

An Attempt at a Critique of All Revelation

Fichte's breakthrough work, published on Kant's recommendation in 1792, was initially mistaken for a work by Kant. It argues that God can be known through the senses and that 'revealed' religious elements must undergo Kantian scrutiny. Its second edition includes a 'Theory of the Will' section. On its strength, Fichte was given a professorship at the University of Jena.

Atheism Controversy

Fichte was expelled from the University of Jena on charges of atheism after publishing 'On the Ground of Our Belief in a Divine World-Governance,' which identified God with the moral order of society. Friedrich Jacobi coined the term 'nihilism' in an open letter attacking Fichte during this dispute (the Atheismusstreit). Fichte had also defended K.L. Reinhold against attacks in the book Aenesidemus by G.E. Schulze.

General / Biographical

Fichte was a German Idealist philosopher, mentor of Schelling, and professor at Jena. His Addresses to the German Nation promoted German unification against Napoleon. The Vocation of the Scholar outlined the scholar's role, and The Characteristics of the Present Age was a set of lectures on the Enlightenment. He also wrote The Vocation of Man, which claims philosophy must begin with a Tathandlung. His 'the I posits itself' was a response to Descartes' dualism.

Summary of Facts

Johann Gottlieb Fichte appears in quizbowl as a German Idealist philosopher whose system, the Wissenschaftslehre (doctrine of scientific knowledge), is built on the principle 'the I posits itself absolutely.' This foundational concept (Tathandlung, or fact/act) opposes dogmatism by grounding consciousness in 'pure selfhood' rather than the thing-in-itself. The I discovers its limits through a 'check' (Anstoss), reconciling freedom with necessity.

His breakthrough work, An Attempt at a Critique of All Revelation (1792), was initially mistaken for Kant's work and was published on Kant's recommendation. It argues God must be consistent with moral law and that religious revelation requires Kantian scrutiny.

Fichte was expelled from the University of Jena on charges of atheism after publishing 'On the Ground of Our Belief in a Divine World-Governance,' which identified God with the moral order. Friedrich Jacobi coined the term 'nihilism' in an open letter attacking Fichte during this dispute. Fichte had also defended K.L. Reinhold against G.E. Schulze's Aenesidemus.

In Foundations of Natural Right, Fichte argued a person's identity requires being 'summoned' by another free individual, a concept that influenced Hegel's recognition and lord-bondsman dialectic. His Addresses to the German Nation promoted unification against Napoleon. The Characteristics of the Present Age addressed the Enlightenment, and The Vocation of Man claims philosophy must begin with a Tathandlung.

Notably, the thesis-antithesis-synthesis formula is often wrongly attributed to Hegel but actually originates with Fichte. He was a mentor of Schelling, and Schopenhauer famously found his lectures boring.
