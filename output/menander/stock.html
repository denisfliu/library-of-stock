Summary of Facts

Menander (c. 342-290 BC) was an Athenian playwright and the foremost representative of New Comedy, which focused on everyday domestic life in contrast to the political satire of Aristophanes's Old Comedy. Aristophanes of Byzantium famously asked of Menander and life, 'which of you imitated the other?', reflecting his posthumous reputation as a realist.

His only near-complete surviving play is Dyskolos (The Grouch), found on a Bodmer papyrus in Egypt. It opens with a prologue by the god Pan, who causes Sostratos to fall in love with the daughter of the curmudgeonly Knemon. After Sostratos or Gorgias saves Knemon from falling into a well, Knemon agrees to accept Sostratos as a son-in-law.

The Girl from Samos (Samia) involves misunderstandings between Demeas, his lover Chrysis, and others over a baby, ending in the marriage of Moschion and Plangon. Other fragmentary works include Perikeiromene (a mercenary cuts off his wife's hair) and The Shield.

Menander originated famous maxims including 'He whom the gods love dies young' and 'Bad company corrupts good character' (later quoted by Saint Paul). He names a villa in Pompeii with a fresco of him reading. His works influenced Moliere and Roman comedians.
