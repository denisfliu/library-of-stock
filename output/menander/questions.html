Questions: Menander

5 tossups · 11 bonuses

Tossups

T1 2024 Bonus Eventus (2024) Diff 10 · Literature
This man names a villa in Pompeii containing a fresco of him seated reading one of his works. In one of his plays, confusion over one character nursing a baby assumed to be hers threatens to derail the wedding between Plangon and Moschion (Mohss-kee-own). Saint Paul’s phrase “Bad company corrupts good character” as well as Caesar’s (*) “the die is cast” are said to be derived from this author, who wrote about Demeas and his lover Chrysis in The Girl from Samos. Pan narrates one of his plays in which he causes Sostratos to fall in love with the title character’s daughter before he saves that man, Knemon, from drowning in a well. For 10 points, identify this New Comedy author of Dyskolos.
ANSWER: Menander
T2 2024 ARCADIA (2024) Diff 8 · History
Robert Curtis argued that jars discovered at this site were labeled “pure” to indicate they were for abstinent people in general, not that they were kosher. The presence of ancient looters at this site is evidenced by a piece of graffiti declaring “house tunneled” and a trio of skeletons with picks and shovels. Begram is the possible origin of a stool-leg’s ivory caryatid unearthed at this site, which depicts the goddess (*) Lakshmi. This center of garum production has a mosaic that declares ‘Cave Canem,’ or Beware the Dog, within the House of the Tragic Poet. Cadaveric spasms attributed to thermal shock resulted in the contorted postures preserved by Giuseppe Fiorelli. For 10 points, the House of the Faun is located in what city, where plastered bodies show the last moments of Vesuvius’s victims?
ANSWER: Pompeii (The trio of skeletons is in the House of Menander and may or may not have been looters, depending on the interpretation.)
T3 2024 ACF Nationals (2024) Diff 9 · Literature
This author’s surprising posthumous reputation as a realist led Aristophanes of Byzantium to ask of this author and life, “which of you imitated the other?” This contemporary of Diphilus and Philemon originated the maxim that “He whom the gods love dies young” in The Double Deceiver. George Bernard Shaw suggested The Rape of the Locks as a title for a fragment by this author, in which a mercenary cuts off his common-law wife’s hair. A prologue recited by the god Pan opens a play by this author in which the title character’s rescue from a well leads him to accept the love-struck Sostratos as a son-in-law. This author of Girl from Samos and Perikeiromene influenced Molière with a play about Knemon, a curmudgeonly old man. For 10 points, name this 4th-century BC playwright of Dyskolos, the only surviving exemplar of Athenian New Comedy.
ANSWER: Menander [or Menandros]
T4 2022 ACF Regionals (2022) Diff 8 · History
W. W. Tarn argued that holy men of this religion, laden with a one hundred gold coin bounty on their heads, benefited from a series of conquests by the Euthydemid dynasty. A ruler who converted to this religion selected Sagala as his capital over the city of Bucephala. Coins that suggest a kingdom’s syncretism with this religion were issued by Demetrius the Invincible. A set of conversations ending in King Menander’s conversion to this religion form the 1st-century text “Milinda’s Questions.” A “starving” figure found at one of this religion’s sites in Sikri reflects Greek influence on the Gandhāra art style. This religion’s spread to Hellenistic Asian cities like Taxila can be seen in Indo-Greek coinage that depicts the dharmachakra wheel. For 10 points, name this religion promoted by Ashoka.
ANSWER: Buddhism [accept specific schools of Buddhism, such as Theravāda Buddhism; accept Greco-Buddhism] (Pushyamitra Shunga offered the bounties on Buddhist holy men. Milinda was the Pāli name for Menander I, and the text is also called the Milinda Pañha.)
T5 2021 ACF Regionals (2021) Diff 8 · Religion
A text from this tradition proclaims “Whoever does not dance does not know what happens” as part of a “Round Dance”; people in that text from this tradition alternately see a man standing on the shore as bald, short, handsome, or a child. The line “I am the wife and the virgin” appears in Thunder, Perfect Mind, a work from this movement, whose name was coined by an opponent, Irenaeus. Due to usage of the word pleroma, Paul was claimed to be a member of this movement. Elaine Pagels translated texts from this tradition at Nag Hammadi, including the Apocryphon of John and the Gospel of Thomas. This set of traditions posited a dualistic world ruled by a “Supreme Being” and a Demiurge. For 10 points, name this early Christian set of beliefs, named for the Greek word for “knowledge.”
ANSWER: Gnosticism [or word forms like Gnostics; accept Ophites, Sethianism, Simonians, Menanders, Basilideans, Valentinianism, or Marcionism until “Irenaeus” is read]

The Greek etymology for this foot may mean “beaten back,” reflecting their original use in marching rhymes that later evolved into dramatic choruses. For 10 points each:

ANSWER: anapests
[10] Greek tragedy and comedy choruses in anapestic tetrameter usually used this device on alternating lines. In regularly metered lines using this device, the last expected syllable in the meter is dropped, making it the opposite of headlessness.
ANSWER: catalexis [or catalectic verse or poetry]
[10] This playwright used catalectic iambic tetrameter recited to aulos accompaniment for one scene in a play in which Sostratos saves the curmudgeonly Knemon from a well.

B3 2023 ACF Winter (2023) Diff 7 · History

ANSWER: Greeks [or Hellenes or Hellenic or Hellenistic; accept Greco-Bactrians; accept Indo-Greeks; accept Macedonians; accept Ionians or Aegeans; accept Yavanas or Yavanarajya; accept Dàyuān or Dàxià] (The Indo-Greek king Menander I Soter was known as Milinda in Pali.)
[10] Greco-Bactrian coins often depict Yuèzhī warriors riding these other animals, whose Ferghana type was noted by Zhāng Qiān (“jong ch’yen”) to have “sweat blood.” Bactria supplied these animals for Bessus and his cavalry at Gaugamela.
ANSWER: horses [or Ferghana horses; or Nisean horses; or Turkoman horses or Turkmene; accept equines or Equus; accept mares or stallions; accept heavenly horses or Tiān Mǎ or Dàyuān Mǎ]
