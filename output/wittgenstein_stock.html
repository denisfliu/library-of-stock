Austrian-born British philosopher of language, one of the most influential figures in analytic philosophy. Author of Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus (picture theory, 'whereof one cannot speak') and Philosophical Investigations (language games, private language argument, beetle in a box). His 'early' and 'late' periods represent a radical break in his thinking.
Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus

Wittgenstein's first book (1921), the only one published in his lifetime. It consists of seven numbered propositions expanding into sub-statements. Proposition 1: 'The world is everything that is the case.' Proposition 7: 'Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent.' The work introduces the picture theory of meaning (propositions picture facts in the world), analyzes truth-functions via NOR operations (Sheffer stroke), and compares itself to a ladder that must be discarded after climbing. Published with a Latin title in homage to Spinoza. Bertrand Russell's logical atomism drew on it. Frank Ramsey worked with C.K. Ogden on the first English translation and convinced Wittgenstein to return to Cambridge. It heavily influenced the Vienna Circle and logical positivism.

Philosophical Investigations & Language Games

Wittgenstein's posthumously published masterwork, representing his 'late' philosophy. It reverses many positions from the Tractatus, arguing that meaning arises from use in context ('meaning as use') rather than from picturing facts. Language games are illustrative examples of words in action: a builder and assistant communicating with 'block,' 'slab,' 'pillar,' 'beam.' Things we call 'games' share 'family resemblances' — overlapping traits with no necessary or sufficient condition. The book opens by criticizing a passage from Augustine's Confessions about naming objects, using the 'five red apples' shopkeeper example.

Private Language Argument & Beetle in a Box

Wittgenstein's argument in Philosophical Investigations that a language understood by only one person is impossible because meaning requires shared standards of correctness. The beetle-in-a-box thought experiment imagines people each with a box containing something called a 'beetle' — since no one can look in anyone else's box, the word 'beetle' cannot refer to a private experience. The 'S' diary example involves someone writing 'S' each time they experience a particular sensation, but there is no standard of correctness. A.J. Ayer criticized the argument for failing to account for 'primary recognition.'

Rule-Following Paradox & Kripke's Interpretation

Wittgenstein's rule-following paradox holds that 'no course of action could be determined by a rule, because any course of action can be made out to accord with the rule.' Saul Kripke developed this in Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language, introducing the 'quus' function — an operation that behaves like addition except when either summand exceeds 57, it returns 5. The 'Kripkenstein' portmanteau combines their names. James Conant argued these are 'Kantian' problems about conditions of possibility, not 'Cartesian' epistemological problems.

On Certainty & Other Late Works

On Certainty responds to G.E. Moore's 'here is one hand' argument, questioning what it truly means to 'know.' Remarks on the Foundations of Mathematics discusses mathematical contradictions (Alan Turing argued 'the real harm will not come unless there is an application, in which a bridge may fall down'). Remarks on Colour analyzed Goethe and Runge to clarify language regarding color. Some Remarks on Logical Form was Wittgenstein's only academic paper.

General / Biographical & Cultural Presence

Wittgenstein studied under Bertrand Russell at Cambridge and came from a wealthy Viennese family. His brother Paul was a pianist who lost his right arm in WWI, inspiring Ravel's Piano Concerto for the Left Hand. Wittgenstein designed a house in Vienna (Haus Wittgenstein/Stonborough House) with Paul Engelmann in a style influenced by Adolf Loos. Thomas Bernhard wrote novels about the Wittgenstein family (Correction, Wittgenstein's Nephew). David Foster Wallace's The Broom of the System incorporates Wittgenstein's philosophy. David Markson's Wittgenstein's Mistress is about the last woman on Earth. Derek Jarman made a film about him. He loved watching movies (especially Tom Mix westerns). He was a patron of Georg Trakl. Jean-Francois Lyotard's concept of metanarratives was inspired by Wittgenstein.

Summary of Facts

Ludwig Wittgenstein is one of the most frequently clued philosophers in quizbowl, with his two major works representing radically different philosophical positions. The Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus (1921), his only book published in his lifetime, consists of seven numbered propositions. Proposition 1 states 'The world is everything that is the case,' and Proposition 7 states 'Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent.' The work introduces the picture theory of meaning, uses NOR operations to define truth-functions, and compares itself to a ladder that must be discarded after climbing. It influenced logical positivism and the Vienna Circle. Frank Ramsey translated it into English with C.K. Ogden.

Philosophical Investigations, published posthumously, reverses the Tractatus's positions. It argues that meaning arises from use in context, not from picturing facts. Language games — such as a builder and assistant communicating with 'block,' 'slab,' 'pillar,' 'beam' — illustrate how words function in practice. Things we call 'games' share 'family resemblances' with no essential common trait. The book opens by criticizing Augustine's Confessions about naming objects.

The private language argument holds that a language understood by only one person is impossible. The beetle-in-a-box thought experiment shows that private experiences cannot be the meaning of words. The rule-following paradox, developed by Saul Kripke into the 'quus' function (addition that returns 5 for sums over 57), shows that no rule can determine its own application. On Certainty responds to G.E. Moore's 'here is one hand' argument.

Wittgenstein came from a wealthy Viennese family. His brother Paul was a pianist who lost his right arm in WWI, inspiring Ravel's Piano Concerto for the Left Hand. Wittgenstein designed Haus Wittgenstein with Paul Engelmann. Thomas Bernhard wrote novels about the family (Correction, Wittgenstein's Nephew). David Markson's Wittgenstein's Mistress and David Foster Wallace's The Broom of the System engage with his philosophy.
