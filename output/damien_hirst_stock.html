                                    Summary of Facts

                                    Damien Hirst is a British contemporary artist and the most prominent member of the Young British Artists (YBA). He is most commonly identified by his use of formaldehyde to preserve animals in tanks, most famously a tiger shark in The Physical Impossibility of Death in the Mind of Someone Living (1991), which was funded by advertising magnate Charles Saatchi. The work was parodied by the Stuckists and conveyed fear of the open ocean by using formaldehyde to emulate sea water.

                                    Hirst's other major formaldehyde works include Mother and Child Divided (which he said showed 'Life's like this, then it stops'), Away from the Flock (a lamb), The Dream (a unicorn), The Golden Calf (a bull with gold-encrusted horns sold at the controversial Sotheby's 'Beautiful Inside My Head Forever' auction), Adam and Eve, God Alone Knows (Carrara marble plinths with carcasses in crucifixion poses), and the Pursuit of Oblivion (umbrella in formaldehyde at the Gagosian Gallery). A disk representing the goddess Hathor between two golden horns also appears in formaldehyde.

                                    For the Love of God (2007) is a platinum-cast human skull encrusted with 8,601 diamonds, featuring the pear-shaped 'Skull Star Diamond' at its center. It was included in a record-breaking Tate Modern retrospective. The Veil Paintings are canvases covered with dots in a grid inspired by Pierre Bonnard, which drew accusations of appropriating Aboriginal Australian dot painting in 2018. Saatchi purchased A Thousand Years at a YBA show. Hirst curated the influential Freeze exhibition, staged a Venice exhibition disguised as an ancient Greek shipwreck display, and created the butterfly-and-pills-themed Empathy Suite hotel room in Las Vegas. He was accused of plagiarism by John LeKay, and Truong Tran protested his use of live butterflies with pornographic clippings.
