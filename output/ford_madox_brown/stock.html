Paddle-steamer visible before White Cliffs of Dover. Inspired by sculptor Thomas Woolner's emigration to Australia. Held at the Fitzwilliam Museum, Cambridge.

Summary of Facts

Ford Madox Brown (1821–1893) was an English painter closely associated with the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood, alongside Dante Gabriel Rossetti and William Holman Hunt, though he was never a formal member. His most famous painting, Work (1852–1865), took thirteen years to complete and depicts navvies digging up a road while Thomas Carlyle and Frederick Maurice observe from the right. The centermost laborer borrows his pose from the Apollo Belvedere. The background includes 'Vote for Bobus' sandwich boards (Bobus being a corrupt politician from Carlyle's writings), and the foreground features a girl in a garnet dress holding a baby. His other major painting, The Last of England (1855), is a tondo (circular painting) showing an emigrant couple turning away from the White Cliffs of Dover under a black umbrella. Brown painted it when he was 'very hard up and a little mad' and considering emigrating himself; it was inspired by sculptor Thomas Woolner's departure for Australia. The painting is held at the Fitzwilliam Museum in Cambridge. Brown also created twelve murals for the Manchester Town Hall (designed by Alfred Waterhouse, the tallest town hall in the UK) and designed stained glass including a piece depicting King Rene's Honeymoon. His grandson was the novelist Ford Madox Ford, author of The Good Soldier.
