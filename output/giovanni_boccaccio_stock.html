Giovanni Boccaccio

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Italian trecento author and humanist, one of the 'three crowns' of Italian literature alongside Petrarch and Dante. Best known for the Decameron, a collection of 100 novelle told by ten Florentines fleeing the plague. Also wrote On Famous Women, the Teseida, Il Filostrato, and The Elegy of Lady Fiammetta. A major source for Chaucer's Canterbury Tales and Christine de Pizan's Book of the City of Ladies. Mentored by the Augustinian monk Dionigi, who also mentored Petrarch.
The Decameron

A collection of 100 novelle framed by a story of ten Florentines (seven women and three men) who flee the Black Plague and tell stories over ten days. The storytellers include Panfilo, Pampinea, Filostrato, Neifile, and Dioneo. Dioneo, the last storyteller each day, has the special right to ignore the day's assigned topic. The collection closes with the tale of Patient Griselda, whose husband Gualtieri pretends to kill their children and marry his 12-year-old daughter as part of a test. Other notable tales include: Alibech being seduced into 'putting the Devil back in hell' (set in Barbary); a Chinese man shaming a rival by agreeing to swap identities; a judge solving deaths of two lovers who rubbed their teeth with sage by finding a venomous toad; eight lovers failing to forestall the virgin wedding of Alatiel; Saladin trying to trick the Jewish Melchizedek about the three Abrahamic religions; Giotto and Messer Forese da Rabatta mocking each other; and the story of Gillette of Narbonne (source for Shakespeare's All's Well That Ends Well). Boccaccio himself appears as a character defending the work from accusations of impropriety by telling a story about Filippo Balducci raising his son in a cave. Some tales are adapted from Sanskrit antecedents like the Panchatantra. Dedicated to women 'cooped up in the house all day.' Inspired Chaucer's Canterbury Tales, Marguerite of Navarre's Heptameron, Basile's Pentamerone, and Lessing's Nathan the Wise (parable of the three rings). Tzvetan Todorov analyzed it using Freudian theory, identifying conversion and avoiding punishment as two major plot structures. Wikipedia

Fiammetta Works

The character Fiammetta, thought to be an alias for Maria d'Aquino (a noblewoman Boccaccio was infatuated with), appears across many of Boccaccio's works including the Teseida, Il Filostrato, and The Decameron. The Elegy of Lady Fiammetta is a novel written as a confession of Fiammetta's love for the merchant Panfilo. On the day she first meets her lover, a flower falls from her crown as a bad omen. The character also appears in an adaptation of the legend of Floris and Blancheflour. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Giovanni Boccaccio is a core quizbowl Literature topic, almost always identified through his masterwork The Decameron and its relationship to Chaucer's Canterbury Tales. As one of the 'three crowns' of Italian literature (with Petrarch and Dante), Boccaccio is associated with the trecento and Italian humanism.

The Decameron dominates his clue profile. The frame story of ten Florentines (seven women and three men) fleeing the Black Plague and telling stories over ten days is the most common identifier. The collection's 100 novelle are told by named narrators including Panfilo, Pampinea, Filostrato, Neifile, and Dioneo, the last of whom has the special privilege of ignoring the day's assigned topic due to his wit. The tale of Patient Griselda, which closes the collection, is particularly frequently clued: her husband Gualtieri tests her loyalty by pretending to kill their children and staging a fake wedding to his 12-year-old daughter. This story was adapted by Chaucer as the 'Clerk's Tale.'

Other frequently referenced Decameron tales include: the innocent Alibech being seduced into 'putting the Devil back in hell'; a Chinese man shaming a rival by agreeing to swap identities; a judge solving poisoning deaths via a venomous toad; Alatiel passing through eight lovers; and Saladin's attempt to trick the Jewish Melchizedek about the three religions (the parable of the three rings, later adapted by Gotthold Lessing in Nathan the Wise). The story of Gillette of Narbonne inspired Shakespeare's All's Well That Ends Well. Boccaccio himself appears as a character in an interlude defending the work from impropriety charges, telling the story of Filippo Balducci raising his son in a cave (the 'gosling' story). The work was dedicated to women 'cooped up in the house all day.' Tzvetan Todorov analyzed the Decameron using Freudian theory. The Decameron directly inspired Marguerite of Navarre's Heptameron and Basile's Pentamerone.

On Famous Women (De Mulieribus Claris) is Boccaccio's second most-referenced work, primarily through its connection to Christine de Pizan's Book of the City of Ladies (which used it extensively as source material) and to Chaucer's Legend of Good Women.

The Teseida, a twelve-book poem about Palemone and Arcita fighting over Emily, was adapted by Chaucer as 'The Knight's Tale.' The Teseida begins with Theseus besieging the Scythians and marrying Hippolyta. Stanzas from it appear translated in Chaucer's unfinished Anelida and Arcite.

The character Fiammetta, thought to be an alias for Maria d'Aquino, recurs across Boccaccio's works. The Elegy of Lady Fiammetta is styled as a confession of Fiammetta's love for the merchant Panfilo. Il Filostrato tells the story of Troilus and Cressida, inspiring both Chaucer and Shakespeare.

Biographically, Boccaccio was a student of Petrarch and was mentored by the Augustinian monk Dionigi. Giosue Carducci characterized Boccaccio as looking 'outward' while Petrarch looked 'inward' and Dante 'upward.' He may have met Chaucer and Petrarch at Violante Visconti's wedding. He also wrote La Caccia di Diana, a catalogue of Neapolitan women, and the Filocolo, which inspired Chaucer's 'Franklin's Tale.'
