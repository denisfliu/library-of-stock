Portuguese poet and author, known for his extensive use of 'heteronyms' -- distinct literary alter egos with their own biographies and styles. Key heteronyms include Alberto Caeiro (the 'Master,' who wrote The Keeper of Sheep), Alvaro de Campos (a Glasgow-educated futurist naval engineer, who wrote 'The Tobacco Shop' and 'Opiario'), Ricardo Reis (an Epicurean doctor who wrote Horatian odes to Lydia), and Bernardo Soares (a semi-heteronym, the bookkeeper credited with The Book of Disquiet). Member of the Orpheu modernist movement. Wrote the poetry collection Mensagem (Message), structured around Portugal's coat of arms. Left a trunk of 25,000+ pages of manuscripts discovered posthumously. Jose Saramago fictionalized the heteronym Ricardo Reis in The Year of the Death of Ricardo Reis.
The Book of Disquiet

Pessoa's seminal work, a fragmentary 'factless autobiography' credited to the heteronym/semi-heteronym Bernardo Soares, a bookkeeper whose boss is named Vasques. Compiled from over 500 sheets found in a suitcase/trunk after Pessoa's death in 1935. Contains 'Dolorous Interludes' in English translation. Translated by Richard Zenith and Margaret Jull Costa, who struggled to order it. Many of the earliest poems are attributed to Vicente Guedes. Opens with 'My soul is a hidden orchestra' (in Margaret Jull Costa's chronological edition). Soares lives in a fourth-floor apartment and writes during rainy days. Contains the observation that having read The Pickwick Papers is a tragedy because one can't read it for the first time again. Includes 'Forest of Estrangement' and advice to 'unhappily married women' about imagining orgasms. Littered with expressions of saudade. Wikipedia

Heteronyms (General)

Pessoa's distinctive literary practice of creating over 75 distinct alter egos (heteronyms), each with their own biography, style, and worldview. Key heteronyms: Alberto Caeiro (the 'Master,' wrote The Keeper of Sheep, published a 'General Program' of neopaganism, died of tuberculosis in his mid-twenties), Alvaro de Campos (Glasgow-educated naval engineer, futurist-turned-sentimentalist, wrote 'The Tobacco Shop' and 'Opiario,' devotee of Walt Whitman), Ricardo Reis (an Epicurean doctor, wrote Greek-inspired odes to Lydia, exiled to Brazil after a failed 1919 rebellion, subject of Saramago's novel), and Bernardo Soares (semi-heteronym, bookkeeper, credited with The Book of Disquiet). Lesser-known heteronyms include the Baron of Tieve (committed suicide from insistence on living only by reason), the astrologer Raphael Baldaya, the Englishman Alexander Search (death at twenty commemorated in an epigraph), the detective Abilio Quaresma, and an English heteronym 'A.A. Crosse' (named for crossword puzzles). Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Fernando Pessoa (1888-1935), Portuguese poet. Member and best-known figure of the Orpheu modernist movement. As a teenager in Durban, South Africa, designed elaborate English-language newspapers filled with fake events. Richard Zenith is his major translator and biographer. Wrote the poem 'If I Die Young' ('roots may be hidden in the ground / But their flowers flower in the open air for all to see'). Wrote the poem 'Antinous' about 'the cold in Hadrian's soul.' Antonio Tabucchi wrote Requiem: A Hallucination featuring Pessoa's spirit as the Guest. Influenced by Gil Vicente ('the Portuguese Plautus'). Often clued alongside compatriot Jose Saramago and Portugal's national epic The Lusiads by Luis de Camoes.

Summary of Facts

Fernando Pessoa (1888-1935) is a Portuguese poet best known for his extensive use of 'heteronyms' -- over 75 distinct literary alter egos with their own biographies and styles -- and for his fragmentary masterwork The Book of Disquiet.

The Book of Disquiet is a 'factless autobiography' credited to the semi-heteronym Bernardo Soares, a bookkeeper whose boss is named Vasques. It was compiled from over 500 sheets found in a trunk after Pessoa's death in 1935 and translated by Richard Zenith and Margaret Jull Costa, who struggled to order the fragments. The work opens with 'My soul is a hidden orchestra' in Jull Costa's chronological edition. Soares lives in a fourth-floor apartment, writes during rainy days, collects books he never reads, and observes that having read The Pickwick Papers is a tragedy. The work also contains the 'Forest of Estrangement' piece and an 'Advice to Unhappily Married Women' section.

Pessoa's key heteronyms include Alberto Caeiro (the 'Master,' who wrote The Keeper of Sheep on a 'triumphant day,' published a neopaganism program, died of tuberculosis in his mid-twenties with the line 'I thank God I'm not good'); Alvaro de Campos (a Glasgow-educated naval engineer, futurist, devotee of Walt Whitman, who wrote 'The Tobacco Shop' -- 'How should I know what I'll be, I who don't know what I am?' -- and 'Opiario,' inspired by opium in the Suez Canal); Ricardo Reis (an Epicurean doctor who wrote Greek-inspired odes to Lydia, exiled to Brazil after a failed 1919 rebellion); and Bernardo Soares. Lesser-known heteronyms include the Baron of Tieve (suicided from insistence on living by reason alone), the astrologer Raphael Baldaya, Alexander Search (English, died at twenty), detective Abilio Quaresma, and 'A.A. Crosse' (named for crossword puzzles).

Ricardo Reis is the subject of Jose Saramago's novel The Year of the Death of Ricardo Reis, in which Reis returns from Brazil to Lisbon after Pessoa's death, converses with Pessoa's ghost, sleeps with the hotel maid Lydia, reads Herbert Quain's The God of the Labyrinth, meets Dr. Sampaio and his daughter Marcenda, and ultimately follows Pessoa into a cemetery.

Mensagem (Message) is a 44-poem collection structured around Portugal's coat of arms, containing the poem 'Ulysses' ('Myth is the nothing that is everything') and O Mostrengo about the spirit Adamastor.

Biographically, Pessoa was the best-known member of the Orpheu modernist movement. As a teenager in Durban, South Africa, he designed elaborate fake English-language newspapers. Richard Zenith is his major translator and biographer. He wrote the poem 'If I Die Young,' prophesying his posthumous legacy, and the poem 'Antinous' about 'the cold in Hadrian's soul.' Antonio Tabucchi's Requiem: A Hallucination features Pessoa's spirit as 'the Guest.' He is often clued alongside compatriot Saramago and Portugal's national epic The Lusiads by Camoes.
