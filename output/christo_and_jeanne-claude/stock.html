Bulgarian-French husband-and-wife duo known for monumental environmental art installations involving wrapping landmarks in fabric (empaquetage). Major works include the Wrapped Reichstag, Running Fence, The Gates in Central Park, The Umbrellas, and Surrounded Islands. Part of the Nouveau Réalisme movement. They flew in separate planes so one could continue their work if the other died.

Summary of Facts

Christo and Jeanne-Claude are a husband-and-wife artistic duo best known for monumental temporary environmental installations involving the wrapping of landmarks and landscapes in fabric, a practice they called empaquetage. They were part of the Nouveau Réalisme movement founded by Yves Klein.

Their most frequently clued work is the Wrapped Reichstag (1995), in which they covered the German parliament building in Berlin with over 100,000 square meters of polypropylene fabric fastened with blue rope. This is essentially a giveaway-level clue that appears in nearly every question about the duo. The Gates (2005), an installation of over 7,500 saffron-colored vinyl gates along pathways in Central Park, New York, is the second most commonly referenced work. Running Fence (1976), a 24.5-mile-long nylon fence in California that was disassembled after just 14 days, also appears frequently.

Less commonly clued but still important works include The Umbrellas (1991), a simultaneous installation of blue umbrellas in Japan and yellow umbrellas in California that was tragically dismantled early after one toppled and killed a person; Surrounded Islands (1983), which extended the perimeter of Biscayne Bay islands with pink fabric after removing forty tons of garbage; Wrapped Coast (1969), where they wrapped 1.5 miles of Australia's Little Bay coastline, making it the largest artwork in the world at the time; and the Wrapped Pont-Neuf (1985) bridge in Paris.

Biographical clues to note: they flew in separate planes so one could continue their work if the other died, and initially only Christo received credit for their joint work. They are frequently compared to Robert Smithson and other land/earth artists. Questions about their work often appear not as direct answerlines but as clues within tossups about locations (Berlin, California, Central Park) or materials (fabric, fences), making cross-referencing essential.
