Summary of Facts

Albert Gleizes was a French Cubist painter and theorist, best known for co-writing Du "Cubisme" (On Cubism, 1912) with Jean Metzinger — the first major theoretical manifesto on Cubism. The text argues that Cubist art is grounded in non-Euclidean (Riemannian) geometry rather than Euclidean geometry, and Gleizes and Metzinger began collaborating after the Salon d'Automne. Guillaume Apollinaire, in his 1913 book The Cubist Painters: Aesthetic Meditations, described Gleizes as representing the 'scientific' branch of Cubism, in contrast to Robert Delaunay's 'orphic' style. The manifesto reproduced Gleizes's large painting The Bathers, which depicts working-class bathers in front of factories with swirling smoke from Clichy in the background; it was set at La Grande Jatte (one of five Gleizes paintings of that location) and was exhibited at the 1912 Salon des Independants. Other key works include Portrait of Jacques Nayral (a frequent giveaway identifier), La Chasse (The Hunt) — depicting a man with a golden hunting horn and hunting dogs — Woman with Phlox (described as almost entirely grey), and Harvest Threshing, one of the largest Cubist paintings alongside Delaunay's La Ville de Paris. Gleizes was an advocate of Crystal Cubism. He was a founding member of the Section d'Or (Golden Section, also known as the Puteaux Group), a Cubist collective associated with both Cubism and Orphism whose meetings were held at Gleizes's home; the name was coined by Josephin Peladan for his fascination with the quantity phi. Gleizes and Metzinger infamously rejected Marcel Duchamp's Nude Descending a Staircase No. 2 from the 1912 Salon des Independants, believing the nude should be shown reclining rather than in motion. Gleizes also chronicled the fortunes of the Salon d'Automne in a work called The Epic: From Immobile Form to Mobile Form. He and Jacques Villon made money from the 1913 Armory Show. The forger John Myatt began his career after his mountain village painting in Gleizes's style was accepted by Christie's. In one painting, Gleizes depicted a woman whose hair is rendered as concentric black-and-white arcs, and who holds a black glove.
