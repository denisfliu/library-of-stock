General / Biographical

Imogen Cunningham (1883–1976) was an American photographer and co-founder of Group f/64 alongside Ansel Adams, Edward Weston, Willard Van Dyke, and Sonya Noskowiak. She championed street photography late in life, alongside (the unrelated) Bill Cunningham. Prints of her work feature Chinese stamps that phonetically spell her name and translate as 'Ideas Without End.' She encouraged spontaneity in street photography by arguing that if Cartier-Bresson asked his subject to step over the puddle again for Behind the Gare Saint-Lazare, the decisive moment would be ruined. Summary of Facts

Imogen Cunningham (1883–1976) was an American photographer best known for her close-up botanical photographs of flowers. Her most famous botanical works include Magnolia Blossom (1925, showing a white magnolia in extreme close-up), Two Callas, and Triangles (a close-up of a nude woman's body forming the title shapes). She also took photograms of plants without a camera, placing them directly on photosensitive paper.

Cunningham was a co-founder of Group f/64, the San Francisco-based photography collective whose members included Ansel Adams, Edward Weston, Willard Van Dyke, and Sonya Noskowiak. The group was named for the f/64 camera aperture setting, which produces maximum depth of field and sharpness. She also photographed Martha Graham in a famous 1931 portrait showing the dancer pressing both hands to her head/face.

Cunningham herself is the subject of a famous photograph by her protégée Judy Dater, taken at Yosemite National Park during an Ansel Adams workshop. In Dater's image, the elderly Cunningham is shown peeking at or surprised by nude model Twinka Thiebaud standing behind a tree — parodying Thomas Hart Benton's painting Persephone.

Late in life, Cunningham championed street photography alongside the unrelated Bill Cunningham (the New York Times fashion photographer). She argued for spontaneity in the genre, suggesting that if Cartier-Bresson had asked his subject to step over the puddle again for Behind the Gare Saint-Lazare, the decisive moment would have been ruined. Prints of her work feature Chinese stamps that phonetically spell out her name and translate as 'Ideas Without End.'
