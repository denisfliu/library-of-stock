Opera in three acts by Giuseppe Verdi, premiered at La Fenice, Venice in 1851. Based on Victor Hugo's controversial play Le roi s'amuse (libretto by Francesco Piave). The title character is a hunchbacked court jester in the service of the rakish Duke of Mantua. When Count Monterone curses Rigoletto, the jester's daughter Gilda falls victim to the Duke's seductions; Rigoletto hires the assassin Sparafucile to kill the Duke, but Gilda sacrifices herself instead. Famous arias include 'La donna è mobile,' 'Caro nome,' 'Pari siamo!,' and the quartet 'Bella figlia dell'amore.'
Rigoletto – The Opera as a Whole

Three-act Italian opera by Giuseppe Verdi, premiered at Teatro La Fenice in Venice in 1851. The libretto by Francesco Piave adapts Victor Hugo's play Le roi s'amuse — a source Hugo himself considered plagiaristic. The opera follows Rigoletto, a hunchbacked court jester to the Duke of Mantua, who after Count Monterone's curse realizes he cannot protect his daughter Gilda from the Duke's predations. It ends with Rigoletto opening a sack to find his dying daughter, having been tricked by his assassin. Michael Mayer's 2013 Met production relocated the opera to a Las Vegas casino in the 1960s. 'La donna è mobile'

The Duke of Mantua's Act III aria, in which he compares women to feathers in the wind ('flighty/fickle'). Sung in B major in 3/8 time; the last measure of the 8-bar melody is frequently left silent by the orchestra. So catchy that tenor Raffaele Mirate was forbidden from humming it in public before the premiere. Rigoletto hears a distant reprise of this aria after receiving the sack he believes contains the Duke's body — revealing that Gilda was killed instead. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Rigoletto is a three-act opera by Giuseppe Verdi that premiered at Teatro La Fenice in Venice on March 11, 1851. Its libretto, written by Francesco Piave, adapts Victor Hugo's 1832 play Le roi s'amuse — a source Hugo allegedly regarded as plagiaristic. To satisfy Austrian censors, the character based on Francis I was changed from a French king to the fictional Duke of Mantua, and the setting moved to Italy.

The title character is a hunchbacked court jester in service to the Duke of Mantua, a serial seducer who opens the opera with the aria 'Questa o quella,' proclaiming that 'this woman or that woman' are all the same to him. When the elderly Count Monterone confronts the Duke over the seduction of his daughter, and Rigoletto mocks him, Monterone places a curse on both the Duke and the jester. This curse — 'La maledizione!' — hangs over the entire opera, and Rigoletto's last words echo it.

Rigoletto is desperately protective of his daughter Gilda, keeping her sheltered from the court. The Duke, however, bribes her chaperon Giovanna and seduces Gilda while disguised as a poor student named 'Gualtier Malde.' In Act I Gilda rhapsodizes about this fake name in her aria 'Caro nome.' Meanwhile, Rigoletto sings 'Pari siamo!,' comparing his own weapon — his sharp tongue — to that of the assassin Sparafucile: 'We are alike.' The courtiers, led by Marullo, blindfold Rigoletto and dupe him into steadying a ladder as they kidnap Gilda from her bedroom, singing the chorus 'Zitti, zitti.' The Duke enjoys her as a conquest.

In Act III, set at an inn run by Sparafucile and his sister Maddalena, the Duke woos Maddalena with the same words he used on Gilda. All four characters react simultaneously in the famous quartet 'Bella figlia dell'amore,' as Gilda watches heartbroken and Rigoletto vows revenge. Rigoletto has hired Sparafucile to kill the Duke. Sparafucile agrees to kill the first person who enters the inn that night, and Gilda — still in love with the Duke — sacrifices herself by entering first. She is stabbed and placed in a sack. As Rigoletto gloats over what he thinks is the Duke's body, he hears a distant reprise of 'La donna è mobile' — the Duke's Act III aria declaring women to be like a feather in the wind — sung by the still-living Duke. Rigoletto tears open the sack to find his dying daughter, and dies crying 'La maledizione!'

'La donna è mobile' was so memorable that tenor Raffaele Mirate was forbidden from humming it in public before the premiere. It is in B major, 3/8 time, with a melody that begins with three accented eighth-note D-sharps; the last measure of the main 8-bar melody is traditionally left silent by the orchestra. Julian Budden described the opera's 'scene and storm trio' — featuring an offstage male chorus humming wordlessly in chromatic parallel thirds to simulate wind — as being 'without antecedent' in operatic history. Franz Liszt included a concert paraphrase of the quartet among his piano arrangements. Michael Mayer's 2013 Met production relocated the opera to a 1960s Las Vegas casino.
