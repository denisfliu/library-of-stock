American feminist art historian, often called the first feminist art historian. Best known for her landmark 1971 essay 'Why Have There Been No Great Women Artists?' which argues that artistic greatness is not inherent genius but is determined by social, cultural, and psychological factors. Also wrote extensively on 19th-century French art, including the monograph Realism, the book Courbet, and the essay 'The Imaginary Orient' about Jean-Leon Gerome's The Snake Charmer. Her other works include Bathers, Bodies, Beauty and 'Women, Art, and Power.'
"Why Have There Been No Great Women Artists?" (1971)

Nochlin's most famous essay, published in 1971. Argues that the question itself hides an implicit bias by portraying women as a 'problem,' and that artistic achievement is not the result of inherent genius but is determined by social, cultural, and psychological factors. The essay includes sections on 'The Question of the Nude' and 'Successes,' and proposes that an 'erroneous intellectual substructure' underlies the assumption that great art is 'miraculous, nondetermined, and asocial.' Summary of Facts

Linda Nochlin is an American art historian widely regarded as the first feminist art historian. She is best known for her groundbreaking 1971 essay 'Why Have There Been No Great Women Artists?', which argues that the question itself conceals an implicit bias by treating women as a 'problem' and that artistic greatness is not the product of inherent genius but is shaped by social, cultural, and psychological factors. The essay describes artistic achievement as commonly but erroneously viewed as 'miraculous, nondetermined, and asocial.' Nochlin uses the example of Cimabue discovering the young Giotto as a shepherd boy to illustrate the myth of innate genius, and notes that many great artists had fathers who were artists (including Picasso). The essay includes a section on 'The Question of the Nude,' discussing the historical ban on women painting from nude models and citing Johann Zoffany's group portrait of the Royal Academy, in which the female members Angelica Kauffman and Mary Moser are depicted only as portraits on the wall. The essay concludes with an extensive discussion of Rosa Bonheur and The Horse Fair, arguing that institutional support was necessary for artistic achievement. It also mentions Judith Leyster. A follow-up essay, 'Thirty Years After,' examines the continuing impact of artists like Louise Bourgeois. Barbara Kruger's exhibition 'Picturing Greatness' was conceptually related to this essay.

Nochlin's other major essay is 'The Imaginary Orient,' which analyzes Jean-Leon Gerome's The Snake Charmer as an exemplar of Orientalist painting. She argues that Gerome's extreme realism tricks viewers into thinking his paintings depict real events, when they actually present Western fantasies of the East. She describes The Snake Charmer as having an 'absence of history' and being an 'insistent, sexually charged mystery,' with ten 'resolutely alienated' men watching with 'childish, trancelike concentration.' The essay also discusses Delacroix's The Death of Sardanapalus as an example of using the East to portray sadistic sexual desires. This essay was collected in The Politics of Vision.

Nochlin wrote the influential monograph Realism on 19th-century French art, which opens by examining Courbet's The Meeting as 'A Portrait of the Artist as a Wandering Jew.' She also wrote a separate book titled Courbet; Courbet's The Wounded Man provides the cover for this monograph. Her study of Realism contrasts Manet's depiction of the demi-monde with the prudish moral commentary of William Holman Hunt and Augustus Egg. She explains Courbet's The Wheat Sifters as representing agricultural progress.

Her 2006 book Bathers, Bodies, Beauty examines how the 'visceral eye' views bathers in art, discusses the 'death of the heroic landscape,' and includes analysis of Jenny Saville's Matrix. In this work, chapters on the nude alternate with chapters on nature.

Among her other contributions, Nochlin wrote 'Women, Art, and Power' which discusses Velazquez's Rokeby Venus and Hannah Hoch's Pretty Girl; an essay on Cezanne titled 'Studies in Contrast' about old peasants with 'huge, sagging bottoms' meant to highlight impotency; an essay on Berthe Morisot analyzing the 'Construction of Work and Leisure'; and she argued against the Hockney-Falco thesis by pointing out the realistic texture of wedding gowns. She wrote about Edward Hopper's windows as tending to 'lock the static figures into place.' She revived Florine Stettheimer's reputation in the 1980s. Kathleen Gilje reimagined Manet's A Bar at the Folies-Bergere with Nochlin as the central figure. She also supported Lewis Mumford's interpretation of Georgia O'Keeffe's flower paintings as sexual.
