French Rococo sculptor (1716–1791), best known for The Bronze Horseman — a monumental equestrian statue of Peter the Great in St. Petersburg, commissioned by Catherine the Great and mounted on the massive Thunder Stone. Also produced Cupid sculptures (Mischievous/Menacing Love) and an early celebrated Milo of Croton. Worked at the Sèvres porcelain manufactory; contributed 'Reflections on Sculpture' to Diderot's Encyclopédie. His daughter-in-law Marie-Anne Collot sculpted the face of Peter the Great.
The Bronze Horseman (1782) →

Falconet's masterpiece — a large bronze equestrian statue of Peter the Great commissioned by Catherine the Great. Situated in Senate Square, St. Petersburg, it stands atop the Thunder Stone, a 1,250-ton granite monolith (the largest single stone ever moved by humans), transported via a bronze-sphere sled devised by Marinos Carburis. The head of Peter was sculpted by Falconet's student and daughter-in-law Marie-Anne Collot. The statue became the title of a famous Alexander Pushkin poem. Peter the Great is shown trampling a snake. Cupid Sculptures: Mischievous Cupid / Menacing Love

A series of small Cupid figures closely associated with Fragonard's Rococo paintings. In Menacing Love (also called Mischievous Cupid), a seated Cupid holds a finger to his lips — a stone copy of this figure appears prominently in Fragonard's The Swing on the left side of the canvas. These works, discussed in Mary Sheriff's Moved by Love, depict a cupid kissing the hand of a woman on a pedestal while her creator kneels in front of her. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Étienne Maurice Falconet (1716–1791) was the preeminent French sculptor of the Rococo movement. His fame in quizbowl rests almost entirely on The Bronze Horseman (1782), an enormous equestrian statue of Peter the Great commissioned by Catherine the Great and erected in Senate Square, St. Petersburg. The work is chiefly identified by its pedestal — the Thunder Stone, a 1,250-ton granite monolith transported across the Finnish countryside using a bronze-sphere sled devised by Greek engineer Marinos Carburis, making it the largest single stone ever moved by humans. The head of Peter the Great was sculpted not by Falconet himself but by his student and daughter-in-law Marie-Anne Collot. Peter is depicted trampling a snake beneath his horse's hooves. The statue later gave its title to Alexander Pushkin's famous narrative poem. Denis Diderot, Falconet's close friend and collaborator, suggested the commission to Catherine the Great.

Before his Russian period, Falconet built his reputation in Paris. He trained under Jean-Baptiste Lemoyne and won early renown with a sculpture of Milo of Croton (Pierre Puget created a competing version showing Milo struggling with a lion). He was patronized by Madame de Pompadour and worked at the Sèvres porcelain manufactory, where he created his Bather and small figurines of children based on François Boucher paintings. He contributed the 'Reflections on Sculpture' entry to Diderot's Encyclopédie, which he also published separately. His sculpture Pygmalion and Galatea is referenced in harder clues alongside the Encyclopédie connection.

Falconet's Cupid sculptures — particularly Menacing Love (also called Mischievous Cupid), in which a seated Cupid holds a finger to his lips — appear prominently in the paintings of his Rococo contemporary Jean-Honoré Fragonard. A stone copy of the Menacing Love figure can be seen on the left side of Fragonard's famous The Swing, overlooking the flirtatious scene; these works are discussed in Mary Sheriff's monograph Moved by Love.
