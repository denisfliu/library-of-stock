Summary of Facts

John Donne is a Metaphysical English poet and Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral. His most frequently clued work is "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning," written for his wife Anne More, which compares parting lovers to "stiff twin compasses" with the line "Thy firmness makes my circle just." The poem opens "As virtuous men pass mildly away" and criticizes "dull sublunary lovers' love."

His Holy Sonnets are heavily clued. "Death, be not proud" declares Death is "slave to fate, chance, kings, and desperate men." "Batter my heart, three-person'd God" asks God to "break, blow, burn and make me new" and ends "nor ever chaste, except you ravish me," with Reason described as a weak "viceroy." "I am a little world made cunningly" asks God to "pour new seas in mine eyes."

"The Canonization" opens "For God's sake hold your tongue, and let me love" and contains "a well-wrought urn becomes / The greatest ashes," inspiring Cleanth Brooks's book title. "The Flea" uses a creature that "sucked me first, and now sucks thee." "To His Mistress Going to Bed" calls the lover's body "my America! my new-found-land."

From Songs and Sonnets, "Go and catch a falling star" lists impossible tasks. "The Good-Morrow" opens "I wonder, by my troth, what thou and I did, till we loved?" Devotions upon Emergent Occasions contains "no man is an island" and "for whom the bell tolls," providing Hemingway's title. Donne secretly married Anne More and delivered "Death's Duel" as his final sermon. Joseph Brodsky wrote an elegy describing Donne as having "sunk in sleep."
