Summary of Facts

The topic 'Metropolis' in Fine Arts quizbowl primarily refers to two works: Fritz Lang's 1927 German Expressionist silent film and Otto Dix's 1928 New Objectivity triptych painting.

Fritz Lang's Metropolis (1927) is one of the most significant silent science fiction films ever made. The film is set in a future dystopian city (the 'Metropolis') where the wealthy elite live above ground while workers toil in underground factories. The tallest structure is the Bruegel-inspired New Tower of Babel. The central conflict involves the inventor Rotwang, who constructs a 'machine human' — a robot — in the likeness of the underground workers' leader, Maria. The protagonist Freder (son of the city's ruler) witnesses a hallucination of workers being fed into the machine-god Moloch as they pull levers resembling clock faces. The film's cinematography was made possible partly by the Schufftan process, a technique invented by Eugen Schufftan that combines miniatures with mirrors to create the illusion of massive scale. A significant portion of the original film was long considered lost, until the missing reels were discovered in Buenos Aires in 2008. The film is sometimes screened with a score by Giorgio Moroder. Two notable posters were made for it: one by Heinz Schulz-Neudamm (the most expensive film poster ever sold) and one by Boris Balinsky (inspired by Piranesi). American artist Chris Burden paid homage with his kinetic sculpture 'Metropolis II,' which used Lego bricks, Lincoln Logs, and thousands of model cars. In the history of science fiction film, the film stands alongside works like Ridley Scott's Blade Runner and Jean-Luc Godard's Alphaville as an influence on the genre.

Otto Dix's Metropolis (1928) is a triptych painting in the New Objectivity style, depicting the social contradictions of Weimar-era Germany. The large central panel shows a glamorous ball scene where well-off society women dance the Charleston to a jazz band — musicians including Alfred Schulze on saxophone play tubas and saxophones in mid-motion. The flanking outer panels show the dark reverse of this prosperity: crippled World War I veterans reduced to begging on wooden peg legs, ignored by passing prostitutes in fur coats. Dix included a self-portrait on the left panel, depicting himself as one of these disabled veterans with two wooden peg legs, attacked by a dog — a reference to themes he also explored in his painting The Trench and his series War. Other figures include a hefty blonde woman with her right breast exposed through a pink coat, clutching a wad of cash, and a man in a dress and pearls waving a pink ostrich feather. The triptych shares its title with the Fritz Lang film.

Michael Daugherty's only symphony is also named Metropolis, described as a 'fantasy in blue'; Ferde Grofé wrote an earlier 'fantasy in blue' piece sharing this city name.
