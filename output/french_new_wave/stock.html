Summary of Facts

The French New Wave (Nouvelle Vague) was an influential French film movement of the late 1950s and 1960s that emphasized innovative techniques — handheld cameras, natural lighting, jump cuts, and non-professional actors — and auteur-driven filmmaking. Jean Rouch's Moi, un Noir was explicitly cited as inspiring the movement through its use of jump cuts and non-professional actors. Directors associated with the movement include Jean-Luc Godard, Francois Truffaut, Agnes Varda, Chris Marker, Alain Resnais, and Jacques Demy.

The movement is divided into two factions: the Right Bank, which included more popular and commercially successful directors like Godard and Truffaut; and the Left Bank, which included the more experimental Chris Marker, Alain Resnais, and Agnes Varda (married to Jacques Demy). Jean Vigo was a key predecessor; the Prix Jean Vigo, a prize named for him, was won by Ousmane Sembene for a Wolof-language film.

The seminal film of the movement is Breathless (A bout de souffle, 1960) by Jean-Luc Godard, in which a Humphrey Bogart-obsessed protagonist named Michel kills a police officer after being caught with a stolen car, in a famous jarring five-shot sequence. The 400 Blows (Les Quatre Cents Coups, 1959) by Truffaut is the other canonical film. Godard also directed Pierrot le Fou and Vivre sa Vie (both starring then-wife Anna Karina) and Alphaville (a neo-noir). Neo-noir films like Le Samourai and Alphaville are called 'neo-noir' within the New Wave tradition.

The French New Wave directly influenced multiple national cinema movements, including the Taiwanese New Wave (Edward Yang, Ang Lee) and had wide international influence.
