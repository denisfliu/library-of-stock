English analytic philosopher, author of Principia Ethica. Introduced the naturalistic fallacy and the open question argument. Known for the 'here is one hand' proof of an external world, Moore's paradox, the paradox of analysis, and his defense of common sense against British idealism. Cambridge colleague of Bertrand Russell.
Principia Ethica & the Naturalistic Fallacy

Moore's major work in ethics (1903), which argues that 'good' is an irreducible, indefinable property — like the color yellow. The 'naturalistic fallacy' is committed when someone attempts to define 'good' in terms of natural properties. The 'open question argument' supports this: for any proposed definition of good, it remains an open question whether that definition is truly good. Moore praises Henry Sidgwick for not committing the naturalistic fallacy and attacks Herbert Spencer's social Darwinism. He accuses John Stuart Mill of committing the fallacy in Utilitarianism. Moore advocated 'ideal utilitarianism' (with Hastings Rashdall), according to which more than just pleasure should be maximized.

Proof of an External World & A Defence of Common Sense

Moore's famous 'here is one hand' argument, from Proof of an External World, inverts a classical skeptical argument by holding up his hand to prove external objects exist. A Defence of Common Sense opens with a list of 'truisms' like 'there exists at present a living human body' and delineates three types of mental facts. Wittgenstein's On Certainty responds to Moore's arguments. Changing a modus ponens to a modus tollens is sometimes called 'Moore's shift' (coined by William Rowe).

Moore's Paradox

Moore's paradox concerns sentences like 'It's raining, but I don't believe it's raining' — statements that are logically consistent but pragmatically absurd. This paradox is a frequent clue in quizbowl, often used as an identifier for Moore.

General / Biographical

Moore was a Cambridge colleague of Bertrand Russell. He wrote 'The Refutation of Idealism,' which claimed that the 'sensation of blue' is an objective, non-experience-dependent fact. His non-naturalism is usually construed as part of moral realism. Russell attacked Moore's consequentialism in a thought experiment about mothers of monstrous men.

Summary of Facts

G.E. Moore is an English analytic philosopher best known for Principia Ethica (1903), which argues that 'good' is an irreducible, indefinable property like the color yellow. The 'naturalistic fallacy' — the error of defining 'good' in terms of natural properties — is the central contribution of the work, supported by the 'open question argument.' Moore praises Henry Sidgwick for not committing the fallacy while attacking John Stuart Mill's Utilitarianism for committing it (the desirable/visible analogy). He also attacked Herbert Spencer's social Darwinism and advocated 'ideal utilitarianism' with Hastings Rashdall.

Moore's 'here is one hand' argument (from Proof of an External World) inverts a classical skeptical argument by holding up his hand to prove external objects exist. A Defence of Common Sense opens with a list of 'truisms' and attacks British idealists. Wittgenstein's On Certainty responds to Moore's arguments. The 'Moore's shift' (coined by William Rowe) describes changing a modus ponens to a modus tollens.

Moore's paradox concerns sentences like 'It's raining, but I don't believe it's raining' — logically consistent but pragmatically absurd. He also formulated the paradox of analysis (definitions cannot be both correct and informative). Moore was a Cambridge colleague of Bertrand Russell and wrote 'The Refutation of Idealism,' which claimed the 'sensation of blue' is an objective fact.
