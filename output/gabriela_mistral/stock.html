Chilean poet and the first Latin American Nobel laureate in Literature (1945). Real name Lucila Godoy Alcayaga. Her pseudonym combines the Italian poet Gabriele D'Annunzio with the Occitan poet Frederic Mistral (1904 Nobel winner), who wrote Mireio. Best known for Sonnets of Death, inspired by the suicide of her lover, the railway worker Romelio Ureta. Other major collections include Desolacion, Tala (mourning her mother's death), Ternura (Tenderness, focused on children), and Lagar (influenced by the suicide of her nephew Yin-Yin). Also known for 'Decalogue of the Artist' and 'Those Who Do Not Dance.' Partner of Doris Dana, her lover and translator.
Sonnets of Death (Sonetos de la Muerte)

Mistral's most famous work, a sequence of three Petrarchan sonnets inspired by the 1909 suicide of her lover, the railway worker Romelio Ureta. Won the Chilean 'Floral Games' literary contest in 1914. The first sonnet opens 'From the frozen/icy niche where men placed you, I will lower you to the humble, sunny earth.' The second sonnet promises to 'leave singing my beautiful vengeance' and states 'we must dream on the same pillow.' The third sonnet confronts God ('You are going to judge me, you know it, Lord!') and describes 'wicked hands' that took his life; the 'rosy/pink boat of his life stopped.' Ursula K. Le Guin omitted these from her translation, saying she was unable to 'feel with' them. Included in the collection Desolacion. Some translations keep the word 'sinos' intact. Wikipedia

"Decalogue of the Artist"

A ten-stanza poem consisting of instructions for artists. Opens with 'You shall love beauty, which is the shadow of God over the Universe.' Tala

Mistral's third poetry collection, written after the death of her mother (Petronila Alcayaga). The title refers to felling trees. Opens with eight poems about the mother's death ('Death of My Mother' cycle), in which the speaker follows her late mother through a series of black mountains / 'purplish landscapes.' The four poems are titled for the Nights of Defeat, Descent, Consummation, and the Old Weavers. A 'Matter' section contains poems about bread, salt, and water. The poem 'Lapida Filial' describes the mother's 'steadfast eyes,' 'wide lap,' and 'beloved breasts' and connects her to 'Anna, Elizabeth, Leah, and Rachel!' 'The Flight' begs the mother to 'Rise up! General / Biographical

Mistral's real name was Lucila Godoy Alcayaga. Her pseudonym combines an Italian poet (Gabriele D'Annunzio, though some sources say it is a personal name) with the Occitan poet Frederic Mistral, who wrote Mireio and won the 1904 Nobel. She was the first Latin American Nobel laureate in Literature (1945), cited as 'a symbol of the idealistic aspirations of the entire Latin American world.' She identified as 'Mestiza de Vasco' due to mixed Amerindian-Basque heritage. Chilean. Her nephew and adopted son Yin-Yin committed suicide, influencing the collection Lagar. Langston Hughes translated her poems (1957, after her death), grouped into 'Cradle Songs' and 'Poems for Mothers.' Doris Dana was her lover and translator; Dana wrote The Elephant and His Secret. Alfonsina Storni was her friend. She taught children, influencing Ternura. She moved to Pablo Neruda's hometown of Temuco and read his poems there. Victoria Ocampo was a correspondent. Her poetry often focuses on mothers, children, and loss.

Summary of Facts

Gabriela Mistral (real name Lucila Godoy Alcayaga) is a Chilean poet and the first Latin American Nobel laureate in Literature (1945). Her pen name combines an Italian poet with the Occitan poet Frederic Mistral, who wrote Mireio and won the 1904 Nobel Prize.

Her most frequently clued work by far is the Sonnets of Death (Sonetos de la Muerte), a sequence of three Petrarchan sonnets inspired by the 1909 suicide of her lover, the railway worker Romelio Ureta. The first sonnet opens with the iconic line 'From the frozen/icy niche where men placed you, I will lower you to the humble, sunny earth.' The second promises to 'leave singing my beautiful vengeance' and insists 'we must dream on the same pillow.' The third confronts God ('You are going to judge me, you know it, Lord!') and describes 'wicked hands' that took his life; it ends with 'the rosy/pink boat of his life stopped.' The sonnets won the Chilean 'Floral Games' literary contest in 1914. Ursula K. Le Guin omitted them from her translation of Mistral's Selected Poems, saying she was unable to 'feel with' them.

The 'Decalogue of the Artist' is a ten-stanza poem giving instructions for artists, opening with 'You shall love beauty, which is the shadow of God over the Universe.' It also declares 'Beauty shall not be an opiate but a strong wine that fires you to action,' 'there is no godless art,' and 'beauty is virginal and is not to be found at carnival or fair.'

Desolacion (Desolation) is Mistral's debut collection, which contains the Sonnets of Death. A poem beginning 'A son, a son, a son! I wanted a son of yours' was referenced in her Nobel citation. The 'children poems' later extracted to form Ternura were originally part of this collection.

Tala is Mistral's third collection, mourning the death of her mother, Petronila Alcayaga. Its title refers to felling trees. It opens with eight poems about the mother's death, in which the speaker follows her through 'black mountains' and 'countries the color of a bruise.' The four Nights poems are titled for Defeat, Descent, Consummation, and the Old Weavers. A 'Matter' section contains poems about bread, salt, and water. 'Lapida Filial' memorializes the mother's 'steadfast eyes,' 'wide lap,' and 'beloved breasts.'

Other notable poems include 'Those Who Do Not Dance' (inviting a cripple and invalid to dance; ending 'the heart of him who joins us not / Is turned to dust'), 'Tiny Feet' (about children's bruised feet), and 'To See Him Again.' Mistral often wrote about children ('our worst crime is abandoning our children / his name is today') and mothers.

Biographically, Mistral identified as 'Mestiza de Vasco' due to mixed Amerindian-Basque heritage. Langston Hughes translated her poems in 1957, grouping them as 'Cradle Songs' and 'Poems for Mothers.' Doris Dana was her lover and translator, who wrote The Elephant and His Secret. Alfonsina Storni was her friend. Her nephew and adopted son Yin-Yin's suicide influenced the collection Lagar. She moved to Pablo Neruda's hometown of Temuco. Victoria Ocampo was a correspondent. She also wrote an essay on 'tropicalismo,' calling it 'literary babble' regarding Jose Marti's poems.
