Polish filmmaker known for the TV miniseries Dekalog (1988), The Double Life of Veronique (1991), and the Three Colours trilogy (Blue, White, Red — 1993–94). The Three Colours films are themed on the principles of the French tricolor: liberty, equality, fraternity. His regular composer was Zbigniew Preisner. Died while writing a trilogy inspired by Dante's Divine Comedy.

The first film of the Three Colours trilogy, themed on liberty. Julie (Juliette Binoche) loses her husband — the composer Patrice de Courcy — and their daughter in a car accident in the opening. Summary of Facts

Krzysztof Kieślowski (1941–1996) was a Polish filmmaker best known for the TV miniseries Dekalog (1988) and the Three Colours trilogy (Blue, White, Red — 1993–94). His regular composer was Zbigniew Preisner, and together they created the fictional Dutch composer Van den Budenmayer, who appears in several of their works. At the time of his death, Kieślowski was writing a trilogy of scripts inspired by Dante's Divine Comedy.

The Three Colours trilogy is themed on the three principles of the French revolutionary motto (and the colors of the French flag): liberty (Blue), equality (White), and fraternity (Red). Three Colours: Blue (1993) opens with a car accident that kills composer Patrice de Courcy and his daughter, leaving Juliette Binoche's character Julie to grieve. The film ends with a tear rolling down Julie's eye during a montage scored with Preisner's 'Song for the Unification of Europe.'

Three Colours: White (1994) follows a Polish hairdresser, Karol, who smuggles himself in a suitcase from France back to Poland to exact revenge on his ex-wife (Julie Delpy), who divorced him for impotence. A protagonist stumbles upon two hairdressers from Poland and France who are divorcing for the same reason.

Three Colours: Red (1994) stars Irene Jacob as Valentine, a model in Geneva who befriends an elderly judge who secretly spies via telephone on his neighbors. A fast-paced montage dollying along phone lines opens the film. Both male protagonists gaze at Valentine's profile on a chewing gum poster while driving. A shot at a bowling alley pans to a broken glass, implying a man's rage at infidelity. The final scene has a storm wrecking a ferry on the English Channel, leaving seven characters from the trilogy to survive. The final shot replicates Valentine's chewing gum poster. Red was Kieślowski's last mention of Van den Budenmayer.

The Double Life of Véronique (1991) has Irene Jacob playing both a Polish soprano and a French music teacher, both named Veronique, whose lives run parallel. A green-tinged sequence shows the music teacher watching a marionette show. The camera pans from a man taking off headphones in a store to the protagonist listening to the same song.

Dekalog (1988) is a ten-episode TV miniseries set in a late-Communist Warsaw housing project, each episode loosely inspired by one of the Ten Commandments. An unnamed witness character (Artur Barcis) appears in all but two installments. The first episode involves a boy drowning in a skating accident despite computer calculations showing the ice was thick enough; a subplot about sour milk introduces a recurring motif. In the second episode, a dying man is revived when a bee crawls out of a glass of strawberry syrup. Two episodes were expanded into A Short Film About Killing and A Short Film About Love — the latter follows a post office worker who spies on an older woman.
