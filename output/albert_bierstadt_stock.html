Summary of Facts

Albert Bierstadt was a German-American landscape painter associated with both the Hudson River School and the Rocky Mountain School. His masterpiece, The Rocky Mountains, Lander's Peak (1863), depicts a Shoshone encampment with wigwams by a waterfall, based on sketches from the Honey Road Survey Party. It was exhibited for 25 cents at a private venue in Boston and is frequently compared to Frederic Edwin Church's Heart of the Andes due to their similar central waterfalls.

The Last of the Buffalo (c. 1888) depicts a Native American on a rearing horse attacking a buffalo. It was rejected from the 1889 Paris Exposition Universelle as 'old-fashioned,' which marked a career lowlight after which Bierstadt mostly stopped painting. Among the Sierra Nevada, California (1868) shows elk dwarfed by mountains.

Bierstadt sold a painting to English railroad tycoon James McHenry for $25,000, with which he built his estate Malkasten. He was friends with philosopher Charles Sanders Peirce, enlisting him in invention schemes like transformable train cars. Congress refused to pay $80,000 for Capitol paintings, so he staged an unauthorized exhibition in the House Chamber. A shipwreck ended his Alaskan journey, inspiring The Wreck of the Ancon. His Roman Fish Market borrows from the Barberini Faun. He occasionally painted in the Luminist style and has a lake in Colorado named after him. Late-career tropical works include The Shore of the Turquoise Sea.
