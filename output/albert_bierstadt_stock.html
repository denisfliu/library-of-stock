Summary of Facts

Albert Bierstadt (1830-1902) was a German-American landscape painter associated with both the Hudson River School and the Rocky Mountain School (alongside Thomas Moran). He immigrated from Germany to New Bedford as a young man and studied at the Dusseldorf Academy for three years before returning to the United States and joining several western expeditions. He worked out of the West Tenth Street Studio Building in New York City.

His masterpiece, The Rocky Mountains, Lander's Peak (1863), depicts a Shoshone encampment with wigwams by a waterfall beneath Lander's Peak (also known as Saber Tooth), based on sketches from the Honey Road Survey Party. It was exhibited for 25 cents at a private venue in Boston and is frequently compared to Frederic Edwin Church's Heart of the Andes due to their similar central waterfalls. A related work, Storm in the Rocky Mountains, shows a Native American camp beneath Lander's Peak during a storm.

The Last of the Buffalo (c. 1888) depicts a Native American on a rearing horse dramatically thrusting a spear at a buffalo, with several carcasses in the foreground and a man on a white horse. It was Bierstadt's last large landscape painting and was rejected from the 1889 Paris Exposition Universelle as 'old-fashioned,' marking a career lowlight. Among the Sierra Nevada, California (1868) shows elk dwarfed by mountains.

Bierstadt sold a painting to English railroad tycoon James McHenry for $25,000, with which he built his estate Malkasten. He was friends with philosopher Charles Sanders Peirce, enlisting him in invention schemes like transformable train cars. Congress refused to pay $80,000 for Capitol paintings, so he staged an unauthorized exhibition in the House Chamber. A shipwreck ended his Alaskan journey, inspiring The Wreck of the Ancon. His Roman Fish Market, Arch of Octavius borrows from the Barberini Faun for a sleeping man with a dog. He commonly depicted cone-shaped clouds. He traveled to the Gaspe Peninsula to paint salmon fishermen on the Cascapedia River. His painting Gosnold at Cuttyhunk shows a large ship beside a smaller one. He occasionally painted in the Luminist style (coined by John Baur in 1954), has a lake in Colorado named after him, and late-career tropical works include The Shore of the Turquoise Sea. William Bliss Baker was a student of Bierstadt.
