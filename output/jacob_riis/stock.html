Summary of Facts

Jacob Riis (1849–1914) was a Danish-American photojournalist and social reformer who became one of the most important early documentary photographers in American history. An immigrant from Denmark who settled in New York, Riis worked as a police reporter covering the Lower East Side's most impoverished neighborhoods, and used his access to produce a landmark body of photographic and journalistic work exposing the lives of the urban poor.

His magnum opus, How the Other Half Lives (1890), subtitled 'Studies among the Tenements of New York,' was a muckraking photojournalistic book that documented conditions in the slums of New York City with an unprecedented combination of text and photographs. Its title was drawn from a phrase in Rabelais's Gargantua and Pantagruel. Among its most powerful images are Bandit's Roost, showing a threatening crowd of men in an alley at 59½ Mulberry Street — the heart of the notorious Mulberry Bend in Little Italy — and Lodgers in Bayard Street, subtitled 'Five Cents a Spot,' showing immigrants crammed into a squalid bedroom. Clotheslines strung across Mulberry Street in his photographs became symbols of the 'poverty line.'

Riis was a technical pioneer: he was among the first photographers to use indoor flash photography, producing light by igniting magnesium powder. He once accidentally set himself on fire in the process. He photographed gangs of street children he called 'Street Arabs' and 'growler gangs,' and often titled his photographs by their cost in cents. His book had an immediate and far-reaching impact: Theodore Roosevelt, then a New York City Police Commissioner, read it and came to Riis in person, writing 'I have read your book, and I have come to help.' Roosevelt later called Riis 'the best American I ever knew,' and the two went on nighttime walks through the slums to find sleeping police officers. The book directly led to the demolition of the Mulberry Bend slum and the construction of Columbus Park, the first park in the Five Points neighborhood.
