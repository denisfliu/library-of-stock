14th-century Middle English author of The Canterbury Tales and Troilus and Criseyde. Called 'the first founder of our fair language' by Thomas Hoccleve. Son of a vintner, diplomat, controller of wool trade.
The Canterbury Tales

Chaucer's magnum opus, a collection of stories told by pilgrims traveling from the Tabard Inn to the tomb of Thomas Becket. The General Prologue describes the 'condition, array, and degree' of the group. Key tales include: the Knight's Tale (Palamon and Arcite fight for Emily's love, adapted from Boccaccio's Teseida); the Wife of Bath's Prologue and Tale (she tears pages from husband Jankin's 'book of wicked wives,' becoming deaf; tells of a knight given a year to learn what women most desire; an old hag turns young after marriage; coined 'belle chose'; Zadie Smith adapted it as The Wife of Willesden); the Pardoner's Tale (three men seek Death, find gold under an oak tree); the Clerk's Tale (Patient Griselda from Boccaccio); the Prioress (Madame Eglantine, pendant saying 'Amor Vincit Omnia,' antisemitic tale of a murdered pious child); the Nun's Priest's Tale (rooster Chauntecleer escapes a fox, references the Peasants' Revolt); the Squire's Tale (magical gifts for Genghis Khan, brass horse, Canace's falcon); the Parson's Tale (sermon on seven deadly sins); Chaucer himself tells the Tale of Sir Thopas (interrupted by Host Harry Bailey for bad doggerel rhyme) and then the prose tale of Melibee. Chaucer's 'retraction' at the end admits his works 'tended toward sin.'

Troilus and Criseyde

A rime royale epic poem set during the siege of Troy. Troilus falls in love with Criseyde, who betrays him for Diomedes. After death, Troilus ascends to the eighth sphere of heaven. Contains the first known use of 'all good things must come to an end.' Dedicated to John Gower. Chaucer invented rime royale for it. Robert Henryson wrote a continuation, The Testament of Cresseid. Chaucer imitated one of Petrarch's sonnets in it. In The Legend of Good Women, Cupid denounces the narrator for writing about Criseyde's infidelity.

The Parliament of Fowls

A dream vision in which Nature convenes birds to pick their mates. Three tercels plead for the hand of a formel eagle, and a woman asks Nature for a year to decide. The narrator falls asleep reading Cicero's Somnium Scipionis and is guided by Scipio Africanus through the gate of the Temple of Venus. Broken bows of former huntresses hang on walls painted with stories of Callisto and Atalanta. The 'Envoy' paraphrases Hippocrates: 'the life so short, the craft so long to learn.' Birds sing a roundel beginning 'Now welcom somer.' Credited with spreading St. Valentine's Day as a lovers' holiday.

The House of Fame

A poem in which the narrator dreams that an eagle carries him to a temple dedicated to illustrious people. The word 'galaxy' is first attested in this poem. Characters explain that words break the air and cause it to ripple, explaining why Fame's house is found between the heavens, earth, and sea. The narrator visits a constantly-moving wicker house and a golden palace. Some scholars believe the last judgment concerns the burning of the Temple of Artemis at Ephesus.

The Book of the Duchess

Chaucer's first long poem, dedicated to John of Gaunt's deceased wife, the duchess Blanche of Lancaster. A knight relates how he lost a game of chess to Fortuna. The narrator encounters Octavian's hunt and consoles the grieving knight.

General / Biographical

Chaucer was a son of a vintner who worked in the house of Elizabeth de Burgh. He served as a diplomat and controller of the wool trade, going on a mission to Genoa. His rise was helped by John of Gaunt. Through his wife Philippa Roet, he was brother-in-law to John of Gaunt. He paid a fine to beat a charge of 'raptus' against Cecily Chaumpaigne (2022 research disputes the rape interpretation). Thomas Hoccleve called him 'the first founder of our fair language.' His assistant Adam Pinkhurst was cursed for negligence. He wore a fashionable paltok showing off his legs. He may have met Boccaccio and Petrarch at Violante Visconti's wedding. He wrote an alphabetical acrostic (his 'ABC') and A Treatise on the Astrolabe. First makar lamented by William Dunbar. His son Thomas became Chief Butler and Speaker of the House of Commons. Granddaughter Alice became Duchess of Suffolk.

Summary of Facts

Geoffrey Chaucer is the 14th-century Middle English author universally identified in quizbowl by The Canterbury Tales. This collection follows pilgrims from the Tabard Inn to the tomb of Thomas Becket in Canterbury. The major tales clued include the Knight's Tale (Palamon and Arcite competing for Emily, adapted from Boccaccio's Teseida), the Wife of Bath (tears pages from her husband Jankin's misogynistic book, goes deaf; a knight must discover what women most desire; coined 'belle chose'; adapted by Zadie Smith as The Wife of Willesden), the Pardoner's Tale (three men find gold under an oak tree while seeking Death), the Prioress Madame Eglantine (pendant inscribed 'Amor Vincit Omnia'), the Nun's Priest's Tale (Chauntecleer the rooster escapes a fox), the Clerk's Tale (Patient Griselda from Boccaccio), and the Squire's Tale (magical gifts for Genghis Khan). Chaucer himself appears as a character who tells the doggerel Tale of Sir Thopas before being interrupted by the Host Harry Bailey.

Troilus and Criseyde is a rime royale epic set during the siege of Troy in which Criseyde betrays Troilus for Diomedes. It contains the first known use of 'all good things must come to an end' and was dedicated to John Gower. The Parliament of Fowls is a dream vision where Nature convenes birds to choose mates; the narrator reads Cicero's Somnium Scipionis and is guided by Scipio Africanus. The House of Fame features the word 'galaxy' for the first time and has an eagle carrying the narrator to a temple. The Book of the Duchess commemorates Blanche of Lancaster. The Legend of Good Women was penance for depicting Criseyde's infidelity.

Biographically, Chaucer was a vintner's son, diplomat, and controller of the wool trade. His rise was aided by John of Gaunt. He paid a fine for a 'raptus' charge against Cecily Chaumpaigne (disputed by modern research). Thomas Hoccleve called him 'the first founder of our fair language.' He is the first makar lamented by William Dunbar.
