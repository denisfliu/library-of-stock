[B1] 2019 Spartan Housewrite (2019) | Diff: 8 | Literature
     Leadin: Answer the following about references to Alexander the Great in world literature for 10 points each.
  Part 1 (ans: The Epic of Sundiata [accept Son-Jara]):
    This Malian epic refers to Alexander as "the mighty king of gold and silver" and the focal point of a history of kings that the griot Balla Fasseke tells to its protagonist.
  Part 2 (ans: "The Man who Would Be King" [by Rudyard Kipling]):
    In this story, Daniel Dravot is revered as a second coming of Alexander the Great by a tribe descended from his army in the Pakistani region of Kafiristan.
  Part 3 (ans: Shahnameh):
    In this epic, a talking tree that Alexander finds at the end of the world tells him that he will die in Babylon. In this epic poem, Rostam accidentally kills his son, Sohrab, in battle.

[B4] 2014 ACF Fall (2014) | Diff: 6 | Literature
     Leadin: One character in this story is revealed not to be a god when a bite draws his blood. For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: "The Man Who Would Be King"):
    Identify this short story, in which Daniel Dravot and Peachy Carnehan become rulers of a short-lived empire in Kafiristan thanks to their guns and knowledge of Freemasonry.
  Part 2 (ans: Rudyard Kipling):
    This author wrote "The Man Who Would Be King." Other stories of his like "Mowgli's Brothers" and "Toomai of the Elephants" were included in his The Jungle Book.
  Part 3 (ans: Just So Stories for Little Children):
    This Kipling children's collection includes "How the Camel Got His Hump" and "How the Leopard Got His Spots." They fancifully explain where certain animals' distinguishing features come from.
