"The Man Who Would Be King"

A novella in which two ex-soldiers, Daniel Dravot and Peachey Carnehan, set themselves up as rulers of Kafiristan. Dravot is revered as a second coming of Alexander the Great. They gain a following by defending archers with Martini-Henry rifles and start a Freemason lodge at The Temple of Imbra. Despite warnings from Billy Fish, Dravot insists on taking an Afghan bride, who bites his nose and reveals he bleeds. Dravot is executed on a rope bridge that is cut; Carnehan survives crucifixion on two pine trees but dies of exposure. The narrator is a Backwoodsman correspondent in British India; the story begins on a train. Inspired by the empire-building exploits of James Brooke. The contract stipulates the two men must abstain from liquor and women.

The Jungle Book

An 1894 collection of stories featuring the 'man-cub' Mowgli, raised by wolves and mentored by the bear Baloo and the panther Bagheera. Mowgli uses fire to drive off the tiger Shere Khan, and in The Second Jungle Book, a village is destroyed after Messua and her husband are imprisoned for adopting Mowgli. The collection also includes 'Rikki-Tikki-Tavi' (a mongoose defeating cobras), a story about Kala Nag taking Little Toomai to see the elephants dance, a story about Kotick the white seal, and a story in which Kaa hypnotizes the Bandar-log monkeys. Neil Gaiman was inspired by it to write The Graveyard Book. In Hanif Kureishi's The Buddha of Suburbia, Karim is asked to wear a loincloth and use an accent to play Mowgli in a stage adaptation.

Kim

A trader teaches Kim a memory game called the 'Play of the Jewels.' Kim's father prophesied his destiny lies with a red bull on a green field. He manipulates policemen Barton and Young into apprehending assassins. Hurree Babu is another spy character.

Summary of Facts

Rudyard Kipling is clued as an Anglo-Indian author and poet, born in India and associated with British imperialism. He was Britain's first Nobel laureate in literature. He is most frequently identified through The Jungle Book, his poems "If--" (ending "you'll be a Man, my son!"), "Gunga Din" (the Indian water-bearer who is "a better man than I am"), and "Recessional" (with its refrain "Lest we forget"), as well as the novel Kim.

The Jungle Book (1894) features the 'man-cub' Mowgli, raised by wolves and mentored by Baloo the bear and Bagheera the panther. Mowgli uses fire to drive off the tiger Shere Khan. The Bandar-log monkeys kidnap Mowgli but Kaa the serpent hypnotizes them. In The Second Jungle Book, a village is destroyed after Messua and her husband are imprisoned for adopting Mowgli. The collection also includes 'Rikki-Tikki-Tavi' (a mongoose defeating the cobras Nag and Nagaina while Darzee's wife feigns a broken wing), Kala Nag taking Little Toomai to see elephants dance, and Kotick the white seal. Neil Gaiman was inspired by it to write The Graveyard Book. In Hanif Kureishi's The Buddha of Suburbia, Karim plays Mowgli in a stage adaptation.

In poetry, Kipling's Barrack-Room Ballads are a major source of clues. "If--" was written to honor Leander Starr Jameson and advises on moral character, calling Triumph and Disaster "two impostors." Two lines from it are inscribed above the entrance to Wimbledon. "Gunga Din" tells of a water-bearer introduced as a 'limpin' lump o' brick-dust' who saves the narrator's life and dies doing so. "Recessional" was composed for Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee and warns against imperial hubris with lines about Nineveh and Tyre. "The White Man's Burden" advocated American colonization of the Philippines and inspired a racist Pears' Soap ad. "Mandalay" features a soldier remembering a Burmese girl by the "old Moulmein Pagoda" where "dawn comes up like thunder" -- the Irrawaddy was nicknamed 'The Road to Mandalay.' "The Ballad of East and West" begins with the famous line about the twain never meeting and features the Colonel's son and the horse-thief Kamal. "Danny Deever" describes a soldier's execution. "Tommy" laments how soldiers are mistreated in peacetime. George Orwell famously called Kipling a "good bad poet."

In prose, "The Man Who Would Be King" follows Daniel Dravot and Peachey Carnehan, who try to rule Kafiristan after passing through the Khyber Pass disguised as toy salesmen. They gain followers by defending archers with Martini-Henry rifles and start a Freemason lodge at The Temple of Imbra. Dravot is worshipped as a second Alexander the Great until his bride bites his nose and reveals he bleeds. Despite warnings from Billy Fish, Dravot insists on the marriage. Dravot is executed when his rope bridge is cut; Carnehan survives crucifixion on two pine trees. The narrator is a Backwoodsman correspondent; the story was inspired by the exploits of James Brooke.

Kim opens with the title character sitting on the great cannon Zam-Zamma outside the Lahore Museum. He travels with Teshoo Lama searching for the 'River of the Arrow' and carries a message to Amballa for horse trader Mehbub Ali on his first spy mission. A trader teaches him the 'Play of the Jewels' memory game. His father prophesied his destiny lies with a red bull on a green field.

Captains Courageous is about the spoiled Harvey Cheyne learning to work on Disko Troop's fishing schooner off the Grand Banks. Puck of Pook's Hill has Dan and Una meeting Puck after performing A Midsummer Night's Dream, and includes 'Weland's Sword.' The Just So Stories include 'How the Camel Got His Hump,' 'How the Leopard Got His Spots,' and 'The Cat That Walked by Himself.' Plain Tales from the Hills introduced recurring soldiers Learoyd, Otheris, and Mulvaney. Kipling also wrote the boarding School stories Stalky & Co. based on his time at the United Services College. He designed the 'ritual of the calling' for Canadian engineers after a 1907 Quebec bridge disaster, including the Iron Ring ceremony. T.S. Eliot's 'The Hollow Men' takes its title partly from a Kipling poem.
