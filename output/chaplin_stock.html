British silent-era actor, director, and composer, best known for his character 'The Little Tramp' — a bowler-hatted, cane-wielding figure of pathos and comedy. Directed and starred in City Lights, Modern Times, The Gold Rush, and The Great Dictator. Also composed the scores for his own films, including 'Smile.' Frequently compared to Buster Keaton and inspired a global wave of Tramp imitators, most notably in Indian cinema.

Summary of Facts

Charlie Chaplin is a British silent-era actor, director, and composer, best known for his recurring character 'The Little Tramp' — a bowler-hatted, cane-wielding figure in ill-fitting clothes who combines comic absurdity with pathos. His films span from his early days at Keystone Pictures (run by producer Mack Sennett, alongside Fatty Arbuckle and Mabel Normand) through his independently produced masterworks at United Artists.

The Great Dictator (1940), his first sound film, satirizes Adolf Hitler as 'Adenoid Hynkel, ruler of Tomainia.' A Jewish barber is mistaken for Hynkel and delivers a famous speech. The film's most iconic scene — Hynkel dancing with an inflatable globe — is set to the orchestral prelude from Wagner's Lohengrin, chosen because both Chaplin and Hitler admired Wagner. Chaplin watched Triumph of the Will at MoMA and used it as source material. Chaplin noted he was born just four days after Hitler in April 1889 and reflected that their roles could have been reversed.

City Lights (1931) is a silent film in which the Tramp raises money to restore the sight of a blind flower girl who mistakes him for a millionaire. Its score, arranged by Alfred Newman, used the habanera 'La Violetera' as a leitmotif for the florist. The film contains a long boxing sequence where the Tramp hides behind a tall referee opposite Hank Mann (an original Keystone Cop). City Lights was Chaplin's first score for a film he himself directed.

Modern Times (1936) follows a factory worker swallowed by machine gears and then jailed. The Tramp accidentally waves a red flag that fell off a truck and is arrested as a Communist protest leader. The film's love theme 'Smile' was composed with David Raksin (who later scored Laura) and was inspired by the first-act love duet from Puccini's Tosca. Michael Jackson's favorite song was 'Smile.' The film stars Paulette Goddard.

The Gold Rush (1925) features the Tramp as the 'Lone Prospector' during the Klondike Gold Rush. Most clued scenes: eating a boiled shoe in a wind-blown cabin teetering on a cliff, and the 'Oceana Roll' potato dance. A statue of Chaplin in Vevey, Switzerland (where he spent his last decades) stares at a giant fork referencing this film.

Monsieur Verdoux (1947) is an uncharacteristic departure in which Chaplin plays a Parisian bank teller who murders wealthy wives. Film critic James Agee wrote a celebrated three-part defense of the film.

Biographically: Chaplin was the son of an alcoholic father and an asylum patient mother, whom he supported by clog dancing as a child. He is the uncle of Jefferson Airplane drummer Spencer Dryden. J. Edgar Hoover orchestrated a smear campaign including trumped-up Mann Act charges stemming from Chaplin's relationship with Joan Barry. Chaplin emigrated to Switzerland after being effectively exiled; he directed A King in New York from there. He won his only competitive Oscar at age 84 for 'Terry's Theme' from Limelight (re-released in 1973). He married Oona O'Neill (daughter of Eugene O'Neill) in 1943, which caused a rift with her father.

His Tramp character directly inspired the Jacques Tati character Monsieur Hulot and Indian cinema's Raj Kapoor (Awaara, Shree 420). Chaplin is frequently linked to Buster Keaton in quizbowl — James Agee's 1949 Life essay 'Comedy's Greatest Era' discusses both alongside Harold Lloyd. The Lettrist schism began when Guy Debord disrupted a Chaplin press conference. Hannah Arendt argued that despite his non-Jewish heritage, Chaplin embodied 'the Jewish pariah mentality.' Hart Crane wrote a poem titled 'Chaplinesque.'
