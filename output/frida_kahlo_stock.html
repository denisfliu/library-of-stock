Frida Kahlo

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Mexican surrealist painter known for her many self-portraits, often featuring her prominent unibrow and monkeys. Married to muralist Diego Rivera. Her art was deeply influenced by a devastating bus accident at age 18, chronic pain, and her turbulent marriage. Andre Breton described her work as 'a ribbon around a bomb.' She lived and worked at the Blue House (Casa Azul) in Coyoacan.
The Broken Column (1944)

One of Kahlo's best-known self-portraits, depicting her with a cracked Ionic column replacing her spine, her body held together by a medical brace, and nails piercing her skin. Represents the lifelong pain she suffered from a bus accident at age 18. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Frida Kahlo (born Magdalena Carmen Frida Kahlo y Calderon) is a Mexican painter universally identified in quizbowl by her marriage to muralist Diego Rivera, her prominent unibrow, and her numerous self-portraits featuring monkeys. Her art was profoundly shaped by a devastating bus accident at age 18 that left her in lifelong pain and unable to bear children.

Her most frequently clued painting is The Broken Column, which shows a cracked Ionic column replacing her spine, her body studded with nails and constrained by a medical brace — a direct representation of her suffering from the bus accident. The Suicide of Dorothy Hale, commissioned by Clare Boothe Luce, depicts socialite Dorothy Hale falling from Hampshire House through cottony clouds onto a sidewalk; Isamu Noguchi controversially removed the dedication, and Kahlo included bloodstains on the frame in the tradition of Mexican votive paintings. What the Water Gave Me is an autobiographical painting showing Kahlo's bare legs in a bathtub with dreamlike images floating on the surface, including a skyscraper rising from a volcano, a dead bird on a gnarled tree, and a naked woman being choked.

Self-Portrait with Thorn Necklace and Hummingbird is her most recognizable giveaway — showing her against green leaves with a thorn necklace whose hummingbird pendant draws blood from her neck, with a cat and monkey on her shoulders. The Two Fridas is a double self-portrait showing one Frida in a white Victorian gown and another in a traditional Mexican dress, connected by a blood vessel between their exposed hearts, with blood dripping from scissors onto the white dress. Henry Ford Hospital depicts Kahlo on a hospital bed after a miscarriage, with six objects (snail, orchid, pelvic bone, plaster model, flower) connected by red cords to her figure, painted in the votive retablo style on sheet metal.

Viva la Vida, Watermelons was her final painting, a still life of sliced watermelons inscribed with the phrase 'Viva la Vida.' Diego Rivera also painted watermelons as his final artwork. Other notable works include A Few Small Nips (bloodstains on the frame), Moses (featuring images of mitosis and fertilization), My Nurse and I (Kahlo as an infant with a masked wet nurse), The Wounded Table (lost since 1955), Tree of Hope Remain Strong (divided into day and night sides), Self-Portrait with Cropped Hair (oversized suit with music lyrics about hair), Self-Portrait as a Tehuana and Diego and I (Rivera's face on Kahlo's forehead), and The Bride Frightened at Seeing Life Opened (doll in a wedding dress, owl, split papaya).

Biographically, Andre Breton described her work as 'a ribbon around a bomb' and arranged her first solo exhibition in New York in 1938. She was categorized as a Surrealist against her will. She lived at the Blue House (Casa Azul) in Coyoacan, where she kept parrots and monkeys as pets and amassed a collection of votive paintings. Her diary, kept during the last ten years of her life and published with an introduction by Carlos Fuentes, features surrealistic watercolor illustrations and contains the famous quote 'Feet, what do I need you for, if I have wings to fly?' The last image in the diary is of an angel, which biographer Hayden Herrera interprets as the Angel of Death. Diego's affair with Kahlo's sister Cristina and Kahlo's subsequent affair with Leon Trotsky at the Blue House are frequently clued biographical details.
