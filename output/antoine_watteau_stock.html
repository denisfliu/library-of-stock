The Embarkation for Cythera (1717)

Fete galante painting showing couples preparing to depart the island sacred to Venus/Cupid, surrounded by swarms of putti/cherubs near a golden gondola. Two versions exist: one in the Louvre, one in Berlin's Charlottenburg Palace. Reception piece that ended the Poussiniste-Rubeniste debate at the Academy. Inspired by Dancourt's play The Three Cousins. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Antoine Watteau (1684-1721) was a French Rococo painter who pioneered the fete galante genre, a term created by the French Academy in 1717 specifically to categorize his paintings of fancily dressed aristocrats in imaginary outdoor settings. His innovation subverted the Academy's rigid hierarchy of genres.

The Embarkation for Cythera (1717) is his most famous painting, showing couples preparing to depart the island sacred to Venus, surrounded by swarms of putti near a golden gondola. Two versions exist: the Louvre original features a garland-wrapped statue of Venus with a bow and quiver at its base on the right, while the Charlottenburg Palace version in Berlin includes a mast in the background. There is scholarly debate over whether the figures are leaving or arriving at the island. The painting was his reception piece at the Academy and is considered to have ended the Poussiniste-Rubeniste debate. It was inspired by a grove scene from Dancourt's play The Three Cousins and possibly by Houdar de la Motte's opera La Vénitienne. Students threw breadcrumbs at it during the French Revolution. It later inspired Baudelaire's poem 'A Voyage to Cythera' (with its image of a corpse on a gallows tree) and William Kentridge's 1980s pastel/charcoal series of urban wasteland. The Goncourt brothers praised it for its 'harmony in those sunny distances.'

Gersaint's Shop Sign was Watteau's last major painting before his death from tuberculosis. Workers pack a portrait of Louis XIV (specifically a Pierre Mignard portrait) into a wooden box while customers admire artworks in this art dealer's boutique. It was intended as a trade sign for Edme Gersaint and features a contrast between older-style narrow-waist gowns and fashionable sack-back gowns.

Gilles (Pierrot) depicts a forlorn-looking man in a white commedia dell'arte costume with pink/red bows on his shoes, with Pantalon riding a donkey and Leandro and Isabella in the background. Dora Panofsky argued the pose derives from Christ in Rembrandt's 1665 Ecce Homo. The painting was allegedly sold by a junk dealer with a label reading 'Buy me, I'm so lonely!' and may have been a sign for a cafe belonging to former actor Belloni. The Thyssen holds a related fete-galante painting of Pierrot 'content.' Amedeo Modigliani's first self-portrait depicts him as this character.

Watteau frequently painted commedia dell'arte characters, including Mezzetin (playing guitar with a backwards-facing female statue) and musical themes in The Scale of Love, The Love Lesson, and The Love Song (often featuring a bust possibly of Pythagoras). He used the trois-crayons (three chalks) technique for drawings. He was taught by Claude Gillot, studied under Claude Audran at the Luxembourg Palace (copying Rubens's Marie de' Medici cycle), and his student Jean-Baptiste Pater also worked in the fete galante genre. A type of fabric pleat is named after him (the Watteau pleat). He was a leading artist of the singerie (monkeys performing human activities). Lucian Freud painted Large Interior, W11 (After Watteau). Other notable works include Ceres (only surviving painting from a four seasons series), The Pleasures of the Dance, Chinese Musician (hurdy-gurdy player, Château de la Muette), and The Jealous Ones. Jean-Baptiste Carpeaux later sculpted a monument to Watteau.
