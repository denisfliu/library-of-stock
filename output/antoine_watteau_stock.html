Summary of Facts

Antoine Watteau was a French Rococo painter who pioneered the fete galante genre, a term created by the French Academy in 1717 specifically to categorize his paintings of fancily dressed aristocrats in imaginary outdoor settings. His most famous painting, The Embarkation for Cythera, shows couples preparing to depart the island sacred to Venus, surrounded by swarms of putti near a golden gondola. Two versions exist: the original in the Louvre and a second in Berlin's Charlottenburg Palace, which includes a mast in the background. Both versions show a man in red with a staff on a hill. There is scholarly debate over whether the figures are leaving or arriving at the island.

Gersaint's Shop Sign was Watteau's last major painting before his death from tuberculosis. It depicts an art dealer's shop where workers pack a portrait of Louis XIV into a wooden box while customers admire artworks. It was intended as a trade sign for the art dealer Edme Gersaint. Gilles (or Pierrot) depicts a forlorn-looking man in a white commedia dell'arte costume with pink or red bowties on his shoes, with Pantalon riding a donkey visible in the background.

Watteau frequently painted commedia dell'arte characters, including Mezzetin, shown playing guitar with a backwards-facing female statue behind him. Several related paintings feature musical themes: The Scale of Love, The Love Lesson, and The Love Song all feature guitar players and sometimes include a bust (possibly of Pythagoras). He used the trois-crayons (three chalks) technique for drawings. He was taught by Claude Gillot and studied under Claude Audran, curator of the Luxembourg Palace, where he copied scenes from Rubens's Marie de' Medici cycle. His student Jean-Baptiste Pater also worked in the fete galante genre. Other notable works include Ceres (the only surviving painting from his four seasons series), The Pleasures of the Dance, and The Surprise. Jean-Baptiste Carpeaux later sculpted a monument to Watteau.
