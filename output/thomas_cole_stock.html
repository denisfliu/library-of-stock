British-American landscape painter, founder of the Hudson River School. Most commonly identified by The Oxbow, The Course of Empire (5-painting series), and The Voyage of Life (4-painting series). Patron: Luman Reed. Depicted alongside William Cullen Bryant in Asher Durand's Kindred Spirits. Also painted The Titan's Goblet and The Architect's Dream.
The Course of Empire (1833–1836)

Series of five paintings depicting the rise and fall of a civilization. Commissioned by Luman Reed. Inspired by Byron's Childe Harold's Pilgrimage ('wealth, vice, corruption') and named after George Berkeley's poem. Cole proposed displaying it around a mantle. Each painting depicts the same rocky outcrop with a precariously balanced boulder, and the time of day progressively darkens. Idea came while Cole occupied Claude Lorrain's old studio in Rome. Ed Ruscha later used the series name for industrial LA paintings (2005 Venice Biennale). Emanuel Leutze's Capitol mural 'Westward the Course of Empire Takes Its Way' also references Berkeley's verse.

Summary of Facts

Thomas Cole (1801–1848) was a British-American landscape painter and the founder of the Hudson River School. He is most commonly identified by The Oxbow, The Course of Empire, and The Voyage of Life.

The Course of Empire (1833–1836) is a series of five paintings commissioned by patron Luman Reed, depicting the rise and fall of a civilization. Named after George Berkeley's poem, each painting shows the same rocky outcrop with a precariously balanced boulder, and the time of day progressively darkens. The Savage State opens with a hunter chasing deer; The Arcadian/Pastoral State shows a boy in blue crossing a stone bridge near a smoking temple; The Consummation features golden statues and an elaborate fountain; Destruction shows a huge fire, a collapsing bridge, and a headless statue based on the Borghese Gladiator; and Desolation closes with birds nesting on an overgrown column under a pale moon. Cole conceived the series while occupying Claude Lorrain's old studio in Rome and was inspired by Byron's Childe Harold's Pilgrimage. Ed Ruscha later appropriated the series name for his LA industrial paintings at the 2005 Venice Biennale. Emanuel Leutze's massive Capitol mural 'Westward the Course of Empire Takes Its Way' also references Berkeley's verse.

The Oxbow (1836), fully titled View from Mount Holyoke, Northampton, Massachusetts, after a Thunderstorm, is a panoramic landscape of the Connecticut River read as an allegory for wilderness versus civilization. A receding thunderstorm dominates the left; cultivated farmland fills the right. The foreground contains Cole's tiny self-portrait in a brown hat and red coat, a cross-shaped chair, a flag, a signed portfolio, and an umbrella jutting across the diagonal. Matthew Baigell argues that shapes in the background spell the Hebrew word 'Shaddai' (God Almighty) when viewed from above. The painting was completed as a respite from the Course of Empire commission.

The Voyage of Life (1842) is a four-part allegorical series: Childhood, Youth, Manhood, and Old Age. A man in red is guided down a river in a golden boat by a guardian angel in white. An hourglass is attached to the boat in the first three paintings. Youth shows the boy reaching toward a heavenly castle surrounded by ethereal domes; in Manhood, demons lurk in dark clouds while the angel is barely visible in the upper left; Old Age shows a bearded man praying as the angel gestures toward divine light. The Hours are depicted on the golden boat structure. Cole created a second set for the National Gallery of Art.

The Titan's Goblet (1833) is a standalone painting of a vast goblet rising from a landscape, containing an ocean with ships, a Greek temple, and ruins on its rim, dwarfing a tiny village below. Erwin Panofsky argued it was inspired by Norse mythology; Ellwood Parry linked it to Turner's Ulysses Deriding Polyphemus.

The Architect's Dream (1840), also standalone, depicts the title figure resting atop a column imagining various architectural styles: a Gothic cathedral with stained-glass windows on the left (suggesting night), while the right shows a daytime sun — an odd juxtaposition. An aqueduct, temples, and a wispy Egyptian pyramid fill the background.

Kindred Spirits (1849) was painted by Asher Durand after Cole's death, depicting Cole and poet William Cullen Bryant standing on a promontory in the Catskill Mountains. Commissioned by Jonathan Sturges and referencing a eulogy based on Keats's 'O Solitude,' it was controversially purchased from the New York Public Library by Alice Walton in 2005 for the Crystal Bridges Museum.

Cole also painted The Past and The Present (a pair showing a castle's ruination over time), scenes from The Last of the Mohicans, and Il Penseroso (a girl kneeling before a painting of the Virgin Mary near Lake Nemi, inspired by a 1646 poem).
