German Late Romantic composer (1864–1949), known above all for his tone poems and operas. Major tone poems include Also Sprach Zarathustra, Ein Heldenleben, Till Eulenspiegel's Merry Pranks, Death and Transfiguration, Don Quixote, and An Alpine Symphony. Operas include Salome, Elektra, Der Rosenkavalier, and Ariadne auf Naxos, several written with librettist Hugo von Hofmannsthal. Late works include Metamorphosen and Four Last Songs.
General / Biographical

German Late Romantic composer (1864–1949). Began career at the Meiningen Court Orchestra as assistant to Hans von Bülow. Co-founded the Salzburg Festival with Hugo von Hofmannsthal and Max Reinhardt. His wife was soprano Pauline de Ahna. Served as president of the Reichsmusikkammer under the Nazis (partly to protect his Jewish daughter-in-law Alice) but was later dismissed after an intercepted letter to Stefan Zweig. Wrote Olympic Hymn for the 1936 Berlin Olympics. Known as a famously relaxed conductor — George Szell said he conducted 'as if he were waiting for the card game that came after.' Called himself 'the last mountain of a large mountain range.'

Summary of Facts

Richard Strauss (1864–1949) was a German Late Romantic composer best known for his orchestral tone poems and operas. He began his conducting career at the Meiningen Court Orchestra as Hans von Bülow's assistant. With librettist Hugo von Hofmannsthal (whom he called a collaborator of genius despite frequent friction) he produced six major operas: Elektra, Der Rosenkavalier, Ariadne auf Naxos, Die Frau ohne Schatten, Die ägyptische Helena, and Arabella. He also co-founded the Salzburg Festival with Hofmannsthal and director Max Reinhardt. His wife was soprano Pauline de Ahna.

His tone poems — a genre he essentially defined — include Also Sprach Zarathustra (1896, based on Nietzsche; famous for the C-G-C sunrise fanfare used in 2001: A Space Odyssey and Elvis Presley concerts; ends with an unresolved clash between B major and C major), Till Eulenspiegel's Merry Pranks (1895; features Till's F major 6/8 solo horn theme and a death-scene with descending trombone seventh and D clarinet squeal), Don Juan, Death and Transfiguration (1889; heartbeat-triplet opening; 'transfiguration motif' F–Bb–C–D–high D–C later quoted in Four Last Songs), Don Quixote (1897; flutter-tongued horns for bleating sheep; the piece Bernstein learned from a sickbed before his 1943 Carnegie Hall debut), Ein Heldenleben (1898; autobiographical, E-flat major; tubas in parallel fifths for critics; 'Works of Peace' quoting earlier Strauss works), An Alpine Symphony (1915; 22 sections; wind machine, thundersheet, 12 offstage horns, heckelphone, cowbells; Karajan's recording was the first CD pressed), and Symphonia Domestica (1904; three themes for father, mother, child; wind-trill clock at 7 a.m.).

Late works include Metamorphosen (1945; 23 solo strings; quotes Eroica funeral march with 'In Memoriam' written in the score; inspired by the bombing of the Vienna Opera House) and Four Last Songs (1948; soprano and orchestra; primarily Hesse texts; quotes 'transfiguration motif' at 'Is this, perhaps, death?'; Jessye Norman's recording with Masur and the Leipzig Gewandhaus is landmark).

His operas: Salome (1905; his own libretto after Wilde; Dance of the Seven Veils; polytonal 'sickening chord' as Salome kisses Jochanaan's head; opens with ambiguous clarinet scale), Elektra (1909; Hofmannsthal libretto; opens D–A–F–D for Agamemnon; 'Elektra chord' polytonal E/C-sharp; Elektra dances herself to death; Chéreau's 2013 Aix-en-Provence production his last work), Der Rosenkavalier (1911; Marschallin fears aging, stops clocks; Octavian breeches role; Baron Ochs; trio 'Ha mir's gelobt'; Octavian and Sophie sing 'Ist ein Traum'), and Ariadne auf Naxos (opera seria + commedia dell'arte performed simultaneously; Zerbinetta's 12-minute coloratura aria 'Grossmächtige Prinzessin').

During the Nazi era Strauss served as Reichsmusikkammer president partly to protect his Jewish daughter-in-law Alice, but was dismissed when his letter to Zweig asking 'if he or Mozart consciously wrote Aryan music' was intercepted. He still wrote the Olympic Hymn for the 1936 Berlin Games. He was sued by Luigi Denza for using 'Funiculi, Funicula' in Aus Italien under the mistaken belief it was a folk song. He once described himself as 'the last mountain of a large mountain range.' George Szell said he conducted as if waiting for the card game that followed the performance.
