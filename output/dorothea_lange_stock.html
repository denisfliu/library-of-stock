American documentary photographer best known for Migrant Mother, a Depression-era image of Florence Owens Thompson and her children at a California pea-pickers camp. Worked for the Farm Security Administration and the War Relocation Authority, documenting Japanese American internment at Manzanar. Also created White Angel Breadline, An American Exodus (with Paul Schuster Taylor), and Death of a Valley (with Pirkle Jones).
Migrant Mother (1936)

Lange's most iconic photograph, taken at a pea-pickers camp in Nipomo, California for the Farm Security Administration. The subject is Florence Owens Thompson (a Cherokee woman), gazing pensively into the distance with two children burying their faces against her back/shoulders. One later version had a retouched lower-right corner to remove Thompson's thumb. It was included in Edward Steichen's Family of Man exhibition. Wikipedia

Manzanar Internment Camp Documentation (1942)

Lange was hired by the War Relocation Authority in 1942 to document conditions at Manzanar, the Japanese American internment camp in California's Owens Valley. Over 800 of her photographs were impounded by the WRA. Works include: Japanese American children saying the Pledge of Allegiance, a flagpole rising between rows of identical barracks, and a man hoisting his grandson on his back. She also photographed the Children's Village at Manzanar. Compare with Ansel Adams, who was invited by camp director Ralph Merritt and published Born Free and Equal, and Toyo Miyatake, who was a prisoner himself. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

American documentary photographer (1895–1965). Worked for the Farm Security Administration (FSA) during the Great Depression. Later worked for the War Relocation Authority. Also had a late-career series on Public Defenders in California. Accompanied partner Paul Taylor on a trip to Asia and photographed Indonesian dancers. Desired to 'grab a hunk of lightning' (quoted in a documentary). Had a MoMA exhibition titled 'Words and Pictures' (April 2020). Used her connections at Life magazine to help fund Edward Steichen's The Family of Man exhibit. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Dorothea Lange was an American documentary photographer who worked primarily for the Farm Security Administration (FSA) during the Great Depression. Her most famous work is Migrant Mother (1936), taken at a pea-pickers camp in Nipomo, California, which depicts Florence Owens Thompson — a Cherokee woman — gazing into the distance with two children burying their faces against her back. The official title refers to 'Human Erosion in California.' Lange later retouched the image to remove Thompson's thumb from the lower-right corner. Early reproductions were accompanied by an incorrect Paul Taylor caption stating Thompson had sold a tent to get food. Migrant Mother became one of the most reproduced photographs in history and was included in Edward Steichen's Family of Man exhibition at MoMA.

Before Migrant Mother, Lange created White Angel Breadline (1933) in San Francisco, showing a man in a beat-up hat with a cup, clasping his hands and facing away from the line at a soup kitchen run by a woman nicknamed the 'White Angel.' During World War II, Lange was hired by the War Relocation Authority to document conditions at Manzanar, the Japanese American internment camp in California's Owens Valley. Over 800 of her Manzanar photographs were impounded by the WRA. Unlike Ansel Adams (who was invited by camp director Ralph Merritt and published Born Free and Equal with a focus on showing camp life favorably), Lange documented both early and late stages of internment and took images of Japanese American children saying the Pledge of Allegiance, a flagpole rising between rows of identical barracks, and the camp's Children's Village.

Lange collaborated with economist Paul Schuster Taylor (her partner) on An American Exodus (1939), a photo-essay documenting displaced Dust Bowl workers including photographs of empty roads. She also collaborated with photographer Pirkle Jones on Death of a Valley, documenting the deliberate flooding of Monticello, California as Lake Berryessa was created; this was published as an issue of Aperture magazine. Late in her career she documented the work of Public Defenders in California. She accompanied Taylor on a trip to Asia and photographed Indonesian dancers' hands and feet. In April 2020, MoMA mounted an online exhibition titled 'Words and Pictures' pairing her photographs with poetry. A documentary about her references her desire to 'grab a hunk of lightning.' She also used her connections at Life magazine to help fund Edward Steichen's The Family of Man exhibit.
