British-born classic Hollywood director (1899–1980), known as the 'Master of Suspense.' Major films include Psycho, Vertigo, Rear Window, North by Northwest, and The Birds. Celebrated by the French New Wave as a technically innovative auteur, particularly through Francois Truffaut's 1966 book-length interview with him. Known for 'Hitchcock blondes,' the MacGuffin concept, and Catholic guilt transference.
Psycho (1960)

Hitchcock's most iconic horror/thriller. Marion Crane is repeatedly stabbed in the shower to a shrieking Bernard Herrmann score, in a scene that pans to a newspaper on a nightstand before a character shouts 'Mother! Oh God, Mother! Blood! Blood!' Norman Bates, a psychologically disturbed motel owner, tells Marion that 'a boy's best friend is his mother' and 'we all go a little mad sometimes.' He stuffs birds and has a split personality triggered by sexual attraction. Robin Wood rose to prominence with his critique of the film. Slavoj Zizek analyzed the three floors of Bates's mansion as superego, ego, and id in The Pervert's Guide to the Cinema (2006). Wikipedia

General / Biographical

British-born director Alfred Hitchcock (1899–1980), called the 'Master of Suspense.' As a child, his father sent him to a police station where he was locked up briefly for misbehaving — he later said 'I'm not against the police; I'm just afraid of them.' Known for preferring blonde female leads ('Hitchcock blondes'), Catholic guilt transference (studied by Eric Rohmer and Claude Chabrol), and the MacGuffin plot device. Francois Truffaut conducted an eight-day interview with Hitchcock (with Helen Scott as interpreter), published in 1966, that cemented Hitchcock's reputation as an auteur and dispelled the view that his work was purely commercial; a 2015 Kent Jones documentary is based on this interview. Laura Mulvey's seminal essay 'Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema' focuses on Hitchcock alongside Josef von Sternberg. Alfred Hitchcock Presents was a TV show where each episode began with Hitchcock walking into the outline of his profile to 'Funeral March for a Marionette.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Alfred Hitchcock (1899–1980) was a British-born classic Hollywood director known as the 'Master of Suspense.' Born in England, he later worked primarily in Hollywood and became one of cinema's most celebrated and analyzed directors.

His most famous film is Psycho (1960), in which Marion Crane is stabbed in a shower to a shrieking Bernard Herrmann score; the scene pans to a newspaper on a nightstand before a character cries 'Mother! Oh God, Mother! Blood! Blood!' The motel owner Norman Bates tells Marion 'a boy's best friend is his mother' and 'we all go a little mad sometimes'; he stuffs birds and has a split personality triggered by sexual attraction that turns him violent. Slavoj Zizek's 2006 documentary The Pervert's Guide to the Cinema mapped the three floors of Bates's mansion onto superego, ego, and id. Robin Wood rose to critical prominence writing about this film.

Vertigo (1958) follows the acrophobic detective Scottie (Jimmy Stewart) as he follows a woman played by Kim Novak; she eventually falls from a bell tower after seeing a nun. Chris Marker's essay-film Sans Soleil revisits the film's San Francisco locations and calls Vertigo the only film to depict 'impossible memory,' recreating its hypnotic POV driving shots. The film also introduced the 'dolly zoom' (sometimes called the 'Vertigo effect'). Laura Mulvey's landmark feminist essay 'Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema' focuses extensively on the male hero's scopophilic eroticism in Vertigo (alongside Rear Window and Marnie). Mulvey also pairs Hitchcock with Josef von Sternberg as the essay's primary subjects.

Rear Window (1954) climaxes with the protagonist using camera flashbulbs to temporarily blind the murderous Lars Thorwald. North by Northwest (1959) features the famous crop duster scene near Bus Stop 41 (described as inspiring James Bond helicopter chases and called 'the first Bond film') and a climax where Cary Grant and Eva Marie Saint hang from Mount Rushmore. The Birds (1963) starred Tippi Hedren, who was bruised by real bird claw marks during filming.

Biographically, Hitchcock is closely associated with the Truffaut interview: Francois Truffaut conducted an eight-day interview (with Helen Scott as interpreter) that was published in 1966 and is credited with dispelling the view that Hitchcock's work was merely commercial, cementing his reputation as a technically innovative auteur. A 2015 Kent Jones documentary is based on this interview. In the interview, Hitchcock called drama 'life with the dull bits cut out,' explained the MacGuffin, and discussed techniques like placing a light inside a glass of milk. Eric Rohmer and Claude Chabrol wrote an extensive study positing Hitchcock's 'Catholic guilt transference.' He is also known for his 'Hitchcock blondes' — preferring blonde female leads such as Kim Novak, Tippi Hedren, and June Tripp. A 2023 Laurence Leamer book describes borderline abusive conditions endured by eight of these actresses. As a child, his father once sent him to a police station to be briefly locked up as punishment; he later said 'I'm not against the police; I'm just afraid of them.' His TV show Alfred Hitchcock Presents began each episode with him walking into the silhouette of his famous profile to 'Funeral March for a Marionette.'
