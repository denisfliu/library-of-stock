Old English manuscript (Cotton MS Vitellius A.XV, second part), held at the British Library. The sole surviving source of the epic poem Beowulf. Also contains the fragmentary poem Judith, an illustrated prose text called Wonders of the East, and a letter from Alexander the Great to Aristotle. Created around the early 11th century. Named after its first known owner, Laurence Nowell. Scorched in the 1731 Ashburnham House fire. Transcribed by Grimur Jonsson Thorkelin in 1787.

Summary of Facts

The Nowell Codex is an Old English manuscript, also known as Cotton MS Vitellius A.XV (of which it forms the second part), held at the British Library. It was created around the early 11th century (circa year 1000). It is most famous as the sole surviving source of Beowulf, the longest poem in Old English, featuring characters such as Onela, Heardred, Hygelac, and Wiglaf in its central section. The codex is sometimes named after the poem it preserves rather than its physical identity. One page of the manuscript shows notably heavy wear, attributed by scholars to the intense reader interest in the dragon episode.

Beyond Beowulf, the Nowell Codex also contains the fragmentary 9th-century Old English poem Judith, about a heroine who decapitates the Assyrian general Holofernes and commands her maidservant to show his head to the people of Bethulia. This poem is explicitly compared in questions to Beowulf's own taking of Grendel's head. The codex further includes the illustrated prose text Wonders of the East, depicting fantastic peoples (blemmyae, hostes) and exotic locales, along with a letter from Alexander the Great to Aristotle.

The manuscript is named after Laurence Nowell, identified as its first known owner. It was first described by Franciscus Junius. Grimur Jonsson Thorkelin transcribed Beowulf from the codex in 1787, and Kemp Malone later improved upon Nikolaj Grundtvig's reconstructions of edge text damaged by fire. The 1731 Ashburnham House fire scorched the edges of the manuscript's pages, though most of the text survived. Kevin Kiernan used ultraviolet light to study the damaged parchment. Scholarly work on the codex includes Andy Orchard's book Pride and Prodigies. Two scribes wrote the manuscript; the first scribe stopped mid-sentence and was replaced by a second scribe who spelled a name differently, using 'I' instead of 'E'.
