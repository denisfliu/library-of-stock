Edna St. Vincent Millay

American poet famous for "Renascence" ("All I could see from where I stood / Was three long mountains and a wood"), "First Fig" ("My candle burns at both ends"), sonnets like "Euclid alone has looked on Beauty bare" and "What lips my lips have kissed," and "The Ballad of the Harp-Weaver." Won the 1923 Pulitzer Prize for Poetry. Associated with the Provincetown Players.
Renascence

Millay's breakthrough poem, written when she was 19. Begins "All I could see from where I stood / Was three long mountains and a wood." The speaker reaches up to touch the sky, feels "Infinity / Pressed down upon the finite Me," sinks six feet underground, then experiences a spiritual rebirth as God washes the grave away in rain. A man starving in Capri prompts the speaker's awareness of universal suffering. Controversially lost a contest in The Lyric Year to Orrick Johns. The final section states "The world stands out on either side, no wider than the heart is wide." Warns that "he whose soul is flat, the sky will cave in on him by and by."

The Ballad of the Harp-Weaver

Won Millay the 1923 Pulitzer Prize for Poetry. A mother freezes to death while magically weaving "the clothes of a king's son, just my size" for her impoverished child using a "harp with a woman's head." The boy was told "There's nothing in the house / But a loaf-end of rye" and was "happy for half an hour's time" rocking to a nursery rhyme. A "wind with a wolf's head" howls around the door.

Sonnets (Other)

Millay was a prolific and accomplished sonneteer. Her collection Fatal Interview contains 52 sonnets, one for each week of a year-long affair. Other notable sonnets include "Love is not all" ("it is not meat nor drink / Nor slumber nor a roof against the rain"), "I will put Chaos into fourteen lines," "I shall forget you presently," a Bluebeard retelling in which the secret chamber is empty, and a "Sonnet in iambic tetrameter." She also wrote "O, Think Not I Am Faithful" ("I am most faithless when I most am true").

General / Biographical

Millay was also known as Nancy Boyd. She was associated with the Provincetown Players alongside Susan Glaspell and Eugene O'Neill. She wrote the anti-war play Aria da Capo (featuring Corydon and Thyrsis, with a masque overseen by Cothurnus) and a libretto for Deems Taylor's opera The King's Henchman. She lived at a farmhouse called Steepletop in upstate New York, which Mary Oliver later visited. She wrote "propaganda poems" for the War Writers' Board and "Justice Denied in Massachusetts" about the Sacco and Vanzetti executions. Also wrote "Dirge Without Music" ("I am not resigned"), "Recuerdo" ("We were very tired, we were very merry"), "Time Does Not Bring Relief," and "Murder of Lidice" about the Nazi massacre. Had a relationship with Sara Teasdale and Thomas Hardy praised her poetry.

Summary of Facts

Edna St. Vincent Millay is one of the most frequently clued American poets in quizbowl. Her breakthrough poem "Renascence," written at age 19, opens with the iconic lines "All I could see from where I stood / Was three long mountains and a wood" and describes the speaker being crushed by infinity, sinking underground, and experiencing spiritual rebirth. It controversially lost a contest to Orrick Johns in The Lyric Year.

Millay's most famous short poem is "First Fig" from A Few Figs from Thistles, which reads "My candle burns at both ends; / It will not last the night; / But ah, my foes, and oh, my friends— / It gives a lovely light!" This is one of the most common giveaway clues for Millay.

As a sonneteer, Millay is known for "Euclid alone has looked on Beauty bare," which imagines "the terrible day, when first the shaft into his vision shone / of light anatomized" and commands those who "prate of Beauty" to "hold their peace." "What lips my lips have kissed" compares the speaker to a tree whose boughs are "more silent than before" and observes that "the rain is full of ghosts tonight." Her collection Fatal Interview contains 52 sonnets charting a year-long affair. Other sonnets include "Love is not all," "I will put Chaos into fourteen lines," a Bluebeard retelling where the secret chamber is empty, and "O, Think Not I Am Faithful."

"The Ballad of the Harp-Weaver" won the Pulitzer and describes a mother who freezes to death while weaving "the clothes of a king's son" for her child using a "harp with a woman's head." Millay was part of the Provincetown Players alongside Susan Glaspell and wrote the play Aria da Capo. She also wrote "Recuerdo" ("We were very tired, we were very merry"), "Justice Denied in Massachusetts" about Sacco and Vanzetti, and "Dirge Without Music." She lived at Steepletop and Thomas Hardy said America had two great attractions: skyscrapers and her poetry.
