Poetry

Warren's poetry is collected in volumes including Promises and Now and Then. He wrote a folk-ballad about Billie Potts, in which a birthmark 'shaped for luck' allows a serial killer's wife to identify their returning son. He used soaring hawk imagery in poems like 'The Heart of Autumn,' 'Mortal Limit,' and 'Evening Hawk.' Another poem imagines hearing history dripping 'in darkness like the leaking pipe in a cellar.' He wrote that John Greenleaf Whittier's 'sappy and easily-parodied nostalgia was a star that still belongs in the constellation of American poetry.'

New Criticism and Southern Agrarians

Warren co-founded the Southern Review with Cleanth Brooks and Charles Pipkin, making him a central figure of New Criticism. Brooks and Warren co-authored the textbook Understanding Fiction, which claimed that a story must have 'a meaning in moral terms.' Warren contributed the pro-segregationist essay 'The Briar Patch' to the Southern Agrarian manifesto I'll Take My Stand (1930), alongside John Crowe Ransom and Allen Tate, but later repudiated that essay and became an integrationist. He was a faculty member at the University of Iowa and a co-founder of the New Criticism movement centered at Vanderbilt University. He compiled a 1965 book of interviews with civil rights activists, in which Ralph Ellison appeared.

Other Works

Warren's other novels include World Enough and Time, which used the Kentucky Tragedy as its basis, and the short story 'Blackberry Winter,' about a tramp doing work on Seth's parents' farm in Tennessee during a cold spell in June, where the protagonist visits Old Jebb and the bedridden Dellie. He served as US Poet Laureate. Brooks quoted Warren's idea about poets submitting 'to the fires of irony' in The Well Wrought Urn.

Summary of Facts

Robert Penn Warren was an American poet and novelist, a central figure of the New Criticism movement and the Southern Agrarians. He co-founded the Southern Review with Cleanth Brooks and Charles Pipkin, and co-authored the textbook Understanding Fiction with Brooks, which argued that a story must have 'a meaning in moral terms.'

His most famous novel, All the King's Men, is narrated by Jack Burden, a reporter who learns that his real father is not 'the Scholarly Attorney,' theorizes 'The Great Twitch,' and researches his ancestor Cass Mastern. The central figure is the politician Willie Stark, based on Louisiana governor Huey Long, who gains popularity after a fire-escape collapse kills three schoolchildren. The novel also features Anne Stanton and Judge Irwin.

Warren contributed the pro-segregationist essay 'The Briar Patch' to the 1930 Southern Agrarian manifesto I'll Take My Stand, alongside John Crowe Ransom and Allen Tate, centered at Vanderbilt University. He later repudiated that essay and became an integrationist. He compiled a 1965 book of interviews with civil rights activists that included Ralph Ellison.

His poetry collections include Promises and Now and Then. He wrote a folk-ballad about Billie Potts in which a birthmark 'shaped for luck' allows a serial killer's wife to identify their son. He used soaring hawk imagery in 'Evening Hawk,' 'Mortal Limit,' and 'The Heart of Autumn.' Another poem imagines history dripping 'in darkness like the leaking pipe in a cellar.' He wrote that Whittier's nostalgia was a 'star' in the 'constellation' of American poetry.

Other works include the novel World Enough and Time (based on the Kentucky Tragedy), the short story 'Blackberry Winter' (about a tramp on Seth's farm during a Tennessee cold spell), and the novel whose character Jeremiah Beaumont appears alongside Anne Stanton and Judge Irwin. He served as US Poet Laureate and was a faculty member at the University of Iowa.
