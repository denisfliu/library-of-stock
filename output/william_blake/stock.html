English Romantic poet, painter, and printmaker. Known for Songs of Innocence and of Experience (containing 'The Tyger,' 'The Lamb,' 'The Chimney Sweeper,' 'The Sick Rose,' 'Jerusalem'), The Marriage of Heaven and Hell, and visual works including The Ancient of Days, Newton, the Great Red Dragon series, The Ghost of a Flea, and Pity. Created his own mythology involving Urizen, the Four Zoas, and Albion. Used relief etching/illuminated printing.

Summary of Facts

William Blake (1757-1827) was an English Romantic poet, painter, and printmaker who created a vast personal mythology and illustrated his own poetry using relief etching (illuminated printing) with copper plates and acid. He colored his etchings with watercolor pigments and called his monotypes on millboards 'frescoes.' Samuel Palmer and a brotherhood of admirers known as 'The Ancients' championed his work. He moved to a cottage in Felpham hired by William Hayley in 1800, where he wrote 'Jerusalem.' The Swedenborgian patron C. A. Tulk introduced him to Coleridge. Blake believed that imagination was 'not a state but human existence itself' and wrote to Reverend John Trusler that 'nature is imagination itself.'

His poetry collection Songs of Innocence and of Experience contains his most famous poems: 'The Tyger' ('What immortal hand or eye / Could frame thy fearful symmetry,' 'burning bright in the forests of the night'; Northrop Frye's study Fearful Symmetry takes its title from it; the penultimate stanza says the stars 'threw down their spears and watered heaven with their tears'), 'The Lamb' ('softest clothing woolly bright,' 'Dost thou know who made thee?'), 'The Chimney Sweeper' (two versions — the Innocence version with Tom Dacre and an angel with a bright key, the Experience version with 'a little black thing among the snow' and 'God and his priest and king make up a heaven of our misery'), 'The Sick Rose' ('invisible worm / That flies in the night'), 'London' ('marks of weakness, marks of woe' near 'the chartered Thames,' the compound adjective 'mind-forg'd manacles'), 'Holy Thursday,' 'The Garden of Love,' 'Earth's Answer,' 'The Human Abstract,' 'Infant Sorrow,' 'The Little Black Boy,' 'The Echoing Green' (George Orwell's 'Such, Such Were the Joys'), 'A Poison Tree' ('I was angry with my foe: I told it not, my wrath did grow'), 'A Little Girl Lost' (Lyca taken to a cave by lions), 'The Fly' ('my thoughtless hand has brushed away'), 'The Clod and the Pebble,' and 'Auguries of Innocence' ('To see a World in a grain of sand').

His poem 'Jerusalem' ('And did those feet in ancient time') was set to music by Hubert Parry and became a patriotic hymn. It denounces 'dark Satanic Mills' and calls for a 'bow of burning gold,' 'arrows of desire,' and 'chariot of fire.' The speaker vows not to 'cease from mental fight.' It serves as the preface to his epic Milton: A Poem, written while Blake was employed by William Hayley. Jez Butterworth named his play Jerusalem after this poem (starring Mark Rylance as 'Rooster' Byron; Phaedra recites it). Joseph of Arimathea's travels with Jesus may have inspired the poem.

The Marriage of Heaven and Hell contains the 'Proverbs of Hell' (including 'Exuberance is beauty,' 'Drive your cart and your plow over the bones of the dead,' 'Sooner murder an infant in its cradle than nurse unacted desires,' 'The tygers of wrath are wiser than the horses of instruction'). It begins with the roaring of Rintrah, was conceived as an inversion of Paradise Lost, and ironically references Swedenborg. Blake claims Milton was 'of the Devil's party without knowing it' and that Milton 'wrote in fetters when he wrote of Angels and God.' Aldous Huxley took the title The Doors of Perception from it, and Olga Tokarczuk borrowed 'Drive Your Plow Over the Bones of the Dead' from its proverbs. In Tokarczuk's novel, Janina translates Blake with her friend Dizzy.

Blake's prophetic mythology centers on Urizen (god of reason holding architect's tools, inspiring Ginsberg's Moloch in 'Howl'), the Four Zoas (Urthona, Tharmas, Luvah, Urizen as emanations of the primordial giant Albion), and works including Jerusalem: The Emanation of the Giant Albion (city of Golgonooza), Milton: A Poem (Milton as a falling star entering the poet's foot, described as a comet; Ololon the skylark), Visions of the Daughters of Albion, Europe a Prophecy, Book of Thel, and Books of Los and Ahania. He stated, 'I must create a system or be enslaved by another man's.'

As a visual artist, his most iconic work is The Ancient of Days — the frontispiece for Europe a Prophecy, showing bearded Urizen crouching from the sun and holding a compass. Newton (1795) depicts a naked Newton on a mossy rock drawing with a compass, the basis for Eduardo Paolozzi's bronze sculpture outside the British Library. The Great Red Dragon watercolor series illustrates Revelation, featuring the Woman Clothed with the Sun and The Beast from the Sea (commissioned by Thomas Butts). The Ghost of a Flea shows a muscular creature on a star-studded stage extending its tongue toward a bowl of blood, part of the Visionary Heads series commissioned by astrologer John Varley. Pity illustrates a Macbeth simile with a woman on a white flying horse snatching a newborn babe. Nebuchadnezzar depicts a mad king crawling with talon-like toenails (Arthur Boyd later depicted the subject 'on fire falling over a waterfall'). His twelve 'large color prints' include Pity, Nebuchadnezzar, Newton, and The House of Death. His Laocoon engraving surrounds the sculpture with aphorisms like 'Christianity is art and not money.' The Spiritual Form of Nelson Guiding Leviathan shows Nelson in a loincloth encircled by a serpent. He created 16 engravings for John Gabriel Stedman's Narrative about slavery in Suriname, 17 wood engravings for Robert Thornton's translations of Virgil, and 12 plates illustrating Paradise Lost. His last commission was illustrations of Dante's Divine Comedy. He also illustrated Thomas Gray's poems, depicting two fish as winged, angelic beings.

Allen Ginsberg had a vision of Blake reading 'Ah! Sunflower' and was inspired to write 'Sunflower Sutra.' T.S. Eliot wrote about Blake in The Sacred Wood. Frank Kermode's The Sense of an Ending opens with an epigraph from Blake's A Vision of the Last Judgment. Joyce Cary's The Horse's Mouth features the Blake-idolizing painter Gulley Jimson. In Jim Jarmusch's film Dead Man, an accountant named William Blake is told by 'Nobody' that he is a reincarnation of the poet.
