Medieval French poet and criminal, active in 15th-century Paris. Most commonly identified by Le Grand Testament, which contains 'The Ballad of Dead Ladies' (with the famous refrain 'Where are the snows of yesteryear?') and 'The Ballad of the Hanged Men.' Called 'our sad bad glad mad brother' by Swinburne.

Summary of Facts

François Villon was a medieval French poet and criminal active in 15th-century Paris, known for writing about the Parisian underworld. He was arrested after killing the priest Philippe Sarmoise and composed Le Grand Testament shortly before his banishment and disappearance in 1463. Swinburne called him 'our sad bad glad mad brother' and 'Prince of All Ballad-Makers.'

His most frequently clued work is 'The Ballad of Dead Ladies' (Ballade des Dames du temps jadis), an ubi sunt poem — the archetypal example of the genre — that ends each stanza with 'Where are the snows of yesteryear?' (Mais où sont les neiges d'antan?). The poem asks about famous women including Héloïse ('the learned nun' who made Abelard 'lose manhood and put priesthood on'), Joan of Arc, Flora, Hipparchia, Thais, and Queen Blanche, as well as Charlemagne. It references Jean Buridan's apocryphal drowning in the Seine. Dante Gabriel Rossetti's English translation is the most famous version.

'The Ballad of the Hanged Men' (Ballade des Pendus) is spoken by executed men who ask the living for pity: 'Brothers, men who live after us, let not your hearts be hardened against us.' The bodies are 'rinsed and laundered by the rain, and by the sunlight dried and blackened too,' swaying 'at the wind's fantasy,' 'pitted like thimbles.' Each stanza ends with 'But pray God that he wills to absolve us all!' This poem was used as the epigraph to Truman Capote's In Cold Blood.

Le Grand Testament is Villon's masterwork, a long collection in octosyllabic verse containing sixteen ballades. It is presented as the work of an old, dying man reflecting on his misspent youth, and contains 'Item' stanzas bequeathing possessions to real and hypothetical people. It opens by describing Villon's humiliation at the hands of Bishop Thibault d'Aussigny.

Other notable poems include 'The Regrets of the Beautiful Heaulmière,' in which an old 'Armouress' wishes to 'be a girl again' and describes how her attractive features have deteriorated (this poem inspired a Rodin sculpture), and 'The Ballad of Fat Margot,' in which the speaker threatens to pawn a prostitute's clothes. Villon wrote a paradoxical self-description of 'dying of thirst beside the fountain,' and published a joking quatrain before his death: 'my neck will learn the weight of my ass.' Four songs in Brecht's Threepenny Opera were lifted from Karl Anton Klammer's translations of Villon. Rabelais included anecdotes about him in Gargantua and Pantagruel, and Clément Marot edited his works. Robert Louis Stevenson wrote 'A Lodging for the Night' about him.
