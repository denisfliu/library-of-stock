Summary of Facts

William Holman Hunt was a founding member of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood (with John Everett Millais and Dante Gabriel Rossetti), known for intensely detailed religious and moralistic paintings. His most iconic work, The Light of the World (1851-53), shows Christ holding a lantern and knocking at a door overgrown with thorns and weeds, with no outside handle (symbolizing that it can only be opened from within). Based on John 8 and Revelation, it hangs at Keble College, Oxford, where Hunt repainted it after discovering sixpence was being charged to view it.

The Awakening Conscience (1853) depicts a woman rising from the lap of her lover (a man in a blue smoking jacket) by a piano, filled with symbolic details: a musical arrangement of Tennyson's 'Tears, Idle Tears' discarded on the floor, a yellow glove on the rug next to a tangled skein of yarn, a greedy-eyed cat playing with a dead bird under the table, and a blue wall design with dancing women bearing globes.

The Scapegoat (1854-55) shows a goat with a red ribbon around its horns dying in the skeleton-littered salt shallows of the Dead Sea, drawing from Leviticus and attempting to synthesize Judaism and Christianity. The Hireling Shepherd (1851) depicts a man showing a death's-head hawkmoth to a girl modeled after local youth Emma Watkins. The Shadow of Death shows Jesus stretching his arms before carpentry tools, prefiguring the crucifixion, while Mary opens a box of the Magi's gifts. His Lady of Shalott includes a split mirror and unwinding thread, worked on with Edward Robert Hughes. He also painted Isabella and the Pot of Basil (showing Isabella after Lorenzo's death, unlike Millais's version) and A Converted British Family Sheltering a Christian Missionary as a companion to Millais's Christ in the House of His Parents.
