General / Biographical

Christine de Pizan (c. 1364-1430), medieval French author born in Venice. Court writer for several Dukes of Burgundy, including John the Fearless. Wrote the 'Quarrel of the Rose' letter to Jean de Montreuil attacking Jean de Meun's immorality. Also wrote 'Letter to the God of Love' attacking Jean de Meun. Her final written work was a poem dedicated to Joan of Arc. Wrote The Mutation of Fortune (in which she turns from a woman into a 'complete, natural man' after her husband's death, citing cases of Iphis and Tiresias), The Path of Long Study (reads The Consolation of Philosophy before being led on a dream-journey by the Cumaean Sibyl), Letter/Epistle of Othea to Hector (Penthesilea embodies charity), The Book of Feats of Arms and Chivalry (defined a 'just war'), and elegiac ballades including 'Alone am I and alone I wish to be.' Promoted female education.

Summary of Facts

Christine de Pizan (c. 1364-1430) is a medieval French author and proto-feminist, asked about almost exclusively through her most famous work, The Book of the City of Ladies.

The Book of the City of Ladies was written in response to the misogyny in Jean de Meun's continuation of The Romance of the Rose. In the work, the autobiographical protagonist reads Matheolus's Lamentations and despairs, fearing she is an 'aberration of nature.' Three personified ladies -- Reason, Rectitude, and Justice -- visit her and help her build a city on the Plain/Field of Letters using famous historical women as building blocks. Reason gives her a jeweled mirror that allows people to 'see themselves as they truly are.' Lady Reason aids in building the foundation in Book I, while Rectitude and Justice help in the other two books. The Virgin Mary is introduced by a woman carrying a vessel of fine gold. The work was likely based on Boccaccio's On Famous Women. The sequel, The Book of the Three Virtues, is an education manual explaining how to run a household.

Christine kicked off the 'Quarrel of the Rose' by writing a letter to Jean de Montreuil attacking the immorality of Jean de Meun's poem. She also attacked de Meun in her proto-feminist treatise 'Letter to the God of Love,' arguing that women cannot be vile or foolish if men expend so much effort to trick and seduce them.

Her other works include The Mutation of Fortune, in which the narrator turns from a woman into a 'complete, natural man' after her husband's death, citing the cases of Iphis and Tiresias; The Path of Long Study, in which she reads Boethius's Consolation of Philosophy before being led on a dream-journey by the Cumaean Sibyl; the Letter/Epistle of Othea to Hector, in which Penthesilea embodies charity; The Book of Feats of Arms and Chivalry, which defined a 'just war' as one fought to achieve peace without pillaging or harming non-combatants; and elegiac ballades including 'Alone am I and alone I wish to be.' Her final written work was a poem dedicated to Joan of Arc. She served as a court writer for several Dukes of Burgundy, including John the Fearless.
