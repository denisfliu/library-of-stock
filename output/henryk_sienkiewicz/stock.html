Polish Nobel laureate in Literature, best known for historical fiction. His most frequently clued work is Quo Vadis, set in Nero's Rome and centering on the love between the Roman patrician Marcus Vinicius and the Christian woman Lygia. Also famous for The Trilogy, a series of three historical novels set in 17th-century Poland: With Fire and Sword, The Deluge, and Fire in the Steppe. Other works include The Teutonic Knights, In Desert and Wilderness, Without Dogma, and the short story 'Janko the Musician.' Associated with Polish positivism.
Quo Vadis

Sienkiewicz's best-known novel, set in Rome during the reign of Emperor Nero. The title comes from a Latin phrase ('Where are you going?') allegedly said by Saint Peter to the risen Christ when Peter was fleeing Rome. The plot centers on the Roman patrician Marcus Vinicius, who falls in love with the Christian hostage Lygia (also called Ligia). Petronius, called the 'Arbiter of Elegance,' serves as Vinicius's uncle and adviser. The scheming Chilo (Chilon Chilonides) helps Vinicius track down Lygia and claims to read the future; he later gets his tongue torn out. Lygia's massive bodyguard Ursus breaks the neck of a charging bull/aurochs in the arena and nearly kills Vinicius. Lygia is strapped nude to the back of a bull during arena games. Nero blames Christians for the Great Fire of Rome and witnesses the burning. The slave Eunice is in love with her master Petronius. Saint Peter, while leaving Rome, is met by Jesus, then returns to Rome to be martyred. Wikipedia

The Trilogy (With Fire and Sword, The Deluge, Fire in the Steppe)

A series of three historical novels set in 17th-century Poland, collectively known as The Trilogy. The novels deal with Polish history during the Khmelnytsky Uprising (With Fire and Sword), the Swedish invasion or 'Deluge' (The Deluge), and the wars leading up to the Battle of Vienna (Fire in the Steppe). Key characters include Jan Skrzetuski and Helena in With Fire and Sword, Kmita and Olenka in The Deluge, and Michal Wolodyjowski in Fire in the Steppe. The brash general Zagloba appears throughout. Other Works

Several other works by Sienkiewicz appear in clues, usually as passing identifiers. Without Dogma is a novel of manners written in diary form about the decadent youth Leon, exemplifying Polish positivism; its protagonist engages in an affair and later commits suicide. On the Bright Shore follows an artist's travels in Paris with Pani Elzen and her sons Romulus and Remus. Summary of Facts

Henryk Sienkiewicz is a Polish Nobel laureate in Literature, most frequently clued for two major bodies of work: Quo Vadis and The Trilogy. Quo Vadis, set in Nero's Rome, is by far the most common single work in clues. Its central plot concerns the Roman patrician Marcus Vinicius falling in love with the Christian woman Lygia (or Ligia), who is a barbarian hostage. Petronius, the 'Arbiter of Elegance,' is Vinicius's uncle and adviser who navigates Nero's court through flattery and wit. The scheming Greek Chilo (Chilon Chilonides) helps Vinicius track down Lygia and later has his tongue torn out. Lygia's giant bodyguard Ursus is famous for breaking the neck of a bull or aurochs in the arena and for nearly killing Vinicius. In a particularly graphic scene, Lygia is strapped nude to the back of a bull during the arena games. Nero blames Christians for the Great Fire of Rome. Saint Peter, while fleeing Rome, encounters the risen Christ and asks 'Quo vadis?' ('Where are you going?'), which gives the novel its title; Peter then returns to Rome to be martyred. The slave Eunice is in love with Petronius.

The Trilogy consists of three novels set in 17th-century Poland: With Fire and Sword, The Deluge, and Fire in the Steppe. With Fire and Sword fictionalizes the Khmelnytsky Uprising (a Cossack revolt) and centers on the love between Jan Skrzetuski and Helena, who is rescued from the Tatars by Pan Longin. A falcon draws together the hands of Helena and the protagonist at the novel's start, where the protagonist assists a revolutionary posing as Abdank. The Deluge features the character Kmita, who pines for Olenka; it opens with the Billevich family whose patriarch Heraclius arranges a marriage in his will, and involves the Swedes being ordered by Radziwill to kill the Colonels. Fire in the Steppe concludes with Michal Wolodyjowski dying while protecting a gunpowder stash just before the Battle of Vienna. The brash general Zagloba is a recurring character.

Other works that appear in clues include The Teutonic Knights (Knights of the Cross), in which Danusia saves Zbyszko from execution after he attacks Kuno von Liechtenstein. Without Dogma is a novel of manners in diary form about the decadent youth Leon, exemplifying Polish positivism, whose protagonist commits suicide. In Desert and Wilderness is a children's novel featuring Nell and the Mahdi in Sudan. On the Bright Shore follows an artist in Paris with Pani Elzen and her sons Romulus and Remus. 'Janko the Musician' is a short story about a peasant child whipped to death for sneaking into a manor to play violin.

Biographically, Sienkiewicz is consistently identified as Polish and a Nobel laureate. He is associated with Polish positivism. A notable anecdote connects him to Baron Wladyslaw Moes, the real-life inspiration for Thomas Mann's Tadzio in Death in Venice: Sienkiewicz reportedly dandled the boy at a wedding when Moes was six, until the child urinated on his lap.
