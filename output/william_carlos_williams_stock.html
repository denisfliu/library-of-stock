William Carlos Williams

American modernist poet and physician from New Jersey. Best known for "This Is Just to Say" (plums in the icebox), "The Red Wheelbarrow" ("so much depends / upon"), the epic Paterson ("no ideas but in things"), and "Asphodel, That Greeny Flower." Invented the triadic line. Associated with Imagism, worked as a pediatrician/obstetrician in Rutherford, NJ. Wrote the introduction to Ginsberg's Howl.
This Is Just to Say

One of the most frequently clued poems in quizbowl. The speaker apologizes for eating "the plums / that were in / the icebox" which the addressee was "probably / saving / for breakfast." Ends with "forgive me / they were delicious / so sweet / and so cold." Williams claimed it was "metrically absolutely regular." His wife Flossie wrote a "reply." Kenneth Koch parodied it in "Variations on a Theme by William Carlos Williams." Wallace Stevens wrote "Nuances on a Theme by Williams" inspired by "El Hombre."

The Red Wheelbarrow

Iconic Imagist poem stating "so much depends / upon / a red wheel / barrow / glazed with rain / water / beside the white / chickens." Henry Sayre compared its artistic merit to Duchamp's Fountain. In "Canthara," Williams recalls learning about the Spanish fly from an old man who owned such an object.

Paterson

Book-length epic poem about the city of Paterson, New Jersey, which "lies in the valley under the Passaic Falls." Repeats the maxim "no ideas but in things." Won the inaugural National Book Award for Poetry. Expanded from a 1926 poem into five and a half books. First section is "The Delineaments of the Giants." Commands "Say it!" before the motto. Imagines "A man like a city and a woman like a flower." Allen Ginsberg's letter appears in Book IV. The poem addresses "blank faces of the houses and cylindrical trees."

Spring and All

A collection whose title poem begins "By the road to the contagious hospital" and describes the approach of spring. Contains "The Red Wheelbarrow." Also contains "To Elsie," which begins "The pure products of America go crazy." Dedicated to Charles Demuth. Describes "twiggy stuff" of bushes: "They enter the new world naked, cold, uncertain of all save that they enter." Williams wrote it as a response to T.S. Eliot's The Waste Land.

Asphodel, That Greeny Flower

A long love poem addressed to Williams's wife Flossie, published in triadic-line form in the collection Journey to Love. The title flower is "like a buttercup / upon its branching stem — save that it's green and wooden." Declares "All women are not Helen, / I know that, / but have Helen in their hearts." Echoes Xenophon: "the sea! The sea!" States "it is difficult / to get the news from poems / yet men die miserably every day / for lack / of what is found there." Contains Homer's catalogue of ships which "fills up the time." Introduced as a "reply to Greek and Latin with the bare hands."

General / Biographical

Williams was a physician (pediatrician/obstetrician) in Rutherford, New Jersey. Associated with Imagism through friendship with Ezra Pound (met at Penn Medical School). Invented the triadic line. Wrote the introduction to Allen Ginsberg's Howl and Other Poems ("Hold back the edges of your gowns, Ladies, we are going through hell"). Also wrote "The Great Figure" ("I saw the figure 5 in gold"), which inspired Charles Demuth's painting I Saw the Figure Five in Gold. Wrote In the American Grain (short biographies of figures like Aaron Burr, Eric the Red, Daniel Boone). "Poem (As the Cat)" describes a cat climbing over a jamcloset. "At the Ball Game" describes a "deadly, terrifying" crowd. "Dance Russe" imagines dancing naked before a mirror. "The Yachts" describes boats contending in a sea. Wrote the short story "The Use of Force" about a doctor forcing a child to open her mouth. Collaborated with Louis Zukofsky for 36 years. Charles Olson's "Projective Verse" built on Williams's "composition by field." Quoted vast sections of "Projective Verse" in his autobiography. Puerto Rican heritage reflected in Al Que Quiere!. Also wrote Kora in Hell, "The Desert Music" (set at US-Mexico border), "Landscape with the Fall of Icarus" (from Pictures from Brueghel), and "The Last Words of My English Grandmother."

Summary of Facts

William Carlos Williams is one of the most frequently clued American modernist poets. His most famous poem, "This Is Just to Say," describes eating "the plums / that were in / the icebox" and ends "forgive me / they were delicious / so sweet / and so cold." Kenneth Koch parodied it in "Variations on a Theme by William Carlos Williams," and Williams's wife Flossie wrote a reply. "The Red Wheelbarrow" is equally iconic: "so much depends / upon / a red wheel / barrow / glazed with rain / water / beside the white / chickens."

Williams's book-length epic Paterson, set in the New Jersey city "under the Passaic Falls," repeats the maxim "no ideas but in things" and won the inaugural National Book Award for Poetry. It was expanded into five and a half books, and Allen Ginsberg's letter appears in Book IV. The collection Spring and All opens with the title poem "By the road to the contagious hospital" and also contains "To Elsie" ("The pure products of America go crazy").

"Asphodel, That Greeny Flower" is a love poem to his wife Flossie in triadic-line form, comparing the title flower to "a buttercup upon its branching stem" and declaring "All women are not Helen, I know that, but have Helen in their hearts." It contains the famous lines about how "it is difficult / to get the news from poems / yet men die miserably every day / for lack / of what is found there."

Williams was a physician (pediatrician/obstetrician) in Rutherford, New Jersey. He invented the triadic line. He wrote the introduction to Ginsberg's Howl ("Hold back the edges of your gowns, Ladies"). "The Great Figure" inspired Demuth's painting. His prose work In the American Grain contains essays on Aaron Burr, Daniel Boone, and others. He collaborated with Louis Zukofsky and influenced Charles Olson's Projective Verse. He also wrote "At the Ball Game," "Dance Russe," "The Yachts," and the short story "The Use of Force."
