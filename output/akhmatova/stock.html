Russian Silver Age poet (born Anna Gorenko), a leading figure of the Acmeist movement. Best known for the long poems Requiem and Poem Without a Hero. Requiem responds to the imprisonment of her son Lev Gumilev during Stalin's Great Purge, opening with the prose section 'Instead of a Preface' in which a blue-lipped woman asks 'Can you describe this?' Poem Without a Hero opens at the Fontanka House and reflects on the Leningrad siege. First husband was the poet Nikolay Gumilev (executed 1921). Called 'half-nun, half-harlot' by Andrei Zhdanov. Also wrote the poem 'Lot's Wife.' Isaiah Berlin visited her in 1945, inspiring the 'Guest from the Future' figure. Rose to fame with the collections Evening and The Rosary.

Summary of Facts

Anna Akhmatova (born Anna Gorenko, 1889–1966) is one of the greatest Russian poets of the 20th century and a leading figure of the Acmeist movement during the Silver Age of Russian poetry.

Her most famous work is Requiem, a cycle of poems written over three decades in response to the imprisonment of her son Lev Gumilev during Stalin's Great Terror. The poem opens with the prose section 'Instead of a Preface,' in which Akhmatova stands in line outside a Leningrad prison and a woman with 'lips blue from the cold' asks 'Can you describe this?' — to which the speaker responds 'I can,' and 'a sort of smile' slides across the woman's face. The section 'The Sentence' contains the lines 'I have so much to do today: I must kill memory once and for all.' The 'Crucifixion' section begins 'Weep not for me, mother' and contrasts Mary Magdalene tearing her hair with the 'silent and alone' Virgin Mary. The poem speaks of 'a hundred million people' shouting through the speaker's 'tortured mouth.' The Epilogue states 'I'd like to call you all by name, but the list has been removed.' The poem ends with a prison dove cooing and 'ships sail softly down' the Neva. The dedication mentions 'Siberian snows.' Requiem was shared via samizdat; Akhmatova would write poems line by line, have friends memorize them, then burn the slips of paper. The poem also memorializes her husband Nikolay Punin and responds to the broader suffering under Stalin's purges.

Poem Without a Hero is Akhmatova's longest work, partly set at the Fontanka House (Fountain House / Sheremet'ev Palace) during the siege of Leningrad. It opens 'I have lit my treasured candles, one by one.' It features a masquerade ball attended by fictional characters including Faust, Dorian Gray, and Thomas Glahn, with the 'Guest from the Future' representing Isaiah Berlin. The poem asks 'What have poets, in any case, to do with sin?' and reflects on the Silver Age with the line 'The hellish harlequinade from 1913 has passed.' 'Silence itself' speaks the 'Fourth and Final Chapter.' The narrator calls Prince Charming 'grace incarnate.' It was dedicated to those who died during the Leningrad siege. When asked to explain the poem, Akhmatova said 'What I have written, I have written.'

Other frequently clued poems include 'Lot's Wife,' which asks 'who will grieve for this woman?' and describes 'a single glance: a sudden dart of pain / stitching her eyes before she made a sound.' 'Cinque' describes a 'mute world' with 'only two voices: yours and mine,' written after Isaiah Berlin's visit. 'The Guest' features the reply 'To be with you in Hell.'

Akhmatova was a member of the Acmeist movement, which met at the Stray Dog Cafe and published in the journal Apollon. Her first husband was the poet Nikolay Gumilev, co-founder of Acmeism, who was executed by the Cheka in 1921. She was called 'half-nun, half-harlot' by the Soviet cultural commissar Andrei Zhdanov. She rose to fame with the poetry collections Evening (with a preface by Mikhail Kuzmin) and The Rosary. She lived at the Fontanka House, now the site of her literary museum. Isaiah Berlin visited her in 1945 in a mythologized all-night meeting after which she collapsed and said 'I am in love.' She was a close friend of Osip Mandelstam, who compared her to Cassandra. Marina Tsvetaeva called her a 'muse of lament' and wrote 'I give you my town of many bells.' She uncharacteristically praised Stalin in a 1950 poem. Born in Ukraine, her poem 'The Moon in a Zenith' refers to Tashkent as 'my dear Asian home.'
