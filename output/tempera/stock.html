Tempera (General / Medium)

Egg tempera is a paint medium in which pigments are bound in egg yolk (and sometimes casein glue). It must be applied in thin layers, producing a translucent, matte appearance. It was the primary European panel-painting medium in the Middle Ages and early Renaissance, before being supplanted by oil painting around 1500. The technique was described in detail by Cennino Cennini in Il libro dell'arte (The Craftsman's Handbook), which gave ratios for mixing and was the first extensive European document on the medium. Gesso — a mixture of plaster and rabbit-skin glue — was first developed as a ground for tempera paintings. A 20th-century revival was led by Paul Cadmus, Jacob Lawrence, and Andrew Wyeth (who learned from Peter Hurd). The Birmingham Group learned the technique from a handbook translated by Edwardian artist Christiana Herringham.

Duccio and the Sienese School

Duccio di Buoninsegna was the foremost medieval artist who worked in egg tempera and gold leaf. His Rucellai Madonna (1285, Siena) depicts three angels on either side of the Virgin and Child and is an early example of gold leaf combined with egg tempera in the Byzantine icon tradition. His Maesta is another major altarpiece in this medium. Medieval Russian icons also used egg tempera.

Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper →

Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper (in the refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie, Milan) used an experimental mixture of oil paint and tempera, applied with white lead undercoat in a fresco-secco technique. This was a departure from the standard method and caused the painting to begin deteriorating almost immediately. Giorgio Vasari described it as a 'muddle of blots' less than 60 years after it was painted. Henry James called it the 'saddest work of art in the world' and it has a 15-minute viewing limit.

Leonora Carrington's Tempera Paintings →

The Surrealist artist Leonora Carrington used egg tempera in several of her paintings, including The Giantess (The Guardian of the Egg), which depicts a giantess enshrouded in golden wheat circled by geese, and The Kitchen Garden on the Eyot, in which a white figure emerges from an orange tree. She used eggs as a material to depict eggs in these works. Carrington wrote that the egg was 'the dividing line between the Big and the Small' during her time in a sanatorium during the Spanish Civil War.

Summary of Facts

Tempera (egg tempera) is a painting medium in which dry pigments are ground and mixed with a water-soluble binder, most commonly egg yolk — hence the name. The medium must be applied in thin, careful layers, which produces a characteristic translucent and matte surface; thick applications crack. Gesso — a mixture of plaster and rabbit-skin glue — was first developed specifically as a ground for tempera panel paintings. Casein glue is another binder sometimes used in the medium.

Tempera was the dominant panel-painting medium in medieval Europe, especially in Italy, and was used extensively in the Byzantine icon tradition. The great Sienese artist Duccio di Buoninsegna exemplifies its medieval use: his Rucellai Madonna (1285) combines gold leaf and egg tempera to depict three angels on either side of the Virgin and Child, and his Maesta altarpiece similarly employs the medium. Gold leaf was frequently combined with tempera in these devotional works. Medieval Russian icons were also typically painted in egg tempera. Tempera was fully supplanted by oil painting during the Renaissance, a transition largely complete by around 1500. Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper in Milan's Santa Maria delle Grazie is a cautionary tale: Leonardo innovated by mixing oil paint with tempera (using a white-lead undercoat in fresco-secco), which caused the work to begin deteriorating almost immediately. Giorgio Vasari described it as a 'muddle of blots' fewer than 60 years after it was painted; Henry James called it 'the saddest work of art in the world,' and today viewers have only 15 minutes to see it.

The primary literary source for the tempera technique is Cennino Cennini's Il libro dell'arte (translated as The Craftsman's Handbook), the first extensive European text on the subject. Cennini provided precise ratios for mixing the medium, advised on pigments (warning that vermilion should be tempered or it turns black), and recommended white lead as an undercoat in fresco-secco finishing. The text also discusses recipes for azure pigments and notes that a 'country-laid' egg yolk improves pale azures.

In the 20th century, tempera underwent a notable revival. Andrew Wyeth — who famously used it for Christina's World — learned the technique from fellow artist Peter Hurd. Paul Cadmus and Jacob Lawrence also contributed to this revival. The British Birmingham Group learned tempera from a handbook translated by Edwardian artist Christiana Herringham. The Surrealist painter Leonora Carrington used egg tempera for several of her mythological and fantastical works, including The Giantess (The Guardian of the Egg) — depicting a giantess enshrouded in golden wheat circled by geese — and The Kitchen Garden on the Eyot, in which a white figure emerges from an orange tree. Carrington used actual eggs as a material to paint eggs in these works, and wrote that the egg represented 'the dividing line between the Big and the Small.' Contemporary artist Kerry James Marshall also worked in egg tempera for a monochrome self-portrait featuring a prominent smile with a missing tooth.
