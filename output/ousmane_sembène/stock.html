Summary of Facts

Ousmane Sembène (1923–2007) was a Senegalese author and filmmaker, widely called the 'father of African cinema' and often identified as the first person from sub-Saharan Africa to direct a feature film. He frequently adapted his own novels and stories into films. He trained at the Gorky Film Studios in the USSR under mentor Mark Donskoy. He is known for saying 'Why be a sunflower and turn toward the sun? I myself am the sun' when asked whether his films were understood in Europe. Several of his works have been restored by Martin Scorsese's World Cinema Project.

His most widely clued works are Xala and God's Bits of Wood. Xala (1973 novel, 1975 film) follows the corrupt businessman El Hadji Kader Beye, who is cursed with xala (impotence) on the night he marries his third wife N'Gone — a punishment for having cheated a beggar. El Hadji flaunts his wealth by having his Mercedes washed with imported mineral water. The film ends with beggars spitting on the naked El Hadji. God's Bits of Wood (Les Bouts de bois de Dieu, 1960) is a novel about the 1947–1948 strike by workers on the Dakar-Niger Railway, led by Ibrahim Bakayoko. Colonial authorities turn off public water fountains in retribution. At the climax, women march from Thiès to Dakar. A character named Samba N'Doulougou serves as a 'walking newspaper.'

Black Girl (La Noire de..., 1966) was Sembène's debut feature and the first feature film from sub-Saharan Africa. It follows Diouana, a young Senegalese woman hired as a maid by a wealthy French couple, who is mistreated and ultimately kills herself. In the final scene, a young Senegalese boy weeps while returning a traditional mask that had been gifted to the French employers. Sembène won the Prix Jean Vigo for this film. Moolaade (2004), his final film, addresses female genital mutilation: Colle uses moolaade (a protective magic spell) to help women and girls avoid the practice. The Black Docker is a novel in which a laborer is put on trial for the murder of Ginette, who had published his award-winning novel about the slave ship Sirius under her own name.
