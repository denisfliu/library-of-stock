Snow-Bound: A Winter Idyl

A long poem about a family gathering around a fireplace during a snowstorm, exchanging stories. A "dear aunt" calls up "her girlhood memories" of "huskings and the apple-bees." The speaker invites the reader to "Sit with me by the homestead hearth / And stretch the hands of memory forth." Describes a "universe of sky and snow" and the creation of a tunnel "walled and overlaid / With dazzling crystal" ("Boys, a path!"). The speaker remembers testing Aladdin's lamp by rousing "the prisoned brutes within" a barn.

Barbara Frietchie

Civil War poem set in "Frederick town" (Frederick, Maryland) about an old woman who waves an American flag over Stonewall Jackson's Confederate troops. She shouts "Shoot, if you must, this old gray head, / But spare your country's flag!" after the soldiers haul down the flag. A man tells her "Who touches a hair of yon gray head / Dies like a dog!" Describes "Forty" flags "with silver stars." Ogden Nash referenced her in "Taboo to Boot," noting she "scratched / When she was itchy." Inspired an 1899 Clyde Fitch play.

Maud Muller

Poem about an encounter between a judge and a country girl who never marry. The judge later wishes he had married her. Contains the famous lines "Of all sad words of tongue or pen, / The saddest are these: 'It might have been!'" The girl offers the judge a drink from her "small tin cup" with spring water ("a sweeter draught / From a fairer hand was never quaffed"). A woman worn down by "child-birth pain" imagines a manly form by her side.

Ichabod

A poem attacking Daniel Webster for endorsing the Fugitive Slave Act. Opens with "So fallen! So lost!" and mourns that "the glory from his gray hairs gone forevermore." Declares "When faith is lost, when honor dies, / The man is dead!" The speaker states "Let not the land, once proud of him, insult him now."

General / Biographical

Quaker poet and abolitionist, one of the Fireside Poets. Also wrote "Telling the Bees" (European folk tradition of informing bees of deaths; "Mistress Mary is dead and gone!"), "The Brewing of Soma" (source of the hymn "Dear Lord and Father of Mankind" / "Forgive our foolish ways!"), "Skipper Ireson's Ride" (captain "tarred and feathered and carried in a cart / by the women of Marblehead"), "The Bartholdi Statue" (praising France for the Statue of Liberty), "The Cypress-Tree of Ceylon," and "At Port Royal" ("Song of the Negro Boatmen"). Estate called Oak Knoll, visited by Dorothea Dix and Pedro II.

Summary of Facts

John Greenleaf Whittier was a Quaker abolitionist poet and one of the Fireside Poets. His best-known work is Snow-Bound: A Winter Idyl, about a family gathering around a fireplace during a snowstorm and exchanging stories. The poem invites the reader to "Sit with me by the homestead hearth."

"Barbara Frietchie" is set in Frederick, Maryland during the Civil War, where the title character waves an American flag over Stonewall Jackson's troops and shouts "Shoot, if you must, this old gray head, / But spare your country's flag!" Ogden Nash parodied her in "Taboo to Boot."

"Maud Muller" tells of a judge and country girl who never marry, containing the famous lines "Of all sad words of tongue or pen, / The saddest are these: 'It might have been!'" "Ichabod" attacks Daniel Webster for endorsing the Fugitive Slave Act, declaring "When faith is lost, when honor dies, / The man is dead!"

Other notable poems include "Telling the Bees" (a folk tradition of informing bees of deaths, ending "Mistress Mary is dead and gone!"), "The Brewing of Soma" (source of the hymn "Dear Lord and Father of Mankind"), and "Skipper Ireson's Ride" (a captain tarred and feathered by the women of Marblehead). His estate Oak Knoll was visited by Dorothea Dix and Pedro II of Brazil.
