The poem contrasts the speaker's writing vocation with his father's and grandfather's manual labor. It is one of the most common giveaway identifiers for Heaney in quizbowl.

Other Works and General

Heaney won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1995. He was Irish, from Northern Ireland, and a founding member (with Brian Friel and Seamus Deane) of the Field Day Theatre Company. He adapted Sophocles' Philoctetes as The Cure at Troy, which contains the oft-quoted passage about 'the longed-for tidal wave of justice'; part of this play was recited by Lin-Manuel Miranda at Joe Biden's inauguration. He also adapted Antigone as The Burial at Thebes. He declared 'No lyric has ever stopped a tank' in his essay 'The Government of the Tongue.' Heaney collaborated with Ted Hughes on the anthology The Rattle Bag. His other collections include Station Island (which features the character Sweeney from the medieval Irish tale Buile Suibhne, which he translated as Sweeney Astray), The Haw Lantern, Wintering Out, and Opened Ground. His poems 'The Early Purges' (about drowning kittens) and 'Churning Day' also appear. He was influenced by W.B. Yeats and Czeslaw Milosz. 'At a Potato Digging' describes 'Centuries / Of fear and homage to the famine god' and 'stinking potatoes' that 'fouled the land.' His poem 'Postscript' says 'it is useless to think you'll park and capture' the sight of swans. Paul Muldoon carried his coffin and recalled bouncing him 'off like snow off a plough' in football. His essay collection Finders Keepers and The Redress of Poetry are also referenced.

Summary of Facts

Seamus Heaney (full name Seamus Justin Heaney) was an Irish poet who won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1995. He is one of the most frequently clued poets in quizbowl, with a large body of questions spanning his major collections, his verse translation of Beowulf, and his political engagement with the Troubles in Northern Ireland.

Heaney's first collection, Death of a Naturalist (1966), contains his most frequently clued poems. 'Digging' opens with 'Between my finger and my thumb / The squat pen rests; snug as a gun' and describes the speaker watching his father dig in the garden, recalling his grandfather cutting turf on Toner's bog and delivering him 'milk in a bottle / Corked sloppily with paper.' The famous declaration 'By God, the old man could handle a spade' contrasts manual labor with writing, and the poem ends 'I'll dig with it.' 'Mid-Term Break' responds to the death of Heaney's younger brother Christopher, who was killed in a road accident. The speaker sits 'all morning in the college sick bay' before coming home to see Big Jim Evans saying 'it was a hard blow,' his mother coughing 'angry tearless sighs,' and his brother wearing 'a poppy bruise on his left temple' after 'the bumper knocked him clear,' lying in 'a four-foot box, a foot for every year.' 'Blackberry-Picking' describes late-August harvesting with 'palms sticky as Bluebeard's,' only for the berries to be spoiled by 'a rat-grey fungus,' ending 'Each year I hoped they'd keep, knew they would not.' The title poem 'Death of a Naturalist' describes collecting 'jampotfuls of the jellied specks' of frogspawn at a 'flax-dam,' then returning to find frogs 'poised like mud grenades' described as 'great slime kings' 'gathered there for vengeance.' 'Personal Helicon,' which closes the collection, reflects on the speaker's childhood fascination with wells, now 'beneath all adult dignity,' ending 'I rhyme / To see myself, to set the darkness echoing.' 'Follower' depicts the speaker's father with shoulders 'globed like a full sail' leaving a 'hobnailed wake,' but by the end 'it is my father who keeps stumbling / Behind me, and will not go away.' Other poems from the collection include 'The Early Purges' (about drowning kittens, concluding 'on well-run farms pests have to be kept down') and 'Churning Day.'

Heaney's bog body poems appear in the collections Wintering Out and North. 'The Tollund Man' describes the speaker wanting to travel to Aarhus to see the figure's 'peat-brown head' with 'his last gruel of winter seeds caked in his stomach,' ending 'In the old man-killing parishes / I will feel lost, unhappy and at home.' 'The Grauballe Man' describes a figure who 'seems to weep the black river of himself' and asks 'Who will say corpse to his vivid cast?' 'Strange Fruit' begins 'Here is the girl's head' describing a decapitated bog body. The collection North also contains 'Whatever You Say, Say Nothing,' which describes a conversation with 'an English journalist in search of views / On the Irish thing' and asks 'Is there a life before death?' The poem 'North' describes 'the secular powers of the Atlantic thundering' and includes Viking imagery. The sequence 'Singing School' opens by quoting Patrick Kavanagh ('we have lived / In important places') and is dedicated to Seamus Deane.

Heaney's 1999 verse translation of Beowulf is one of his most commonly cited achievements. It famously opens with 'So.' in place of the Old English 'Hwæt!' and employs a style Heaney called 'Scullionspeak' that emphasized 'the four-squareness of the utterance.' The translation updates kennings for modern English.

Heaney adapted Sophocles' Philoctetes as The Cure at Troy, which contains the oft-quoted passage about 'the longed-for tidal wave of justice'; Lin-Manuel Miranda recited part of it at Joe Biden's inauguration. He also adapted Antigone as The Burial at Thebes. He was a founding member of the Field Day Theatre Company with Brian Friel and Seamus Deane. His essay 'The Government of the Tongue' declares 'No lyric has ever stopped a tank.' Station Island features the character Sweeney from the medieval Irish tale Buile Suibhne, which Heaney translated as Sweeney Astray. Other collections include The Haw Lantern, Opened Ground, and Wintering Out. His poem 'Casualty' describes a man 'blown to bits / out drinking in a curfew.' 'Clearances,' dedicated to his mother, describes a felled chestnut tree as 'a soul ramifying and forever / silent, beyond silence listened for.' 'Bogland' begins 'We have no prairies' and describes 'The ground itself is kind, black butter.' 'At a Potato Digging' describes 'Centuries / Of fear and homage to the famine god.' He was influenced by W.B. Yeats and Czeslaw Milosz, and his poem 'Out of the World' is dedicated to Milosz. He collaborated with Ted Hughes on the anthology The Rattle Bag. Paul Muldoon carried his coffin and recalled bouncing him 'off like snow off a plough' during a football game.
