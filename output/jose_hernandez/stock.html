Martin Fierro

A two-part gaucho epic poem. The protagonist is conscripted into the army after not voting for a judge. He insults a woman in a bar and kills a black man in a knife fight. He befriends Sergeant Cruz, and they go to live among the natives but are taken prisoner (worth 'more alive than dead'). Cruz later dies from smallpox after nursing a native chief. In the second part (The Return), Fierro's son recounts how his tutor robbed him of inheritance from his aunt. The poem culminates in a famous singing contest (payada) between Fierro and the younger brother of the black man he killed, in which they debate abstract philosophical themes -- the nature of quantity, measurement, weight, time, sky, earth, sea, night, and love. The illiterate man can't answer a riddle about months with Rs. At the end, four characters each change their names and lay down guitars. The poem is written in the verse structure of the payada (rural ballad). Borges wrote 'The End' imagining the two payada participants meeting again, with one killing the other. Borges's essay 'The Argentine Writer and Tradition' notes the payada dispenses with local color. Estanislao del Campo's satirical poem Fausto features the gaucho Anastasio the Chicken. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Jose Hernandez (1834-1886) is an Argentine writer known exclusively in quizbowl for his two-part gaucho epic poem Martin Fierro, the greatest work of the gaucho literary genre.

Martin Fierro follows the title gaucho who is conscripted into the army after not voting for a judge. He insults a woman in a bar and kills a black man in the ensuing knife fight, then befriends Sergeant Cruz and goes to live among the natives. They are taken prisoner, and Cruz later dies of smallpox after nursing a native chief. In the second part (The Return), Fierro's son tells how his tutor robbed him of inheritance from his aunt. The poem culminates in its most famous passage -- a singing contest (payada) between Fierro and the younger brother of the black man he killed. In this duel, one contestant compares the law to a spider's web that catches only small insects, and they debate abstract themes including quantity, measurement, weight, time, and love. An illiterate man can't answer a riddle about months with Rs. At the poem's end, four characters change their names and lay down their guitars. The poem's famous lines include 'I'm the bull in my own herd / and a braver bull in the next one' and 'Singing I'll die, singing they'll bury me.'

The poem is deeply connected to Jorge Luis Borges, who wrote 'The End' imagining the payada participants meeting again (with one killing the other) and whose essay 'The Argentine Writer and Tradition' notes the payada dispenses with local color and deals with abstract philosophical themes. Borges rejected Leopoldo Lugones's claim that Martin Fierro was the quintessential gaucho epic. The poem is central to Argentine criollismo literature and is associated with other gaucho literature including Ricardo Guiraldes's Don Segundo Sombra and Estanislao del Campo's satirical poem Fausto.
