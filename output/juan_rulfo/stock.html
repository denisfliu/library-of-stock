Mexican author of magical realist fiction, best known for the novel Pedro Paramo and the short story collection The Burning Plain (El Llano en llamas). Pedro Paramo, set in the ghost-inhabited town of Comala, was deeply admired by Gabriel Garcia Marquez. Despite a very small output (one novel, one story collection, one posthumous novella), Rulfo is considered one of the most important Latin American writers.
Pedro Paramo

A novel in which Juan Preciado travels to the town of Comala, at 'the very mouth of hell,' to fulfill his dying mother's wish and find his father Pedro Paramo. All the residents of Comala turn out to be ghosts. Pedro Paramo loves Susana San Juan and lets the town die of hunger out of spite when mourning her death. Characters include hostess Eduviges Dyada, cook Damiana Cisneros, and deaf mule-driver Abundio Martinez — all revealed to be dead. Garcia Marquez said reading it was a life-changing experience and could recite it forwards and backwards. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Juan Rulfo was a Mexican author considered one of the most important Latin American writers despite a remarkably small body of work. He is best known for the novel Pedro Paramo and the short story collection The Burning Plain (El Llano en llamas).

Pedro Paramo follows Juan Preciado, who fulfills a promise to his dying mother by traveling to the town of Comala to find his father, Pedro Paramo. Comala is at 'the very mouth of hell,' and all its residents — including the hostess Eduviges Dyada, the cook Damiana Cisneros, and the deaf mule-driver Abundio Martinez — turn out to be ghosts whose murmurs fill the town. Pedro Paramo loves Susana San Juan and, when mourning her death, lets the town die of hunger out of spite. Gabriel Garcia Marquez described reading Pedro Paramo as a life-changing experience, said he felt blocked as a novelist until reading Rulfo, allegedly read the book twice in one night, and claimed he could recite it forwards and backwards. The novel influenced One Hundred Years of Solitude.

The Burning Plain is a short story collection exploring the bleakness of rural Mexico and its revolution, first translated into English by Georg D. Schade. 'Tell Them Not to Kill Me!' follows a man who, thirty-five years after killing Don Lupe Terreros over a cattle pasturing dispute, faces a firing squad and makes the title plea to his son Justino. 'Talpa' depicts the narrator and his sick brother's wife Natalia arranging the brother's death during a pilgrimage. 'Because We Are So Poor' describes Tacha, who may end up like her disreputable sisters after a cow is washed away by a flood. 'No Oyes Ladrar los Perros' (Don't You Hear the Dogs Barking) is about a father carrying his wounded bandit son Ignacio to a doctor. The title story 'The Burning Plain' features a mock bullfighting execution and begins with the epigraph 'Now they killed [a dog] / But [her offspring] remain.' The collection also includes 'They Gave Us the Land' and 'North Pass.' Rulfo also wrote The Golden Cockerel, a novel published in 1980, twenty years after it was written.
