Irish satirist known for Gulliver's Travels, A Modest Proposal, A Tale of a Tub, The Battle of the Books, and scatological poems like 'The Lady's Dressing Room.' Also associated with the pseudonym Isaac Bickerstaff and the Drapier's Letters against William Wood's coinage.
Gulliver's Travels

Swift's most famous novel, a satirical picaresque following Lemuel Gulliver, 'first a surgeon, then a captain of many ships,' through lands including Lilliput, Blefuscu, Brobdingnag, Laputa, and the land of the Houyhnhnms. In Lilliput, Gulliver destroys the fleet of the Blefuscudians. The two islands war over the correct way to crack open an egg. In Laputa, a floating island, overly intellectual inhabitants are so absorbed in thought that servants must hit them with 'flappers.' On the island of Lagado, the Academy of Projectors contains 'The Engine.' Gulliver encounters the race of talking horses, the Houyhnhnms, whose nobility contrasts with the dirtiness of the Yahoos. In Brobdingnag, gigantic women are described. The novel was shown in early drafts to Pope, Arbuthnot, and Gay as members of the Scriblerus Club. Edmund Curll produced an 'incredibly inaccurate' companion key to the novel. Mark Twain named a wrecked steamboat after Swift in Adventures of Huckleberry Finn.

A Modest Proposal

Swift's most famous satirical essay, in which he proposes that the Irish should sell their children as food to the English to alleviate poverty. He suggests 'lessening the number of papists among us,' making 'admirable gloves for ladies and summer boots for fine gentlemen,' and discouraging people from 'fighting for the pretender in Spain.' The essay cites 'the famous Psalmanazar, a native of the island of Formosa.' It is commonly cited as one of the greatest uses of sustained irony in the English language. Its argument structure ridicules the economist William Petty and followers of Francis Bacon.

A Tale of a Tub

A satire of Christian religion in which three brothers — Peter, Martin, and Jack — inherit flawless coats from their father, representing Catholicism, Anglicanism, and Dissent (Calvinism) respectively. Martin is the only one to retain his original coat. It contains numerous digressions including the famous 'Digression on Madness,' which explains what happens 'when a Man's Fancy gets astride of his Reason.' The work distinguishes between the true critic and ordinary critics. It was published with The Battle of the Books and parodied Robert Boyle's Occasional Reflections Upon Several Subjects in 'Meditations Upon a Broomstick.' The Aeolists believe learning happens through wind, so they love burping. The title refers to a tub thrown overboard to distract an attacking whale.

The Battle of the Books

A satire of the Quarrel of the Ancients and the Moderns, depicting classical and contemporary writers coming to life and fighting in a library (St. James's Library). A central episode involves a bee destroying a spider's web, which Aesop judges by observing that bees provide mankind with 'sweetness and light' (a phrase later borrowed by Matthew Arnold). The goddess Criticism lives atop 'a snowy Mountain in Nova Zembla' and is dressed by her mother Pride in scraps of torn paper. Momus recruits the goddess Criticism after Jupiter consults the book of fate. Wotton tries to kill Sir William Temple. Richard Bentley steals the armor of Phalaris and Aesop. Bentley receives a helmet nine times too big for him in an armor exchange with Virgil. It begins: 'Satire is a sort of glass wherein beholders do generally discover everybody's face but their own.'

The Lady's Dressing Room

A scatological poem in which Strephon sneaks into the title location and discovers, to his horror, that 'Celia, Celia, Celia shits!' He is advised to see women as 'Tulips rais'd from Dung.' The poem's last couplet reads 'Such order from confusion sprung, / such gaudy tulips raised from dung.' It sparked a literary feud with Lady Wortley Montagu, who wrote a savage poem imagining the 'reasons that induced' Swift to write it. The poem is sometimes discussed alongside descriptions of gigantic Brobdingnagian women in Gulliver's Travels as evidence of Swift's misogyny.

Isaac Bickerstaff / Drapier's Letters / Other Works

Swift published works under several pseudonyms. As Isaac Bickerstaff, he predicted the death of the astrologer John Partridge in a hoax pamphlet; Richard Steele later used the Bickerstaff pseudonym for the fictional editor of The Tatler. The Drapier's Letters attacked William Wood's copper coinage scheme in Ireland, opposing debased currency. Swift's poem 'Verses on the Death of Dr. Swift' discusses Rochefoucauld's maxim about finding 'something that doth not displease us' in friends' adversity, and describes his pride in opposing Wood's coinage. The poem 'Cadenus and Vanessa' is named for an anagram of his profession (Dean = Cadenus) and his lover Esther Vanhomrigh (from whose name he coined 'Vanessa'). His correspondence with Esther Johnson was collected as Journal to Stella. 'A Description of a City Shower' describes 'Triumphant Tories, and despondent Whigs' joining 'to save their wigs' and features 'Drown'd puppies...dead cats, and Turnip-Tops.' 'An Argument Against Abolishing Christianity' warns the titular action would cause a stock market crash. 'Thoughts on Various Subjects, Moral and Diverting' contains the epigram 'just enough religion to make us hate, but not enough to make us love one another,' and provided the title for John Kennedy Toole's A Confederacy of Dunces. His Latin epitaph states his heart can no longer be torn by 'saeva Indignatio' (savage indignation), translated by W.B. Yeats. He was a member of the Scriblerus Club with Pope, Arbuthnot, and Gay. 'Directions to Servants' directly addresses the Chambermaid, Footman, and Laundress. He lampooned Bentley for altering Paradise Lost. He was an employee of William Temple.

Summary of Facts

Jonathan Swift was an Anglo-Irish satirist and member of the Scriblerus Club alongside Alexander Pope, John Arbuthnot, and John Gay. He is primarily known in quizbowl for a handful of major works that appear with great regularity.

His most famous work is Gulliver's Travels, a satirical novel following Captain Lemuel Gulliver through fantastical lands. The most commonly clued episodes include Lilliput and Blefuscu (tiny people warring over how to crack eggs), Brobdingnag (giants), Laputa (a floating island of absent-minded intellectuals who need 'flappers' to stay attentive, with the Academy of Projectors in Lagado featuring a computer-like device called 'The Engine'), and the land of the Houyhnhnms (rational talking horses contrasted with the bestial Yahoos). The novel was shared in draft with fellow Scriblerus Club members.

A Modest Proposal is Swift's iconic satirical essay arguing that the Irish should sell their children as food to the English, proposing benefits like 'lessening the number of papists' and making 'admirable gloves for ladies.' It cites 'the famous Psalmanazar, a native of Formosa' and is considered one of the greatest examples of sustained irony in English.

A Tale of a Tub is a religious satire in which three brothers — Peter (Catholicism), Martin (Anglicanism), and Jack (Calvinism) — inherit coats from their father representing their faiths. It contains the famous 'Digression on Madness' and was published alongside The Battle of the Books. That companion piece depicts the Quarrel of the Ancients and the Moderns as a literal fight in St. James's Library, featuring the fable of a bee destroying a spider's web, from which Aesop derives the phrase 'sweetness and light' (later borrowed by Matthew Arnold).

Swift's scatological poetry, especially 'The Lady's Dressing Room,' is frequently clued: Strephon sneaks into the title room and discovers 'Oh! Celia, Celia, Celia shits!' He is advised to regard women as 'tulips raised from dung.' This poem sparked a feud with Lady Wortley Montagu.

Under the pseudonym Isaac Bickerstaff, Swift predicted the death of the astrologer John Partridge in a famous hoax; Richard Steele later adopted this pseudonym for the fictional editor of The Tatler. The Drapier's Letters opposed William Wood's copper coinage scheme in Ireland. Swift coined the name 'Vanessa' from his lover Esther Vanhomrigh's name for the poem 'Cadenus and Vanessa.' His correspondence with Esther Johnson was published posthumously as Journal to Stella. His Latin epitaph claims his heart can no longer be torn by 'saeva Indignatio' (savage indignation), famously translated by W.B. Yeats. 'Thoughts on Various Subjects, Moral and Diverting' provided the title for John Kennedy Toole's A Confederacy of Dunces.
