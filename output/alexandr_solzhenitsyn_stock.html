Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Soviet/Russian author and dissident. Best known for One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich (novella about life in a Siberian labor camp) and The Gulag Archipelago (nonfiction based on testimonies of 227 survivors of the Soviet prison system). Also wrote Cancer Ward, In the First Circle, the historical cycle The Red Wheel (beginning with August 1914), the poem 'Prussian Nights,' and the story 'Matryona's Place.' Nobel Prize in Literature winner whose works were often distributed through samizdat. Exiled from the Soviet Union in 1974. Gave the 1978 Harvard commencement address on 'A World Split Apart.'
One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich

Solzhenitsyn's most frequently clued work, a novella set in a Siberian labor camp. The protagonist Ivan Denisovich Shukhov is a member of Gang/Squad 104 (the 104th squad of zeks). He helps Pavlo acquire extra gruel, smuggles a hacksaw blade into camp to mend shoes, sews bread into his mattress, registers only a temperature of 99 degrees after visiting nurse Vdovushkin, and works building a wall for the Power Station. He is roommates with Alyosha the Baptist, who maintains his faith despite harsh conditions. The deaf Senka is revealed to have survived Buchenwald. Other members include Tyurin (the squad leader), Fetyukov, and Caesar (whose food packages the protagonist shares in). The protagonist ends the day feeling 'almost happy' after obtaining tobacco and extra food. Published in the literary journal Novy Mir in 1962, with editor Alexander Tvardovsky writing a special preface. Vladimir Sorokin's Day of the Oprichnik was inspired by this work's twenty-four-hour structure. Wikipedia

The Gulag Archipelago

Solzhenitsyn's landmark nonfiction work about the Soviet forced labor camp system, based on the testimony of 227 survivors. Dedicated 'to all those who did not live to tell it.' Published in France after arriving there through samizdat; Novy Mir finally published it in three parts in 1989. Contains an epigraph by the poet Wilhelm Muller noting 'How heavy are the stones themselves.' A chapter called 'The White Kitten' tells the story of Georgi Tenno, who escapes but is recaptured after refusing to kill innocent villagers. The work also discusses show trials, Boris Savinkov's supposed suicide, and the real-life figure Otto Irbe. Solzhenitsyn compared Shakespeare's worst villains (Macbeth, Iago) to 'little lambs' whose imagination 'stopped short at a dozen corpses. Because they had no ideology.' He was a leading voice in accusing Mikhail Sholokhov of plagiarizing And Quiet Flows the Don. Wikipedia

Cancer Ward

A novel set in a hospital in Kazakhstan (Uzbekistan in some clues). The protagonist Oleg Kostoglotov is exiled to the cancer ward and has romances with the nurse Zoya and the doctor/radiotherapist Vera Gangart. Aleksei Shulubin is one of the patients. The teenager Dyomka eventually has his leg amputated. The novel ends with Kostoglotov writing letters to Dyomka, Zoya, and Vera Gangart. On the day he leaves the hospital, Kostoglotov visits a zoo and reads a sign about the 'senseless cruelty' of an 'evil man' and also reads about a monkey being blinded. Inspired by Solzhenitsyn's own experience with cancer. Irving Howe looked favorably upon it while deriding August 1914. Wikipedia

In the First Circle

A novel set during December 24-28, 1949, in a sharashka (research and development bureau / special prison for intellectuals). Practitioners of 'phonoscopy' compare spectrographs of a recorded phone call, leading to the arrest of the diplomat Innokenty Volodin, who had called the American embassy under an NKVD wiretap. Main characters include Gleb Nerzhin, who befriends the staunch Communist Lev Rubin, and the brilliant mathematician Sologdin, who betrays his integrity for freedom. The title references the first circle of Dante's Inferno (Limbo). Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Aleksandr Isayevich Solzhenitsyn (1918-2008), Soviet/Russian author and dissident. Won the Nobel Prize in Literature but waited four years to receive it; the Swedish government refused to let him lecture at its Moscow embassy. Exiled from the Soviet Union in 1974 due to his denunciations of Stalinism. Lived in exile in the United States until returning to Russia after the fall of the USSR. Gave the 1978 Harvard commencement address ('A World Split Apart'), discussing the West's decline and loss of civil courage. His works were often circulated through samizdat. Wrote the essay Two Hundred Years Together and the novel August 1914 (part of The Red Wheel cycle), both of which drew accusations of anti-Semitism (Elie Wiesel called him 'too intelligent' to be an anti-Semite). Wrote Rebuilding Russia advocating a unified Rus' state. Also wrote the poem 'Prussian Nights' (based on marching with the Red Army), the long poem Dorozhenka (composed entirely before writing any down), the play The Love-Girl and the Innocent, and the story 'Matryona's Place.' His story collection Apricot Jam includes 'binaries' about contrasting characters.

Summary of Facts

Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn (1918-2008) is a Soviet/Russian author and dissident, asked about primarily through his novella One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich, his nonfiction work The Gulag Archipelago, and the novel Cancer Ward.

One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich follows the zeks of Gang/Squad 104 in a Siberian labor camp. The protagonist Shukhov smuggles a hacksaw blade into camp to mend shoes, sews bread into his mattress, visits nurse Vdovushkin but is sent to work with only a temperature of 99 degrees, helps Pavlo acquire extra gruel, and shares in Caesar's food packages. His roommate Alyosha the Baptist maintains his faith despite harsh conditions. The deaf Senka is revealed to have survived Buchenwald. Other squad members include the leader Tyurin and Fetyukov. The protagonist ends the day 'almost happy' after obtaining tobacco and extra food. The novella was published in Novy Mir in 1962, with editor Alexander Tvardovsky writing a special preface. Vladimir Sorokin's Day of the Oprichnik was inspired by its twenty-four-hour structure.

The Gulag Archipelago is based on the testimony of 227 survivors and is dedicated 'to all those who did not live to tell it.' It was published in France through samizdat, and Novy Mir finally published it in three parts in 1989. It contains an epigraph by the poet Wilhelm Muller ('How heavy are the stones themselves'). A chapter called 'The White Kitten' relates the story of Georgi Tenno, who escapes but is recaptured after refusing to kill villagers. The work discusses Boris Savinkov's supposed suicide and the real-life Otto Irbe. Solzhenitsyn compared Shakespeare's villains Macbeth and Iago to 'little lambs' because 'they had no ideology.'

Cancer Ward follows Oleg Kostoglotov, exiled to a hospital in Kazakhstan, where he has romances with the nurse Zoya and the doctor Vera Gangart. The teenager Dyomka has his leg amputated. On the day he leaves, Kostoglotov visits a zoo and reads about the 'senseless cruelty' of an evil man who blinded a monkey. The novel ends with Kostoglotov writing letters to Dyomka, Zoya, and Vera. Irving Howe looked favorably upon it while deriding August 1914.

In the First Circle is set in a sharashka during December 24-28, 1949. Practitioners of 'phonoscopy' compare spectrographs of a phone call, leading to the arrest of diplomat Innokenty Volodin. Main characters include Gleb Nerzhin, the Communist Lev Rubin, and the mathematician Sologdin. The title references the first circle of Dante's Inferno.

Biographically, Solzhenitsyn won the Nobel Prize in Literature but waited four years to receive it, as Sweden refused to let him lecture at its Moscow embassy. His Nobel lecture quotes the proverb 'ONE WORD OF TRUTH SHALL OUTWEIGH THE WHOLE WORLD.' He was exiled from the USSR in 1974 and gave the 1978 Harvard commencement address on 'A World Split Apart.' His works were circulated through samizdat. August 1914, the first 'knot' of The Red Wheel cycle (which includes November 1916), and the essay Two Hundred Years Together drew accusations of anti-Semitism, though Elie Wiesel called him 'too intelligent' to be an anti-Semite. He also wrote the poem 'Prussian Nights' (containing 'A girl's been turned into a woman / A woman turned into a corpse'), the long poem Dorozhenka (composed entirely from memory before writing it down), the play The Love-Girl and the Innocent, the story 'Matryona's Place,' and the story collection Apricot Jam (featuring 'binaries' about contrasting characters). He advocated for a unified Rus' state in Rebuilding Russia and was a leading voice accusing Sholokhov of plagiarizing And Quiet Flows the Don.
