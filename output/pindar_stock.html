Theban lyric poet, one of the Nine Lyric Poets and the best preserved. Known for his epinikia (victory odes) celebrating athletic victors at Panhellenic festivals. His four books of odes — Olympian, Pythian, Nemean, Isthmian — honor athletes like Hieron of Syracuse. Rival of Bacchylides.

Summary of Facts

Pindar was a Theban lyric poet (c. 518-438 BCE) and the best preserved of the Nine Lyric Poets of ancient Greece. He is best known for his epinikia (victory odes), which celebrated athletic victors at the Panhellenic festivals. His surviving works are organized into four books: Olympian, Pythian, Nemean, and Isthmian odes.

His most famous poem, the first Olympian Ode, opens with the enigmatic assertion 'Water is best' (ariston men hudor), followed by praise of gold that 'stands out supreme of all lordly wealth.' This ode is dedicated to Hieron of Syracuse and his horse racing victory. It notably revises the myth of Pelops by denying that Tantalus fed his son to the gods, instead claiming Pelops was taken by Poseidon. The eighth Olympian Ode contains a digression about the construction of Troy's walls.

Pindar's Pythian Odes include one that tells the story of Typhon imprisoned under Mount Etna and another giving advice like 'Steer your men with the rudder of justice.' His poetry often meditates on mortality, as in the famous lines 'Creatures of a day! What is anyone? What is no one? Man is the dream of a shadow.'

The Pindaric ode form consists of three parts: strophe, antistrophe, and epode, in which the chorus moves left, right, and to the center of the stage. This form was later imitated by Horace and influenced English poets like Abraham Cowley. His rival Bacchylides also wrote victory odes for Hieron of Syracuse. Scholars debate whether his works were performed by a chorus or an individual singer.

Pindar was quoted by Nietzsche ('become such as you are, having learned what that is') and in the preface to Camus's The Myth of Sisyphus ('do not aspire to immortal life, but exhaust the limits of the possible'). Alexander the Great reportedly ordered his house spared during the razing of Thebes.
