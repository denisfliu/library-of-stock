Summary of Facts

Umberto Eco was an Italian semiotician, novelist, and essayist. His debut novel The Name of the Rose is set in a Benedictine abbey in 1327, where the Franciscan monk William of Baskerville (inspired by Sherlock Holmes, Thomas Aquinas, and Roger Bacon) and his novice Adso of Melk investigate a series of murders. William deduces that a missing horse is called Brunellus. The blind monk Jorge of Burgos has poisoned the pages of a lost second book of Aristotle's Poetics (on comedy), which he condemned because of its association with "infidel Moors" and because he believed comedy destroyed faith. The murders include Adelmo, Venantius (whose corpse is found in a vat of pig's blood), and Berengar. The secret room in the library is called the finis Africae. The inquisitor Bernard Gui arrests a peasant girl whom Adso had sex with (his first sexual Experience) for witchcraft. Jorge kills himself by eating the poisoned pages and burning down the labyrinthine library. William tells Adso that the "epoch of penitence is over." The cellarer Salvatore frequently repeats "penitenziagite." The 1986 film adaptation stars Sean Connery and Christian Slater; Eco compared the novel to a "club sandwich." William Weaver translated the novel.

Foucault's Pendulum follows three editors -- Belbo, Diotallevi, and Casaubon -- who use a computer named Abulafia (after a Kabbalah philosopher) to construct connections between random words and build "The Plan," a fictional conspiracy theory about the Knights Templar. They use Colonel Ardenti's manuscript about the Knights Templar as the basis. The Plan involves telluric currents and the allegedly immortal Count Aglie (Saint-Germain). Other characters examine telluric currents. Eventually the creators come to believe The Plan is true, and Belbo is hanged from the titular scientific instrument in Paris. The Tres Society kills Belbo. The novel is divided into ten sections representing the ten Sefirot of the Kabbalah. Although named after Michel Foucault (the philosopher), the novel deliberately eschews any reference to him -- it refers to Leon Foucault's pendulum. A character divides the world into "cretins, fools, morons, and lunatics." Eco's depiction of Sergei Nilus adapts Danilo Kis's story "The Book of Kings and Fools."

Travels in Hyperreality is an essay collection. The title essay (originally called "Faith in Fakes") compares the LBJ Presidential Library, waxworks museums on Fisherman's Wharf, Superman's Fortress of Solitude, and Disneyland as examples of how "the American imagination demands the real thing and, to attain it, must fabricate the absolute fake." The opening section on "Fortresses of Solitude" discusses Wunderkammern (museums of memories). Eco discusses how holography "could only prosper in America" and analyzes Hearst Castle. He coined the term "fac-different" with an analogy to Asimov's laws of robotics. In "Travels in Hyperreality," he expanded Baudrillard's analysis of Disneyland.

How to Travel with a Salmon is another essay collection. The title essay describes how a hotel computer glitch causes him to be charged for minibar alcohol, which he had actually moved to store a smoked fish he bought in Stockholm. The collection also includes an essay about L'Avventura, one describing difficulties with a hotel clerk who spoke a dialect from "Kefiristan," and "How to Use the Coffeepot from Hell." Other essay collections include Misreadings (parodies including Columbus's landing on TV and a parody of Lolita about an old woman named Granita).

"Ur-Fascism" (or "Eternal Fascism") lists fourteen features of fascism, drawing on Eco's Experience growing up in Mussolini's Italy. He uses Wittgenstein's family resemblance to describe the fourteen ways. The final principle claims "Ur-Fascism speaks Newspeak." He called for "communications guerilla warfare" in a separate essay.

The Prague Cemetery is a picaresque novel about "the most hateful man in the world," the antisemitic forger Simone Simonini (Captain Simonelli). Abbe Dalla Piccola alters Simonini's diary while he sleeps. The novel weaves together the Templars, the Illuminati, the Dreyfus Affair, and the Protocols of the Elders of Zion. Simonini is disguised as Abbe Dalla Piccola when he strangles Diana Vaughan at a Satanist orgy.

The Island of the Day Before follows the 17th-century explorer Roberto della Griva, who is marooned on a ship and believes he is on the International Date Line. He writes love letters to Lady Lilia and muses on his imaginary evil twin. A German Jesuit named Gaspar fails to teach him to swim after the shipwreck of the Daphne. He is tasked with discovering the secret of longitude.

Baudolino follows an Italian peasant who becomes the adopted son of Frederick Barbarossa. He recounts a fifteen-year search for Prester John to Niketas Choniates after saving him during the sack of Constantinople. A historical figure's corpse is thrown into a river after dying at an Armenian noble's estate.

The Mysterious Flame of Queen Loana follows Yambo, an amnesiac who loses his memories after a stroke. He discovers a Shakespeare First Folio among his grandfather's books, which causes him to relive lost memories.

Numero Zero is Eco's final novel, in which the paranoiac Braggadocio becomes convinced Mussolini survived WWII while hack journalists Colonna and another have an affair.

As an academic, Eco was a pioneer of semiotics. He proposed categories of recognition, ostension, replica, and invention in A Theory of Semiotics. He co-founded the journal Versus. The Open Work contrasted classical art with the aleatoric music of Stockhausen and Boulez. He argued that texts produce "model readers" in The Role of the Reader. He outlined nine possible plot moves in James Bond novels in "Narrative Structures in Fleming." He analyzed metaphor in Finnegans Wake. He conducted debates on "iconism" with Christian Metz, Roland Barthes, and Pier Paolo Pasolini. He wrote on Superman's myth. He analyzed Casablanca as using "intertextual archetypes." He delivered the Charles Eliot Norton lectures at Harvard. He called Ulysses a "summa turned upside down" in The Aesthetics of Chaosmos. He sometimes used the pseudonym Dedalus. He cameoed in the film La Notte. He proclaimed "the list is the origin of culture" in The Infinity of Lists.
