French Symbolist artist, known for colorful erotic paintings of Biblical and mythological scenes. Most commonly identified by Oedipus and the Sphinx, The Apparition / Salome paintings, and Jupiter and Semele. House-museum in Paris. Friend of Theodore Chasseriau. Associated with Pierre Puvis de Chavannes and Odilon Redon in the Symbolist movement. Protagonist of Huysmans's A rebours collects his paintings.
Oedipus and the Sphinx (1864)

Moreau's breakthrough painting, an immediate success at the 1864 Salon. Depicts the Sphinx clinging to Oedipus's chest in a chasm. Influenced by Ingres's version of the same subject. Moreau obsessively copied Carpaccio's painting and used St. George as the facial model for Oedipus. Wikipedia

The Apparition / Salome Paintings

Series of paintings depicting the Biblical Salome, daughter of Herod. In The Apparition, Salome points at the floating, haloed, decapitated head of John the Baptist, with blood pouring from the neck. Multiple versions with variations including a tattooed Salome, a black panther, and a Diana of Ephesus statue. Floating heads inspired by ukiyo-e prints. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Gustave Moreau was a French Symbolist artist born in 1826, renowned for his colorful, erotic paintings of Biblical and mythological scenes. He belonged to the Symbolist movement alongside Pierre Puvis de Chavannes and Odilon Redon, and was a friend and student of Theodore Chasseriau. His house-museum in Paris contains most of his best-known works, and the protagonist of Joris-Karl Huysmans's novel A rebours is described as collecting his paintings.

Moreau's most frequently clued work is Oedipus and the Sphinx (1864), which depicts a winged Sphinx clinging to Oedipus's chest in a chasm, with a foot and hand jutting from the rocks at the bottom alongside a crown and bones. Oedipus holds a red spear point-down. The painting was influenced by Ingres's version of the same subject. Moreau obsessively copied Vittore Carpaccio's painting (variously identified as a St. George scene or Ten Thousand Martyrs of Ararat) and used St. George as the facial model for Oedipus.

Nearly as frequently clued are his Salome paintings, centered around The Apparition, in which Salome points at the floating, haloed, decapitated head of John the Baptist with blood pouring from the neck. In all versions, the central figure stretches her left arm pointing to the right. Different versions include distinguishing features: one prominently features a statue of Diana of Ephesus towering in the background; a black panther appears in the foreground of one and at the central figure's feet in another; one version depicts a tattooed Salome with outlines in white and black. The floating heads were supposedly inspired by ukiyo-e prints that Moreau copied, and Salome wears a revealing dress made almost entirely of jewels.

Jupiter and Semele (1895) is another major work, depicting Jupiter seated on a massive gold throne with a red halo, garlanded with flowers. Semele, struck dead, is draped over his arm/thigh, and a woman with an open wound in her thigh recoils at the glory of the divine lover.

Other notable works include The Life of Humanity, a polyptych with a 3x3 grid depicting the Golden, Silver, and Iron Ages via Adam, Hesiod and Orpheus, and Cain; Les Licornes (The Unicorns), depicting three unicorns surrounded by princesses, inspired by the rediscovery of The Lady and the Unicorn tapestries; Orpheus, showing a woman holding the head and lyre of the title bard; The Chimeras; and Young Man and Death, dedicated to mentor Chasseriau, depicting death as a ghostly woman floating along a diagonal axis. One other painting features a heavily-garlanded Pan sprawling in front of an eagle, flanked by personifications of Death (holding a bloody sword) and Pain (wearing a crown of thorns).
