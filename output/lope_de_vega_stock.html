Extremely prolific Spanish Golden Age playwright (wrote some 1,500 plays). Best known for Fuenteovejuna (The Sheep Well) and The Dog in the Manger. Nicknamed 'The Phoenix of Wits' by Cervantes, who also called him a 'Monster of Nature.' Contemporary of Cervantes and friend of Quevedo. Believed all tragedies should be three acts long. Also wrote poetry, including 'Sonnet Impromptu' and religious sonnets. Called his lover Elena Osorio 'Filis' in his poems. His plays belong to the comedia de capa y espada (cloak and sword) genre.
Fuenteovejuna (The Sheep Well)

Lope de Vega's most famous play, set during the reign of Ferdinand and Isabella. The tyrannical Commander Gomez de Guzman of the Order of Calatrava terrorizes the village of Fuenteovejuna. He attempts to rape Laurencia and orders the arrest of Esteban and hanging of Frondoso. A disheveled Laurencia delivers a rousing monologue calling men 'spinning women' and telling them to wear 'headdresses and petticoats,' urging them to revolt. The peasants storm the citadel and kill the Commander. When a judge arrives and tortures 300 villagers, every single one insists that 'Fuenteovejuna did it.' The play ends with King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella pardoning the entire village. Mengo explains that poetry is like a baker making doughnuts. A peasant steals the Commander's crossbow to prevent him from raping Laurencia. Laurencia says her ideal life would be eating food instead of being annoyed by men. Wikipedia

The Dog in the Manger

Also known as El Perro del Hortelano (The Gardener's Dog). Based on an Aesop fable about a dog that prevents others from using something it cannot use itself. The Countess Diana falls in love with her socially inferior secretary Teodoro but refuses to let him marry her lady-in-waiting Marcela, while also refusing to marry him herself. The gracioso (comic servant) Tristan resolves the point of honor by fabricating a noble Byzantine lineage for Teodoro. Diana asks an aggressive suitor whether he isn't satisfied with the flesh he already has (referring to gifts of meat). This is a comedia de capa y espada (cloak and sword play). Wikipedia

Punishment Without Revenge (Justice Without Revenge)

A villainous duke discovers his bastard son's tryst with Casandra (his new wife). He ties Casandra under a white veil and tricks his son into stabbing her, after the affair began when the son rescued her from an overturned coach. In one scene, a servant snuffs out a candle with the toss of a hat to cover up his master's affair. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Lope de Vega (full name Felix Lope de Vega y Carpio, 1562-1635) was an extraordinarily prolific Spanish Golden Age playwright who wrote over 1,500 plays. Cervantes nicknamed him 'The Phoenix of Wits' and called him a 'Monster of Nature.' He believed all tragedies should only be three acts long, which was controversial. His comedias rejected Aristotelian unities. Friend of Quevedo and contemporary of Cervantes. Also a poet whose 'Sonnet Impromptu' is anthologized frequently and whose religious sonnets were referenced by Lorca for their 'duende.' Called his lover Elena Osorio 'Filis' in his poems. A poem by him inspired the response 'Letter to Belardo' by an anonymous female poet under the alias Amarilis. He first created the character of Pedro Crespo (later used by Calderon in The Mayor of Zalamea). Also wrote the novel The Pilgrim in his Own Country. Goethe wished he had produced as 'vast and colourful' an oeuvre. Some scholars theorize he may have written the fake Don Quixote sequel. His plays set in Valencia include The Widow from Valencia and a play where Floriano and Erifila fall in love in Valencia's asylum.

Summary of Facts

Lope de Vega (Felix Lope de Vega y Carpio, 1562-1635) is the most prominent Spanish Golden Age playwright in quizbowl, known above all for his extraordinary prolificacy — he wrote over 1,500 plays. The clue that appears in virtually every question about him is from Fuenteovejuna (The Sheep Well): when tortured about who killed the tyrannical Commander Gomez de Guzman of the Order of Calatrava, every villager insists that 'Fuenteovejuna did it.' The play is set during the reign of Ferdinand and Isabella, who pardon the village at the end. Other important Fuenteovejuna clues include a peasant stealing the Commander's crossbow to prevent the rape of Laurencia, Laurencia's rousing speech calling men 'spinning women,' and Mengo's analogy comparing poetry to a baker making doughnuts. The Dog in the Manger (El Perro del Hortelano) is his second most asked-about work. Titled for an Aesop fable about a dog that prevents others from using what it cannot use, it centers on the Countess Diana who falls in love with her secretary Teodoro but refuses to let him marry her maid Marcela. The gracioso Tristan resolves the plot by fabricating a Byzantine noble lineage for Teodoro. Punishment Without Revenge (also called Justice Without Revenge) features a duke who discovers his bastard son's affair with his wife Casandra and tricks the son into stabbing her while she is tied under a white veil. Biographically, Cervantes nicknamed him 'The Phoenix of Wits' and called him a 'Monster of Nature.' He controversially believed all tragedies should be three acts long, and his comedias rejected Aristotelian unities. He is also known as a poet; his 'Sonnet Impromptu' appears in most Spanish Golden Age anthologies, and Lorca referenced the 'duende' in his religious sonnets. He called his lover Elena Osorio 'Filis' in his poems, and a poem of his inspired the 'Letter to Belardo' by an anonymous female poet using the alias Amarilis. He first created the character Pedro Crespo, later used by Calderon in The Mayor of Zalamea. Goethe admired the vastness of his oeuvre. Some scholars theorize he wrote the fake Don Quixote sequel.
