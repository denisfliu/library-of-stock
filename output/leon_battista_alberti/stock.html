Summary of Facts

Leon Battista Alberti was a Florentine Renaissance polymath — architect, painter, and theorist — who articulated the ideal of the Renaissance man with the phrase 'a man can do all things if he will.' His most important written work is Della Pittura / De Pictura (1435), the first post-classical treatise on painting, which introduced the concept of using a vanishing point for illusionistic perspective (he developed a method dubbed the 'legitimate construction' by later scholars) and discussed istoria/historia (narrative composition) as painting's highest achievement. The second book opens with the story of Narcissus, comparing painting to 'embracing the surface of the pool.' The treatise praises 'bodies in many dissimilar poses' as 'especially pleasing,' cites the story of a cobbler antagonizing Apelles (Alexander the Great's portraitist), and praises Botticelli's Adoration of the Magi for including a self-portrait that exemplifies historia. A challenge he posed led Renaissance artists to create versions of The Calumny of Apelles. His treatment of perspective was later iterated upon by Piero della Francesca. The Hunt in the Forest by Paolo Uccello appears on the cover of the Penguin Classics edition of Alberti's On Painting. Svetlana Alpers's The Art of Describing critiques writings that rely on an 'Albertian mode' when analyzing Dutch Golden Age art. Anthony Grafton wrote a book on Alberti's tendency to 'personalize' his 'ancient sources.'

Alberti also wrote De Re Aedificatoria, a ten-book treatise on architecture updating Vitruvius's De Architectura, in which he proclaimed columns to be the most noble building element. His major architectural works include: the polychrome (white marble) facade of Santa Maria Novella in Florence, commissioned by the wool merchant Giovanni Rucellai (featuring scrolls linking upper and lower registers, flat pilasters, and an oriel window; scroll buttresses later copied by Giacomo della Porta for Il Gesù); the Tempio Malatestiano in Rimini (remaking a Franciscan church that may have been the first pagan temple in Europe since the Classical era, with three equal arches, a chapel of the planets, and sarcophagi for patron Sigismondo Malatesta — target of a 1461 crusade by Pope Pius II — and his wife Ixotta, proclaimed 'full of pagan gods and profane things'); the Basilica of Sant'Andrea in Mantua (deep-arched entrance within a temple front, colossal and Corinthian columns, original design lacking transept and dome — the lack of facade columns was described as a turning point in his career); the Palazzo Rucellai and funerary chapel in San Pancrazio in Florence (exterior and loggia decorated with patron Rucellai's sail-blowing-in-wind symbol; 30 marble squares in the chapel); and an unfinished bell tower in Ferrara of alternating pink and white marble bands (part of the Este school led by Andrea Mantegna). Ironically, while he called columns the noblest element, they are absent from his facade of San Sebastiano in Mantua.

Beyond architecture and painting theory, Alberti wrote Philodoxius, a satirical Latin comedy still thought to be ancient a century after his death, and invented the first polyalphabetic cipher (De Cifris). He was also a gifted athlete. According to Vasari, Luca Pacioli plagiarized Alberti's ideas on geometric proportions. Alberti is sometimes credited with writing the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili or making its woodcut illustrations.
