General / Biographical

Kafka is identified as a Czech/German-language author. Max Brod preserved and published his work posthumously. Kafka wrote a vindictive 100-page Letter to His Father about breaking off his engagement to Felice Bauer. His Zurau Aphorisms include 'A cage went in search of a bird.' Borges' essay 'Kafka and his Precursors' argues writers create their own precursors. W.G. Sebald calls him 'Dr. K' in Vertigo. Georges Bataille discussed his 'perfect puerility.' Summary of Facts

Franz Kafka is a German-speaking Czech author known for his novels The Trial, The Castle, and Amerika, and his short fiction including The Metamorphosis, 'In the Penal Colony,' 'A Hunger Artist,' 'A Report to an Academy,' 'Josephine the Singer,' 'The Judgment,' 'Investigations of a Dog,' 'The Burrow,' and many others. His friend Max Brod preserved and posthumously published most of his work, against Kafka's explicit wishes.

The Metamorphosis is his most famous work and the most common giveaway. In it, Gregor Samsa wakes up transformed into a monstrous vermin (or insect). His sister Grete initially feeds him milk (which he discovers he no longer likes) and moves his furniture so he can climb walls. His father throws an apple that becomes lodged in his back, eventually contributing to his death. Three bearded lodgers leave the house after encountering Gregor. Grete eventually suggests getting rid of him, after which Gregor plans his death. The final sentence contains a word meaning either 'traffic' or 'sexual intercourse' ('an unending stream of traffic was just going over the bridge'). Gabriel Garcia Marquez said Kafka's opening line showed him that people were 'allowed to write things like that.'

The Trial follows the bank worker Josef K., who is arrested by two unnamed government agents for an unknown crime. He receives advice from the sickly lawyer Herr Huld and the cynical painter Titorelli. A priest tells him the parable 'Before the Law,' in which a man with a 'long, thin, Tatar beard' lets himself take bribes from a man he eventually tells 'No one but you could gain admittance.' Josef K. discovers two policemen being flogged in the junkroom of a bank. At the end, he is taken to a quarry and killed, exclaiming 'Like a dog!' The story 'A Dream' features Josef K. seeing a gravedigger etch his name on a tombstone. He has an affair with Miss Burstner and is killed just before his 31st birthday.

The Castle follows the land surveyor K., who fails to gain access to the mysterious title structure. The elusive bureaucrat Klamm works there, and a messenger named Barnabas relays letters from him. K. meets two nearly identical servants, both called 'Arthur.' A barmaid breaks off her engagement to K. A village fire brigade puts on a celebration.

Amerika (also called The Man Who Disappeared) follows Karl Rossmann's arrival in the title country. Its first chapter was published separately as 'The Stoker,' in which Karl develops a connection with the title worker aboard a steamship. Karl sees the Statue of Liberty wielding a sword. The last chapter describes Karl traveling to the Nature-Theater of Oklahoma, which promises a job to anyone who applies. Gyorgy Lukacs contrasted Kafka with Thomas Mann, and critics noted Kafka's debt to Dickens in this novel.

'In the Penal Colony' is one of Kafka's most frequently clued short stories. An officer shows the Traveler a torture device consisting of a Bed, Harrow, and Inscriber that carves words into prisoners' bodies. The device inscribes 'Be Just' on the Officer's back before malfunctioning and killing him. A servant falls asleep on duty and is whipped by his captain. The old Commandant's grave is hidden under a table in a teahouse, with an inscription prophesying he will rise again. The new Commandant withholds funding for the machine. At the end, a man threatens characters with a heavy rope to keep them from jumping into his boat. Deleuze and Guattari viewed the torture device as a prototypical 'desiring machine.' Walter Benjamin discusses this story in Illuminations.

'A Hunger Artist' depicts a performer who starves himself professionally. A man distributes photographs showing the hunger artist at the fortieth day of fasting. He admits on his deathbed he never found food he liked eating. At the end, a panther replaces him in his cage. David Foster Wallace's essay 'Some Remarks on Kafka's Funniness' describes this as a 'radical literalization' of the trope 'starved for attention.'

'A Report to an Academy' is narrated by the ape Red Peter, who describes being captured by the firm of Hagenbeck on the Gold Coast, learning to drink schnapps (burned by a sailor with a pipe when unsuccessful), and choosing the Music Hall over freedom. He recalls living in a three-sided cage on a steamer.

'Josephine the Singer, or the Mouse Folk' features a mouse who shortens her whistling-like performances and whose 'piping' is questioned by the narrator. She gets out of work by claiming artistic inclinations require rest. During her performances, Mouse Folk are able to dream. She eventually disappears.

'Investigations of a Dog' features an unnamed canine attempting to understand where food comes from. The narrator meets seven musical dogs walking on their hind legs and asks 'Whence does the earth procure this food?'

'The Judgment' features Georg Bendemann, whose father lambasts his upcoming marriage to Frieda Brandenfeld and accuses him of wanting him dead, compelling Georg to jump off a bridge and drown.

'The Great Wall of China' explains the piecemeal construction of the wall, built to protect from northern nomads. The embedded parable 'An Imperial Message' concerns a herald who cannot leave an enormous palace after being entrusted with an emperor's dying message.

'The Burrow' features a creature obsessed with security and silence, describing the 'moss covering' separating it from the outside world. Kafka also wrote 'The Vulture' (a man being eaten by a bird), 'The Cares of a Family Man' (about the mysterious Odradek, a 'flat star-shaped spool for thread'), 'The Hunter Gracchus' (a dead hunter wandering on his boat, telling a burgomaster), 'A Country Doctor' (receiving mysterious horses from a man, imagining the rape of his maid Rosa), 'Blumfeld, an Elderly Bachelor' (menacing bouncing balls), and 'The New Advocate' (a horse becomes a lawyer).

Biographically, Kafka wrote a vindictive 100-page letter to his father (Letter to His Father) explaining why he broke off his engagement to Felice Bauer. He recounted being locked out on a balcony as punishment for crying for water. His diaries were begun at Max Brod's suggestion and contain drafts of his first collection, Contemplation. The Zurau Aphorisms include 'A cage went in search of a bird.' His own drawings were compiled in a 2022 book by Andreas Kilcher. Borges' essay 'Kafka and his Precursors' argues that writers create their own precursors. W.G. Sebald calls him 'Dr. K' in Vertigo. Herta Muller was compared to Kafka for similar use of German as a minority language.
