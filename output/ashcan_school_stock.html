Summary of Facts

The Ashcan School was an American realist art movement focused on depicting gritty scenes of poor urban life in New York City. It was closely associated with the group known as 'The Eight,' which was organized by Robert Henri after George Luks's rejection from a gallery exhibition prompted Henri to storm out and form a new group. The name 'Ashcan School' originated from the caption of a drawing in the Philadelphia Record, disparaged by cartoonist Art Young. The Macbeth Galleries housed the biggest exhibition of the group's artists, many of whom had studied under Thomas Anshutz (who in turn studied under Thomas Eakins in Philadelphia).

Robert Henri was the leader of the movement and author of The Art Spirit, a collection of his teachings and lectures that encouraged interest in color and light as 'means of expression.' He painted Snow in New York and The Masquerade Dress (a portrait of his second wife Marjorie Organ). George Bellows, Henri's student, is famous for his boxing paintings including Stag at Sharkey's, Dempsey and Firpo, and Both Members of This Club (which depicts a white boxer and the black fighter Joe Gans, satirizing private sport clubs). Bellows also painted Cliff Dwellers (1913), showing laundry hanging between apartment buildings over a crowd in the Lower East Side.

John Sloan painted sympathetic portrayals of working-class New Yorkers including McSorley's Bar, Wake of the Ferry, Hairdresser's Window, and Movies, Five Cents. He was known for socialist themes and painted a bespectacled self-portrait with a crooked tie on a window panel. George Luks painted The Wrestlers and street scenes like Houston Street and Hester Street. William Glackens was another key member who helped purchase the initial paintings for Philadelphia's Barnes Foundation. Maurice Prendergast exhibited with the group but his Post-Impressionist style differed from the other members. Stuart Davis studied under Henri before gravitating toward cubism.
