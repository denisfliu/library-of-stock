Summary of Facts

John Berryman is an American Confessional poet best known for The Dream Songs, a collection of 385 three-stanza, eighteen-line poems featuring his alter ego Henry ("Huffy Henry") and Henry's friend Mr. Bones. The collection's most famous opening line is "Huffy Henry hid the day, / unappeasable Henry sulked," and Dream Song 14 memorably declares "Life, friends, is boring. We must not say so," with the speaker concluding he has no "Inner Resources." The first part was published as 77 Dream Songs (1964) and expanded in His Toy, His Dream, His Rest (1968), whose second volume opens with "Opus Posthumous."

The Dream Songs are deeply shaped by Berryman's father's suicide, which occurred when Berryman was eleven. Multiple poems address this trauma: one describes the father rising "with his gun" and going "outdoors by my window" to do "what was needed," while another has the speaker spitting on "this dreadful banker's grave." A plea to "save us from shotguns & fathers' suicides" appears alongside the description of life as "a handkerchief sandwich." The speaker variously calls himself "the king of the pool," "the little man who smokes and smokes," "the blackt-out man," and "Pussy-cat!" Henry controversially adopts blackface minstrel dialect in some poems. The collection also includes elegies for William Carlos Williams ("lovely man" who "delivered infinite babies") and twelve elegies dedicated to Delmore Schwartz.

Dream Song 29 concludes "Nobody is ever missing" and provided the titles for all four season finale episodes of the TV series Succession. One poem ends with a dog leaving behind "me, wag" after departing into mountains.

Berryman's other major work is Homage to Mistress Bradstreet, a long poem addressed to colonial poet Anne Bradstreet, asking "moved you not, restless, waiting for [your husband]?" and concluding with "Hover, utter, still, a sourcing whom my lost candle like the firefly loves." The poem was illustrated by Ben Shahn.

Berryman committed suicide in 1972 by jumping off the Washington Avenue Bridge in Minneapolis. His birth name was John Allyn Smith Jr. He published a critical study of Stephen Crane in 1950. Robert Lowell wrote a poem for him stating "I used to want to live to avoid your elegy." Helen Vendler analyzed his Dream Songs in The Given and the Made. His life and death have been referenced in music by The Hold Steady ("Stuck Between Stations"), Okkervil River ("John Allyn Smith Sails"), and Nick Cave.
