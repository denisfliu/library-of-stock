Italian composer (1567–1643), spanning the Renaissance-to-Baroque transition. Key figure in the development of opera and the madrigal. General / Biographical

Italian composer (1567–1643) working during the Renaissance-Baroque transition. Served as music director of St. Mark's Cathedral in Venice from 1613 until his death. His madrigals were attacked by theorist Giovanni Artusi for their 'licentious' dissonances; Monteverdi and his brother responded by coining the terms 'prima prattica' (first practice) and 'seconda prattica' (second practice). The seconda prattica subordinated counterpoint to textual expression. In 2000, the Monteverdi Choir (founded by Sir John Eliot Gardiner in 1964) embarked on a pilgrimage to dozens of European churches to record Bach's cantatas. At least three Monteverdi libretti were written by Giulio Strozzi, adoptive father of composer Barbara Strozzi. Wikipedia

L'Orfeo

Opera in five acts, premiered in Mantua in 1607 under the patronage of the Duke of Mantua. The earliest opera still regularly performed. A brass toccata (repeated three times) opens the work before the personification of Music (La Musica) delivers the prologue; that toccata was reused as the 'Deus in adjutorium' opening of the 1610 Vespers. The opera follows Orpheus (Orfeo), who descends to the Underworld to recover Eurydice. In the finale, Apollo descends on a cloud (deus ex machina) to invite Orpheus to heaven — replacing Alessandro Striggio's original ending. Wikipedia

Vespers of 1610 (Vespro della Beata Vergine)

Sacred collection published in 1610, dedicated to the Blessed Virgin Mary. Opens with 'Deus in adjutorium' (reusing the toccata from L'Orfeo) followed by a choir with trumpet fanfares. Includes the duet 'Pulchra es' and concludes with a long Magnificat setting. The motet 'Nigra sum' is written for tenor; the two-tenor piece 'Duo Seraphim' adds a third tenor to symbolize the Trinity. Combines old 'first practice' (cantus firmus writing) with the new seconda prattica. Sir John Eliot Gardiner founded the Monteverdi Choir in 1964 specifically to perform it. Wikipedia

Madrigals (Eight Books)

Monteverdi published eight books of madrigals (a ninth was published posthumously). The fifth book (including 'Cruda Amarilli') drew Artusi's attack. The eighth book (1638), subtitled 'Madrigals of War and Love,' includes 'Lamento della ninfa' and 'Il Combattimento di Tancredi e Clorinda,' which pioneered pizzicato and tremolo. Also contained the 'Sestina' inspired by the death of opera singer Caterina Martinelli, opening with 'Burned Remains.' A Sestina in the sixth book includes a nymph's lament with basso ostinato. Summary of Facts

Claudio Monteverdi (1567–1643) was an Italian composer who straddled the Renaissance-Baroque divide. He is credited with pioneering the opera and transforming the madrigal. He served as music director of St. Mark's Cathedral in Venice from 1613 until his death, a post previously held by Giovanni Gabrieli.

Monteverdi published eight books of madrigals. His Fifth Book (containing 'Cruda Amarilli' and 'Anima mia, perdona') was attacked by the theorist Giovanni Artusi, who criticized Monteverdi's 'licentious' dissonances. In response, Monteverdi and his brother coined the terms 'prima prattica' (first practice — the old Palestrinian rules) and 'seconda prattica' (second practice — dissonance subordinated to text expression, also called stile moderno). Artusi's anonymous attacks called Monteverdi's unpublished madrigals 'castles in the air, chimeras founded upon sand.'

Monteverdi's Eighth Book of Madrigals (1638), subtitled 'of War and Love,' is notable for two landmark works. 'Il Combattimento di Tancredi e Clorinda' depicts a battle between a Christian knight and a Saracen warrior woman, pioneering the stile concitato (agitated style) through novel uses of pizzicato and tremolo — among the earliest in orchestral music. 'Lamento della ninfa' features a solo soprano who sings 'at the tempo of her emotion' over a basso ostinato ground in A minor (descending fourth), while three men sing in strict time.

L'Orfeo (1607), composed for the Duke of Mantua, is the earliest opera still regularly performed. It opens with a brass toccata (a trumpet fanfare repeated three times), which Monteverdi reused as the 'Deus in adjutorium meum intende' opening of his 1610 Vespers. The personification of Music (La Musica) delivers the prologue; her ritornello is an early operatic leitmotif. The opera's vocal highlight is 'Possente spirto,' Orpheus's aria as he attempts to persuade Charon to ferry him across the Styx — Monteverdi wrote two alternative vocal lines for the soloist and included prominent call-and-response with a trumpet. The original ending (by librettist Alessandro Striggio the Younger, in which the Bacchae murder Orpheus) was replaced by a deus ex machina finale in which Apollo descends on a cloud to take Orpheus to heaven.

The Vespers of 1610 (Vespro della Beata Vergine) opens with 'Deus in adjutorium' (the repurposed L'Orfeo toccata), includes the tenor-voice motet 'Nigra sum,' the two-tenor 'Duo Seraphim' (a third tenor added to symbolize the Trinity), the duet 'Pulchra es,' and concludes with a large Magnificat setting. It combines cantus firmus writing (the old first practice) with the new seconda prattica. Sir John Eliot Gardiner founded the Monteverdi Choir in 1964 specifically to perform this work.

Monteverdi's final opera, L'incoronazione di Poppea (1642), set in ancient Rome, ends with the duet 'Pur ti miro, pur ti godo' between Nero and Poppea. The first revival was in Naples in 1651. At least three of Monteverdi's libretti were written by Giulio Strozzi, the adoptive father of composer Barbara Strozzi, who carried forward the seconda prattica style. Gian Francesco Malipiero created the first complete edition of Monteverdi's works between 1926 and 1942.
