British sculptor known for creating casts of negative space -- the interiors and undersides of everyday objects such as rooms, houses, stairs, and beds. First female winner of the Turner Prize (1993), which she won for House, a concrete cast of an entire Victorian house. Also known for Embankment at the Tate Modern's Turbine Hall and the Judenplatz Holocaust Memorial in Vienna. Member of the Young British Artists (YBAs).

Summary of Facts

Rachel Whiteread is a British sculptor best known for creating casts of negative space -- the volumes inside, underneath, and around everyday objects and architectural spaces. Her technique involves making plaster, concrete, resin, or rubber molds of the interiors of rooms, furniture, and structures, effectively 'mummifying the air' within them.

Her most frequently asked-about work is House (1993), a full concrete cast of a Victorian terraced house at 193 Grove Road in Mile End, East London. House won her the 1993 Turner Prize, making her the first female winner of that award -- the single most common giveaway clue for identifying Whiteread. The work was controversial: a borough council voted to demolish it, and graffiti reading 'Wot for' and 'Why not' appeared on it before its removal.

Embankment (2005) filled the Tate Modern's Turbine Hall with mountains of white polyethylene boxes, inspired by a box found among her late mother's belongings. The Judenplatz Holocaust Memorial (2000) in Vienna features a library of titleless concrete books with their spines turned inward, sometimes described as a 'Nameless Library.' Untitled Monument (2001) was an inverted transparent resin cast of Trafalgar Square's fourth plinth, placed upside-down on top of the plinth itself.

Earlier works include Ghost (1990), a plaster cast of an entire room's interior, and Untitled (Stairs), cast from the space above a stairwell. Shallow Breath (1988) was cast from the underside of a bed.

Whiteread is a member of the Young British Artists (YBAs), grouped with Tracey Emin and Damien Hirst. The Turner Prize is a very common cross-reference point. Questions about Whiteread tend to appear at difficulty 8-10, often as bonus parts in questions about installation art, architectural elements (stairs, houses, beds), or Holocaust memorials. The primary giveaways are 'first female Turner Prize winner' and 'casts of negative space.'
