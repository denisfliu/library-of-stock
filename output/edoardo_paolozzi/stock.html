Scottish-Italian sculptor, printmaker, and collagist, widely regarded as a pioneer of Pop Art. A founding member of the Independent Group in London. Best known for his collage 'I Was a Rich Man's Plaything' (1947) and his 'As Is When' print series (1965) depicting Ludwig Wittgenstein. His bronze Newton sculpture (1995) stands outside the British Library. Delivered the influential lecture 'Bunk!' at the ICA in 1952, presenting his early collages.

Paolozzi's most famous work, a collage considered one of the first works of Pop Art. Summary of Facts

Eduardo Paolozzi (1924–2005) was a Scottish-Italian artist celebrated as one of the pioneers of Pop Art. Born in Edinburgh to Italian immigrant parents, he became a central figure in London's post-war avant-garde scene, co-founding the Independent Group at the Institute of Contemporary Arts (ICA) in the early 1950s alongside figures such as Richard Hamilton, Lawrence Alloway, and the Smithsons. His 1952 lecture 'Bunk!' — at which he projected a rapid-fire sequence of American magazine clippings, comic strips, and advertisements — is widely regarded as a foundational moment in Pop Art history.

His most famous work, the collage 'I Was a Rich Man's Plaything' (1947), is considered one of the first Pop Art works. It depicts a woman posing in a red dress above a warplane, a hand holding a gun, the word 'POP' bursting in a cloud of smoke from a pistol, and advertising imagery for Coca-Cola and cherry pie, alongside the text 'Intimate Confessions' and 'Keep 'Em Flying!'. The collage anticipated the movement's characteristic blend of consumer imagery, mass media, and irony.

His 1965 silkscreen print series 'As Is When' depicted the philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein in Pop Art style, with accompanying texts: one print shows Wittgenstein 'admiring' film stars Betty Hutton and Betty Grable, with a text noting he typically ate pork pies while watching movies from the front row of the cinema.

Among his sculptural works, the bronze Newton (1995), installed in the piazza outside the British Library in London, is particularly prominent. Inspired by William Blake's monotype print of the same subject — which shows Newton seated naked on a mossy rock, bending over a scroll with a compass — Paolozzi's version renders Newton with machine-like bolts joining his limbs to his body, reflecting his interest in the relationship between humanity and technology.

His collage 'Real Gold' features Mickey Mouse alongside a can of lemon juice in front of a woman in a gold swimsuit, further demonstrating his recurring use of popular consumer icons. Paolozzi's later career encompassed large-scale public sculptures and continued printmaking, and he was knighted in 1988.
