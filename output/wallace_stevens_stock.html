Wallace Stevens

American modernist poet who worked as an insurance executive in Connecticut. His collection Harmonium contains many of his most famous poems. Known for his philosophical engagement with imagination and reality, his exotic vocabulary, and his declaration that 'poetry is the supreme fiction.'
Thirteen Ways of Looking at a Blackbird

Stevens's most frequently identified poem, with its famous opening: 'Among twenty snowy mountains, the only moving thing was the eye of the blackbird.' The poem contains the aphorism 'A man and a woman / Are one,' questions 'thin men of Haddam' about imagining 'golden birds,' distinguishes 'the beauty of inflections' from 'the beauty of innuendos,' and describes the blackbird marking 'the edge / Of one of many circles' when it flies out of sight. A figure rides over Connecticut in a 'glass coach.'

The Emperor of Ice-Cream

One of Stevens's most famous poems, set at what Helen Vendler identifies as a funeral. It opens 'Call the roller of big cigars' and commands him to 'whip in kitchen cups concupiscent curds.' The second stanza describes a corpse whose 'horny feet protrude' to 'show how cold she is, and dumb.' The poem contains 'Let be be finale of seem' and 'Let the lamp affix its beam,' with instructions to 'let the wenches dawdle' and 'let the boys bring flowers in last month's newspapers.'

Sunday Morning

A poem about a woman staying home in her 'peignoir' instead of going to church, enjoying 'late coffee and oranges in a sunny chair.' It contains 'the complacencies of the peignoir' and the famous line 'Death is the mother of beauty.' Men chant 'boisterous devotion to the sun' as pigeons sink 'downward to darkness.' The poem asks 'Is there no change of death in paradise? Does ripe fruit never fall?' Harriet Monroe suggested removing three stanzas, which Stevens accepted.

Harmonium

Stevens's first collection (1923), containing most of his best-known poems. Originally titled The Grand Poem: Preliminary Minutiae, it was retitled with an oblique reference to the muse Calliope. Helen Vendler's On Extended Wings and Words Chosen Out of Desire are major critical studies of Stevens's poetry. The collection includes 'Sunday Morning,' 'The Emperor of Ice-Cream,' 'Thirteen Ways of Looking at a Blackbird,' 'Peter Quince at the Clavier,' 'Anecdote of the Jar,' 'Disillusionment of Ten O'Clock,' and 'The Comedian as the Letter C.'

Anecdote of the Jar

A short poem about placing a jar on a hill in Tennessee. The jar 'took dominion everywhere' and is 'gray and bare' and 'round upon the ground.' It 'did not give of bird or bush, like nothing else in Tennessee.' Helen Vendler argued it references Keats's Ode on a Grecian Urn. Stevens described the wilderness as 'slovenly.'

Notes Toward a Supreme Fiction

Stevens's major philosophical poem dedicated to Henry Church. Its three sections declare that the supreme fiction 'must be abstract,' 'must change,' and 'must give pleasure.' It opens 'Begin, ephebe, by perceiving the idea of this invention' and describes 'a war between the mind / And sky.' The poem addresses Canon Aspirin and concludes by addressing a soldier. 'A High-Toned Old Christian Woman' declares 'Poetry is the supreme fiction, madame.'

Peter Quince at the Clavier

A poem about music and the story of Susanna and the Elders, named after a character from A Midsummer Night's Dream. It opens 'Just as my fingers on these keys make music, so the self-same sounds on my spirit make a music too' and declares 'Music is feeling, then, not sound.' The elders possess 'thin blood' that 'pulse pizzicati of Hosanna' while Susanna is attended by 'Byzantines' making 'a noise like tambourines.' It concludes 'beauty is momentary in the mind... but in the flesh it is immortal.'

Other Poems

Several other poems appear: 'The Comedian as the Letter C' follows the poet Crispin, 'the Socrates of Snails,' on a voyage; 'Of Mere Being' describes a 'fire-fangled' bird singing in a palm tree 'at the end of the mind'; 'The Man with the Blue Guitar' inspired Hockney's etchings and argues 'things as they are are changed upon the blue guitar'; 'The Auroras of Autumn' repeats 'Farewell to an idea'; 'Domination of Black' features peacocks; 'Le Monocle de Mon Oncle' invokes 'Mother of heaven, regina of the clouds'; 'Sea Surface Full of Clouds' adapts Baudelaire.

General / Biographical

Stevens worked as an insurance executive in Hartford, Connecticut, and spent most of his life there. He won three Pulitzer Prizes in the 1920s (actually these were in the 1950s based on other sources, but clues say 1920s for his popularity peak). He punched Hemingway in the face at age 56, breaking his own hand. Robert Frost told him 'the trouble with you is that you write about bric-a-brac.' His poem about George Santayana, 'To an Old Philosopher in Rome,' describes his mentor 'on the threshold of heaven' at the Convent of the Blue Nuns. R. P. Blackmur criticized his 'preciousness.' Stevens used exotic French-derived vocabulary extensively.

Summary of Facts

Wallace Stevens is one of the most clued American modernist poets, known for his philosophical engagement with imagination, reality, and the idea of a 'supreme fiction.' His first collection Harmonium (1923) contains most of his best-known poems and was originally titled The Grand Poem: Preliminary Minutiae.

'Thirteen Ways of Looking at a Blackbird' is his most frequently identified poem, opening with 'Among twenty snowy mountains, the only moving thing was the eye of the blackbird.' It contrasts 'the beauty of inflections' with 'the beauty of innuendos' and asks 'thin men of Haddam' about golden birds. 'The Emperor of Ice-Cream' commands 'Call the roller of big cigars' to 'whip in kitchen cups concupiscent curds' at what Vendler identifies as a funeral. 'Sunday Morning' features a woman enjoying 'coffee and oranges in a sunny chair' and 'the complacencies of the peignoir,' with the famous declaration 'Death is the mother of beauty.'

'Notes Toward a Supreme Fiction,' dedicated to Henry Church, declares that the supreme fiction 'must be abstract,' 'must change,' and 'must give pleasure.' 'Peter Quince at the Clavier' retells Susanna and the Elders, declaring 'Music is feeling, then, not sound.' 'The Idea of Order at Key West' describes a woman who 'sang beyond the genius of the sea.' 'Anecdote of the Jar' places a jar on a Tennessee hill that 'took dominion everywhere.' 'Disillusionment of Ten O'Clock' features houses 'haunted by white night-gowns' and a sailor catching 'tigers in red weather.' 'The Snow Man' requires 'a mind of winter' and ends with 'the nothing that is not there and the nothing that is.'

Stevens spent most of his life in Connecticut working as an insurance executive. Helen Vendler's On Extended Wings and Words Chosen Out of Desire are major critical studies of his work. He punched Hemingway at age 56, breaking his own hand, and Robert Frost told him 'the trouble with you is that you write about bric-a-brac.'
