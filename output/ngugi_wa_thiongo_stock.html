Ngugi wa Thiong'o

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Kenyan author, born James Ngugi. Central figure in debates over language and colonialism in African literature. After writing several novels in English (A Grain of Wheat, Petals of Blood), he famously abandoned English in favor of Gikuyu (Kikuyu), a decision explained in his essay collection Decolonising the Mind. Imprisoned after the performance of his play I Will Marry When I Want at the Kamiriithu Community Centre. His works frequently depict the Mau Mau Rebellion, Kenyan independence (Uhuru), and postcolonial corruption. The fictional town of Ilmorog appears in multiple novels. His son Mukoma wa Ngugi is also a writer.
A Grain of Wheat

Ngugi's most frequently asked-about novel, set around the time of Kenya's independence (Uhuru). Mugo confesses to betraying the Mau Mau resistance fighter Kihika, who had killed District Officer Robson. Mugo's confession removes suspicion from Karanja, who had allied with the British. Mumbi becomes pregnant with Karanja's child while her husband Gikonyo is in a detention camp. On the day of Uhuru, a character breaks his left arm in a foot race. General R. fills in when Mugo refuses to give a speech at the independence celebration. At the end, Gikonyo finally forgives Mumbi and accepts her out-of-wedlock child. Wikipedia

Petals of Blood

Set in the fictional town of Ilmorog (which becomes 'New Ilmorog' after a railroad is built). Title borrowed from Derek Walcott's poem 'The Swamp' (originally titled Ballad of the Barmaid). Munira is a schoolmaster who burns down Wanja's brothel (after seeing Karega enter it), incidentally killing three brewery directors (Chui, Kimeria, Mzigo). Inspector Godfrey investigates the fire. Abdulla is a one-legged Mau Mau veteran who owns a shop, cares for the orphan Joseph, and leads a march; his donkey is killed when a plane crashes into the village. The theng'eta drink is brewed from a plant; its potency described by the equation P=3T. During a drought, villagers walk to the capital. The Siriana school becomes oppressive after former student Chui returns to lead it. Surveyors planning a Trans-African Highway die in a plane crash. Wikipedia

I Will Marry When I Want

A play co-written with Ngugi wa Mirii, originally in Gikuyu (Ngaahika Ndeenda). Kiguunda and his wife Wangeci lose their one-and-a-half acre plot of land after putting up the title deed as collateral for their daughter Gathoni's church wedding to Kioi's son John. The businesspeople Ahab and Jezebel (biblical names) trick them into this. The title deed framed on the wall repeatedly falls, foreshadowing its loss. A character uses a sword to chase away those trying to convert him. Singers gather singing 'The Satan of poverty must be crushed.' Performed at the Kamiriithu Community Education and Cultural Centre, which was subsequently burned down by police. Ngugi was imprisoned for this play. Kikuyu song and dance inspired its composition. Both homes in the play have pictures of a hairy Nebuchadnezzar and a board saying 'Christ Is the Head of This House.' Wikipedia

Wizard of the Crow

A satirical novel set in the fictional Free Republic of Aburiria, ruled by a despotic Ruler who orders the construction of 'Marching to Heaven,' a massive tower / 'superwonder of the world.' Kamiti and Nyawira moonlight as the titular wizard/sorcerer figure. A beggar and protestor jointly grift as a sorcerer who heals the voice of the Ruler. Three ministers have their eyes, ears, and tongues enhanced. American doctors wish to patent a mysterious illness. The dissident movement 'The Voice of the People' distributes plastic snakes. The pregnant Ruler gives birth to 'Baby Democracy.' Every television screen splits into seven pieces after a club is thrown at a cameraman. Written in Gikuyu and self-translated into English. Decolonising the Mind

Essay collection in which Ngugi explains his decision to abandon writing fiction in English in favor of Gikuyu. Calls the use of English by African writers a 'cultural bomb' that 'annihilates a people's belief in their names.' Ends with a chapter titled 'The Quest for Relevance.' Describes how colonial schools punished students for speaking Gikuyu (teachers had students pass around a button to identify rule-breakers). Discusses Kikuyu song and dance and oral traditions. The town of Ilmorog appears in two sequential novels whose switch in languages is explained in this collection. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Born James Ngugi in 1938 in Kenya. Rejected his English name as colonialist. Imprisoned by Daniel Arap Moi's regime after I Will Marry When I Want. His son Mukoma wa Ngugi is also a writer (Black Star Nairobi, The Rise of the African Novel). His daughter Wanjiku wa Ngugi wrote The Fall of Saints. Gayatri Spivak dedicated the essay 'In Praise of a Friend' to him. Ngugi advocates 'Globalectics' in his criticism. Abdulrazak Gurnah wrote a monograph on his novel Matigari. Also wrote The Trial of Dedan Kimathi (with Micere Mugo), an orange seller helps children smuggle a gun in bread. Mother, Sing for Me (Maitu Njugira) dramatized resistance to the Kipande forced labor system and led to the shutdown of his theater troupe in 1982. Wrestling with the Devil discusses I Will Marry When I Want. The Perfect Nine is a novel-in-verse about 99 suitors competing to become progenitors of nine clans. Described qualifying for a conference having only published 'The Fig Tree' and 'The Return.' Wrote in English early (Weep Not, Child; The River Between; A Grain of Wheat; Petals of Blood) then switched to Gikuyu. Called George Lamming 'the prime example of peasant revolt in a white-dominated world' in Homecoming.

Summary of Facts

Ngugi wa Thiong'o (born James Ngugi, 1938) is the most frequently asked-about Kenyan author in quizbowl. His most dominant clue pair involves A Grain of Wheat, where Mugo confesses to betraying the Mau Mau fighter Kihika (who killed District Officer Robson), and Decolonising the Mind, the essay collection in which he explains his decision to abandon English for Gikuyu and calls English a 'cultural bomb.' These two works appear in virtually every question about him. Petals of Blood, set in the fictional town of Ilmorog, features Munira burning down Wanja's brothel (killing three brewery directors), the one-legged veteran Abdulla whose donkey is killed in a plane crash, and the theng'eta drink (whose potency follows the equation P=3T). Its title comes from Derek Walcott's poem 'The Swamp.' I Will Marry When I Want, co-written with Ngugi wa Mirii, is the play that led to his imprisonment. Kiguunda loses his one-and-a-half acre plot of land after being tricked by the businesspeople Ahab and Jezebel into financing a church wedding. It was performed at the Kamiriithu Community Centre, which police subsequently burned down. Wizard of the Crow is a satirical novel set in the fictional Aburiria, where the despotic Ruler orders the construction of 'Marching to Heaven.' Kamiti and Nyawira adopt the title wizard persona; three ministers have their eyes, ears, and tongues enhanced. The Black Hermit is an early play where Remi causes Thoni's suicide. Devil on the Cross was written on toilet paper in prison. The River Between depicts Waiyaki's failed attempt to unite two villages. Other works referenced include The Trial of Dedan Kimathi, Mother Sing for Me, and The Perfect Nine. His son Mukoma and daughter Wanjiku are also writers.
