Katherine Mansfield (born Kathleen Mansfield Beauchamp, also known as Kathleen Mansfield Murry) was a New Zealand-born modernist short story writer who is overwhelmingly identified in quizbowl through her story "The Garden Party." In that story, Laura Sheridan oversees preparations for her family's garden party, including directing the placement of a marquee and receiving cream puffs from Godber's bakery.
The Garden Party

Mansfield's most frequently clued work by far, appearing in virtually every question about her. It follows Laura Sheridan as she prepares for her family's garden party, learns of neighbor Mr. Scott's death from Godber's delivery man, tries to cancel the party, and ultimately delivers leftover food to the grieving family. The story opens with "And after all the weather was ideal" and ends with Laura stammering "Isn't life-" to her brother Laurie.

Summary of Facts

Katherine Mansfield (born Kathleen Mansfield Beauchamp, also known as Kathleen Mansfield Murry) was a New Zealand-born modernist short story writer who is overwhelmingly identified in quizbowl through her story "The Garden Party." In that story, Laura Sheridan oversees preparations for her family's garden party, including directing the placement of a marquee and receiving cream puffs from Godber's bakery. The story opens with the line "And after all the weather was ideal." Laura's sister Jose plays the song "This Life is Weary" on the piano while the maid Sadie requests sandwich flags. When Godber's delivery man brings news that the neighboring carter Mr. Scott has been killed by his horse, Laura tries to cancel the party, but her mother Mrs. Sheridan insists it go on. Laura feels ashamed of her fancy black hat trimmed with gold daisies when she delivers a basket of leftover food to the grieving Scott family. Upon seeing Scott's "wonderful, beautiful" corpse, described as "given up to his dream," Laura sobs and stammers "Isn't life-" to her brother Laurie, who responds "Isn't it, darling?" The band at the party wears green coats and is described as "too like frogs for words" by Kitty Maitland. The story also features the character Laura previously appearing in "Her First Ball."

Mansfield's story "Bliss" centers on Bertha Young, who feels an overwhelming sense of bliss symbolized by a pear tree in her garden that stretches up "like the flame of a candle." She contemplates the tree with her guest Pearl Fulton, with whom she shares a moment of "unearthly light, understanding each other perfectly." The guests include the Norman Knights (one dressed in an orange coat with monkeys), The Poet Eddie Warren (who complains about a taxi driver and praises a poem beginning "Why must it always be tomato soup?"), and her husband Harry. At the end, Bertha discovers Harry is having an affair with Pearl Fulton, but the pear tree remains "as lovely as ever and as full of flower." Bertha insists on feeding her baby, Little B, herself, upsetting her nanny.

"Miss Brill" depicts a lonely English teacher who sits on a park bench at the Jardins Publiques wearing her beloved fur, which she imagines crying. She describes her breathing as "light and sad" before correcting herself to "gentle." A young couple mocks her fur, comparing it to a "fried whiting," and the story ends with her thinking "she heard something crying" as she puts the fur away.

"The Doll's House" features the recurring Burnell family and centers on a dollhouse given by old Mrs. Hay. The shunned Kelvey children (whose father is in prison and who wear clothes made from tablecloths and curtains) are ostracized by the other children. At the end, Kezia lets the Kelvey girls see the dollhouse, and Else whispers "I seen the little lamp."

"The Daughters of the Late Colonel" follows sisters Constantia (Con) and Josephine (Jug) coping with their father's death. Nurse Andrews annoys them by taking excessive helpings of bread and butter. They discuss giving their father's watch to their nephew Cyril, debate whether to fire the maid Kate, and realize they no longer need to silence the barrel organ that always annoyed their father, since he is dead. One sister stares at a mantelpiece Buddha statue.

"The Fly" features "the boss," a businessman whose son died in Belgium during World War One. He is reminded of the death by a visit from Woodifield, and proceeds to drop blobs of ink on a fly, killing it.

"Prelude" (adapted from the novel The Aloe) follows the Burnell family, including sisters Kezia and Lottie trying to fit into a luggage-packed buggy. A character chops off a duck's head with a tomahawk to demonstrate it can walk headless. Linda Fairfield realizes that life is "laughable, simply laughable" while observing an aloe. The story ends with Beryl Fairfield rejecting the advances of drunken Harry Kember. Kezia is laughed at for requesting strawberries and cream.

"How Pearl Button Was Kidnapped" describes a girl taken by "blue men" (or "two dark women") to the beach, where she has an enjoyable day before being carried back to the "House of Boxes." The character Pearl Button was inspired by Mansfield's relationship with Maata Mahupuku.

Biographically, Mansfield was born in New Zealand, was the inspiration for the character Gudrun in D.H. Lawrence's Women in Love, and possibly caught fatal tuberculosis from her close friend Lawrence. Her husband was John Middleton Murry, who published her work in journals like Adelphi. She was friends with Virginia Woolf. She died of tuberculosis at age 34. Claire Harman's biography All Sorts of Lives defends her against charges of plagiarizing Chekhov's story "Sleepy." She joined George Gurdjieff's Institute for the Harmonious Development of Man shortly after publishing The Garden Party and Other Stories. Janet Frame's mother worked as a maid for Mansfield's family. Eudora Welty suggested Mansfield wrote her regional stories out of homesickness while "eleven thousand miles from home." In Bharati Mukherjee's The Tiger's Daughter, the protagonist Tara writes her dissertation on Mansfield at Vassar. Mansfield described her stories as a "cry against corruption" in a letter to her husband, and wrote "How hard it is to escape from places" because "you leave little bits of yourself fluttering on the fences." Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar suggested in No Man's Land that Scott's death was inspired by the death of Mansfield's brother.
