Ryunosuke Akutagawa

Japanese short story writer of the early 20th century, considered a master of the form. Best known for 'In a Grove' (seven conflicting testimonies about a samurai's death), 'Rashomon' (a man robs an old woman at a gate in Kyoto), and 'Hell Screen' (an artist watches his daughter burn to complete a painting). Also wrote 'The Spider's Thread,' 'Cogwheels,' 'The Nose,' and the novel Kappa. His suicide in 1927 was a significant cultural event; his suicide note is titled 'A Note to a Certain Old Friend.' Names Japan's most prestigious literary prize (the Akutagawa Prize). Adapted 'The Parable of the Onion' from The Brothers Karamazov into 'The Spider's Thread.' His stories were combined into Akira Kurosawa's film Rashomon.
"In a Grove"

A short story presented as a sequence of seven conflicting testimonies to the High Police Commissioner about the death of the samurai Takehiro. Witnesses include a traveling priest, a woodcutter, the bandit Tajomaru, Takehiro's wife Masago, and Takehiro's own ghost (who speaks through a medium and claims he committed suicide). A sudden gust of wind reveals the woman's inspiring resemblance to a Bodhisattva. The setting (a bamboo grove) and the plot were combined with 'Rashomon' by screenwriter Shinobu Hashimoto into Akira Kurosawa's film Rashomon. The 'Rashomon effect' describing contradictory accounts derives from this story. Wikipedia

"Rashomon"

A short story set at the Rashomon gate in Kyoto. A young man sheltering from the rain watches an old woman rip hairs from corpses to make wigs. She justifies herself by saying the dead woman had sold snake meat as dried fish. Disgusted but emboldened, the man steals her clothes and disappears into the night. The story ends with the woman 'staring down into the abyss of night.' The gate setting was used in Kurosawa's film Rashomon, combined with the plot of 'In a Grove.' Wikipedia

"Hell Screen"

A story about the artist Yoshihide, commissioned by Lord Horikawa to paint a hell screen. Yoshihide binds apprentices with chains and attacks them with a horned owl to study pained expressions ('Monkey-hide'). He creates Five Levels of Rebirth by studying a dead corpse. He requests that his employer burn a woman in a carriage to complete the painting; Lord Horikawa has Yoshihide's daughter Yuzuki and her pet monkey burned alive in the carriage instead. Yoshihide hangs himself after completing the painting. Yoshihide is drawn from a painter of the same name in the third scroll of the Uji Shui. Wikipedia

"The Spider's Thread"

A story adapted from the 'Parable of the Onion' in Dostoevsky's The Brothers Karamazov. The thief Kandata, whose one act of kindness in life was sparing a spider, is given a chance to climb out of Hell (the Pool of Blood / Blood Lake) using the title spider's thread. He fails when he selfishly claims the thread is his alone, causing it to break and him to fall back in. The Buddha sends him the thread through a lotus pond. Wikipedia

"Cogwheels" / "Spinning Gears"

A semi-autobiographical, posthumously published story in which the hallucination-plagued narrator (Boku) sees translucent spinning cogwheels blocking his vision and raincoat-wearing men. He picks up Crime and Punishment but finds a misprinted Brothers Karamazov. He learns his arsonist brother-in-law jumped in front of a train while wearing a raincoat. He identifies a 'neighbourhood Swede' as August Strindberg, whose Inferno impressed him. The narrator is tormented by thoughts about Yoshihide's fate in 'Hell Screen.' The story ends with the narrator asking 'Isn't there someone who will be good enough to strangle me in my sleep?' Prefigures Akutagawa's own suicide. Wikipedia

"The Nose"

A story about the priest Zenchi Naigu, whose extremely long nose (five or six inches, hanging below his chin) is his source of constant embarrassment. He needs a two-foot-long stick to accompany him when eating. A disciple boils the nose and stomps on it until millet-like beads of fat are extracted, making it shrink. After the procedure, Naigu is unexpectedly ridiculed (people preferred him with the long nose). He feels satisfied 'as if he had copied out the Lotus Sutra.' He compares himself to Liu Bei from Romance of the Three Kingdoms. Written by a non-Russian author who shares the story title with Gogol. Wikipedia

Their society cannibalizes the unemployed, believes in eradicating heredity, and practices a religion called Lifeism/Viverism. One kappa philosopher Geeru admires philosophers who killed themselves. Suicide Note / "A Note to a Certain Old Friend" / "A Fool's Life"

Akutagawa's suicide in 1927 was a major cultural event. His suicide note, titled 'A Note to a Certain Old Friend,' describes 'diseased nerves, lucid as ice' and discusses his 'vague sense of unease towards the future.' Its postscript recalls discussing 'Empedocles on Etna' under linden trees and feeling 'I was one who fancied himself a god.' He states he does 'not regard suicide as a sin, as Westerners do' and concludes that 'use of a drug seemed to be perhaps the most satisfactory way.' He references Kleist and Racine as predecessors. 'A Fool's Life' is a last work of 51 prose vignettes set at a bookstore, including the aphorism 'life is not worth a single line of Baudelaire.' Kawabata quoted the suicide note in his Nobel lecture. Miyamoto Kenji discussed the impact in 'The Literature of Decline.' Osamu Dazai's No Longer Human may have been written in response to Akutagawa's death.

General / Biographical

Japanese short story writer (1892-1927). Names Japan's most prestigious literary prize (the Akutagawa Prize, semi-annual). His suicide in 1927 was a major cultural event. Had a longstanding debate with Tanizaki Jun'ichiro over the importance of plot in literature. His stories were inspired by diverse sources including The Brothers Karamazov, Pu Songling's Strange Tales from a Chinese Studio, and the Uji Shui. His son Yasushi Akutagawa composed film scores. The title assassin in Jim Jarmusch's Ghost Dog steals a collection of his fiction during a mob hit. A popular COVID-era book club spent 85 days reading Tolstoy, but Akutagawa is also noted for being read in book club contexts. Kurosawa's film Rashomon adapted his stories.

Summary of Facts

Ryunosuke Akutagawa is one of the most heavily clued non-Western literature authors in quizbowl. His stories generate a remarkably dense and consistent set of clues.

'In a Grove' is his most frequently referenced work, recognizable by its structure of seven conflicting testimonies to the High Police Commissioner about the death of the samurai Takehiro. The witnesses include a woodcutter, a traveling priest, the bandit Tajomaru (who claims to have lured the victim with promises of cheap mirrors), Takehiro's wife Masago (who appears as a Bodhisattva figure but later considers herself 'forsaken by Kannon'), and Takehiro's own ghost speaking through a medium, who claims he committed suicide. This story's plot was combined with the setting of 'Rashomon' (the gate in Kyoto) by screenwriter Shinobu Hashimoto into Kurosawa's film Rashomon, giving rise to the 'Rashomon effect' describing contradictory accounts.

'Rashomon' itself features a man sheltering from rain at the gate who watches an old woman rip hair from corpses to make wigs. The old woman justifies herself by saying the dead woman sold snake meat as dried fish. Disgusted, the man steals the old woman's clothes. 'Hell Screen' depicts the artist Yoshihide ('Monkey-hide'), who binds apprentices with chains and attacks them with owls and snakes to study their pained expressions. Lord Horikawa commissions him to paint a hell screen; Yoshihide's daughter and her pet monkey are burned alive in a carriage as inspiration, after which Yoshihide hangs himself. The character derives from the Uji Shui.

'The Spider's Thread' adapts the parable of the onion from The Brothers Karamazov: the thief Kandata, whose one act of kindness was sparing a spider, is given a chance to climb out of Hell using a spider's thread sent by the Buddha through a lotus pond, but falls back when he selfishly claims the thread as his alone. 'Cogwheels' / 'Spinning Gears' is a semi-autobiographical story in which the narrator hallucinates translucent spinning cogwheels, encounters raincoat-wearing figures, picks up Crime and Punishment but finds a misprinted Brothers Karamazov, and ends by asking 'Isn't there someone who will be good enough to strangle me in my sleep?' His arsonist brother-in-law is hit by a train. The story prefigures Akutagawa's own suicide.

'The Nose' features the priest Zenchi Naigu, whose enormously long nose is boiled, stomped on, and has millet-like fat beads extracted to shrink it, but he is then ridiculed for looking normal. Naigu compares himself to Liu Bei from Romance of the Three Kingdoms. Kappa is a late novel narrated by psychiatric Patient 23 who visits a kappa society where fetuses choose whether to be born, the unemployed are cannibalized, and Viverism/Lifeism is practiced. 'Yam Gruel' follows the red-nosed Goi, who dreams of eating his fill of yam gruel but loses his appetite after his wish is fulfilled. 'Dragon' features the long-nosed monk E'in who causes a mass delusion by posting a sign near Sarusawa Pond saying a dragon will ascend.

Akutagawa's suicide in 1927 is a major biographical anchor. His suicide note, 'A Note to a Certain Old Friend,' references 'diseased nerves, lucid as ice,' recalls discussing 'Empedocles on Etna' under linden trees, and references Kleist and Racine. 'A Fool's Life' is a set of 51 vignettes including 'life is not worth a single line of Baudelaire.' Kawabata quoted the suicide note in his Nobel lecture. The Akutagawa Prize, Japan's most prestigious literary award, is named after him.
