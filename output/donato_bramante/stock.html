Donato Bramante was a High Renaissance Italian architect active in Milan and Rome, best known for the Tempietto at San Pietro in Montorio, his original Greek-cross plan for St. Peter's Basilica (commissioned by Pope Julius II), and the Cortile del Belvedere in the Vatican. In Milan he designed the dome of Santa Maria delle Grazie and the trompe-l'oeil choir of Santa Maria presso San Satiro. He earned the nickname 'Il Ruinate' for demolishing old buildings in Rome.
The Tempietto

Cortile del Belvedere (Belvedere Courtyard)

Bramante designed the Cortile del Belvedere for the Vatican Palace, a hugely influential series of terraced outdoor spaces for Julius II. The Apollo Belvedere and the Laocoon were originally displayed there. The courtyard gives its name to the Apollo Belvedere sculpture.

Summary of Facts

Donato Bramante was the preeminent High Renaissance architect working in both Milan and Rome. His most celebrated independent building is the Tempietto (1502), a small perfectly circular martyrium in the courtyard of the church of San Pietro in Montorio in Rome, commissioned by Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain to mark the site of St. Peter's crucifixion. The Tempietto is surrounded by sixteen Doric columns accessible after climbing three steps on its base. Its design uses several different orders, including Tuscan columns surrounding a Doric-appearing set of pilasters. Giorgio Vasari suggested that Bramante was the model for Euclid (or Archimedes) in Raphael's School of Athens — the bent figure using a compass on a black slate at the right of the canvas.

Bramante was the first and original architect of the modern St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, commissioned by Pope Julius II. His design was based on a Greek cross plan with a dome modeled on the Pantheon. When Julius II died, Bramante was replaced by Sangallo, Fra Giocondo, and Raphael. Michelangelo later retained and simplified Bramante's Greek cross floor plan. Bramante earned the Roman nickname 'Il Ruinate' for demolishing so many old buildings to make room for new ones.

Bramante also designed the Cortile del Belvedere (Belvedere Courtyard) for the Vatican Palace for Julius II, a series of terraced outdoor spaces. The Apollo Belvedere is named for this courtyard where it was originally displayed.

The Bramante Staircase in the Vatican Museums — a double-helix spiral staircase — was commissioned by Pope Julius II and named for Bramante, though a later version was designed by Giuseppe Momo. A cloister named for Bramante is located in Santa Maria della Pace.

In Milan, as court architect to Ludovico Sforza, Bramante designed the sixteen-sided dome of Santa Maria delle Grazie and a famous trompe-l'oeil painting that makes an alcove appear to be an entire choir around the altar of Santa Maria presso San Satiro. He also designed the Palazzo Caprini, later demolished in the 17th century; it was a residence for Raphael and influenced Palladio's urban palazzi. According to Ascanio Condivi, Bramante was jealous of Michelangelo and arranged for him to paint the Sistine Chapel, knowing he had no fresco training — but later tried to have him replaced by Raphael when Michelangelo excelled. Bramante also organized a contest, judged by Raphael and won by Jacopo Sansovino, to determine the correct orientation of a broken arm in the Laocoon sculpture.
