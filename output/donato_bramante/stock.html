High Renaissance Italian architect known for the circular Tempietto at San Pietro in Montorio (commissioned by Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain), the original Greek-cross plan for St. Peter's Basilica (for Julius II), and the Cortile del Belvedere at the Vatican. In Milan, he served as court architect to Ludovico Sforza and designed the dome of Santa Maria delle Grazie and the trompe-l'oeil choir of Santa Maria presso San Satiro. Vasari says he was the model for Euclid in Raphael's School of Athens; Condivi claims he jealously arranged for Michelangelo to paint the Sistine Chapel. Nicknamed 'Il Ruinate' for demolishing old Roman buildings.
The Tempietto

The Tempietto (1502) is a small perfectly circular martyrium in the courtyard of San Pietro in Montorio in Rome. Surrounded by sixteen Doric columns accessible after climbing three steps, it was commissioned by Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain to mark the site of St. Peter's crucifixion. Vasari notes Bramante was modeled as the figure of Euclid in Raphael's School of Athens.

Cortile del Belvedere (Belvedere Courtyard)

Bramante designed the Cortile del Belvedere for the Vatican Palace, a hugely influential series of terraced outdoor spaces for Julius II. The Apollo Belvedere and the Laocoon and His Sons were originally displayed there. A section below Pirro Ligorio's nicchione (the largest niche built since antiquity) features an ancient bronze pinecone. The Pio-Clementine Museum now displays the Apollo and Belvedere Torso named for this courtyard.

Summary of Facts

Donato Bramante was the preeminent High Renaissance architect working in both Milan and Rome. His most celebrated independent building is the Tempietto (1502), a small perfectly circular martyrium in the courtyard of the church of San Pietro in Montorio in Rome, commissioned by Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain to mark the site of St. Peter's crucifixion. The Tempietto is surrounded by sixteen Doric columns accessible after climbing three steps on its base. Its design uses several different orders, including Tuscan columns surrounding a Doric-appearing set of pilasters. Giorgio Vasari suggested that Bramante was the model for Euclid (or Archimedes) in Raphael's School of Athens — the bent figure using a compass on a black slate at the right of the canvas.

Bramante was the first and original architect of the modern St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, commissioned by Pope Julius II. His design was based on a Greek cross plan with a dome modeled on the Pantheon. A medallion by the goldsmith Caradosso is one of the few surviving hints at the original external design. When Julius II died, Bramante was replaced by Sangallo, Fra Giocondo, and Raphael. Michelangelo later retained and simplified Bramante's Greek cross floor plan after reluctantly accepting the commission. Bramante earned the Roman nickname 'Il Ruinate' for demolishing so many old buildings to make room for new ones.

Bramante also designed the Cortile del Belvedere (Belvedere Courtyard) for the Vatican Palace for Julius II, a series of terraced outdoor spaces. The Apollo Belvedere and the Laocoon and His Sons were originally displayed there; the courtyard also contains a section below Pirro Ligorio's nicchione with a bronze pinecone, and now the Pio-Clementine Museum displays sculptures named for this courtyard.

The Bramante Staircase in the Vatican Museums — a double-helix spiral staircase — was commissioned by Pope Julius II and named for Bramante, though a later version was designed by Giuseppe Momo. A cloister named for Bramante is located in Santa Maria della Pace.

In Milan, as court architect to Ludovico Sforza, Bramante designed the sixteen-sided dome of Santa Maria delle Grazie and a famous trompe-l'oeil painting that makes an alcove appear to be an entire choir around the altar of Santa Maria presso San Satiro. He also designed the Palazzo Caprini, later demolished in the 17th century; it was a residence for Raphael and influenced Palladio's urban palazzi. According to Ascanio Condivi, Bramante was jealous of Michelangelo and arranged for him to paint the Sistine Chapel, knowing he had no fresco training — but later tried to have him replaced by Raphael when Michelangelo excelled. Bramante also organized a contest, judged by Raphael and won by Jacopo Sansovino, to determine the correct orientation of the Laocoon's broken arm.
