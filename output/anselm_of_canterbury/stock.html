11th-century Archbishop of Canterbury and father of Scholasticism, best known for the ontological argument for God's existence ('a being than which no greater can be conceived') in the Proslogion and the satisfaction theory of atonement in Cur Deus Homo. His motto was 'faith seeking understanding' (fides quaerens intellectum).
The Ontological Argument (Proslogion)

Anselm's Proslogion contains the ontological argument for God's existence, arguing that one can imagine 'the greatest possible conceivable being,' and that such a being must exist because existence is more perfect than non-existence. Anselm wrote the argument to persuade the 'Fool of the Psalms.' Norman Malcolm argued that Chapter Three of the Proslogion constitutes a separate, second argument distinct from Chapter Two. Gaunilo of Marmoutiers responded by postulating a 'Lost Island' to show the argument could prove the existence of anything perfect, in a text titled In Behalf of the Fool. Immanuel Kant objected that existence is 'not a predicate.' Kurt Gödel developed a modal version that was criticized by Jordan Sobel as leading to modal collapse. Later elaborators include Alexius Meinong (variant based on his theory of objects), Descartes (fifth Meditation), Charles Hartshorne (modal logic and dipolar attributes), Alvin Plantinga (modal version using axiom S5 in The Nature of Necessity), Robert Maydole (MPA and temporal-contingency arguments), and David Lewis (argued actuality is indexical in a 1970 article). Graham Oppy's namesake text divides the ontological argument into six subtypes. The Proslogion also discusses the 'harmony, fragrance, sweetness, softness, and beauty' of a being which cannot be thought not to exist.

Satisfaction Theory of Atonement (Cur Deus Homo)

In Cur Deus Homo, framed as a dialogue with his student Boso, Anselm criticized the dominant ransom theory of atonement. He argued instead that Christ's crucifixion was necessary not as a 'ransom' but to bring 'satisfaction' to God for humanity's offenses. Anselm developed the stance that humanity's sin could only be atoned by the resurrection. This satisfaction theory replaced the earlier ransom theory as the dominant account in Catholicism, superseding Gregory of Nyssa's comparison of Christ's incarnation to bait on a fishhook. Irenaeus had earlier developed a 'recapitulation theory' of atonement. In Evangelicalism, the main successor model is the 'penal substitution' theory. Peter Abelard opposed the satisfaction theory, claiming Christ's death was to exemplify perfect love.

General / Biographical

Archbishop of Canterbury, one-time Abbot of Bec. His motto was 'faith seeking understanding' (fides quaerens intellectum); he famously wrote 'I believe so that I may understand.' He also wrote the Monologion. R.W. Southern wrote two biographies of him and argued he originated a genre of 'ardent and effusive self-disclosure.' At the Council of Bari, he compared the Father-Son relationship to man's relationship to himself through memory. Considered the 'father of Scholasticism.' Anselm's On Truth employed contrasting student and teacher characters, exemplifying the Latin dialogue form. He also claimed that 'the eye of a needle' referred to a small pedestrian gate in the walls of Jerusalem.

Summary of Facts

Saint Anselm of Canterbury is an 11th-century philosopher and theologian best known for the ontological argument for God's existence and the satisfaction theory of atonement. He served as Archbishop of Canterbury and was formerly Abbot of Bec. He is considered the 'father of Scholasticism.'

The ontological argument, presented in the Proslogion, begins with the definition of God as 'that which none greater can be imagined' and argues that such a being must exist, because existence is more perfect than non-existence. The Proslogion opens with the invocation 'come now, insignificant man, fly for a moment from your affairs' and uses the example of a painter conceiving his painting before executing it. Anselm wrote the argument to persuade the 'Fool of the Psalms.' Norman Malcolm later argued that Chapter Three of the Proslogion constitutes a second, distinct argument from Chapter Two. The Proslogion also discusses the 'harmony, fragrance, sweetness, softness, and beauty' of a being that cannot be thought not to exist.

Gaunilo of Marmoutiers famously responded with the 'Lost Island' objection in his text In Behalf of the Fool. Immanuel Kant objected that existence is 'not a predicate.' Among later elaborators and critics, Descartes's variation makes up most of his fifth Meditation; Charles Hartshorne used modal logic and dipolar attributes, publishing his update in The Logic of Perfection; Alvin Plantinga developed a 'victorious' modal version in The Nature of Necessity using axiom S5, building on Hartshorne and Malcolm; Kurt Gödel developed a modal version criticized by Jordan Sobel as leading to modal collapse; David Lewis argued in 1970 that the argument is invalid because actuality is indexical; Alexius Meinong developed a variant based on his theory of objects; and Robert Maydole developed the MPA and temporal-contingency arguments in its support. Graham Oppy's namesake text divides the ontological argument into six subtypes. Iris Murdoch's The Sovereignty of the Good also includes a version. Duns Scotus's arguments for the univocity of being were based on Anselm's work.

In Cur Deus Homo, framed as a dialogue with his student Boso, Anselm criticized the dominant ransom theory of atonement and argued that Christ's crucifixion was necessary to bring 'satisfaction' to God for humanity's offenses, not as a ransom. His satisfaction theory replaced the earlier ransom theory (associated with Gregory of Nyssa's 'bait on a fishhook' analogy) as the dominant account in Catholicism. In Evangelicalism, the main successor model is the 'penal substitution' theory. Peter Abelard later opposed the satisfaction theory, claiming Christ's death was to exemplify perfect love.

Anselm's philosophical motto was 'faith seeking understanding' (fides quaerens intellectum), and he famously wrote 'I believe so that I may understand.' R.W. Southern wrote two biographies of him and argued in The Making of the Middle Ages that Anselm originated a genre of 'ardent and effusive self-disclosure.' At the Council of Bari, Anselm compared the Father-Son relationship to man's relationship to himself through memory, published in On the Procession of the Holy Spirit. He also wrote the Monologion and On Truth, the latter employing contrasting student and teacher characters in the Latin dialogue form. He claimed that 'the eye of a needle' referred to a small pedestrian gate in the walls of Jerusalem.
