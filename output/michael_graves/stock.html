Portland Building

Graves's most celebrated architectural work, located in Portland, Oregon. A postmodern landmark featuring small windows, a neoclassical facade with Art Deco elements, horizontal lines within a trapezoid at the top, and vertical lines along its four sides. Topped by a large copper statue of a woman with a trident (by Raymond Kaskey) on a teal pedestal. Regarded as a major work of postmodern architecture. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Michael Graves (1934–2015) was an American architect and designer. He was an original member of the New York Five (with Richard Meier, Peter Eisenman, and others), a modernist group that became prominent after a contentious MOMA meeting. In the 1970s, Graves defected from modernism to postmodernism, joining the 'Grays' (as opposed to the 'Whites'). He designed the Denver Public Library. After becoming paralyzed, he grew interested in healthcare design. He also designed a Children's Theatre Company addition in Minneapolis. Outside architecture, Graves was a product designer: Ettore Sottsass invited him into the Memphis design group after seeing his 1980 Venice Biennale contribution, for which Graves designed a dressing table modeled after an Art Deco skyscraper. He designed the famous Alessi tea kettle with a blue handle and a red bird on the spout.

Summary of Facts

Michael Graves was an American architect and product designer, best known as a leading figure of postmodern architecture. He was an original member of the New York Five — a group of modernist architects (also including Richard Meier and Peter Eisenman) that rose to prominence after a contentious MOMA meeting. The group was known as the 'Whites' in contrast to the 'Grays,' a distinction popularized by Robert Stern. In the 1970s, Graves famously defected from the Whites/modernists to the Grays/postmodernists, becoming one of postmodernism's leading practitioners.

His most celebrated building is the Portland Building (Portland Municipal Services Building) in Portland, Oregon. This postmodern landmark is known for its tiny windows, horizontal lines within a trapezoid at the top, vertical lines along its sides, and a large copper statue by Raymond Kaskey of a woman with a trident — placed on a teal platform above the entrance. The building's design incorporates Art Deco elements.

Graves designed a pair of hotels for Walt Disney World in Florida: the Swan and Dolphin hotels, named for their massive rooftop animal sculptures. The Swan Hotel features a banana-leaf-patterned facade and statues inspired by the Triton Fountain (the triton was made to smile). The Dolphin Hotel has a neoclassical facade with its columns replaced by giant statues of the Seven Dwarves. Graves also worked on Disney's New Urbanist model town, Celebration. He designed the Denver Public Library and a 2001 Children's Theatre Company addition in Minneapolis.

Beyond architecture, Graves was a prominent product designer. After seeing his contribution to the 1980 Venice Biennale, Ettore Sottsass invited him to join the Memphis design group, for which Graves designed a dressing table modeled after an Art Deco skyscraper. He is also widely known for designing the Alessi tea kettle — featuring a blue handle and a red bird on the spout. After becoming paralyzed, Graves shifted his focus toward healthcare design and produced a line of furniture for hospitals.
