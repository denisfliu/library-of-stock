The Rime of the Ancient Mariner

Coleridge's most famous poem, about a mariner who kills an albatross and brings a curse upon his ship. The crew hangs the dead albatross around his neck. The mariner encounters Death and Life-in-Death playing dice for the crew's souls. He blesses water snakes and the curse is partially lifted. The poem includes the famous line 'Water, water, every where, / Nor any drop to drink.' The wedding guest 'cannot choose but hear.' The poem was published in Lyrical Ballads (1798) with Wordsworth.

Kubla Khan

A fragment of a poem, allegedly composed during an opium-induced dream and interrupted by a 'person from Porlock.' It opens 'In Xanadu did Kubla Khan / A stately pleasure-dome decree.' It describes 'caverns measureless to man' and 'A sunny pleasure-dome with caves of ice.' The sacred river Alph runs 'through caverns measureless to man / Down to a sunless sea.' The poem ends with the image of one who has 'drunk the milk of Paradise.'

Biographia Literaria

Coleridge's major critical work, proposing the concept of 'the willing suspension of disbelief' and the 'esemplastic power' of imagination (to counter David Hartley's theories, after whom Coleridge named his son). It distinguishes between Primary and Secondary Imagination and Fancy. The first chapter prints three parodic sonnets by the alter ego Nehemiah Higgenbottom to illustrate Coleridge's appreciation for William Lisle Bowles. It contains extensive analysis of Wordsworth's poetic theory.

Conversation Poems and Other Works

Coleridge wrote a series of 'conversation poems' including 'Frost at Midnight' (which opens describing the title phenomenon's 'secret ministry' and hopes for his infant son to 'wander like a breeze / By lakes and sandy shores,' declaring 'all seasons shall be sweet'). 'Dejection: An Ode' laments his lost creative powers. 'Christabel' is an unfinished Gothic poem. He co-authored Lyrical Ballads (1798) with Wordsworth and their planned utopian community 'Pantisocracy' with Robert Southey never materialized. He was addicted to opium/laudanum. Paul Muldoon imagined Southey and Coleridge starting an American Pantisocracy in Madoc: A Mystery.

Summary of Facts

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was an English Romantic poet, literary critic, and philosopher, and one of the founders of the Romantic movement alongside William Wordsworth.

'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' is his most famous poem, about a mariner who kills an albatross and brings a curse upon his ship. The crew hangs the dead albatross around his neck. Death and Life-in-Death play dice for the crew's souls. The mariner blesses water snakes and the curse begins to lift. Famous lines include 'Water, water, every where, / Nor any drop to drink' and the wedding guest who 'cannot choose but hear.' The poem appeared in Lyrical Ballads (1798).

'Kubla Khan' opens 'In Xanadu did Kubla Khan / A stately pleasure-dome decree' and describes 'caverns measureless to man' and 'a sunless sea.' The poem was allegedly composed during an opium dream and interrupted by a 'person from Porlock.' It describes 'A sunny pleasure-dome with caves of ice' and ends with 'the milk of Paradise.'

Biographia Literaria is Coleridge's major critical work, proposing the 'willing suspension of disbelief' and the 'esemplastic power' of imagination to counter David Hartley's associationist theories (Coleridge named his son Hartley). It distinguishes Primary and Secondary Imagination from Fancy and includes parodic sonnets by the alter ego Nehemiah Higgenbottom.

'Frost at Midnight,' one of the 'conversation poems,' opens with the title phenomenon's 'secret ministry' and hopes for his infant son to 'wander like a breeze / By lakes and sandy shores,' declaring 'all seasons shall be sweet.' He planned the utopian community 'Pantisocracy' with his brother-in-law Robert Southey, which never materialized. He was addicted to opium/laudanum.
