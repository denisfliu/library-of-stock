Summary of Facts

Charles Chesnutt is an African-American author best known for The Conjure Woman, a collection of dialect stories told by Uncle Julius McAdoo, a freed Black man, to the Northerner John and his wife Annie who have moved to North Carolina. The stories mimic the sentimental, dialect-filled plantation fiction of writers like Thomas Nelson Page to depict the brutality of slavery.

"The Goophered Grapevine" has Uncle Julius warn of a "goopher" (curse) placed on a plantation vineyard by conjure woman Aunt Peggy. Henry, who eats the cursed grapes, becomes young in spring and old again in fall after rubbing sap on himself. "Po' Sandy" tells of Tenie transforming her husband into a tree, only for his body to be sawed into planks while still alive. "Dave's Neckliss" depicts a devout Christian falsely accused of stealing bacon who is forced to wear a ham, causing him to believe he is becoming a piece of meat and eventually hang himself in a smokehouse.

"The Wife of His Youth" tells of Blue Veins Society member Mr. Ryder, who plans to marry a fellow mulatto woman but meets 'Liza Jane searching for her former husband Sam Taylor. Chesnutt's early stories were published in The Atlantic Monthly. He wrote a biography of Frederick Douglass and a posthumously published novel about Paul Marchand, a "free man of color" in 1820s New Orleans.
