Summary of Facts

Magdalena Abakanowicz was a Polish sculptor and fiber artist who worked primarily in two modes: large-scale woven textile installations and figurative crowd sculptures. Her textile works, called "abakans" (named after the first six letters of her surname), are monumental woven fiber pieces with many folds that hang suspended from the ceiling, completed primarily in the 1960s and 1970s. Her sculptural works consist of large groups of rough-textured, headless humanoid figures, often made from burlap, inspired by her fear of crowds and evoking the horrors of World War II. Notable crowd sculptures include Agora (106 headless and armless figures in Chicago), Unrecognized, Androgyne, Seated Figures, and Puellae (30 figures cast from unique child models, located in the National Gallery of Art's sculpture garden in Washington, D.C.).
