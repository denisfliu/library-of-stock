Chinese author (pen name of Zhou Shuren), considered the father of modern Chinese literature. Pioneer of vernacular Chinese (baihua) writing, closely associated with the May Fourth and New Culture movements. Known for short stories collected in Call to Arms and Wandering, especially 'A Madman's Diary' (inspired by Gogol, narrator sees 'Eat People' in Confucian classics) and 'The True Story of Ah Q' (peasant who claims 'spiritual victories'). Abandoned a medical career after watching a lantern slide of a Chinese execution. Lectured on 'What Happens After Nora Leaves Home?' Coined the 'gods and demons' genre for Chinese fantasy novels. Described China as an 'iron house without windows' in the preface to Call to Arms.

Summary of Facts

Lu Xun (1881–1936), pen name of Zhou Shuren, is considered the father of modern Chinese literature. He abandoned a medical career in Japan after watching a lantern slide of a Chinese man about to be executed, deciding instead to treat the spirit of his country's people through literature. He wrote in vernacular Chinese (baihua) and was a key figure in the May Fourth Movement and the New Culture Movement. His grandfather Zhou Jiefu was involved in a bribery scandal involving the chief examiner in the 1890s. Lu Xun died of tuberculosis and was photographed by Sha Fei shortly before his death. Mao Zedong praised him highly, and the Chinese Communist Party encouraged reading his works during the Cultural Revolution.

His first and most important collection is Call to Arms (Na Han, 1922), whose famous preface describes China as an 'iron house without windows' where sleepers are fated to painlessly suffocate — Lu Xun's metaphor for his mission to awaken the Chinese people. His second collection is Wandering.

'A Madman's Diary' (Kuangren Riji) is the first major work of modern Chinese literature written in vernacular. Inspired by Nikolai Gogol's 'Diary of a Madman,' it was published in the magazine New Youth and opens Call to Arms. It uses a frame narrative in which the narrator is said to have recovered and gotten an 'official appointment.' The narrator reads a history book repeating the words 'Benevolence, Righteousness, and Morality' and sees the command 'Eat People' written between the lines, becoming convinced everyone around him — including his elder brother — is a cannibal. He fears his brother killed his sister when she was five. He remembers a crowd with 'green faces and protruding fangs' and believes a neighbor's dog gives him dirty looks because it is in on the plot. His elder brother deals with reports of a famine from Wolf Cub Village. The story ends with the plea 'Save the children...'

'The True Story of Ah Q' is a novella about a Chinese peasant, also included in Call to Arms. Its introduction debates whether to call it an official, unauthorized, or supplementary biography, and speculates the protagonist's other name might be 'Moon Pavilion.' Ah Q has ringworm scars on his head that make him deem the words 'lamp,' 'candle,' 'bright,' and 'light' taboo. After losing fights to men like Whiskers Wang and Mr. Zhao, he slaps himself in the face to turn his defeats into 'spiritual victories.' He rubs the bald head of a nun from the Convent of Quiet Self-Improvement and is cursed to 'die sonless.' He throws himself at a maid's feet yelling 'sleep with me!' and is beaten (a man yells 'turtle's egg!'); Young D takes his jobs afterward. He is forced to sell a padded quilt as a fine. He calls a man with a false pigtail the 'Imitation Foreign Devil.' He steals turnips and is chased by a dog. He is perturbed when the lice he eats make less sound than those eaten by a bewhiskered man. At the end, he is executed after the revolution; he fails to sign his confession with a perfect circle (thinking 'only idiots can make perfect circles') and fails to sing a line from an opera before his death.

'Medicine' features a father who buys a blood-soaked bread roll (mantou) as a cure for his son's tuberculosis. The blood comes from an executed revolutionary, implied to be Qiu Jin, who was beheaded in Shaoxing.

'Kong Yiji' follows an elderly scholar who failed the imperial examinations, became impoverished, and resorts to thievery while copying manuscripts. He dislikes how townspeople call a 'long bench' a 'straight bench' and has a ringworm scar. He drags himself by his hands to a tavern to order wine. It was first published in New Youth magazine.

Other stories include 'New Year's Sacrifice' (a woman tells the story of her son being eaten by wolves before dying), 'Soap' (Si-Ming buys the title object for his wife), 'Storm in a Teacup' (protagonist obsessed with Romance of the Three Kingdoms; plays on mass queue-cutting during the Xinhai Revolution), 'The Passerby' (preceded every performance of Gao Xingjian's The Bus Stop), 'In the Wine Shop,' 'Regret for the Past,' and 'Divorce.'

Lu Xun also gave a famous lecture at a Beijing women's college titled 'What Happens After Nora Leaves Home?' about Henrik Ibsen's A Doll's House, discussing economic freedom and predicting Nora would have nowhere to go. He coined the 'gods and demons' (shenmo) genre for Chinese fantasy novels in a 'Brief History' of Chinese fiction. He advocated 'worshipping Darwin and Ibsen' in essays collected in Jottings Under Lamplight. He admired Gogol and translated Dead Souls into Chinese. He started a woodcut movement in 1930s–1940s China. He also criticized China's overreliance on filial piety using the example of a man who kills his baby to feed his mother.
