Summary of Facts

Jean Genet was a gay French playwright and novelist. His most frequently clued play is The Maids, inspired by the case of Christine and Lea Papin, in which the maids Solange and Claire role-play the murder of their mistress Madame. One title character tells the other to leave her 'eternal gloves' in the kitchen, accusing her of wearing them to seduce a milkman. The two protagonists love the milkman Mario. A poisoned tea is prepared by Solange and Claire, but the woman avoids drinking it when she notices a phone is off the hook. At the climax, one character describes rising out from a woman's 'icy form' and proclaiming 'We are beautiful, drunk, joyous, and free!'

Genet's debut novel, Our Lady of the Flowers, was written while he was in prison and depicts the drag queen Divine, who shares an attic room with Darling Daintyfoot. The protagonist is beaten by her rival Mimosa II (who shares that name with Mimosa I and Mimosa the half-IV). Genet fictionalized his lovers under names like 'Seck Gorgui' and 'Darling Daintyfoot.' A character venerates twenty photos of murderers in his prison cell. Jean-Paul Sartre called the novel an 'epic of masturbation,' and the performer Harris Milstead (who starred in John Waters's Pink Flamingos) took the stage name 'Divine' after the novel's protagonist. Sartre wrote the book Saint Genet: Actor and Martyr, structured around one 'conversion' and three 'Metamorphoses' in Genet's life. Susan Sontag's Against Interpretation calls Sartre's book a 'cancer' whose 'cargo of brilliant ideas' accompanies a 'ghastly repetitiveness.'

The Balcony is set in Irma's brothel, the 'House of Illusions,' where patrons dress up as a judge, a Bishop, and a general. The prostitute Chantal leads a revolution after leaving the brothel. In The Screens, Said pursues Leila, 'the ugliest woman in town,' during what is implied to be Algeria's war of independence. The play satirizes colonizers such as Mr. Blankensee, who owns the self-proclaimed best flower garden in Africa. It is described as a five-hour, ninety-eight character play. In The Blacks, a Queen in whiteface presides over a kangaroo trial with an all-Black cast. A beflowered coffin opens to reveal an old woman whom Deodatus Village claims to have strangled.

Genet's other novel The Miracle of the Rose features the rapist Harcamone, who at the climax dreams of his heart becoming a flower. The Thief's Journal is an autobiographical work recounting Genet's time as a sex worker throughout Europe, including in Barcelona's Barrio Chino, where he encounters Salvador and the one-armed pimp Stilitano, who breaks down crying in a hall of mirrors. Sartre reads The Thief's Journal as being inspired by Genet's choice to become a thief. Un Chant d'Amour is Genet's only film, in which two prisoners fantasize about frolicking in the countryside and blow smoke into each other's mouths. Querelle was adapted into a film by Rainer Werner Fassbinder (his final film). Two of Genet's plays inspired Todd Haynes's film Poison. In a 2014 Lincoln Center staging of one of his plays by Benedict Andrews, on-stage cameras filmed Cate Blanchett and Isabelle Huppert trying on Alexander McQueen dresses. The film Little Girl Blue exposes Genet's abuse of Mona Achache's mother Carole. In Kathy Acker's Blood and Guts in High School, the character Janey meets Genet in Tangier and travels with him.
