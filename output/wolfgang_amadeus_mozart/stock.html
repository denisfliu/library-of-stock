Austrian composer (1756–1791), prodigious child who toured Europe with his father Leopold and sister Nannerl. Composed in all major genres: opera, symphony, concerto, chamber music, and sacred music. Key works include the Jupiter Symphony, Requiem (unfinished), Don Giovanni, The Marriage of Figaro, The Magic Flute, and the Clarinet Concerto. Died at 35 leaving the Requiem unfinished, completed by Franz Xaver Süssmayr.
General / Biographical

Austrian composer (1756–1791), born in Salzburg. Child prodigy who toured Europe as a pianist with his father Leopold and sister Nannerl (Maria Anna). At age six allegedly promised to marry Marie Antoinette after performing for her. At age 14 legendarily transcribed Allegri's Miserere after two hearings. Patron rivals included Antonio Salieri. Died at 35 in Vienna, leaving his Requiem unfinished; it was completed by Franz Xaver Süssmayr. Apocryphally described by Emperor Joseph II as having 'too many notes.' Summary of Facts

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756–1791) was an Austrian composer born in Salzburg, widely considered one of the greatest musical geniuses in Western history. As a child prodigy, he toured Europe extensively with his father Leopold (himself a composer and violin pedagogue) and his sister Nannerl (Maria Anna). At six he allegedly promised to marry Marie Antoinette after performing for her; at 14, he legendarily transcribed Allegri's Miserere in its entirety from just two hearings. He spent much of his adult career in Vienna, where he collaborated with the librettist Lorenzo Da Ponte on three great operas: The Marriage of Figaro, Don Giovanni, and Così fan tutte. His main rival was Antonio Salieri, memorably depicted in Peter Shaffer's play Amadeus and Milos Forman's film adaptation. He died at 35 in 1791, leaving his Requiem in D minor unfinished; it was completed by Franz Xaver Süssmayr.

His final symphony, Symphony No. 41 in C major ('Jupiter'), written in summer 1788, is his most frequently clued orchestral work. Its finale is famous for combining five themes in a culminating five-voice fugato; the first of those themes is the whole notes C–D–F–E. The first movement quotes his aria 'Un bacio di mano.' The nickname 'Jupiter' was probably given by Johann Peter Salomon. The symphony was written simultaneously with Symphony No. 39 (E-flat, which substitutes clarinets for oboes) and Symphony No. 40 (the 'Great G minor'). Elaine Sisman wrote a book-length study of the Jupiter. An earlier G minor symphony (No. 25, the 'Little G minor') opens with a Sturm und Drang first movement — G, down to D, up to E-flat, down to F-sharp — and opens the film Amadeus.

Mozart's unfinished Requiem in D minor (K. 626) is his other most frequently clued work. The Lacrimosa is the last movement partly in his own hand; Süssmayr completed the rest. The 'Confutatis maledictis' immediately precedes the Lacrimosa. The seven-measure introduction opens with bassoons playing D–C-sharp–D–E–F before the basset horns join. The Pie Jesu and Hostias movements share a fugue theme. The double-fugue Kyrie references a German arrangement of Handel's Messiah that Mozart made. His Great Mass in C minor (K. 427) is another unfinished large-scale mass: 'Et incarnatus est' is the last section he wrote; it was later reused in the cantata Davide penitente.

His last completed work is the Clarinet Concerto in A major (K. 622), written for Anton Stadler ('Notschibinitschibi'), a Freemason who played a basset clarinet with an extended range built by Theodor Lotz. Published posthumously by Johann André, who had to transpose low notes upward. The adagio dips from G-sharp to G, then reprises the rising notes A–D–F-sharp.

His piano sonatas are anchored by No. 11 in A major (K. 331), famous for the Rondo alla Turca that opens with B–A–G-sharp–A landing on C. The first-movement andante grazioso theme (dotted eighth, sixteenth, eighth, quarter, eighth) was borrowed by Max Reger for his Variations and Fugue. The 'Facile' sonata is K. 545 (opens with C–E–G above an Alberti bass). He wrote 27 piano concertos, among them the 'Jeunehomme' (for Victoire Jenamy), the 'Coronation,' and the 'Elvira Madigan'; Cuthbert Girdlestone's study categorizes their slow movements as 'galant, romance, dream, meditative, and minor.'

His operatic output includes The Marriage of Figaro (Figaro schemes with Susanna against Count Almaviva; the page Cherubino sings 'Voi che sapete'; Peter Sellars's 1988 production was set at Trump Tower), Don Giovanni (the statue of the Commendatore drags the title character to hell; Sellars staged it as a blaxploitation story in the South Bronx), The Magic Flute (singspiel with libretto by Emanuel Schikaneder, who also played Papageno; inspired Tippett's Midsummer Marriage), and Così fan tutte (Ferrando and Guglielmo disguise as Albanians; the maid Despina uses a large magnet). Works like Eine kleine Nachtmusik and A Musical Joke (a parody of incompetent composers featuring out-of-place whole-tone scales) remain among his most popular pieces. His four horn concertos were dedicated to Joseph Leutgeb; the bassoon concerto in B-flat is the most performed in the repertoire; the Concerto for Flute and Harp (K. 299) was written for a father–daughter pair he met in Paris.
