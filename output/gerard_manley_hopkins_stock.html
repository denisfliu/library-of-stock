Summary of Facts

Gerard Manley Hopkins was a Victorian English Jesuit priest and poet, best known for pioneering 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system with a stressed first syllable followed by a variable number of unstressed syllables, which he argued was closest to natural speech. He used diacritics (known as 'outrides') to mark unusual stressed syllables. His poems were not widely available in his lifetime; they were published posthumously by his lifelong friend Robert Bridges, who served as UK Poet Laureate during World War I (1913). Hopkins led off the influential Faber Book of Modern Verse despite being long dead. His reading of Duns Scotus inspired the concept of 'inscape' (the essential unique nature of a given thing) and 'instress,' which he used in his private journals. He once abandoned the poem 'A Voice from the World' (a response to Christina Rossetti's 'The Convent Threshold') due to the gap between his religious and aesthetic ideals, and his reading of Duns Scotus later helped reconcile the two.

His most frequently clued poem is 'The Windhover,' which opens 'I caught this morning morning's minion, king- / dom of daylight's dauphin, dapple-dawn-drawn Falcon.' The poem praises 'Brute beauty and valour and act, oh, air, pride, plume' and 'the achieve of, the mastery of the thing!' The final stanza ends with 'blue-bleak embers' that 'Fall, gall themselves, and gash gold-vermillion,' and the word 'Buckle' is the center of various interpretations. The speaker's 'heart in hiding stirred' for the title bird. The words 'sheer plod' are marked with diacritics in the final stanza. Erin Sebo compared the opening 'I caught' to the Old English riddle opening 'Ic seah' ('I see').

'Pied Beauty' begins 'Glory be to God for dappled things' and praises 'All things counter, original, spare, strange.' It describes 'skies of couple-colour as a brinded cow,' 'rose-moles all in stipple upon trout that swim,' and 'fresh-firecoal chestnut-falls.' It concludes with 'He fathers-forth whose beauty is past change.' The poem uses the curtal sonnet form and phrases like 'Swift, slow; sweet, sour' and 'Praise him' exemplify spondees in sprung rhythm.

'The Wreck of the Deutschland' is an ode dedicated 'To the happy memory of five Franciscan Nuns exiled by the Falk Laws' who drowned in a shipwreck near the mouth of the Thames. It opens with 'Thou mastering me / God!' Death declares 'Some find me a sword; some the flange and the rail; flame, fang, or flood.' It notes 'Nor rescue, only rocket and lightship, shone.' The ship 'drove in the dark to leeward' and 'struck-not a reef or rock / But the combs of a smother of Sand.' It uses the title to refer to both the vessel and the country from which it departed. Bridges claimed to have been 'shamefully worsted' by a 'frontal assault' on this 35-stanza poem. It ends with the tongue-twisting 'Our hearts' charity's hearth's fire, our thoughts' chivalry's throng's Lord.' The 'anvil-ding' mentioned in the poem haunted Elizabeth Bishop, who repeated the sound 'Clang' in her poem 'In the Village.' Christopher Okigbo's 'Lament of the Silent Sisters' was inspired by it. The editors of a Jesuit magazine 'dared not print' it.

The 'Terrible Sonnets' (or 'Sonnets of Desolation') are six sonnets expressing a crisis of faith and religious doubt, written during a depressive period while Hopkins was a professor in Ireland. They include 'Carrion Comfort' (where the speaker tells despair 'Not, I'll not, carrion comfort, Despair, not feast on thee' and ends 'That night, that year / Of now done darkness I wretch lay wrestling with (my God!) my God'), 'No Worst, There is None' (which asks 'Mary, mother of us, where is your relief?' and ends 'all / Life death does end and each day dies with sleep'), 'To Seem The Stranger Lies My Lot' (lamenting isolation 'at a third Remove' in Ireland), and 'Thou Art Indeed Just, Lord.' Hopkins never sent these sonnets to Bridges and described them as 'the thin gleanings of a long weary while.' His last words were reportedly 'I am so happy, I am so happy. I loved my life.'

'Binsey Poplars' begins with the note 'felled 1879' and mourns how 'aspens dear' were 'not spared, not one that dandaled a sandalled.' The poem states 'after-comers cannot guess the beauty been' and describes 'airy cages' that 'quelled or quenched in leaves the leaping sun.' Hopkins created the verb 'unselve' in this poem to demonstrate his theory of 'inscape.' It ends 'Rural scene, a rural scene / Sweet especial rural scene' and mentions 'Ten or twelve, only ten or twelve / Strokes of havoc.'

'God's Grandeur' states the title attribute 'will flame out, like shining from shook foil' and 'gathers to a greatness, like the ooze of oil.' It asks 'Why do men then now not reck his rod?' and observes 'Generations have trod, have trod, have trod; / And all is seared with trade.' Despite this, 'the last lights off the black West went' and 'morning, at the brown brink eastward, springs.'

'Duns Scotus's Oxford' describes a 'Towery city and branchy between towers,' 'Cuckoo-echoing, bell-swarmed, lark charmed, rook-racked, river-rounded,' and praises the philosopher 'who of all men most sways my spirits to peace.' It concludes with 'Mary without spot,' referencing the Immaculate Conception debate. 'Spring and Fall' is addressed to a young Margaret ('Margaret, are you grieving / Over Goldengrove unleaving?') and describes 'worlds of wanwood leafmeal,' noting 'as the heart grows colder, it will come to such sights colder.' 'As Kingfishers Catch Fire' ('As kingfishers catch fire, dragonflies draw flame') declares 'each mortal thing does one thing and the same' -- 'What I do is me: for that I came.' 'The Leaden Echo and the Golden Echo' uses a form of echo verse, with the Leaden Echo ending in 'despair, despair, despair, despair' and the Golden Echo in 'Yonder, yes yonder, yonder, / Yonder.' Hopkins also adapted the Welsh poetic technique cynghanedd, as in 'Left hand, off land, I hear the lark ascend' from 'The Sea and the Skylark.' 'Felix Randal' describes the death of a man who suffered 'some fatal four disorders.' Hopkins also wrote 'That Nature is a Heraclitean Fire and of the Comfort of The Resurrection,' which describes the title event as a 'heart's clarion' and 'world's wildfire' and states 'this... poor potsherd, patch, matchwood... is Immortal Diamond.' His few poems published in his lifetime include 'Winter with the Gulf Stream' and 'Barnfloor and Winepress.'
