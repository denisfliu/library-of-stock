Italian Renaissance/Mannerist painter from Parma (born Antonio Allegri). Known for illusionistic ceiling frescoes using di sotto in su technique. Most commonly identified by Assumption of the Virgin (Parma Cathedral), the Loves of Jupiter series (Jupiter and Io, Danae, Abduction of Ganymede, Leda and the Swan), and Camera di San Paolo frescoes. Teacher of Parmigianino. His Jupiter and Io appears in Hogarth's Marriage A-la-Mode.

Summary of Facts

Correggio (Antonio Allegri da Correggio) was an Italian Renaissance/Mannerist painter from Parma, best known for illusionistic ceiling frescoes using the di sotto in su technique. His Assumption of the Virgin in Parma Cathedral features spiraling layers of clouds that disguise the octagonal architecture, with figures appearing to jump toward the viewer. That fresco includes four saints standing in seashells that hold up the base of the structure, and Giovanni Lanfranco later paid homage with his own spiraling Assumption for Sant'Andrea della Valle in Rome.

His Loves of Jupiter series, commissioned by Federico II Gonzaga and given to Holy Roman Emperor Charles V, depicts four amorous stories from Ovid's Metamorphoses: Jupiter and Io (a stormy cloud with a humanoid face embraces a woman who tilts back her head), Danae (putti test arrows on a touchstone at the foot of a bed beneath a shower of gold), Abduction of Ganymede (an eagle carries a boy flailing his legs over a dog and stump), and Leda and the Swan. Louis d'Orleans attacked Leda with a knife and ordered the series cut up, but Charles-Antoine Coypel saved and reconstructed the pieces. Jupiter and Io appears in Hogarth's Marriage A-la-Mode (The Toilette scene).

Correggio painted Diana over a fireplace in the Camera di San Paolo (an abbess's bedroom), and his Vision of St. John on Patmos pairs gospel writers with Doctors of the Church, with its central figure visible from the choir but not from the ground. He also painted a Noli me Tangere (Christ with a gardening tool), a Rest on the Flight to Egypt (St. Francis appearing from behind a tree), and Venus and Cupid with a Satyr (satyr discovering them asleep in a forest). He was the teacher of Parmigianino and helped complete Mantegna's family chapel in Mantua. In Reynolds's portrait of David Garrick Between Tragedy and Comedy, a woman painted in the style of Correggio pulls Garrick while a woman in the style of Guido Reni pulls from the other side. The protagonist of Knut Hamsun's Hunger fails to sell an essay about Correggio.
