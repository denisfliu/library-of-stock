Japanese Nobel laureate novelist (1935-2023). Known for semi-autobiographical fiction inspired by his brain-damaged son Hikari. Most frequently clued works are A Personal Matter (about Bird coming to terms with his son's brain hernia), The Silent Cry (about brothers Mitsusaburo and Takashi in their Shikoku village), and Nip the Buds, Shoot the Kids (about reformatory boys abandoned in a plague-stricken village). Also wrote Aghwee the Sky Monster, Rouse Up O Young Men of the New Age, and the short story 'Prize Stock.'
A Personal Matter

Oe's most frequently clued novel. Semi-autobiographical story about a man nicknamed Bird who must come to terms with his newborn son, who was born with a brain hernia. Bird collects maps of Africa hoping to travel there, is fired from a cram school after showing up drunk, and decides not to go to Africa to care for his son. He considers diluting the milk to let the infant die (while listening to a broadcast about Soviet nuclear testing), makes a fool of himself at a boxing arcade game, and his friend Himiko (part white, her grandfather married a Russian from Vladivostok) helps him. The Silent Cry

Novel (also known as Football on the First Day of Man'en / Man'en Gannen no Futtoboru) about brothers Mitsusaburo (a one-eyed former English professor, narrator) and Takashi in their ancestral village in Shikoku. Takashi organizes village youth by teaching them American football, and they loot the supermarket of a Korean shopkeeper known as 'The Emperor.' Takashi reveals he slept with his mentally disabled sister, who then committed suicide. A character paints his face crimson and hangs himself (or shoots himself). Won the 1967 Tanizaki Prize. Nip the Buds, Shoot the Kids

Oe's first novel, about 15 teenage boys from a reformatory school who are evacuated to a plague-stricken village and abandoned by the villagers. They are taught by a character named Li, and the narrator falls in love with a girl who dies after contracting plague from the village dog Leo. The novel ends with the protagonist Li being chased into the forest by villagers, with his fate left ambiguous. Animals killed by the epidemic fill the village. Wikipedia

Aghwee the Sky Monster

Short story in which a 'fat baby big as a kangaroo' in a white cotton nightgown harasses the composer D, who imagines seeing the image of his dead child in the heavens. D kills himself by jumping into traffic. The narrator becomes companion to a banker's son because he needs money to buy an annotated edition of Romain Rolland's L'Ame Enchantee. Part of the collection The Day He Himself Shall Wipe My Tears Away.

Other Works

Less frequently clued works. Rouse Up O Young Men of the New Age features K tending to his son Eeyore, and looks to the poetry of William Blake. K writes the play Gulliver's Foot and the Country of the Little People and remembers almost drowning in Carp Cave. The Day He Himself Shall Wipe My Tears Away features musical imagery including a Bach Cantata sung by rebels and 'Happy Days are Here Again,' with the narrator wearing his father's goggles awaiting a cancer diagnosis. Summary of Facts

Kenzaburo Oe (1935-2023) was a Japanese Nobel Prize-winning novelist whose work is deeply shaped by the birth of his son Hikari with brain damage, a recurring motif across his fiction.

His most frequently clued novel is A Personal Matter, a semi-autobiographical story about a man nicknamed Bird whose newborn son is born with a brain hernia. Bird collects maps of Africa and dreams of traveling there, is fired from a cram school after showing up drunk, listens to a broadcast about Soviet nuclear testing while agonizing over diluting his son's milk, and makes a fool of himself at a boxing arcade game before beating up the thugs who upstaged him. His friend Himiko (part white, her grandfather married a Russian from Vladivostok) assists him. Bird ultimately decides not to go to Africa and instead cares for his son.

The Silent Cry (Man'en Gannen no Futtoboru / Football on the First Day of Man'en) follows the brothers Mitsusaburo (a one-eyed former English professor married to Natsumi) and Takashi in their ancestral village in Shikoku. Takashi organizes village youth by teaching them American football, and they loot the supermarket of a Korean shopkeeper known as 'The Emperor.' Takashi reveals he slept with his mentally disabled sister, who then committed suicide. A character paints his face crimson and hangs himself. The birth of a character based on Hikari and the crimson-faced suicide drive Mitsu's depression. The novel won the 1967 Tanizaki Prize.

Nip the Buds, Shoot the Kids is Oe's first novel, about 15 teenage boys from a reformatory school who are evacuated to a plague-stricken village and abandoned by the villagers. They are taught by Li, and the narrator falls in love with a girl who dies after contracting plague from the village dog Leo. The novel ends with the protagonist being chased into the forest by villagers, his fate left ambiguous.

Aghwee the Sky Monster is a short story in which a 'fat baby big as a kangaroo' in a white cotton nightgown appears to the suicidal composer D, who imagines seeing his dead child in the heavens and kills himself by jumping into traffic.

Other works include Rouse Up O Young Men of the New Age (featuring K tending to his son Eeyore, drawing on William Blake's poetry), The Day He Himself Shall Wipe My Tears Away (with a Bach Cantata and 'Happy Days are Here Again'), Seventeen and Death of a Political Youth (inspired by the assassination of a socialist party chairman), 'Prize Stock' (featuring Harelip who wardens a captive airman), Outcries (featuring Korean-Japanese character Takao), and Somersault (translated by Philip Gabriel). His first published work was the short story 'Lavish was the Dead.'
