Summary of Facts

John Dryden was the first English Poet Laureate, appointed under Charles II, for which he received a 'butt of Canary wine.' He was a central figure at Will's Coffee House. He wrote Astraea Redux praising Charles II as a 'happy prince' after the Restoration, despite having earlier praised Cromwell in 'Heroic Stanzas.' He is credited with originating the rule against ending sentences with prepositions.

Mac Flecknoe is a mock-epic satirizing Thomas Shadwell as the 'True-Blue Protestant Poet' who 'never deviates into sense' and is 'mature in dullness from his tender years,' 'confirm'd in full stupidity.' It opens 'All human things are subject to decay.' Flecknoe, pondering 'which of all his sons was fit to reign,' anoints Shadwell with 'a mighty mug of potent ale.' Bruce and Longville drop Shadwell through a trapdoor. Dryden advises Shadwell to retire to 'some peaceful province in acrostic land' where he can 'torture one poor word ten thousand ways.' The poem references The Virtuoso and inspired Pope's Dunciad.

Absalom and Achitophel opens 'In pious times, ere priestcraft did begin' and satirizes the Duke of Monmouth and Earl of Shaftesbury as biblical rebels against David (Charles II). George Villiers (Duke of Buckingham) is depicted as the servant Zimri. The second part, co-written with Nahum Tate, lampoons Shadwell and Settle as Og and Doeg.

The Hind and the Panther is a three-part allegorical poem in heroic couplets written after Dryden's conversion to Catholicism. The milk-white Hind represents the Catholic Church ('immortal and unchang'd') and the Panther the Anglican Church. Quakers are compared to hares, Presbyterians to wolves. James II appears as a lion protecting the Hind. A fable about Swallows convinced not to migrate ends with them dying in the cold. Dryden coined 'blaze of glory.'

All for Love adapts Antony and Cleopatra, set entirely in Alexandria. Dolabella muses 'men are but children of a larger growth.' The eunuch Alexas delivers a ruby bracelet. Serapion opens with 'portents of prodigies.' Cleopatra kills herself with a dagger rather than a snake, departing from Shakespeare. The prologue states 'errors, like straws, upon the surface flow.' Marriage a la Mode features Doralice singing 'Why should a foolish marriage vow'; Aureng-Zebe features Nourmahal's poisoning attempt; Conquest of Granada coined 'noble savage.'

Alexander's Feast ('None but the brave deserve the fair') describes Timotheus inspiring Alexander to set a palace on fire; Handel set it to music for St. Cecilia's Day. Dryden collaborated with William Davenant on The Enchanted Island (adaptation of The Tempest) and wrote the libretto for Purcell's King Arthur semi-opera (acclaimed 'Frost Scene'). Essay of Dramatick Poesie features Neander calling Shakespeare 'the greatest soul.' Annus Mirabilis chronicles the Great Fire of London. He translated Virgil's Aeneid and Georgics. He was parodied as Bayes in Villiers's The Rehearsal. T.S. Eliot blamed him (with Milton) for the 'dissociation of sensibility.'
