General / Biographical

Alan Hovhaness (born Alan Vaness Chakmakjian, 1897–2000) was an Armenian-American composer known for writing 67 symphonies and a deep obsession with mountains. His father was from Armenia. He was commissioned by Leopold Stokowski (conductor of the Philadelphia Orchestra) for Mysterious Mountain. John Cage witnessed the debut of Hovhaness's Loudaszak, which may have inspired Cage's aleatoric style. Yolanda Kondonassis premiered his 'Spirit of Trees' for guitar and harp. He also wrote 'Garden of Adonis' for harp and flute.

Other Symphonies

Hovhaness wrote 67 symphonies in total. His Symphony No. 1 ('Exile') commemorates the Armenian Genocide with movements titled 'Lament,' 'Conflict,' and 'Triumph.' Symphony No. 21 was dedicated to Vasken, the Catholicos of Etchmiadzin, and features the city's bells. Symphony No. 22 ('City of Light'), commissioned by the Birmingham Symphony (whose conductor's father-in-law conducted that orchestra), opens with five D major chords with a dissonant vibraphone F natural on the third. Symphony No. 50 ('Mount St. Helens') depicts a volcanic eruption; a solo flute bridges the opening 'dawn theme' and the eruption in the final movement, and the second movement is nicknamed 'Spirit Lake.' Symphony No. 66 is 'Hymn to Glacial Peak.' He also wrote 'To the Appalachian Mountains.'

Summary of Facts

Alan Hovhaness (born Alan Vaness Chakmakjian, 1897–2000) was an Armenian-American composer celebrated for his prolific output — most notably a set of 67 symphonies — and his obsession with mountains. His father was from Armenia. He is primarily associated with Armenian-American musical identity alongside Aram Khachaturian.

Hovhaness's best-known work is Symphony No. 2, nicknamed 'Mysterious Mountain,' which was commissioned by and premiered under Leopold Stokowski of the Philadelphia Orchestra. His first symphony, 'Exile,' commemorates the Armenian Genocide and has three movements titled 'Lament,' 'Conflict,' and 'Triumph.' Symphony No. 21 was dedicated to Vasken, the Catholicos of Etchmiadzin (the seat of the Armenian Apostolic Church), and its three movements feature the city's bells of war. Symphony No. 22, 'City of Light' (depicting 'a million lights'), was commissioned by the Birmingham Symphony and opens with five D major chords while the vibraphone plays a dissonant F natural on the third chord. Symphony No. 50, 'Mount St. Helens,' depicts a volcanic eruption: a solo flute bridges the opening 'dawn theme' and the eruption in the final movement, and the second movement is nicknamed 'Spirit Lake.' Symphony No. 66 is titled 'Hymn to Glacial Peak.' Other symphonic titles include 'To the Appalachian Mountains.'

Hovhaness also wrote the symphonic poem And God Created Great Whales (its title from the Book of Genesis), which juxtaposes actual recordings of whale songs with melodies drawn from a major pentatonic scale. Along with John Tavener's The Whale, it is considered one of the earliest works to promote saving whales. John Cage witnessed the debut of Hovhaness's Loudaszak, which may have influenced Cage's development of aleatory music. Yolanda Kondonassis premiered Hovhaness's 'Spirit of Trees' for guitar and harp; he also wrote 'Garden of Adonis' for harp and flute.
