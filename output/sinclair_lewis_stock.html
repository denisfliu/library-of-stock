Sinclair Lewis

American novelist who satirized middle-class conformity and small-town life. Known for Babbitt, Main Street, Arrowsmith, Elmer Gantry, Dodsworth, and It Can't Happen Here. Set many novels in the fictional Midwestern state of Winnemac. First American to win the Nobel Prize in Literature. Born in Sauk Centre, Minnesota.
Babbitt

A satirical novel set in the fictional city of Zenith, in the state of Winnemac, about the middle-class realtor George Babbitt. He takes pride in his 'Solid Citizen' uniform and is a member of the Booster's Club. Key figures in Zenith include coal-dealer Vergil Gunch and poet 'Chum' Frink. Babbitt's friend Paul Riesling is imprisoned for shooting his wife Zilla. He fires Stanley Graff for cheating customers. Babbitt has an extramarital affair with Tanis Judique but breaks it off when his wife Myra falls seriously ill, renewing his devotion to her. He briefly adopts a bohemian lifestyle and admires the radical politics of Seneca Doane. He blesses his son Ted's elopement with Eunice.

Main Street

A satirical novel about Carol Milford (later Carol Kennicott), who marries Dr. Will Kennicott and struggles with the claustrophobia of small-town life in Gopher Prairie, which was inspired by Lewis's birthplace of Sauk Centre, Minnesota. Carol attempts to reform the town and proposes a Community Day, but receives a lukewarm response; she calls her newborn daughter 'a bomb to blow up smugness.' The Thanatopsis Club cancels a production of Shaw's Androcles and the Lion because Raymie Witherspoon objects to its irreligious ideas. Vida Sherwin criticizes The Damnation of Theron Ware. Harassment of the Nonpartisan League sparks Carol to escape to Washington, D.C. Famously rejected for the 1921 Pulitzer Prize.

Arrowsmith

A novel about an idealistic doctor, Martin Arrowsmith, who first practices in Banjo Crossing and later in rural Wheatsylvania, North Dakota, before traveling to a Caribbean island where he administers a phage (bacteriophage) to cure an outbreak of the plague. Lewis declined the Pulitzer Prize for this novel, writing that 'every compulsion is put upon writers to become safe, polite, obedient, and sterile.'

Dodsworth

A 1929 novel about Sam Dodsworth, who tours Europe after selling the Revelation Motor Company and retiring. His wife Fran requests a divorce so she can marry the Austrian nobleman Kurt von Obersdorf, but this falls through when Kurt's mother objects. Sam meets the enticing widow Edith Cortright in Venice. Fran ends her affair with Kurt after his mother's objection. The novel was adapted into a film directed by William Wyler, who spent an entire day filming Ruth Chatterton burning a letter.

Elmer Gantry

A novel set in the fictional state of Winnemac, like several other Lewis works. Dedicated to H.L. Mencken, who covered the Scopes Monkey Trial for the Baltimore Sun. Elmer Gantry, like Babbitt and Sam Dodsworth, inhabits the same fictional Midwestern state.

Other Works and General

Lewis set several novels in the fictional state of Winnemac (sometimes spelled Winnemeac), and George Annand's pictorial map based on Lewis included many of his fictional locations. He wrote a children's story about a circus bear named Bongo. His novel Kingsblood Royal raised early awareness for the civil rights movement and features a banker who discovers he is one-sixty-fourth black, based on NAACP leader Walter White. The Man Who Knew Coolidge consists of six monologues by traveling salesman Lowell Schmaltz. Lewis won the Nobel Prize in Literature, during which he attacked the New Humanists for 'confining literature to the fight between man's soul and God.' O'Hara's character Julian English's behavior was criticized by Lewis as 'nothing but infantilism.'

Summary of Facts

Sinclair Lewis is an American novelist born in Sauk Centre, Minnesota, known for satirizing middle-class conformity and small-town American life. He set many of his novels in the fictional Midwestern state of Winnemac (sometimes Winnemeac), including Babbitt, Elmer Gantry, Arrowsmith, and Dodsworth.

Babbitt is set in the city of Zenith and follows the middle-class realtor George Babbitt, a proud member of the Booster's Club whose friends include Vergil Gunch and the poet 'Chum' Frink. His friend Paul Riesling is imprisoned for shooting his wife Zilla. Babbitt has an affair with Tanis Judique but breaks it off when his wife Myra falls ill. He briefly adopts a bohemian lifestyle, admires Seneca Doane's radical politics, and blesses his son Ted's elopement with Eunice.

Main Street follows Carol Kennicott (née Milford) in her attempts to reform Gopher Prairie, inspired by Lewis's hometown. She calls her newborn 'a bomb to blow up smugness.' The Thanatopsis Club cancels a production of Shaw's Androcles and the Lion, and harassment of the Nonpartisan League sparks Carol to escape to Washington, D.C. The novel was famously rejected for the 1921 Pulitzer Prize.

Arrowsmith follows an idealistic doctor who practices in Banjo Crossing and Wheatsylvania before traveling to a Caribbean island to administer a bacteriophage against a plague outbreak. Lewis declined the Pulitzer Prize for this novel, declaring 'every compulsion is put upon writers to become safe, polite, obedient, and sterile.'

Dodsworth (1929) follows Sam Dodsworth's European tour after retiring from the Revelation Motor Company. His wife Fran pursues Kurt von Obersdorf but is rejected by Kurt's mother. Sam meets the widow Edith Cortright in Venice. The novel was adapted into a film by William Wyler.

Elmer Gantry is set in Winnemac and was dedicated to H.L. Mencken. It Can't Happen Here (1935) depicts President Buzz Windrip's fascist 'Corpo' government, with Doremus Jessup writing editorials for The Vermont Vigilance; it was staged by the Federal Theater Project. Kingsblood Royal features a banker who discovers he is one-sixty-fourth black. The Man Who Knew Coolidge consists of six monologues by traveling salesman Lowell Schmaltz. Lewis was the first American to win the Nobel Prize in Literature, during which he attacked the New Humanists.
