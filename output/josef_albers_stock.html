Homage to the Square (1950–1976)

Series of over 1000 paintings exploring color relationships through three or four nested squares. Painted with oil on Masonite using a palette knife. Summary of Facts

Josef Albers was a German-American abstract artist and influential art educator. He is most commonly identified as the creator of the Homage to the Square series, a monumental project spanning over 25 years (1950–1976) in which he explored color relationships through paintings of three or four nested squares on Masonite, applied with a palette knife. Individual works in the series bear subtitles such as Ascending (held by the Whitney), Joy, Glow, Skyward, Somber Promise, and Earthbound. The series earned him the Metropolitan Museum's first solo exhibition for a living artist in 1971.

Albers taught the introductory Vorkurs course at the Bauhaus, where Tom Wolfe memorably described his paper-folding exercise. He met his wife, textile artist Anni Albers (née Fleischmann), at the Bauhaus glass workshop. After the Nazis closed the Bauhaus in 1933, Philip Johnson arranged for both Josef and Anni to teach at Black Mountain College in North Carolina. During their time there, they made repeated trips to Mexico to study pre-Columbian art, which inspired both the Homage to the Square series and his Variant/Adobe series.

Albers later became chair of the Department of Design at the Yale School of Fine Arts, where he wrote his hugely influential textbook Interaction of Color (1963). The book distinguishes between 'factual fact' and 'actual fact' and demonstrates the Bezold Effect. Its 50th anniversary edition included a companion app. At Yale, Albers also taught Eva Hesse. His wife Anni famously forbade him from selling any painting that used the color red, as she felt it represented love and passion.

Other notable works include the Structural Constellations (white-on-black wireframe engravings), the portfolio Formulation: Articulation (containing silkscreens of his Graphic Tectonics zinc plate artworks), and the mural Manhattan, commissioned by Walter Gropius for the Pan Am Building lobby. A font called 'Achitype Albers' was designed based on his geometric experiments. He lived in Orange, Connecticut, and was known for taking visitors to a local restaurant called the Plank House.
