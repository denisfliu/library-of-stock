Summary of Facts

Francis Picabia was a French Dadaist and Cubist painter, described as the richest member of the Dadaists. His most frequently clued work is Machine Turn Quickly, which depicts two interlocking gears labeled 'femme' (smaller) and 'homme' (larger). He created 'mechanomorph' portraits, including one of Alfred Stieglitz depicted as a bellows-style camera with a gear shift under the word 'Ideal' (titled Here, This is Stieglitz Here), published as the cover of 291 magazine. He founded the magazine 391, inspired by Stieglitz's 291 gallery; Max Goth contributed an astrological poem to its first edition.

His Cubist painting Dances at the Spring was shown at both the 1912 Section d'Or exhibition and the Armory Show. He placed a stuffed toy monkey on cardboard as a Portrait of Cezanne / Renoir / Rembrandt. His Transparencies were a late-career series of mythological works with interwoven dark outlines, painted while living in Mougins, evoking the photographic technique of double exposure. Caoutchouc (1909) is a candidate for the first abstract painting, though it 'looks a little bit like a bowl of fruit.'

Star Dancer and Her School of Dance was inspired by Stacia Napierkowska's rehearsals on a transatlantic voyage. He collaborated with Tristan Tzara on the Dadaglobe project. He co-wrote Rene Clair's film Entr'acte (accompanying the 1924 ballet Relache by Erik Satie), in which he fires a cannon alongside Satie while Duchamp plays chess with Man Ray; the film features a hearse pulled by a camel and a dancer filmed from beneath glass.

Rrose Selavy (Duchamp's alter ego) gained the extra 'r' in her name from an almost illegible signature on Picabia's Cacodylate Eye. His Fig Leaf is an Oedipus-inspired drawing of a man standing on a ball, painted over Hot Eyes using Ripolin to critique censorship. His Optophone I may be a precursor to Op Art. I See Again in Memory My Dear Udnie uses automobile parts like spark plugs to evoke genitals. He depicted 'Marie' (likely Marie Laurencin) as the blue fan belt of a car, and painted disembodied arms worshiping a cow (inspired by photographer Erwin Blumenfeld). He painted portraits of Suzy Solidor and a bare-shouldered Gertrude Stein against green mountains. His wife was Germaine Everling. Dada in America was centered in New York, home to both Picabia and Duchamp.
