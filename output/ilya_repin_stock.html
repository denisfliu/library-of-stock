Summary of Facts

Ilya Repin was the most prominent 19th-century Russian Realist painter and leader of The Wanderers (Peredvizhniki). He is most commonly identified by Barge Haulers on the Volga and Reply of the Zaporozhian Cossacks.

Barge Haulers on the Volga depicts eleven laborers struggling to pull a boat up the Volga River. Clement Greenberg famously contrasted this work with Picasso in 'Avant-Garde and Kitsch,' suggesting a peasant would prefer Repin's kitsch over Picasso's avant-garde.

Reply of the Zaporozhian Cossacks shows mustached men at a table laughing uproariously as they compose an insulting letter to Ottoman Sultan Mehmed IV. The letter insults the sultan as 'a Macedonian mechanic' and 'a nasty glob of spit.' It received a record-breaking payment in 1891.

Ivan the Terrible and His Son Ivan depicts a crazed monarch clutching his dying son, with blood on a red carpet and a cane on the floor. It was vandalized in 2018 by Igor Podporin with a metal pole because he believed it was historically inaccurate.

Religious Procession in Kursk Province shows Orthodox priests carrying a gilded icon of the Virgin Mary amid a large crowd, with a bearded man trying to strike a blond boy with a stick and a hunchbacked boy prevented from joining.

Other works include They Did Not Expect Him (a woman seeing her gaunt son return), Sadko in the Underwater Kingdom (mermaid procession), Dragonfly (his daughter on a branch), What Freedom!, and Saint Nicholas Saves Three Innocents. He painted portraits of the art collector Pavel Tretyakov and surgeon Evgeny Pavlov. He studied under Maria Fortuny and earned a cult following in the Soviet Union.
