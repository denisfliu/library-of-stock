American postmodernist author and essayist. Most commonly identified by the novel Infinite Jest (Enfield Tennis Academy, Hal Incandenza, addictive 'Entertainment' film, Quebecois Wheelchair Assassins) and essays 'A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again' (Caribbean cruise on the Nadir), 'Consider the Lobster,' and 'This Is Water' (Kenyon commencement speech). Also wrote The Pale King (unfinished, IRS in Peoria), The Broom of the System, and 'E Unibus Pluram.'

Summary of Facts

David Foster Wallace (1962–2008) is a defining figure of late-20th-century American postmodernist literature. Infinite Jest (1996) is his primary quizbowl identifier. Set at the Enfield Tennis Academy near Boston, it follows Hal Incandenza — a tennis prodigy who memorizes the OED and smokes weed in underground tunnels — whose father James created 'the Entertainment' (also called 'the samizdat'), a film so addictive that viewers lose all interest in anything else. James committed suicide by microwaving his head; the Entertainment was his attempt to cure Hal's anhedonia. The Quebecois Wheelchair Assassins (A.F.R.) seek the film; U.S.O.U.S. officer Hugh Steeply (often disguised as a woman named 'Helen') liaises with double-agent Remy Marathe. Don Gately stays at the Ennet House Drug and Alcohol Recovery House. Joelle van Dyne (the 'Prettiest Girl of All Time' / PGOAT) stars in the film and runs a radio show '60 min +/-' under the name 'Madame Psychosis' while veiling her face. Michael Pemulis is the greatest Eschaton player; Eschaton degenerates into chaos over the map-territory relation. Orin Incandenza (deformed Mario's brother, Phoenix Cardinals punter) traps cockroaches under upside-down glasses. The novel has 388 endnotes under 'Notes and Errata,' including James Incandenza's complete filmography. Chronological markers include the 'Year of the Depend Adult Undergarment' and the 'Year of Glad' (Subsidized Time). The closing scene shows Hal unable to communicate, possibly due to the drug DMZ. James Wood coined 'hysterical realism' for Infinite Jest and Zadie Smith's White Teeth.

Wallace's essays are equally important. 'A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again' describes a Caribbean cruise on a ship he renames the Nadir (from Zenith); features hypnotist Nigel Ellery and waiter Tibor. 'Consider the Lobster,' first published in Gourmet magazine, contemplates the ethics of boiling animals alive at the Maine Lobster Festival, noting that lobsters have nociceptors but cannot produce endorphins. 'This Is Water,' his 2005 Kenyon College commencement address, opens with the parable of two young fish and an older fish and argues that 'the only thing capital-T True is how you choose to think.' 'E Unibus Pluram' traces television's influence on American fiction (praising the 'complicated ironies' in DeLillo's 'most photographed barn' passage from White Noise). 'Authority and American Usage' calls lexicographer Bryan Garner — who synthesizes descriptivism and prescriptivism — a genius, using 'SNOOT' in all caps. 'Big Red Son' covers the Adult Video News Awards at Caesars Palace (described as 'as big as 20 Wal-Marts'). 'Lynch Keeps His Head' discusses David Lynch and Blue Velvet. 'Up, Simba' covers John McCain's 2000 presidential campaign for Rolling Stone (later published as a book titled for McCain's campaign bus). 'Derivative Sport in Tornado Alley' describes playing tennis in Champaign, Illinois and being blown into a fence with Gil Antitoi. A 1997 review dismissed John Updike as 'a penis with a thesaurus' and 'Champion Literary Phallocrat.' Brief Interviews with Hideous Men uses Q-and-A format and includes 'Forever Overhead' (second-person, 13th birthday at a swimming pool) and 'The Depressed Person.' 'Good Old Neon' opens with 'my whole life I've been a fraud' and ends with the narrator driving into a bridge abutment; it was written after the death of high school classmate Neal and is collected in Oblivion. His senior thesis critiqued Richard Taylor's fatalism argument about sea battles using a Montague grammar-derived 'System J.'

The Pale King (2011), his unfinished posthumous novel edited by Michael Pietsch, is set at an IRS office in Peoria, Illinois; characters include 'Irrelevant' Chris Fogle (turns pages, signs Memo 20s) and David Cusk (fear of sweating causes constant sweating). D.T. Max's biography Every Love Story is a Ghost Story chronicles Wallace's life.

The Broom of the System, his debut novel, follows Lenore Beadsman — switchboard operator at publisher Frequent and Vigorous — who owns a talking bird named Vlad the Impaler (also Ugolino the Significant) and seeks her missing grandmother who escaped a nursing home. Publisher Rick Vigorous receives stories about 'second-order vanity.' Lenore visits her brother nicknamed 'Antichrist' in Massachusetts. A character orders nine steaks to become so fat he takes up the entire universe. Stonecipheco builds a pineal-gland additive for baby food; another company builds a desert in Ohio. The title refers to a Wittgenstein riddle about whether a broom's handle or bristles are more useful. David Lipsky's Although Of Course You End Up Becoming Your Self documents the Infinite Jest tour. Wallace died by suicide in 2008.
