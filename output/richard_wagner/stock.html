German composer (1813–1883), known primarily for his operatic works and the concept of the Gesamtkunstwerk (total work of art). Major works include Tristan und Isolde, the Ring Cycle, Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg, Tannhäuser, Lohengrin, and Parsifal. Founded the Bayreuth Festival. Wrote the antisemitic essay 'Jewishness in Music.' Had a famous falling out with Friedrich Nietzsche. Influenced Bruckner and was admired by Hitler.
General / Biographical

German composer known for his concept of the Gesamtkunstwerk (total work of art), leitmotifs, and music dramas. Father-in-law of Franz Liszt (Cosima was Liszt's daughter and Wagner's wife). His son-in-law Houston Stewart Chamberlain wrote The Foundations of the Nineteenth Century. Tristan und Isolde

Music drama by Wagner, harmonically revolutionary. Famous for the 'Tristan chord' (F, B, D-sharp, G-sharp), which is introduced in the opening bars (cellos: eighth A up to long F, eighth E, long D-sharp). Ends with the soprano Isolde's 'Liebestod' (Mild und leise), in which she asks if she sees how her deceased lover glows. Debussy called it 'decidedly the finest thing I know.' Wikipedia

Der Ring des Nibelungen (The Ring Cycle)

A four-part, approximately 16-hour opera cycle: Das Rheingold, Die Walküre, Siegfried, and Götterdämmerung. Based loosely on the Volsunga saga and Nibelungenlied. Bayreuth Festival

Annual festival in Bavaria dedicated entirely to Wagner's operas, founded by Wagner himself. Premiered Das Rheingold. A 'canon' of 10 works is performed there (excluding Rienzi). After Wagner's death, run by his widow Cosima, then his admirer Winifred, who maintained correspondence with Hitler. Oksana Lyniv became the first woman to take a major conducting role there (2021). Summary of Facts

Richard Wagner (1813–1883) was a German composer who transformed opera into what he called the Gesamtkunstwerk ('total work of art') — a unified fusion of music, drama, poetry, and visual art. His ideas were developed in the essays 'Art and Revolution,' 'The Artwork of the Future,' and 'Opera and Drama.' He introduced the concept of the leitmotif (recurring musical phrase representing a character or idea), which he advocated for in 'Opera and Drama.' He was deeply influenced by the philosophy of Arthur Schopenhauer. Franz Liszt was his father-in-law (Cosima was Liszt's daughter and Wagner's wife); his son-in-law Houston Stewart Chamberlain wrote The Foundations of the Nineteenth Century, which drew on Wagner's racial theories. Wagner wrote the virulently antisemitic essay 'Jewishness in Music,' attacking Giacomo Meyerbeer and Felix Mendelssohn. He is buried at the estate 'Wahnfried' (meaning 'peace from delusion'), next to his Newfoundland dog Russ; Leonard Bernstein joked that his grave was just large enough to dance upon.

Wagner had a famous relationship with Friedrich Nietzsche, who praised him in The Birth of Tragedy (invoking the Apollonian and Dionysian balance) but later repudiated him in 'The Case of Wagner' and 'Nietzsche Contra Wagner,' calling him a 'symptom of nihilism' and a 'Cagliostro of modernity.' The falling out may have been precipitated by Parsifal's Christian themes. George Bernard Shaw wrote a Marxist interpretation of the Ring Cycle called 'The Perfect Wagnerite,' and Alex Ross wrote the 2020 book Wagnerism: Art and Politics in the Shadow of Music.

Wagner designed the 'Wagner tuba' to fill the acoustic gap between horns and trombones; Bruckner was the first to use it in a symphonic setting (his seventh symphony). Bruckner idolized Wagner, dedicated his Third Symphony to him, quoted Die Walküre and Tristan in it, and represented Wagner's death with a cymbal crash in his seventh symphony.

Tristan und Isolde is his most harmonically revolutionary work, famous for the 'Tristan chord' (F, B, D-sharp, G-sharp), introduced in the opening bars (cellos: pickup A, long F, pickup E, long D-sharp). The chord is dissonant and ambiguous, left unresolved until the climactic 'Liebestod' (Mild und leise), in which Isolde asks King Marke and Brangäne if she sees how her deceased lover glows 'softly and gently.' Debussy called it 'decidedly the finest thing I know' and humorously deployed the chord in 'Golliwog's Cakewalk' (instructed 'avec une grande émotion'). Conducting the opera was the occasion of death for both Joseph Keilberth and Felix Mottl.

The Ring Cycle (Der Ring des Nibelungen) is a four-part, approximately 16-hour cycle (Das Rheingold, Die Walküre, Siegfried, Götterdämmerung), based loosely on the Volsunga saga (omitting the Atli story). Its most famous excerpt, 'The Ride of the Valkyries,' underscores the helicopter attack on a Vietnamese village in Apocalypse Now. Leitmotifs guide plot connections; Hans von Wolzogen coined their common nicknames; the Siegfried leitmotif is a French horn call. Some leitmotifs originated from the Siegfried Idyll, written for Cosima's birthday.

Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg is Wagner's only mature comedy (over four-and-a-half hours), featuring a song contest in which the protagonist (coached by cobbler Hans Sachs) defeats Sixtus Beckmesser (whose original name 'Hanslich' was one letter off from critic Eduard Hanslick). The densely contrapuntal C-major overture combines three themes; the aria 'Wahn! Wahn! Überall Wahn!' laments a riot caused by Beckmesser's interrupted serenade. Tannhäuser features a song contest at Wartburg between the title character and Wolfram von Eschenbach, a 'Pilgrims' Chorus,' and ends with the title character's staff sprouting leaves. Its Paris premiere was disrupted by noisemakers after Wagner added a bacchanale to Act I. Lohengrin features the 'Bridal Chorus' and ends after Elsa of Brabant dies, having asked the title knight his name; Charlie Chaplin used the Lohengrin prelude in The Great Dictator for Hynkel's globe-dance and the Jewish barber's speech. Parsifal's villain Klingsor gave Debussy a name for Wagner.

Wagner founded the Bayreuth Festival in Bavaria, where his operas (except Rienzi) are staged annually. After his death, it was run by his widow Cosima, then Winifred Wagner, who maintained close ties with Hitler and handed the festival to the Nazis during WWII. The 1976 centenary production of the Ring Cycle by Patrice Chéreau was Shaw-inspired socialist realism; Frank Castorf enraged audiences in 2013 by theming the Ring around oil. Oksana Lyniv became the first woman to take a major conducting role there in 2021.
