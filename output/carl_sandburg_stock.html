American poet associated with Chicago, known for the poem 'Chicago' ('Hog Butcher for the World') and 'Fog.' Also a historian who wrote a six-volume biography of Abraham Lincoln that won the 1940 Pulitzer Prize for History.
Chicago

Sandburg's best-known poem, addressing the city as 'Hog Butcher for the World, / Tool Maker, Stacker of Wheat, / Player with Railroads and the Nation's Freight Handler.' The poem describes Chicago's 'big shoulders' and calls it 'stormy, husky, brawling.' The speaker acknowledges the city is 'wicked,' 'crooked,' and 'brutal,' having seen 'the gunman kill and go free' and 'painted women under the gas lamps,' but embraces its vitality.

Abraham Lincoln (biography)

Sandburg's six-volume biography divided into The Prairie Years and The War Years. The War Years won the 1940 Pulitzer Prize for History. It inspired Robert Sherwood's play Abe Lincoln in Illinois. Sandburg devoted much of The War Years to the congressional plot against Secretary of State William Seward.

Fog

A very short poem describing fog that 'comes on little cat feet' and 'sits looking over harbor and city on silent haunches.' One of Sandburg's most anthologized works.

Grass

A poem in which grass speaks, commanding 'Pile the bodies high at Austerlitz and Waterloo / shovel them under and let me work.' The grass covers over the dead of major battles.

Other Works

Sandburg compiled the American Songbag, a folk music collection. He wrote the introduction/prologue to The Family of Man photography exhibition. Other poetry collections include Corn Huskers, The People, Yes, and the Chicago Poems. He was associated with Sufjan Stevens's album Illinois (Illinoise!), which draws on Sandburg's poetry. He addressed a poem to evangelist Billy Sunday. 'A Father to His Son' considers whether to tell a child 'Life is hard; be steel; be a rock' or 'Life is a soft loam; be gentle.'

Summary of Facts

Carl Sandburg is an American poet and historian most associated with Chicago, which he immortalized in his poem 'Chicago' as 'Hog Butcher for the World, / Tool Maker, Stacker of Wheat' and the 'City of the Big Shoulders.' The poem acknowledges the city's wickedness but embraces its 'stormy, husky, brawling' vitality.

Sandburg's other major contribution is his six-volume biography of Abraham Lincoln, divided into The Prairie Years and The War Years, which won the 1940 Pulitzer Prize for History and inspired Robert Sherwood's play Abe Lincoln in Illinois. His short poem 'Fog' describes the phenomenon coming 'on little cat feet' and sitting 'on silent haunches.' 'Grass' commands 'Pile the bodies high at Austerlitz and Waterloo.' He compiled the American Songbag and wrote the prologue to The Family of Man photography exhibition. Sufjan Stevens's album Illinoise! draws on his poetry, with 'Carl Sandburg Visits Me in a Dream' as a track subtitle.
