Being and Time (Dasein and Fundamental Ontology)

Heidegger's 1927 magnum opus, which introduces the concept of Dasein ('being there' or 'being-in-the-world') as the form of being specific to humans. It begins with a discourse on the 'forgetting of Being' in Western thought and advocates destroying ancient ontology to recover original meanings of philosophical concepts. Key concepts include 'thrownness' (humans are 'thrown' into the world), 'care' as an orientation toward death, and 'Das Man' ('the they') which neuters the individual self. Originally dedicated to Edmund Husserl (later rescinded due to Husserl's Jewish ancestry). The work separates analysis of individual entities from existence itself.

The Hammer and Ready-to-Hand vs. Present-at-Hand

Heidegger's most famous example: using a hammer to distinguish between 'ready-to-hand' (Zuhandenheit, the practical engagement with tools in use) and 'present-at-hand' (Vorhandenheit, the theoretical contemplation of objects as detached things). When a tool breaks, it shifts from ready-to-hand to present-at-hand. This example appears in Being and Time and illustrates Heidegger's broader concept of equipment (das Zeug), where any piece of equipment traces a path through 'involvement space' to create a vast network (dubbed the 'strong systematicity condition' by a critic).

The Origin of the Work of Art

An essay in which Heidegger argues that art creates strife between 'Earth' and 'World,' beginning with an 'Ursprung' or 'primal leap.' He examines Van Gogh's painting of a pair of shoes (peasant boots), uses a Greek temple as an example of something that has lost the ability to effect the Earth-World struggle, discusses three definitions of a 'thing' (rejecting all three), and connects art to the concept of aletheia ('unconcealedness' or 'disclosure'). He reuses the notion of equipment as 'ready-to-hand' from Being and Time. The essay opens by describing the hermeneutic circle. Derrida discussed Heidegger's analysis of the Van Gogh painting in Restitutions.

Nazism, Antisemitism, and the Black Notebooks

Heidegger joined the Nazi Party in 1933 and served as rector of the University of Freiburg, where he gave an inaugural speech ('The Self-Assertion of the German University') claiming science must become 'the power that shapes the body of the German university' and that academic freedom was 'not genuine.' He allegedly cut off library access for his former Jewish mentor Edmund Husserl. His 2014 posthumously published Black Notebooks revealed further antisemitic views, including that Jews were 'worldless' and 'uprooted from Being-in-the-World.' Victor Farias wrote a controversial attack on his ideas. Emmanuel Faye published a jeremiad against him. His Nazism did not prevent his affair with Hannah Arendt, who was his student.

Connections to Eastern Philosophy and the Kyoto School

Heidegger had significant connections to Japanese philosophy. Keiji Nishitani studied Nietzsche with him for two years at Freiburg and compared Zen practice to Heidegger's definition of aletheia as 'unveiling.' Heidegger claimed D.T. Suzuki's work was 'what I have been trying to say in all my writings.' Okakura Kakuzo (author of The Book of Tea) may have inspired his concept of Dasein. He read aloud Zhuangzi's discussion of the happiness of fishes after giving 'On the Essence of Truth.' He co-translated part of the Tao Te Ching at his cabin. The Kyoto School (founded by Kitaro Nishida) drew heavily on his work.

Davos Debate with Cassirer

In 1929, Heidegger debated Ernst Cassirer (a neo-Kantian author of The Philosophy of Symbolic Forms) at Davos. Heidegger argued that freedom meant 'becoming free for the finitude of existence,' while Cassirer argued freedom was a human ideal. The debate is referenced in Allan Bloom's The Closing of the American Mind.

Summary of Facts

Martin Heidegger is a German philosopher best known as the author of Being and Time (1927), his magnum opus in which he introduced the concept of Dasein ('being there'), the form of being specific to humans. The work begins with a discourse on the 'forgetting of Being' in Western thought and advocates destroying ancient ontology to recover the original meanings of philosophical concepts. Key concepts include 'thrownness' (humans are 'thrown' into the world), 'care' (Sorge) as an orientation toward death, 'being-toward-death,' and 'Das Man' ('the they'), which neuters the individual self. Being and Time was originally dedicated to his mentor Edmund Husserl, though the dedication was later rescinded on account of Husserl's Jewish ancestry.

The most frequently clued concept from Being and Time is Heidegger's hammer example, which distinguishes between 'ready-to-hand' (Zuhandenheit) and 'present-at-hand' (Vorhandenheit). When a tool is in use, it is 'ready-to-hand' and transparent; when it breaks, it becomes 'present-at-hand,' an object of theoretical contemplation. A critic dubbed this framework the 'strong systematicity condition,' in which any equipment traces a path through 'involvement space' to create a vast network.

Heidegger's essay 'The Origin of the Work of Art' argues that art creates strife between 'Earth' and 'World,' beginning with an Ursprung or 'primal leap.' He examines Van Gogh's painting of a pair of peasant shoes, uses a Greek temple as an example of something that has lost the ability to effect the Earth-World struggle, and connects art to the concept of aletheia ('unconcealedness' or 'disclosure'). He discusses three definitions of a 'thing' but rejects all three, and opens by describing the hermeneutic circle. Jacques Derrida discussed Heidegger's Van Gogh analysis in his book Restitutions.

'The Question Concerning Technology' argues that the essence of technology is 'enframing' (Gestell), which makes the world a 'standing reserve.' It traces instrumentality back to 'fourfold causality' and argues that Aristotle's four causes do not suffice. A related discussion uses a silversmith creating a sacrificial vessel to illustrate how poesis exceeds the four causes.

Heidegger's lecture 'What is Metaphysics?' claims the fundamental question of metaphysics is 'why is there anything at all?' His quote 'the nothing itself nothings' is frequently criticized as nonsense. He defined fear in relation to particular things and angst in relation to indefinite things.

Heidegger's Nazism is a major aspect of his legacy. He joined the Nazi Party in 1933 and served as rector of the University of Freiburg, where he gave the speech 'The Self-Assertion of the German University,' claiming science must become 'the power that shapes the body of the German university' and that academic freedom was 'not genuine.' He allegedly cut off his mentor Husserl's library access. The 2014 posthumous publication of his Black Notebooks (edited by Peter Trawny) revealed further antisemitism, including his claim that Jews were 'worldless' and 'uprooted from Being-in-the-World.' Victor Farias and Emmanuel Faye both published critical books. His 1966 interview 'Only a God Can Save Us' was published posthumously a decade later. Despite all this, he had a famous affair with his student Hannah Arendt.

Heidegger had significant connections to Eastern philosophy and the Kyoto School. Keiji Nishitani studied Nietzsche with him for two years at Freiburg and compared Zen practice to his definition of aletheia as 'unveiling.' He claimed D.T. Suzuki's work was 'what I have been trying to say in all my writings.' Okakura Kakuzo may have inspired his concept of Dasein. He read Zhuangzi's discussion of the happiness of fishes after giving 'On the Essence of Truth' and co-translated part of the Tao Te Ching at his cabin.

In 1929, he debated Ernst Cassirer at Davos, arguing that freedom meant 'becoming free for the finitude of existence.' His later works discuss the 'fourfold' (earth, sky, divinities, mortals) and 'Building Dwelling Thinking.' He originated the dictum 'language speaks' and said 'language is the house of being.' Derrida borrowed his technique of sous rature (erasure). Richard Rorty praised him as 'edifying.' Adorno criticized his language in Jargon of Authenticity. Graham Harman developed object-oriented philosophy based on his work. Hubert Dreyfus's commentaries led to the portmanteau 'Dreydegger.' His student Gadamer built hermeneutics in Truth and Method on his work. His cabin at Todtnauberg is where he wrote parts of Being and Time and was later visited by Paul Celan, the subject of a poem. He responded to Sartre's 'Existentialism Is a Humanism' with his 'Letter on Humanism,' which Peter Sloterdijk later responded to in 'Rules for the Human Zoo.'
