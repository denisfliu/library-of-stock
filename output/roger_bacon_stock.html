Opus Maius

Roger Bacon's best-known work, sent to Pope Clement IV, urging a reformed curriculum covering 'natural philosophy' and 'experimental science.' It contains sections on 'The Four General Causes of Human Ignorance,' semiotics, and optics (the Perspectiva). It also contains the first written formula for gunpowder in Europe, recommending charcoal from willow trees. The work connects Bacon's interest in natural phenomena with the understanding of Scripture.

Summary of Facts

Roger Bacon is a 13th-century English Franciscan friar known as Doctor Mirabilis. His best-known work, the Opus Maius, was sent to Pope Clement IV and urges a reformed curriculum covering 'natural philosophy' and 'experimental science.' It discusses 'The Four General Causes of Human Ignorance' and contains sections on semiotics and optics (the Perspectiva). The Opus Maius also contains the first written formula for gunpowder in Europe, recommending charcoal from willow trees. His main intellectual legacy was advocating empiricism.

Bacon's work on language was also significant. His De signis expanded on Augustine's theory of linguistic signs as triadic relations, and his idea that a common grammar underlies all languages influenced the Modist school of speculative grammarians, whose most famous member was Thomas of Erfurt. His early work, the Summa Grammatica, drew on Robert Kilwardby's commentaries on Priscian. He also wrote a seven-chapter commentary on the Secretum Secretorum, a text purportedly by Aristotle for Alexander the Great. Robert Grosseteste was his teacher.

A famous legend credits Bacon with creating a brazen (brass) talking head that could answer any question. This legend was popularized in Robert Greene's Elizabethan comedy Friar Bacon and Friar Bungay, where the head's only utterances are 'TIME is,' 'TIME was,' and 'TIME is PAST' before being destroyed when the servant Miles fails to wake Bacon. Daniel Defoe mentioned the brazen head in his Journal of the Plague Year.

Bacon frequently shares clue space with the later Francis Bacon (author of Novum Organum and the 'four idols of the mind'), and questions sometimes ask for the shared surname 'Bacon.'
