Thomas Tallis (c. 1505–1585) was an English Renaissance composer and organist who served the Chapel Royal under Henry VIII, Edward VI, Mary I, and Elizabeth I. He is best known for the 40-voice motet Spem in alium, the Phrygian psalm tune 'Why Fum'th in Fight' (which inspired Vaughan Williams' Fantasia on a Theme by Thomas Tallis), and the Lamentations of Jeremiah. Along with his student William Byrd, he held a 21-year royal monopoly on polyphonic music printing.
Spem in alium

Tallis's most celebrated work — a 40-voice motet scored for eight choirs of five voices each. Its title means 'I have never put my hope in any other.' Its text is drawn from the Book of Judith and ends 'respice humilitatem nostram.' Likely written for Elizabeth I's 40th birthday; may have been commissioned by the Duke of Norfolk to outdo Alessandro Striggio's Ecce beatam lucem. The Egerton and Gresham manuscripts are its principal sources; its structure may have been inspired by the architecture of Nonsuch Palace. The length of the work may reflect the gematria value of 'Judith' and the composer's surname (both = 69). The Kronos Quartet recorded it for their album Black Angels; Peter Gregson used multitracking to perform all 40 voices on a single cello. Janet Cardiff created a contemporary sound installation based on it. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Tallis served as organist and composer for the Chapel Royal through four Tudor monarchs. His first critical biography was published in 2016 by John Harley. He was a composer of flexible religious taste, writing Catholic polyphony under Mary Tudor and Protestant psalm tunes for Parker under Elizabeth I. He also wrote 'If ye love me,' a four-part anthem with vernacular text.

Summary of Facts

Thomas Tallis (c. 1505–1585) was an English Renaissance composer and organist who served the Chapel Royal under four successive Tudor monarchs: Henry VIII, Edward VI, Mary I, and Elizabeth I. He adapted his religious style to each reign — writing elaborate Catholic polyphony under Mary Tudor, including a Christmas Mass 'Puer natus est nobis' possibly composed in anticipation of an heir, and plainer vernacular settings under Elizabeth I.

His greatest work, Spem in alium, is a 40-voice motet scored for eight separate choirs of five voices arranged in a horseshoe around the audience. The title means 'I have never put my hope in any other,' and the text is adapted from the Book of Judith, ending 'respice humilitatem nostram.' The work was likely written for Elizabeth I's 40th birthday and was likely commissioned by the Duke of Norfolk to outdo Alessandro Striggio's Ecce beatam lucem. The Egerton and Gresham manuscripts are the work's principal sources, and its structure may have been inspired by the architecture of Nonsuch Palace. The length of the work may play on the gematria value of 'Judith' and the composer's surname, both equalling 69. The Kronos Quartet recorded it for their album Black Angels, and Peter Gregson used multitracking to perform all 40 voices on a single cello. Janet Cardiff created a celebrated contemporary sound installation, placing forty speakers (each playing one voice) in the Fuentiduena Chapel and at the National Gallery of Canada's permanent Rideau Street Chapel exhibit.

Tallis is also closely associated with his Phrygian psalm tune 'Why Fum'th in Fight' (also called the 'Third Mode Melody'), one of nine tunes he wrote for Archbishop Matthew Parker's Psalter. This melody became the thematic basis of Ralph Vaughan Williams' Fantasia on a Theme by Thomas Tallis (1910), a work for double string orchestra and string quartet — scored in three sections of differing size — premiered at the 1910 Three Choirs Festival held at Gloucester Cathedral. A related tune from Parker's Psalter — the 'Tallis Canon' — is used as the hymn 'Glory to thee, my God, this night.' His early antiphon Salve intemerata, also in the Phrygian mode, later supplied material for a five-part mass of the same name.

Tallis's Lamentations of Jeremiah set the first and second Tenebrae lessons for Maundy Thursday in a distinctive ATTBB scoring; he began each verse with a Hebrew letter, and the work has been cited as evidence of his enduring Catholic sympathies. Richard Terry argued in Downside Review that the Lamentations demonstrated Tallis's lifelong allegiance to Rome. Like Gregorio Allegri, Tallis composed two settings of the Lamentations. His four-part anthem 'If ye love me' is a widely-known syllabic setting with a vernacular text from the Gospel of John. His Short Service in the Dorian Mode includes Magnificat and Nunc Dimittis sections. His motet O nata lux de lumine is notable for a striking cross-relationship (false relation) in its final cadence. He also wrote a Miserere nostri.

Tallis taught William Byrd, who later wrote the elegy 'Ye Sacred Muses' commemorating his teacher's death. In 1575, Elizabeth I granted Tallis and Byrd a joint 21-year monopoly on the printing of polyphonic music in England. They co-published the Cantiones Sacrae, a collection of 34 motets dedicated to Elizabeth I, which was a commercial failure despite the monopoly. The Tallis Scholars, an a cappella ensemble specializing in Renaissance polyphony, was founded by Peter Phillips and records on the Gimell Records label. The first critical biography of Tallis was published in 2016 by John Harley.
