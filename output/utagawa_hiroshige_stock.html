Summary of Facts

Utagawa Hiroshige was a Japanese ukiyo-e artist of the Utagawa school, considered the last great master of ukiyo-e. His most famous series is The Fifty-Three Stations of the Tokaido (1833–1834), depicting locations along the road from Edo to Kyoto, beginning at Nihonbashi Bridge.

His One Hundred Famous Views of Edo includes the iconic Sudden Shower over Shin-Ohashi Bridge (thin slanted black lines for rain; people running for cover) and Plum Park in Kameido (portrait-oriented, abstracted branches of the 'Sleeping Dragon' tree). Vincent van Gogh copied several of these prints, including Bridge in the Rain and Plum Park. The plum tree print's prominent horizontal branch and red hue also inspired Gauguin's Vision After the Sermon. The series was finished after his death by his student, who adopted his name.

Hiroshige also created Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji (like Hokusai) and hundreds of bird-and-flower prints. He was parodied by Kuniyoshi using cats with punny names. An eagle dominates a print of a snow-covered plain.
