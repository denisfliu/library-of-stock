Questions: John Greenleaf Whittier

5 tossups · 6 bonuses

Tossups

T1 2024 ACF Winter (2024) Diff 7 · Literature
The title character of a poem by this author, who inspired an 1899 Clyde Fitch play, was said to have “scratched / When she was itchy” in Ogden Nash’s poem “Taboo to Boot.” In a poem by this author, a man tells the title character, “Who touches a hair of yon… head / Dies like a dog!” A poem by this author, whose speaker describes “Forty” objects with “silver stars,” is set in “Frederick town.” This poet wrote that “of all sad words,” the saddest are “It might have been.” The title character of a poem by this author says, “Shoot, if you must, this old gray head, / But spare your country’s flag.” This author wrote “A Winter Idyl” about a family exchanging stories during a snowstorm. For 10 points, name this Quaker poet of “Maud Muller,” “Barbara Frietchie,” and Snow-Bound.
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier
T2 2024 ACF Regionals (2024) Diff 8 · Literature
In a poem based on an actual event, this author described a captain who was “tarred and feathered and carried in a cart / by the women of Marblehead.” In one of this author’s poems, a woman worn down by “child-birth pain” imagines a manly form by her side and that “joy was duty and love was law.” This author wrote that “When faith is lost, when honor dies, / The man is dead” in a poem attacking a politician. In a poem about an encounter between a judge and a country girl, this author claimed that the saddest words are “it might have been.” An old woman in a poem by this author shouts, “Shoot, if you must, this old gray head” while waving an American flag over Stonewall Jackson’s troops. For 10 points, name this 19th-century American poet of “Maud Muller” and “Barbara Frietchie.”
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier (The first poem is “Skipper Ireson’s Ride.” The third poem is “Ichabod” about Daniel Webster.)
T3 2015 Missouri Open (2015) Diff 8 · Literature
This author praised “France, the beautiful!” for a gift they gave America in his poem “The Bartholdi Statue.” The speaker of another work by this author begs the “Dear Lord and Father of Mankind” to “Forgive our foolish ways!” This author set another poem in (*) Frederick town, whose title character instructs Stonewall Jackson to “Shoot, if you must, this old gray head.” For 10 points, name this Fireside poet who wrote “The Brewing of Soma” and “Barbara Frietchie,” along with the the “Winter Idyl” “Snow-Bound.”
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier
T4 2013 VCU Closed (2013) Diff 8 · Literature
This author wrote “Life’s ‘great things,’ like the Syrian lord / our hearts can do and dare” in “The Cypress-Tree of Ceylon” In another poem, he wrote “Oh nebber you fear, if nebber you hear, / De driver blow his horn!” during the “Song of the Negro Boatmen,” part of “At Port Royal,” and he also wrote “When faith is lost, when honor dies / The man is dead!” in a poem condemning (*) Daniel Webster’s endorsement of the Fugitive Slave Act. The title character does not marry the Judge in a poem that says “Of all sad words of tongue and pen, the saddest are these: ‘It might have been!’” The call “Boys, a path!” leads to the creation of “A tunnel walled and overlaid / With dazzling crystal” in another of his poems. For 10 points, name this author of “Ichabod,” “Maud Muller,” and “Snow-Bound,” whose title character says “Shoot, if you must, this old gray head, but spare your country’s flag” in “Barbara Frietchie.”
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier
T5 2012 Penn-ance (2012) Diff 8 · Literature
The speaker of one work by this author says, “we had read / Of rare Aladdin’s wondrous cave, / And to our own his name we gave,” and then “test[s] his lamp’s supernatural powers” by “rous[ing] the prisoned brutes within” a barn. One of this author’s character says “a sweeter draught / From a fairer hand was never quaffed” after another character “filled for him her small tin cup” with spring water. One of this author’s works invites the reader to (*) “Sit with me by the homestead hearth / And stretch the hands of memory forth,” and a work about a judge who later wishes to have married the title character says, “of all sad words of tongue or pen, / The saddest are these: “It might have been!” This author wrote of a family that gathers around a fire during a storm in a work subtitled “A Winter Idyl.” For 10 points, name the poet who wrote “Maud Muller” and “Snow-Bound.”
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier

Bonuses

B1 2025 ACF Nationals (2025) Diff 9 · Literature
George Pope Morris’s most famous poem implores the title character to spare one of these objects. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this object that appears in the title of a Samuel Woodworth poem about his childhood memory of drinking from an old bucket. Emily Dickinson’s poem “I robbed the woods” ends by asking what one of these objects will say.
ANSWER: oak tree [prompt on tree] (The poems are “Woodman, Spare that Tree” and “The Old Oaken Bucket.”)
[10] Dorothea Dix and Pedro II were among the many visitors to this poet’s estate Oak Knoll. This “fireside poet” wrote the poems “Maud Muller” and Snow-Bound: A Winter Idyl.
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier
[10] This American author described a “[s]mall bat and wren” who “[h]ouse in the oak” in his ode to abolitionist W. H. Channing. The line “beauty is its own excuse for Being” appears in this author’s poem “The Rhodora.”
ANSWER: Ralph Waldo Emerson
B2 2024 NASAT (2024) Diff 8 · Literature
In this poem, Stonewall Jackson leads a group of Confederate soldiers through a city in Maryland. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this poem whose title character is described as the "bravest of all in Frederick town" while "she took up the flag the men hauled down."
ANSWER: "Barbara Frietchie"
[10] "Barbara Frietchie" is by this American poet. This poet wrote about life after the Civil War in the long poem "Snow-Bound."
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier
[10] This other American poet referenced Barbara "Fritchie" in the poem "Taboo to Boot." This poet wrote about Wee Willie Keeler who says he has "fire in my eye / and tobacco in my jaw!" in a poem about Baltimore.
ANSWER: Ogden Nash [Frederic Ogden Nash]
B3 2019 NASAT (2019) Diff 8 · Literature
A chore-girl's performance of this action that titles one of the author's “Home Ballads” involves draping black shrouds since “Mistress Mary is dead and gone.” For 10 points each:
[10] Name this action, a European folk tradition in which certain animals would be informed of births, deaths, marriages, and other events in a human family's life.
ANSWER: telling the bees [or telling of the bees]
[10] This Quaker poet of “Telling the Bees” wrote about “a universe of sky and snow” in “Snow-Bound.”
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier
[10] This author of the poem “Song of the Queen Bee” is better known as an editor who co-wrote a style guide with William Strunk and as an author of children's literature such as Charlotte's Web.
ANSWER: E (lwyn) B (rooks) White
B4 2017 ACF Regionals (2017) Diff 8 · Literature
This poem’s speaker reflects that, although “a year has gone, as the tortoise goes, / heavy and slow,” still “the same rose blows, and the same sun glows, / and the same brook sings of a year ago.” For 10 points each:
[10] Name this poem that reflects on recent changes in the speaker’s life in the presence of the title creatures. At the end of this poem, the speaker reveals that he is unable to forget a girl singing “Mistress Mary is dead and gone!”
ANSWER: “Telling the Bees”
[10] In this poem, a “dear aunt” calls up “her girlhood memories” of “huskings and the apple-bees” while sitting by the fireplace with the rest of a family, whose members tell each other stories while waiting out a winter storm.
ANSWER: “Snow-Bound: A Winter Idyl”
[10] This American “Quaker poet” of “Telling the Bees” and “Snow-Bound” also wrote about Barbara Frietchie, a Marylander who tells Stonewall Jackson to “shoot, if you must, this old gray head, but spare your country’s flag.”
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier
B5 2016 Listory (2016) Diff 9 · Literature
For 10 points each, name these things about poetry inspired by the Civil War:
[10] The line “Many a soldier’s kiss dwells on these bearded lips” concludes this poet’s “The Wound-Dresser.” Forty three of his Civil War poems are collectively called “Drum-Taps.”
ANSWER: Walt Whitman
[10] This woman from a poem by a different author says “Shoot, if you must, this old grey head, / But spare your country’s flag” after putting up the Stars and Stripes during a Confederate raid.
ANSWER: Barbara Frietchie [accept either name] [by John Greenleaf Whittier]
B6 2012 Chicago Open (2012) Diff 10 · Literature
This speaker of this poem states “Let not the land, once proud of him, insult him now.” FTPE:
[10] Name this poem which begins with the phrases “So fallen! So lost!” and mourns that “the glory from his gray hairs gone forevermore.”
ANSWER: “Ichabod”
[10] “Ichabod” was written to attack Daniel Webster by this poet, who also wrote the long work “Snow-Bound” and the Civil War work “Barbara Fritchie.”
ANSWER: John Greenleaf Whittier
[10] The hymn “Dear Lord and Father of Mankind” is taken from this Whittier poem, which alludes to a Vedic religious practice. The characters performing the title action request “Bring honey from the hollow oak, bring milky sap.”
ANSWER: “The Brewing of Soma”
