J. M. Coetzee

South African (later Australian) author, Nobel laureate. Best known for Disgrace, Waiting for the Barbarians, Life and Times of Michael K, and the Elizabeth Costello character/novels. Won the Man Booker Prize twice (for Life and Times of Michael K and Disgrace). Known for his use of 'autrebiography' (autobiography as biography of another), metafiction, and concern for animal welfare. His heteronyms/alter egos include Elizabeth Costello and the fictionalized 'John Coetzee' of the Boyhood/Youth/Summertime trilogy. The Caine Prize for African Writing is partially funded through his Nobel winnings.
Disgrace

Coetzee's most frequently clued novel. Professor David Lurie loses his job at Cape Town for sexually pressuring/seducing his student Melanie Isaacs. He believes he has 'solved the problem of sex rather well' through weekly meetings with the prostitute Soraya (the novel opens with him visiting her). After his dismissal, he moves to his daughter Lucy's farm. Three home invaders set his hair on fire and rape Lucy (his lesbian daughter). He recognizes Pollux as one of the attackers, concluding a neighbor aided the attack. He works at Bev Shaw's animal shelter, developing compassion for the dogs he euthanizes, and smashes the limbs of dead dogs so they fit in an incinerator. He had written the opera Byron in Italy. The novel ends with him saying 'Yes, I am giving him up' as he gives up an injured dog to be euthanized. David imagines Madame Bovary 'strutting before the mirror' in the early pages. Won the Man Booker Prize. Wikipedia

Elizabeth Costello / The Lives of Animals

Elizabeth Costello is a recurring fictional character and alter ego of Coetzee — an Australian author who wrote The House on Eccles Street, a feminist reimagining of Ulysses from Molly Bloom's perspective. She delivers lectures at Appleton College on 'The Lives of Animals' (based on lectures Coetzee actually delivered at Princeton). She gives an acceptance speech on the realism in Kafka's 'A Report to an Academy' upon receiving the Stowe Award. She contrasts Rilke's 'The Panther' with Ted Hughes's poems, arguing Hughes 'asks us to imagine our way into that way of moving.' She argues with Emmanuel Egudu over whether Africans favor oral literature. Her lecture 'The Problem of Evil' attacks Paul West's The Very Rich Hours of Count von Stauffenberg. Her claim about thinking her way into the existence of a bat or chimpanzee is mocked in a story by Peter Singer. She also sets up the amputee Paul Rayment on a blind date in Slow Man. A 2003 novel titled for her ends with a sequel to Hofmannsthal's 'Lord Chandos Letter' and includes her interrogation by judges at a prison camp about entry to the afterlife. Wikipedia

Waiting for the Barbarians

A 1980 novel set in an unnamed frontier fort town. The Magistrate (unnamed narrator) comes into conflict with Colonel Joll and the Third Bureau, who employ brutal torture tactics. The Magistrate has a relationship with a girl who has been blinded/tortured by the Third Bureau. He collects poplar slips with messages in a foreign alphabet, leading to suspicion he is communicating with the enemy. He returns the tortured girl to the barbarians and is accused of treason. He has a recurring dream of approaching a hooded girl building a snow castle. The title comes from a Constantine Cavafy poem. Inspired by Dino Buzzati's The Tartar Steppe. Adapted into opera by Philip Glass and a stage version by Alexander Marine. The Magistrate suggests Colonel Joll's sunglasses might cause prisoners to think he is blind. Wikipedia

Life and Times of Michael K

A novel about the cleft-lipped/harelipped gardener Michael K, who carries his mother (in a wheelbarrow/rickshaw he builds) to her birthplace in Prince Albert to bury her ashes. He works as a gardener in Cape Town before the journey. He takes refuge in the mountains after being treated like a servant by the Visagies' grandson at a farm. He is registered as 'C.M.' in a camp. A doctor bonds with him as he refuses to eat and escapes to Sea Point. Learns the Railways support Jakkalsdrif for cheap labor and prices go up when people from there shop in town. Inspired by Kleist's Michael Kohlhaas. Won the Man Booker Prize. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

J.M. Coetzee (John Maxwell Coetzee, b. 1940), South African author who later emigrated to Australia. Won the Nobel Prize in Literature and the Man Booker Prize twice. The Caine Prize for African Writing is partially funded through his Nobel winnings. Refers to autobiographies as 'autrebiographies' (biographies of others). His 'autrebiographical' trilogy -- Boyhood, Youth, and Summertime -- fictionalizes his own life: working for IBM, studying at the University of Cape Town, writing a thesis on Ford Madox Ford, publishing pseudo-poems from an Atlas Computer using The Heights of Macchu Picchu. In Summertime, after his death, Julia and acquaintances describe him as 'not built for love' to biographer Mr. Vincent. David Attwell's interviews appear in Doubling the Point. Correspondence with Paul Auster in Here and Now. Wrote about Robert Walser's 'genius' and his 'pencil system' / microscripts. Discussed Olive Schreiner's farm novel genre in White Writing. Drew on Dostoevsky's The Possessed for The Master of Petersburg. His first novel Dusklands features Eugene Dawn stabbing his son while working on 'The Vietnam Project.' Second novel In the Heart of the Country follows sheep farmer's daughter Magda's revenge fantasies. Also wrote Diary of a Bad Year (essays interspersed with letters and narrations by Senor C. and secretary Anya) and The Childhood of Jesus trilogy. The novella The Pole follows a Spanish woman discovering love poems from an elderly pianist. Also wrote a novella about the 'lives of animals.'

Summary of Facts

J. M. Coetzee (John Maxwell Coetzee, b. 1940) is a South African-born (later Australian) author and Nobel laureate, asked about primarily through his novel Disgrace, his recurring character Elizabeth Costello, and the novels Waiting for the Barbarians and Life and Times of Michael K.

Disgrace is by far the most clued work. Professor David Lurie loses his Cape Town position for sexually pressuring his student Melanie Isaacs. He believes he has 'solved the problem of sex rather well' through weekly meetings with the prostitute Soraya. After his dismissal, he moves to his daughter Lucy's farm, where three home invaders set his hair on fire and rape Lucy. He later recognizes one attacker, Pollux. He develops compassion for dogs while working at Bev Shaw's animal shelter, smashing dead dogs' limbs so they fit in an incinerator. He had written the opera Byron in Italy. The novel ends with Lurie saying 'Yes, I am giving him up' as he euthanizes a dog.

Elizabeth Costello is a fictional Australian author and Coetzee's alter ego who wrote The House on Eccles Street, a feminist retelling of Ulysses from Molly Bloom's perspective. She delivers lectures at Appleton College on 'The Lives of Animals,' based on lectures Coetzee gave at Princeton. She gives a speech on Kafka's 'A Report to an Academy' upon receiving the Stowe Award, contrasts Rilke's 'The Panther' with Ted Hughes's poems, attacks Paul West's The Very Rich Hours of Count von Stauffenberg in a lecture on 'The Problem of Evil,' and sets up the amputee Paul Rayment on a blind date in Slow Man. Peter Singer mocked her claim about thinking her way into the existence of a bat.

Waiting for the Barbarians (1980) follows an unnamed Magistrate in a frontier town who clashes with Colonel Joll and the Third Bureau over their brutal torture. He has a relationship with a girl blinded by the Bureau, collects poplar slips suspected of enemy communication, returns the girl to the barbarians and is accused of treason. He has a recurring dream of a hooded girl building a snow castle. The title comes from a Cavafy poem. Philip Glass adapted it into an opera.

Life and Times of Michael K follows a cleft-lipped gardener who carries his mother's ashes from Cape Town to Prince Albert in a wheelbarrow. He takes refuge in mountains after mistreatment by the Visagies' grandson, is registered as 'C.M.' in a camp, refuses to eat, and escapes to Sea Point. Inspired by Kleist's Michael Kohlhaas.

Foe retells Robinson Crusoe from the perspective of Susan Barton, marooned with Cruso and his tongueless slave Friday.

Coetzee's 'autrebiographical' trilogy (Boyhood, Youth, Summertime) fictionalizes his own life: studying at Cape Town, writing a thesis on Ford Madox Ford, working for IBM, publishing pseudo-poems from an Atlas Computer. In Summertime, biographer Mr. Vincent interviews Julia and others after 'John Coetzee's' death. His first novel Dusklands features Eugene Dawn stabbing his son during 'The Vietnam Project.' He won the Man Booker Prize twice. His correspondence with Paul Auster appears in Here and Now. He wrote about Robert Walser's 'genius' and discussed the South African 'farm novel' genre in White Writing. The Caine Prize is partly funded through his Nobel winnings.
