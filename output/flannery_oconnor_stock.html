A Good Man Is Hard to Find

O'Connor's most famous short story and the title story of her first collection. A family road trip goes horribly wrong when the Grandmother's mistaken memory of a house's location leads them off the main road. After a car crash caused by the cat Pitty Sing, the family encounters the escaped convict known as The Misfit. The Grandmother's final gesture of grace — touching the Misfit and saying 'you're one of my own children' — is met with three gunshots. The story also features a stop at Red Sammy Butts's restaurant, where the family eats in Tennessee, and the children John Wesley and June Star.

Everything That Rises Must Converge

The title story of O'Connor's second collection, exploring racial tensions in the segregated South. Julian accompanies his racist mother on a bus ride to her YMCA reducing classes. His mother wears a distinctive purple hat and proudly mentions her grandfather owned 200 slaves. When a Black woman boards wearing the same hat, the mother condescendingly tries to give the woman's child a penny, prompting the woman to strike her with her purse. Julian's mother collapses from a stroke and dies, devastating Julian despite his pretense of moral superiority.

Wise Blood

O'Connor's first novel, set in Taulkinham, Tennessee. The veteran Hazel Motes founds the 'Church Without Christ,' where 'the blind don't see and the lame don't walk.' He encounters the street preacher Asa Hawks, who pretends to be blind (having faked blinding himself with quicklime), and Hawks's nymphomaniac daughter Sabbath Lily. Enoch Emery steals a shriveled mummy from a museum to serve as the 'new Jesus' and later murders the actor playing Gonga the Gorilla and steals his suit. Hazel blinds himself with quicklime and practices self-mortification — walking with stones and glass in his shoes and barbed wire around his chest — until his landlady discovers this.

The Violent Bear It Away

O'Connor's second novel about the teenager Francis Marion Tarwater (named after the Swamp Fox), who is kidnapped and raised by his great-uncle Mason to become a prophet. Mason wants Tarwater to baptize the mentally disabled boy Bishop, son of the atheist schoolteacher Rayber. When Mason dies, Tarwater gets drunk instead of burying him and flees to the city. On a fishing trip, Tarwater simultaneously drowns and baptizes Bishop. The novel ends with Tarwater being raped and abandoned in a forest. Rayber depends on a hearing aid, which old Tarwater once caused by shooting him.

General / Biographical

O'Connor is identified as a Southern Gothic author, extremely Catholic, who lived with over 40 peacocks on her estate Andalusia in Milledgeville, Georgia. Her essay 'The King of the Birds' describes her passion for ranching peacocks. She reworked her Iowa Writers' Workshop thesis story 'The Geranium' into 'Judgment Day.' Her essay collection Mystery and Manners contains 'The Fiction Writer and His Country' and lectures on her work. She is sometimes called by her full name Mary Flannery O'Connor. Critics grapple with the racism in her work alongside its Catholic themes of grace.

Summary of Facts

Flannery O'Connor is one of the most frequently clued American short story writers in quizbowl, identified consistently as a Southern Gothic author whose Catholic faith profoundly shaped her fiction. She lived on her estate Andalusia in Milledgeville, Georgia, where she famously kept over 40 peacocks, a passion documented in her essay 'The King of the Birds.' Her full name was Mary Flannery O'Connor.

Her most iconic story, 'A Good Man Is Hard to Find,' is clued with extraordinary frequency. The key moments include the family's stop at Red Sammy Butts's restaurant, the car crash caused by the cat Pitty Sing, and the climactic encounter with The Misfit, who kills the entire family after the Grandmother touches him and says 'you're one of my babies, you're one of my own children.' This moment of grace is central to O'Connor's theological vision.

'Everything That Rises Must Converge' is nearly as prominent, centering on Julian and his racist mother's bus ride to the YMCA. The mother's purple hat — identical to one worn by a Black woman — and her condescending offer of a penny to a Black child precipitate her fatal stroke. The story explores racial tension and moral blindness in the segregated South.

'Good Country People' features the philosophy PhD Hulga (born Joy) and the Bible salesman Manley Pointer, whose hollowed-out Bible contains whiskey and pornographic cards. He seduces Hulga in a barn loft and steals her wooden leg, a devastating act of violation.

O'Connor wrote two novels. Wise Blood follows the veteran Hazel Motes in Taulkinham as he founds the 'Church Without Christ.' Key details include Enoch Emery stealing a mummy for the 'new Jesus,' killing the Gonga the Gorilla actor, Asa Hawks faking blindness, and Motes's eventual self-blinding. The Violent Bear It Away concerns Francis Marion Tarwater, raised by his great-uncle Mason to be a prophet. The novel's central act is Tarwater's simultaneous drowning and baptism of the mentally disabled boy Bishop.

Other frequently clued stories include 'Revelation,' where Mary Grace calls Mrs. Turpin a 'wart hog from hell' in a waiting room, prompting a vision of a heavenly procession; 'The Life You Save May Be Your Own,' where one-armed Tom Shiftlet abandons deaf Lucynell Crater at a restaurant; 'Greenleaf,' where Mrs. May is gored by a bull after crying 'Jesus stab me in the heart'; 'The Lame Shall Enter First,' where Sheppard's neglect drives Norton to suicide; and 'Parker's Back,' where O. E. Parker's tattoo of Christ earns him a beating from his iconoclastic wife Sarah Ruth.

O'Connor's critical and personal essays also appear in clues. She reworked her Iowa Writers' Workshop thesis 'The Geranium' into 'Judgment Day.' Her essay collection Mystery and Manners contains reflections on her craft, including her comparison of herself to Dr. Johnson's blind housekeeper. Contemporary critics grapple with racism in her work alongside its Catholic themes of grace and redemption.
