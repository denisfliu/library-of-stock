Summary of Facts

John Everett Millais was an English painter and co-founder of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood, alongside Dante Gabriel Rossetti and William Holman Hunt. He is best known for Ophelia (1851-52), depicting Shakespeare's character floating in a stream surrounded by brightly colored flowers before drowning, with Elizabeth Siddal posing in a cold bathtub as the model (her father later requested payment for medical bills). The landscape was inspired by the Hogsmill River.

His other signature work, Christ in the House of His Parents (1849-50), controversially depicted Jesus as a red-headed boy in a lower-class carpentry shop with wood shavings on the floor, holding up a bleeding hand and kissing his mother, with a dove on a ladder representing the Holy Spirit. Charles Dickens criticized the painting, while John Ruskin defended it. William Holman Hunt created a companion piece.

Millais married Effie Gray after her unconsummated marriage to Ruskin, and she posed for The Order of Release (a woman handing papers to a guard while her wounded husband with an arm in a sling embraces her and a dog jumps up) and Peace Concluded (two girls play with toy animals as their father reads of the Crimean War's end). Other works include Bubbles (sold to Pears Soap, commercializing the Homo Bulla motif), The North-West Passage (portrait of Trelawny as a sailor, who was outraged by the inclusion of grog), The Boyhood of Raleigh (deconstructed on Talk Talk's album cover), The Ruling Passion (ornithologist with a stuffed bird of paradise), Sir Isumbras at the Ford (parodied by Frederic Sandys), The Eve of St. Agnes, Pizarro Seizing the Inca of Peru, and portraits of Cardinal Newman and the Princes in the Tower. The Brotherhood published in the periodical The Germ, and Millais frequently hired Fanny Eaton, daughter of a Jamaican slave, as a model.
