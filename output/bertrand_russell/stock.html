On Denoting & Theory of Definite Descriptions

Russell's landmark 1905 essay in the philosophy of language. It introduces the theory of definite descriptions, distinguishing phrases that describe one and only one object ('the father of Charles II was executed') from indefinite descriptions. The essay famously analyzes 'the present king of France is bald' — a phrase that describes a non-existent entity — and argues against Alexius Meinong's theory that non-existent objects can 'subsist.' It also considers the sentence about 'George IV wanting to know whether Scott was the author of Waverley.' P.F. Strawson criticized the essay in 'On Referring,' leading Russell to write 'Mr. Strawson on Referring.'

Principia Mathematica & Russell's Paradox

Russell co-authored the three-volume Principia Mathematica (1910-1913) with Alfred North Whitehead, axiomatically constructing set theory and presenting a ramified theory of types. The work famously proves 1+1=2 and remarks 'the above proposition is occasionally useful.' Russell's paradox concerns the set of all sets that do not contain themselves, discovered when Russell found an inconsistency in Frege's Basic Law V. The paradox was resolved by Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory. Godel's incompleteness theorems are considered the nail in the coffin of the Principia's program.

Russell's Teapot & Why I Am Not a Christian

Russell formulated the teapot argument — that the burden of proof lies on the claimant, not the skeptic — by imagining a teapot too small to see floating in space between Earth and Mars. The full argument appears in his book Why I Am Not a Christian, which includes a talk given at the National Secular Society criticizing the 'first cause' argument and arguing that Christ was not the 'best and wisest of men.'

A History of Western Philosophy

Russell's 1945 survey of philosophy from the ancient Greeks through the modern era, divided into 'Ancient Philosophy,' 'Catholic Philosophy,' and 'Modern Philosophy.' It criticizes Aquinas for finding arguments for conclusions already drawn, dismisses Aristotelian notions of 'essence' and 'substance,' and attacks Bergson as anti-rational. Russell credits the book with helping him win the 1950 Nobel Prize in Literature.

Neutral Monism & Logical Atomism

Russell's metaphysical positions. Logical atomism holds that the world is composed of 'simples' and that compound propositions ('molecular') consist of multiple 'atomic' propositions. Neutral monism holds that there is a single existing substance that can be viewed as mental or physical depending on context — illustrated by the postal worker analogy (sorting letters alphabetically = mental, geographically = physical). Russell's The Analysis of Mind introduced neutral monism, rejecting dualism.

Anti-War Activism & Political Writings

Russell was a prominent anti-war activist. He organized the Russell-Einstein Manifesto (1955) condemning nuclear weapons, and with Jean-Paul Sartre led a tribunal that found the US guilty of war crimes in Vietnam. He advocated a four-hour workday in 'In Praise of Idleness' and supported guild socialism. His opposition to American entry into World War I resulted in his dismissal from Trinity College. He founded the Beacon Hill School with his second wife Dora and wrote On Education.

The Problems of Philosophy & Other Works

Russell's 1912 introductory text discusses appearance vs. reality (using a table example), knowledge by acquaintance, and argued against coherence theory. He also wrote The ABC of Atoms, The ABC of Relativity, Why Men Fight, and Proposed Roads to Freedom. Ray Monk wrote his biography (The Spirit of Solitude / The Ghost of Madness).

Summary of Facts

Bertrand Russell is one of the most heavily clued philosophers in quizbowl, with major contributions spanning logic, philosophy of language, epistemology, and political activism. His 1905 essay 'On Denoting' introduces the theory of definite descriptions, analyzing 'the present king of France is bald' and arguing against Meinong's theory of non-existent objects. P.F. Strawson's critique in 'On Referring' is a frequent follow-up clue.

With Alfred North Whitehead, Russell co-authored Principia Mathematica, which axiomatically constructs set theory and presents a ramified theory of types. Russell's paradox — concerning the set of all sets that do not contain themselves — was discovered when he found an inconsistency in Frege's Basic Law V. The paradox motivated Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory.

Russell formulated the 'teapot' argument for atheism, imagining a teapot too small to see floating in space. Why I Am Not a Christian collects his arguments against theism. A History of Western Philosophy (1945), divided into three sections, criticizes Aquinas and Bergson and helped Russell win the 1950 Nobel Prize in Literature.

His metaphysical positions include logical atomism (atomic and molecular propositions) and neutral monism (a single substance viewed as mental or physical, illustrated by the postal worker analogy). He advocated political causes throughout his life: the Russell-Einstein Manifesto condemned nuclear weapons, he led a tribunal with Sartre finding the US guilty of Vietnam war crimes, advocated a four-hour workday in 'In Praise of Idleness,' and was dismissed from Trinity College for opposing WWI.
