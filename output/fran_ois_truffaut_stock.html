French New Wave director and film critic; one of the founders of auteur theory, writing for Cahiers du Cinéma. Called 'The Gravedigger of French Cinema' for his ruthless criticism. Best known for The 400 Blows (1959) and Jules and Jim (1962). Also the director of Fahrenheit 451, Day for Night, and The Green Room. Wrote a landmark book-length interview with Alfred Hitchcock. Cameo appearance in Close Encounters of the Third Kind as a scientist.
The 400 Blows (Les Quatre Cents Coups, 1959)

Truffaut's debut feature and semi-autobiographical film. Follows Antoine Doinel (Jean-Pierre Léaud), a troubled schoolboy in Paris. Antoine's shrine to Balzac catches fire, leading to plagiarism accusations. He steals a typewriter and is sent to a juvenile detention center. The film ends on a celebrated freeze-frame of Antoine's face as he runs to the sea. Dedicated to André Bazin's memory. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

François Truffaut was a central figure of the French New Wave (Nouvelle Vague), active both as a film critic and director. As a critic at Cahiers du Cinéma — co-founded by his mentor André Bazin — Truffaut was notorious for his brutal writing and was nicknamed 'The Gravedigger of French Cinema.' His 1954 essay 'A Certain Tendency of the French Cinema' savaged what he saw as a soulless 'quality tradition' in French film (destroying the careers of the screenwriting duo known by the portmanteau Aurenchebost), and he became the first critic to use the word 'auteur' in a film-theoretic sense. He later published a landmark 1966 book-length interview with Alfred Hitchcock, compiled over eight days with the help of former spy Helen Grace Scott, which is widely credited with cementing Hitchcock's reputation as a technical master rather than merely a commercial entertainer. Andrew Sarris later elaborated on Truffaut's auteur concept in English.

Truffaut's debut feature The 400 Blows (Les Quatre Cents Coups, 1959) is dedicated to André Bazin's memory and is considered one of the founding works of the French New Wave alongside Alain Resnais's Hiroshima, mon amour. The film follows the semi-autobiographical Antoine Doinel (played by Jean-Pierre Léaud), who burns down his shrine to Balzac and is accused of plagiarism by a character called 'Sourpuss,' then steals a typewriter and is sent to a juvenile detention center. The film ends on one of cinema's most celebrated shots: a freeze-frame of Antoine's face as he runs toward the sea. Truffaut himself had a cameo in the film. The film also includes an improvised interrogation scene where Antoine is questioned about trying to return the stolen typewriter, and a shot of him flipping upside-down on a carnival centrifuge/Gravitron.

Antoine Doinel appears in four more Truffaut films. In Stolen Kisses, he works at a shoe store as a cover for private detective work; Christine Darbon becomes his love interest. In Bed and Board he marries Christine (a violin instructor) and has an affair with a Japanese woman named Kyoko. In a short film Antoine manufactures vinyl records and falls in love at a Berlioz recital. Stolen Kisses opens with a pan to a 'closed' sign on the Cinémathèque française — Truffaut was actively involved in the 1968 protests against the attempted firing of its director Henri Langlois.

Jules and Jim (1962) follows two writers — Jules (Austrian) and Jim (French) — who both fall in love with the temperamental Catherine. A character sings 'La Marseillaise' to disprove having an accent, which fades into a WWI montage. Catherine jumps into a river after feeling excluded from her companions' discussion of Strindberg. Fahrenheit 451 (1966) is Truffaut's only English-language feature; it stars Oskar Werner as a fireman who burns books, and notably omits Bradbury's mechanical hound. Day for Night (La Nuit américaine, 1973) is a film about the making of a film ('Meet Pamela') in which Truffaut plays the director; it features a recurring dream sequence of a boy stealing Citizen Kane promotional material, and opens with a tracking shot ending in a slap followed by Truffaut calling cut. Shoot the Piano Player (1960), Truffaut's follow-up to The 400 Blows, stars pop singer Charles Aznavour as Eddie Saroyan/Charlie, a pianist pursued by gangsters; it includes karaoke-style captions for a Boby Lapointe song about 'Raspberry.' The Green Room (1978) is an adaptation of Henry James's 'The Altar of the Dead' and several other James stories, in which Truffaut cast himself as a WWI veteran who obsesses over a shrine to his dead wife and lights candles for deceased friends in a burned-out chapel — an homage to Bazin. The Last Metro (1980) is set during the Nazi occupation of France, its title referring to the consequence of the curfew. Truffaut also played a scientist in Steven Spielberg's Close Encounters of the Third Kind, using hand signs to convey the notes D, E, C, C, G to an extraterrestrial. Along with Godard, he called for the cancellation of the 1968 Cannes Film Festival during the May '68 protests. The original Bonnie and Clyde screenplay (written by David Newman and Robert Benton, inspired by Breathless) was initially offered to Truffaut.
