Legendary Japanese film director (1910–1998), often called 'the Emperor' of cinema. Best known for Seven Samurai, Rashomon, Ran, Ikiru, High and Low, and Yojimbo. A defining figure of Golden Age Japanese cinema alongside Yasujiro Ozu and Kenji Mizoguchi. Long-term collaborator with actor Toshiro Mifune. His work heavily influenced Western cinema, especially via George Lucas and Sergio Leone.

General / Biographical

Akira Kurosawa (1910–1998) was a Japanese filmmaker considered one of the three great directors of Golden Age Japanese cinema, alongside Yasujiro Ozu and Kenji Mizoguchi. He had a 16-film collaboration with actor Toshiro Mifune. He wrote the memoir Something Like an Autobiography. His films profoundly influenced Western cinema, particularly George Lucas and Sergio Leone. Kurosawa is known for his trademark use of horizontal wipe transitions. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Akira Kurosawa was a Japanese film director whose career spanned from the 1940s through the 1990s. He is considered one of the three great directors of the Golden Age of Japanese cinema, alongside Yasujiro Ozu and Kenji Mizoguchi. His most frequently clued film is Seven Samurai (1954), in which a group of peasants hire seven samurai to defend their village from bandits; the film ends with two samurai dying to musket fire in a rainy battle as a survivor remarks 'The farmers have won, not us.' The Magnificent Seven (1960) by John Sturges was a Western remake starring Yul Brynner. George Lucas adapted the two bickering peasant framing characters from Seven Samurai into C-3PO and R2-D2 for Star Wars.

Kurosawa's Rashomon (1950) presents multiple contradictory testimonies about a samurai's murder and his wife's rape; it ends with a woodcutter taking in an abandoned baby. Toshiro Mifune played the bandit Tajomaru. This film is said to be the first to aim the camera directly at the sun. Ran (1985) adapts Shakespeare's King Lear: warlord Hidetora Ichimonji divides his kingdom among three sons with disastrous results. The blind peasant Tsurumaru is the only one to escape the carnage; the film ends with a blood-orange sky behind a blind man on ruins. The court jester Kyoami appears. Toru Takemitsu's score includes 'Flute of Darkness' and 'Hell's Picture Scroll.'

Ikiru (1952) follows a bureaucrat secretly diagnosed with stomach cancer (told he has a 'mild ulcer') who devotes his remaining months to building a playground in a slum. Inspired by Tolstoy's The Death of Ivan Ilyich, the film ends with the protagonist singing 'The Gondola Song' on a snowy swing set. Kurosawa's trademark horizontal wipe transitions appear in Ikiru, linking bureaucrats who shuffle women between departments. High and Low (1963), adapted from Ed McBain's King's Ransom, stars Toshiro Mifune as shoe executive Kingo Gondo, whose chauffeur's child is accidentally kidnapped instead of Gondo's own son. The film's climax features a rigged briefcase thrown from a bathroom window during a train sequence; a single shot of pink smoke is the only color in the otherwise black-and-white finale.

Yojimbo (1961) features a bodyguard pitting two criminal families against each other, inspired by Dashiell Hammett's Red Harvest. Sergio Leone remade it without permission as A Fistful of Dollars; Kurosawa sued and called it 'a fine movie, but it was my movie.' Django (1966) starring Franco Nero was another unauthorized remake. Sanjuro (1962) was the sequel to Yojimbo. Throne of Blood (1957) adapts Macbeth for feudal Japan, with Mifune in the lead. Drunken Angel (1948) was Kurosawa's first of sixteen collaborations with Mifune; an alcoholic doctor bonds with a yakuza gangster who has tuberculosis, and Kurosawa used the score to evade censors. Kagemusha (1980) won the Palme d'Or and shows a thief impersonating the deceased warlord Shingen; George Lucas helped fund the film after Star Wars' success. Dreams (1990) is an anthology film including 'Crows,' in which an art student hears Chopin's Raindrop Prelude and enters Van Gogh's Wheatfield with Crows, meeting a Martin Scorsese-played Van Gogh. Dersu Uzala (1975), Kurosawa's only 70mm film, was made for the Soviet Mosfilm studio and follows Siberian hunter Dersu Uzala. Hidden Fortress (1958) uses two bickering peasants as framing characters, directly adapted by Lucas.

Kurosawa wrote the memoir Something Like an Autobiography, whose opening chapter traces a bathtub sense-memory that influenced the Dreams segment, and another chapter describes his crew's failed attempt to film on Mt. Fuji's summit. He was also the first choice to direct the 1964 Tokyo Olympics documentary but was replaced by Kon Ichikawa after a dispute with producers. His crime drama Stray Dog featured a character named Murakami. Lucas described Kurosawa's set design philosophy with the term 'immaculate reality.'
