Nasrid palace complex in Granada, Spain named for the red clay used to build it ('the Red Fortress'). Prime example of Moorish / Islamic architecture featuring the Court of the Lions (twelve marble lion fountain), Court of the Myrtles (rectangular reflecting pool), muqarnas stalactite vaulting, and elaborate geometric tile patterns that inspired M.C. Escher. Adjacent to the Generalife gardens.
Court of the Lions (Patio de los Leones)

The most famous courtyard of the Alhambra, built by the Nasrid dynasty. Features four projecting pavilions facing each other in front of a basin resting on twelve marble lion statues. The basin may have been taken from an earlier palace. Surrounded by arcaded galleries. Adjacent rooms include the Hall of the Two Sisters and the Hall of the Abencerrages, which have exceptionally elaborate Muqarnas domes across from each other. Archways and the Hall of Kings within the Palace of Lions are dominated by elaborate muqarnas. The Court also connects to the Hall of the Ambassadors.

Court of the Myrtles (Patio de los Arrayanes)

Muqarnas and Geometric Decoration

The Alhambra contains the most intricately patterned muqarnas (honeycomb-shaped stalactite vaulting) in Islamic architecture. These consist of small cells called alveoles and are used to decorate domes and mihrabs. The decorations uniquely use all seventeen wallpaper groups; the tessellated geometric tile patterns inspired M.C. Escher, who visited in 1936 and copied the tessellations. Niches in the complex once held large blue-and-gold vases unique in the region for being purely decorative. Owen Jones made chromolithographs of the wall decorations that influenced his Crystal Palace interiors.

General / Architectural Identity

The Alhambra is a Nasrid palace complex in Granada, Spain. Its name derives from the Arabic for 'the Red One' (al-Hamra), referring to the red clay used to build it. It was built for the Muslim Nasrid rulers of al-Andalus and overlooks the city of Granada. After the Reconquista, it inspired the fusion Mudejar style. The Duke of Wellington planted English elms in the complex during the Peninsular War. Charles V later built a palace within it. Marie Menken's 1961 experimental short film was shot in the complex. Washington Irving lived in and wrote Tales from the Alhambra (1832) here.

Recuerdos de la Alhambra (Francisco Tárrega)

Guitar tremolo etude by Francisco Tárrega inspired by a visit to Granada. The 'i,' 'm,' and 'a' fingers play a melody entirely tremolo while the thumb plays a counter-melody. Often performed by classical guitarists as a virtuoso showpiece.

Summary of Facts

The Alhambra is a Nasrid palace complex in Granada, Spain, whose name derives from the Arabic al-Hamra, meaning 'the Red One,' a reference to the red clay used to construct it. Built for the Muslim rulers of al-Andalus, it stands as the most celebrated monument of Moorish architecture in Western Europe and is a prime example of Mudejar art, which fused Islamic and Christian styles after the Reconquista.

The complex's most famous feature is the Court of the Lions (Patio de los Leones), a courtyard whose central basin rests on twelve marble lion statues. The court features four projecting pavilions facing each other and is connected to the Hall of the Two Sisters and the Hall of the Abencerrages — two rooms with exceptionally elaborate Muqarnas domes positioned across from each other. The Hall of the Boat, named for its ceiling's resemblance to the hull of a ship, connects via a double arch to the Hall of the Two Sisters. Inscriptions on the court's fountain, composed by the poet Ibn Zamrak, compare the building to 'houses of the constellations'; scholars Yasser Taabba and Oleg Grabar have debated whether these texts relate to the Muztallite-Asharite theological controversy or are merely generic stellar comparisons. Another poem inscribed on a fountain compares its water to 'the lover who in vain / tries to hide his tears from his beloved.'

The Court of the Myrtles (Patio de los Arrayanes) features a large rectangular reflecting pool framed by myrtle shrubs and arcades, lying to the south of the Tower of Comares. The Gate of Justice, built in the regional horseshoe arch style, leads to a patio surrounded by arcades with stucco sebka designs. A large Hall of the Ambassadors occupies the Comares Tower.

The Alhambra's muqarnas — honeycomb-shaped stalactite vaults composed of small cells called alveoles — are considered the most intricately patterned in Islamic architecture. The geometric tile decorations uniquely use all seventeen wallpaper groups. During a 1936 visit, M.C. Escher copied these tessellations, an encounter that catalyzed his lifelong obsession with the regular division of the plane, visible in works such as Verbum and his series of tessellated knights and birds. Owen Jones made innovative chromolithographs of the Alhambra's wall decorations that influenced his interiors for the Crystal Palace; Jones's sketches also influenced the design of Anglo-American smoking rooms. The complex's niches once held large blue-and-gold vases unique in the region for being purely decorative.

The Alhambra is connected to the Generalife, the adjacent Nasrid summer palace known for its jasmine-scented gardens and water courtyards. During the Peninsular War, the Duke of Wellington planted English elms within the palace complex. Later, Charles V commissioned a Renaissance palace within the Alhambra, during whose protracted construction Moorish laborers were expelled. Washington Irving lived in the building while compiling his Tales from the Alhambra (1832), an influential collection of travel essays and historical legends including 'The Legend of the Arabian Astrologer' and 'The Legend of the Three Beautiful Princesses.' The filmmaker Marie Menken shot a 1961 experimental short film in the complex dedicated to Kenneth Anger.

The Alhambra also inspired a celebrated guitar tremolo etude — Recuerdos de la Alhambra (Memories of the Alhambra) — by Francisco Tárrega, in which the 'i,' 'm,' and 'a' fingers play a continuous tremolo melody while the thumb provides a counter-melody. The first movement of Manuel de Falla's Nights in the Gardens of Spain and Joaquin Rodrigo's Junto al Generalife were both inspired by the adjacent palace. Claude Debussy's 'Evening in Granada' (from Estampes) and the piano duet Lindaraja were also reportedly influenced by the Alhambra.
