Max Beckmann

German Expressionist / New Objectivity painter. Most commonly identified by The Night and the triptych Departure. Known for many triptychs including Argonauts, Carnival, and Blind Man's Bluff. Labeled 'degenerate' by the Nazis; fired from Frankfurt's Städel School. Frequently painted his wife Quappi and self-portraits.
The Night (1918–1919)

Depicts a horrific scene of intruders invading a room — a man is hanged from the rafters while a woman is bound and raped. Addresses post-WWI horror and disillusionment. Housed at Kunstsammlung Nordrhein-Westfalen, Düsseldorf. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Max Beckmann was a German Expressionist and New Objectivity painter, most commonly identified by The Night and his triptych Departure. The Night (1918–1919) depicts a horrific scene in which a man is hanged from rafters by intruders while his wife is bound to a wall/beam, reflecting post-WWI disillusionment. Robert Storr analyzed Beckmann's 'effect,' noting that 'no distinction is made between animate and inanimate imagery.' Candles and stockings in the work allude to Hogarth's The Rake's Progress.

Departure (1932–1935) was the first of nine triptychs Beckmann created. Its central panel shows a crowned Fisher King standing in a boat on bright blue water next to a woman and child symbolizing the Holy Family. The dark side panels depict bound and tortured figures. The individual panels are titled The Castle (left), The Staircase (right), and The Homecoming (center). It hangs at MoMA. Beckmann wrote 'Scenes from Shakespeare's Tempest' on the back of one triptych to trick Nazi authorities who searched his home.

Beckmann's other major triptychs include The Argonauts (his last, which quotes from his early Young Men by the Sea and features Medea posing for a painter), Blind Man's Bluff (with kneeling figures holding candles), and Carnival (painted after fleeing Germany). He painted nine triptychs in total after moving to Amsterdam in 1937 in response to a Nazi radio address about 'degenerate art.'

Beckmann's Perseus series features striking imagery: Perseus' Last Duty shows a black dog with breasts watching as a hero in nylon stockings decapitates nude Medusa; in another work, an orange-haired Perseus carries Andromeda upside-down onto a boat.

Biographically, Beckmann was fired from his professorship at Frankfurt's Städel School in 1933 when his art was labeled 'degenerate.' Ten of his paintings appeared in the Nazi 'Degenerate Art' exhibition. His wife, nicknamed Quappi, appears in several works including The Bark. He later taught at Washington University in St. Louis. His Self-Portrait with Trumpet/Saxophone helped establish his American reputation. Early works include The Sinking of the Titanic and Young Men by the Sea. Other works include Bird's Hell, Still Life with Three Skulls, Acrobat on Trapeze, and The Liberated One. He also wrote 'The Artist in the State.' While living above a morgue, he suffered nightmares and a mental breakdown.
