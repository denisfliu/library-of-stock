Summary of Facts

Hilary Mantel was a British author best known for the Wolf Hall trilogy about Thomas Cromwell, winning the Booker Prize for both Wolf Hall (2009) and Bring Up the Bodies (2012) — the first British woman to win the Booker twice. The trilogy's third volume, The Mirror and the Light (2020), ends with Cromwell's beheading as he repeatedly thinks "so now get up," the first sentence of the series spoken by his father Walter.

Wolf Hall is named for the Seymour family estate, though none of its action takes place there — the title symbolizes the danger Cromwell encounters. The novel opens in Putney with young Cromwell being beaten by his blacksmith father Walter, who says "So now get up." Cromwell raises Rafe Sadler at Austin Friars and serves Cardinal Thomas Wolsey. Characters repeat the mantra "Arrange your face," and one character who introduces himself as "Call me Risley" earns the nickname "Call-Me." Cromwell fashions angel wings from peacock feathers for his daughter Grace and names his falcons after his deceased wife Liz and daughters Anne and Grace, all lost to sweating sickness. Part Two ends with Cicero's story of how Simonides invented the art of memory. Thomas More appears as a fanatic who accuses Cromwell of heresy; the novel ends with More's beheading.

Bring Up the Bodies follows Cromwell's scheme with Lady Rochford and Mark Smeaton to bring down Anne Boleyn, avenging Cardinal Wolsey. Stephen Gardiner serves as the primary antagonist throughout the trilogy. The Mirror and the Light concludes with Cromwell's own execution.

"The Assassination of Margaret Thatcher" is a controversial short story in which an IRA operative, posing as a replacement boiler man Mr. Duggan, enters the narrator's home on Trinity Place overlooking a hospital to shoot Margaret Thatcher, ending with "Fucking rejoice." The story was rejected by the Daily Telegraph. Her collection of the same name also includes "The Heart Fails without Warning" and "Sorry to Disturb."

Mantel also wrote A Place of Greater Safety, a historical novel about the French Revolution featuring Camille Desmoulins, Danton, and Robespierre; Fludd, about a curate who magically keeps a whiskey bottle full during drinking with the faithless priest Angwin; and the memoir Giving Up the Ghost about her Catholic school upbringing. She described the world as not run "from the castle walls, but from counting houses."
