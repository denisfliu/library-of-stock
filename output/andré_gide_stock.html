Summary of Facts

André Gide (1869-1951) was a French novelist who won the Nobel Prize in Literature. His Protestant upbringing and homosexuality are central themes.

The Immoralist follows Michel, a tubercular scholar who embraces hedonism during his honeymoon in Tunisia. He watches the boy Moktir steal scissors from his wife Marceline and develops an interest in Arab boys. Marceline eventually dies of tuberculosis herself.

The Counterfeiters is a metafictional novel with a published Journal explaining its development. Boris is killed during a botched initiation when a real bullet replaces a fake one, orchestrated by Ghéridanisol. Bernard runs away after finding a love letter and steals a bag using a dropped checkroom ticket.

Strait is the Gate is about Jerome's love for his religious cousin Alissa, who refuses to consummate their love, strives for purity, and dies. The Pastoral Symphony concerns a pastor who adopts the blind girl Gertrude and leads her to believe the world is sinless; after surgery, she sees the truth and falls for his son Jacques.

Gide wrote Corydon defending homosexuality, The Fruits of the Earth ('Families, I hate you!'), and contributed to The God That Failed about disillusionment with communism after touring the Soviet Union.
