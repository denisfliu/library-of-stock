Existentialism

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
A philosophical movement associated primarily with Jean-Paul Sartre, characterized by the claim that 'existence precedes essence,' an emphasis on subjectivity, and concepts such as abandonment, anguish, and bad faith. It is often equated with humanism and contrasted with nihilism and quietism.
Existentialism Is a Humanism (Sartre) →

Sartre's 1946 lecture, originally delivered at the Club Maintenant, is the single most-clued text associated with existentialism. It coined the phrase 'existence precedes essence,' defended the movement against Communist accusations of quietism, and named Karl Jaspers and Gabriel Marcel as its Christian proponents. It uses the example of a paper-knife to illustrate essence preceding existence in objects, and a student choosing between war and caring for his mother to illustrate abandonment. Heidegger responded to it in his 'Letter on Humanism' to Jean Beaufret.

Christian Existentialism

A branch of existentialism that uses religious thought to address the anxiety of being in a seemingly absurd world. Key figures include Paul Tillich (who contrasted 'spiritual,' 'ontic,' and 'moral' anxiety across eras), Karl Jaspers, Gabriel Marcel (who called himself a 'philosopher of existence' and wrote The Mystery of Being), and Soren Kierkegaard (author of Repetition and Three Upbuilding Discourses, known for the 'leap of faith'). Jakob Bohme's concept of the Ungrund influenced later Christian existentialists like Nikolai Berdyaev and Lev Shestov.

General / Key Associations

Existentialism as a movement is most strongly associated with Jean-Paul Sartre and his work Being and Nothingness. It deals with feelings of nothingness and angst, alienation from society, and the human condition in an absurd world. The movement asserts there is no intrinsic meaning to life and individuals must create their own. Key histories of the movement include Walter Kaufmann's Existentialism from Dostoevsky to Sartre (which examines 'What is Transcendence?') and William Barrett's Irrational Man (which concludes with an analysis of the 'place of the furies'). R.D. Laing derived his concept of ontological security from existentialist readings. Hubert Dreyfus drew on existentialism to criticize AI in What Computers Can't Do. Roland Barthes's Writing Degree Zero responds to Sartre's application of existentialism to literature.

Summary of Facts

Existentialism is a philosophical movement most closely associated with Jean-Paul Sartre, whose Being and Nothingness is its foundational text. The movement deals with themes of nothingness, angst, alienation from society, and the human condition in a seemingly absurd world. It asserts that there is no intrinsic meaning to life, and individuals must create their own meaning.

The single most important text for understanding existentialism in quizbowl is Sartre's 1946 lecture 'Existentialism Is a Humanism,' originally delivered at the Club Maintenant. This essay coined the famous slogan 'existence precedes essence,' a reversal of traditional philosophical views implying that humans do not have inherent value but must create it themselves. The essay uses the example of a paper-knife (whose essence is defined before its existence by a craftsman's formulae) to contrast with human existence, where no such predetermined essence exists. It also uses the example of a student who must choose between going to war and caring for his bereaved mother to illustrate the concept of 'abandonment' — the idea that no ethical system (Kantian or Christian) can make such decisions for us. The essay defends existentialism against Communist accusations of quietism, rehabilitates the concepts of 'abandonment' and 'anguish,' and defends the 'pure subjectivity' of its atheistic variant. It names Karl Jaspers and Gabriel Marcel as the movement's Christian proponents. The essay also references Maggie Tulliver from The Mill on the Floss and La Sanseverina from The Charterhouse of Parma.

Martin Heidegger responded to Sartre's lecture in his 'Letter on Humanism,' addressed to his student Jean Beaufret. Heidegger claimed Sartre had misunderstood his work and distanced himself from humanism. In this letter, Heidegger modified the word 'existence' by spelling it 'ek-sistenz' (making the second letter a K followed by a hyphen), referring to man's 'standing out' into the truth of 'being' and his 'dwelling' in language. The letter opens with the claim that 'We are far from understanding...the essence of action.'

Christian existentialism is a significant branch that uses religious thought to address the anxiety of being in an absurd world. Paul Tillich contrasted three types of anxiety across historical eras: the 'ontic' anxiety of death in late antiquity, the 'moral' anxiety of damnation in the late Middle Ages, and the 'spiritual' anxiety of emptiness and loss of meaning in the modern era. Gabriel Marcel described himself as a 'philosopher of existence,' claimed that 'we live in a broken world and require transcendence,' and wrote The Mystery of Being. Soren Kierkegaard, the author of Repetition and Three Upbuilding Discourses, is usually considered part of this movement and is known for proposing a 'leap of faith.' Jakob Bohme's concept of the Ungrund influenced later Christian existentialists like Nikolai Berdyaev and Lev Shestov.

Black existentialism focuses on the liberation of Black people amid oppression. Lewis Gordon wrote on bad faith and Antiblack Racism, and Cornel West's Race Matters includes a chapter on 'Nihilism in Black America' that analyzes Black existentialism. Sartre's concept of bad faith (mauvaise foi), illustrated in Being and Nothingness by the example of a cafe waiter whose movements are 'too precise,' is central to this tradition.

Key histories of the movement include Walter Kaufmann's Existentialism from Dostoevsky to Sartre, which examines the question 'What is Transcendence?,' and William Barrett's Irrational Man, which concludes with an analysis of 'the place of the furies.' Existentialism has influenced fields beyond philosophy: R.D. Laing derived his concept of ontological security from existentialist readings, Hubert Dreyfus drew on it to criticize artificial intelligence in What Computers Can't Do, and Roland Barthes's debut book Writing Degree Zero is seen as a response to Sartre's application of existentialism to literature.
