Argentine author known for labyrinthine, philosophical short stories collected in Ficciones and El Aleph. Major stories include 'The Aleph' (a point containing all other points), 'The Library of Babel' (an infinite library of hexagonal rooms), 'The Garden of Forking Paths' (the spy Yu Tsun and a labyrinth-novel), 'Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius' (an encyclopedia of a fictional idealist world), 'Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote,' 'Death and the Compass' (Erik Lönnrot vs. Red Scharlach), 'Funes the Memorious' (perfect memory after a fall), and 'The Circular Ruins' (dreaming a man into existence). Also a poet and essayist. Went blind. Collaborated with Adolfo Bioy Casares. Published Cortázar's first story. Deeply influenced by Kafka, whom he translated. Known for interest in labyrinths, mirrors, infinity, and detective fiction.

Summary of Facts

Jorge Luis Borges (1899–1986) is an Argentine author known for labyrinthine, philosophical short stories collected in Ficciones and El Aleph, as well as poetry and essays.

His most frequently clued story is 'The Aleph,' in which the narrator (named Borges) mourns Beatriz Viterbo and visits the cellar of her cousin Carlos Argentino Daneri, a poet writing 'The Earth' to describe every inch of the planet. Lying on the nineteenth step of the cellar staircase, the narrator sees the Aleph: a point in space containing all other points, including 'a silvery cobweb in the center of a black pyramid' and 'convex equatorial deserts.' Daneri follows 'verbal ostentation,' inventing the word 'lactinacious' for 'milky.'

'The Library of Babel' imagines a universe of hexagonal galleries containing every possible combination of letters in book form. Most books are meaningless. 'Purifiers' search for the 'Crimson Hexagon.' The story was inspired by the infinite monkey theorem and Lasswitz's Traumkristalle, with an epigraph from Robert Burton about 'the variation of 23 letters.'

'The Garden of Forking Paths' follows the German spy Yu Tsun, who escapes Richard Madden, visits the Sinologist Stephen Albert, and shoots him to communicate a city's name to German intelligence. Albert discovers that Yu Tsun's ancestor Ts'ui Pen's 'novel' and 'labyrinth' are one and the same, a book exploring all possible outcomes, analogized to a guessing game where the answer is 'chess.' The story may anticipate the Many Worlds interpretation of quantum mechanics.

'Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius' features the narrator and Bioy Casares discovering an encyclopedia of the fictional world of Tlön, founded on Berkeley's idealism, with a nounless language where 'hlor u fang axaxaxas mlo' means 'to moonate.' The heresiarch declares 'mirrors and copulation are abominable.' Millionaire Ezra Buckley finances the encyclopedia on condition it 'makes no pact with the impostor Jesus Christ.' The story ends with Borges translating Browne's Urn Burial.

'Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote' describes a fictional French writer who recreates Don Quixote word-for-word by 'being' Cervantes. The narrator considers Menard's version superior. Menard's surviving works include diatribes against Valéry.

'Death and the Compass' is a detective story where Erik Lönnrot investigates murders forming the Tetragrammaton pattern. Red Scharlach traps and kills Lönnrot at the fourth point. Lönnrot ascends the villa of Triste-le-Roy.

'Funes the Memorious' concerns Ireneo Funes, who gains perfect memory after falling from a horse. He assigns arbitrary names to numbers, is irritated that different temporal views of a dog share one noun, and cannot generalize.

'The Circular Ruins': a wizard dreams a man into existence; in the twist, the wizard himself was dreamed. 'The Book of Sand': a book with no beginning or end, traded for a Wycliffe Bible. 'Borges and I' ends: 'I do not know which of us has written this page.' 'The South': Juan Dahlmann accepts a knife fight. 'Emma Zunz': Emma shoots Loewenthal to avenge her father. 'Three Versions of Judas': God incarnated as Judas. 'On Exactitude in Science': a map at mile-to-mile scale. 'Deutsches Requiem': SS officer Otto zur Linde. 'Averroës' Search': Averroës can't translate Aristotle because he doesn't know what a play is. 'The Immortal': a troglodyte named Argos is Homer.

As a poet: 'Poem of the Gifts' ('I had always imagined Paradise as a kind of library'), 'A Rose and Milton,' 'General Quiroga Rides to His Death in a Carriage.' His essay 'Kafka and His Precursors' argues that writers create their own precursors. The Book of Imaginary Beings describes 120 creatures. He went blind and wrote the essay 'Blindness.' He collaborated with Bioy Casares under the pseudonym H. Bustos Domecq. He published Cortázar's 'House Taken Over.' He was passionate about Old English; his tombstone bears a line from 'The Battle of Maldon.' He translated Kafka into Spanish. His foremost English translators include Alastair Reid and Norman Thomas di Giovanni.
