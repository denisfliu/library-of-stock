Summary of Facts

Anton Chekhov (Anton Pavlovich Chekhov) was a Russian playwright and short story writer, widely considered the master of the short story form. He called Medicine his "lawful wife" and literature his "mistress."

"The Lady with the Dog" (or "Lapdog") is Chekhov's most frequently clued story. The unhappily married banker Dmitri Gurov, who originally refers to women as "the lower race," begins an affair with Anna Sergeyevna (wife of the nobleman Von Dideritz) while visiting the resort town of Yalta, where she walks her white Pomeranian. She is startled when Gurov later drops in on her at a performance of The Geisha in her hometown. They reunite and begin a "double life" that "flowed in secret." The story ends with the realization that "the most complicated and difficult part was only just beginning." Gurov eats a watermelon after having sex. Vladimir Nabokov called it "one of the greatest stories ever written." Joyce Carol Oates updated it, setting it in Nantucket.

"Gooseberries" is part of Chekhov's "Little Trilogy" along with "The Man in the Case" and "About Love." In it, Ivan Ivanich (a veterinary surgeon) tells the story of his brother Nikolai, who leaves a government job to buy a farm. Nikolai marries the rich widow of a postmaster and uses her money to buy an estate, where he adopts upper-class mannerisms and gets upset if peasants don't call him "Your Lordship." Nikolai cries the first time he eats fruit from his own bushes. The narrator remarks that "there ought to be someone standing with a little hammer" to remind "every contented, happy man" that unhappy people exist. George Saunders drew the title of A Swim in the Pond in the Rain from this story.

"The Man in the Case" features Byelikov, a teacher of Greek who attempts to hide from the world. He is mocked by a cartoon showing him with a woman (Varinka) captioned "Anthropos in love" and breaks off relations when she laughs after her brother pushes him down stairs. The teacher Burkin narrates the Little Trilogy.

"About Love" features Alehin, who falls in love with his friend Luganovich's wife Anna but they only admit feelings as they are separated forever.

"The Bet" features a lawyer who agrees to be imprisoned for 15 years in exchange for two million rubles. A gun is fired to celebrate his mastery of six languages. The banker plans to kill him near the end. After 15 years, the lawyer kisses the banker on the head, renounces the money, and leaves five hours early, having grown to despise humanity.

"Ward No. 6" draws on Chekhov's medical background. Dr. Andrei Rabin converses with his patient Gromov about Stoic philosophy (Rabin rejects Stoicism). Rabin Goes to Warsaw with the postmaster Mikhail to shake off health doubts. Khobotov schemes to consign Rabin to the ward. Rabin is beaten by his caretaker, has a stroke, and is locked in his own asylum. He has a dying vision of a herd of running deer.

"The Darling" features a woman devoted to and who marries three successive men: a theater owner, a lumber merchant, and a veterinarian.

"The Black Monk" features a protagonist named Korvin who is told by the title apparition that he is a genius. He marries the daughter of a horticulturalist.

"Rothschild's Fiddle" (or "Violin"): a coffin-maker nicknamed "Bronze" who plays in a klezmer orchestra overcomes his antisemitism after his wife Marfa dies. The story ends with the title Jew inheriting the violin.

"In the Ravine": a woman Goes unpunished after pouring boiling water on Lipa's baby.

"The Student": a clerical apprentice tells the story of Peter's denial of Christ to two widows on Good Friday; Vasilisa is moved to tears.

"Gusev": based on a real burial at sea witnessed during Chekhov's journey to Sakhalin. Virginia Woolf praised it in "Modern Fiction."

Other stories include "Grisha" (a boy gets drunk and wails "Go away, stove!"), "The Lottery Ticket," "Anna on the Neck," and "The Beauties."

The Cherry Orchard is Chekhov's most clued play. Madame Lyubov Ranevskaya sells her estate to Lopakhin, a former serf and businessman. Firs, the old servant, is forgotten and locked in the estate at the end, saying "They've gone away. They've forgotten about me" and lying down to die. A mysterious sound of a breaking string is heard twice -- once in Act II (Firs recalls it was heard when Alexander II freed the serfs) and again at the very end, followed by the distant thud of axes chopping down the title trees. The play is the only Chekhov work where a gun is shown but never fired, breaking his own principle. Ranevskaya painfully recalls the drowning of her son while talking with his former tutor Trofimov. Gayev takes a bank job. A postmaster attends a party and flirts with the maid Dunyasha. Two men kiss at a party where Charlotta does card tricks.

The Seagull features a love triangle between the ingenue Nina Zarechnaya, the playwright Konstantin Treplev, and the popular writer Boris Trigorin. Nina performs Konstantin's experimental symbolist play by a lake, which opens when the moon rises. In it, Nina says "all is cold, void, and terrible" and claims "in me, the consciousness of man has joined hands with the instinct of animal." Satan is represented by two red dots and sulfur fumes. Only Dr. Dorn enjoys the play. Trigorin receives a medallion from Nina inscribed "If you ever need my life, come and take it" (page 121 of his novel). Konstantin shoots the title bird and later shoots himself (twice, the last time fatally). Doctor Dorn claims his Medicine bottle made the sound to calm Irina Arkadina. Stanislavski revived the play at the Moscow Art Theatre. Aaron Posner adapted it as Stupid Fucking Bird.

Uncle Vanya: Vanya has spent 25 years tending Professor Serebryakov's estate. In Act III, he tries to shoot Serebryakov but misses, then sinks into a chair. He steals morphine. He exclaims he could have been the next Schopenhauer or Dostoevsky. Dr. Astrov performs a botched surgery while drunk. Helena struggles over the gun with Vanya. The pistol is a classic example of Chekhov's gun.

Three Sisters: about the Prozorov family (Olga, Masha, Irina). Begins on the anniversary of their father's death/Irina's name-day. Baron Tuzenbach is killed in a duel with Soleni over Irina's hand. Chebutykin sings "Ta-ra-ra-boom-di-ay" as Olga says "if only we knew." A military band fades into the distance.

Chekhov's one-act plays include The Bear (Smirnov confesses love for Popova), On the Harmful Effects of Tobacco (Nyukhin gives a boring lecture), The Tragedian in Spite of Himself (Tolkachov rants and screams "I want blood!"), and others.

Chekhov's Gun is his famous dramatic principle: if a gun is shown in Act I, it must be fired by the end. His letters reject labels like "liberal" and "conservative" in favor of the "holy of holies" of the human body and freedom. He wrote the travelogue Sakhalin Island about Siberian penal colonies. Raymond Carver, called the "American Chekhov," depicted his death in the story "Errand." Christopher Durang parodied his works in Vanya and Sonia and Masha and Spike. He lived in the White Dacha in Yalta.
