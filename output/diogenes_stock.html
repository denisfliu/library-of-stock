Diogenes of Sinope (the Cynic)

Diogenes of Sinope is the most iconic Cynic philosopher, known for extreme acts of nonconformity. He lived in a ceramic jar/wine barrel, carried a lantern in daylight searching for an 'honest man,' told Alexander the Great to 'stand out of his sunlight,' and plucked a chicken to refute Plato's definition of man as a 'featherless biped.' He originated the term 'cosmopolitan' (citizen of the world). He was called a 'dog' and embraced the label. Conflicting accounts of his death attribute it to eating raw octopus or fighting a dog over octopus. He allegedly refuted Zeno of Elea's paradoxes against motion by simply getting up and walking.

Diogenes Laertius (Biographer)

Diogenes Laertius is the 3rd-century author of Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers, one of the most important surviving sources for ancient Greek philosophy. The work preserves anecdotes about Heraclitus, Empedocles, Chrysippus, and many others. It is addressed to an 'enthusiastic Platonist' and divides philosophers into Ionian and Italian schools. Laertius is thought to possibly be a skeptical philosopher and a student of Sextus Empiricus. He claimed Plato's name meant 'broad-shouldered' and was a wrestling nickname.

Diogenes of Apollonia

Diogenes of Apollonia was a pre-Socratic philosopher who believed air was the fundamental substance (like Anaximenes) but added that it was intelligent. Theophrastus called him the last of the physiologoi. Aristotle's History of Animals preserves his fragmentary writings on human blood vessels. The character of Socrates in Aristophanes' The Clouds parodies his views.

Diogenes Teufelsdrockh (Sartor Resartus)

Diogenes Teufelsdrockh is a fictional philosopher in Thomas Carlyle's novel Sartor Resartus, a professor of Things in General at Weissnichtwo University. His thought, laid out in Clothes, Their Origin and Influence, parodies German idealism. He passes through the 'centre of indifference' to reach the 'Everlasting Yea' of resolute faith.

Summary of Facts

The name Diogenes is shared by several important figures in philosophy, and quizbowl clues frequently exploit this ambiguity. Diogenes of Sinope, the Cynic philosopher, is the most iconic: he lived in a ceramic jar, carried a lantern in daylight searching for an honest man, told Alexander the Great to step out of his sunlight, and plucked a chicken to refute Plato's definition of man as a 'featherless biped.' He originated the term 'cosmopolitan' and was called a 'dog,' embracing the label that gave Cynicism its name. His death is variously attributed to eating raw octopus or fighting a dog over octopus.

Diogenes Laertius, the 3rd-century biographer, wrote Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers, which is one of the most important surviving sources for ancient Greek philosophy. His work preserves anecdotes about Empedocles (jumping into Mt. Etna), Heraclitus ('panta rhei'), and many others. Book X preserves Epicurus's surviving letters. The work is addressed to an 'enthusiastic Platonist' and divides philosophers into Ionian and Italian schools. Laertius himself may have been a skeptical philosopher and a student of Sextus Empiricus.

Diogenes of Apollonia was a pre-Socratic who believed air was the fundamental substance (like Anaximenes) but added that it was intelligent. Theophrastus called him the last of the physiologoi, and Aristophanes' The Clouds parodies his views alongside Socrates's.

In fiction, Diogenes Teufelsdrockh is the protagonist of Thomas Carlyle's Sartor Resartus, a professor of 'Things in General' whose work Clothes, Their Origin and Influence parodies German idealism.
