Philosophical school of epistemology holding that all knowledge derives from sensory experience, often contrasted with rationalism. Key figures include John Locke, David Hume, and George Berkeley. In quizbowl, the topic is dominated by Quine's 'Two Dogmas of Empiricism' and related discussions of logical positivism, the Vienna Circle, and Wilfrid Sellars's 'Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind.'
Two Dogmas of Empiricism (Quine)

W. V. O. Quine's 1951 paper 'Two Dogmas of Empiricism' is the single most clued text under this answerline. It attacks two central tenets of logical positivism: the analytic-synthetic distinction and reductionism. Quine uses the examples of 'no unmarried man is married' vs. 'no bachelor is married' to discuss cognitive synonymy and analyticity. He argues that no individual statement is empirically verifiable on its own (confirmation holism / Duhem-Quine thesis), compares physical objects to Greek gods, and imagines science as a 'force field' with experience at the boundary. Paul Grice and P. F. Strawson wrote a 'Defense' of analyticity in response; Hilary Putnam 'Revisited' it.

Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind (Sellars)

Wilfrid Sellars's 1956 paper 'Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind' introduced the 'Myth of the Given' — an attack on the foundationalist idea that basic sensory concepts can both provide knowledge and justify beliefs. It also introduced the 'logical space of reasons,' arguing that characterizing a state as knowing is not giving an empirical description but placing it in a space where justification is expected. John McDowell's Mind and World builds on Sellars's critique.

Logical Positivism / Vienna Circle

Logical positivism (logical empiricism) was the philosophy of the Vienna Circle, led by Moritz Schlick and Rudolf Carnap, which embraced the verification principle and rejected metaphysics. A. J. Ayer's Language, Truth, and Logic brought it to Anglophone readers. It declined after Thomas Kuhn's Structure of Scientific Revolutions. Wittgenstein's Tractatus heavily influenced it. Otto Neurath analogized scientific knowledge to a ship rebuilt while sailing. Bas van Fraassen's 'constructive empiricism' is an anti-realist successor.

General / Definition and Key Figures

Empiricism is defined as the epistemological stance that all knowledge derives from experience, contrasted with rationalism which holds that some knowledge is innate. Key figures include John Locke (tabula rasa), David Hume (impressions and ideas), and George Berkeley. The concept traces back to Aristotle's notion of the mind as tabula rasa at birth. Leibniz critiqued a central work of empiricism (Locke's Essay) to defend innate ideas. Hume's 'missing shade of blue' is a famous counterexample from within the tradition. William James characterized empiricists as 'tough-minded, pessimistic, and skeptical.' Kant claimed to have ended the rationalism-empiricism debate.

Summary of Facts

Empiricism as a quizbowl answerline is dominated by two major texts: Quine's 'Two Dogmas of Empiricism' and Sellars's 'Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind,' along with questions about logical positivism and the Vienna Circle.

Quine's paper attacks the analytic-synthetic distinction and reductionism. The bachelor/unmarried man example illustrates cognitive synonymy. Quine argues for confirmation holism (no individual statement is testable alone — the Duhem-Quine thesis) and compares physical objects to Greek gods. He criticizes Carnap's Aufbau and state-descriptions. Grice and Strawson wrote a 'Defense' of analyticity in response.

Sellars's paper introduced the 'Myth of the Given' (attacking foundationalism) and the 'logical space of reasons.' McDowell's Mind and World extends Sellars's critique.

Logical positivism (the Vienna Circle: Schlick, Carnap, Neurath, Feigl) embraced the verification principle and rejected metaphysics. Ayer's Language, Truth, and Logic popularized it in English. Wittgenstein's Tractatus was a key influence. The movement declined after Kuhn.

As a general concept, empiricism holds that all knowledge derives from experience, contrasted with rationalism's innate ideas. Key figures are Locke (tabula rasa), Hume (impressions/ideas, missing shade of blue), and Berkeley. The tradition traces to Aristotle. Kant claimed to have resolved the empiricism-rationalism debate. Van Fraassen's 'constructive empiricism' and Longino's 'contextual empiricism' are modern variants.
