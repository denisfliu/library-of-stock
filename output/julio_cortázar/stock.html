Argentine writer and key figure of the Latin American Boom. Best known for the nonlinear novel Hopscotch and a large body of short stories including 'Axolotl,' 'Blow-Up,' 'Bestiary,' 'House Taken Over,' 'The Continuity of Parks,' 'The Night Face Up,' 'Letter to a Young Lady in Paris,' 'The Pursuer,' and 'The Idol of the Cyclades.' Self-exiled to Paris. Translated Edgar Allan Poe's complete prose works into Spanish. Created the fictional creatures famas and cronopios.
Hopscotch (Rayuela)

Cortázar's most famous novel, about Horacio Oliveira and his lover La Maga, set partly in Paris and partly in Buenos Aires. Contains 155 chapters, of which the last 99 are 'expendable.' Opens with a 'Table of Instructions' that advises reading in multiple nonlinear orders (starting at chapter 73). Horacio is a member of the bohemian Serpent Club. He confuses Talita (Traveler's wife) with La Maga. Horacio and Traveler work at an insane asylum. The novel includes the literary criticism of a mysterious writer called Morelli, whose musings Cortázar expanded into the novel 62: A Model Kit. Horacio searches for the 'kibbutz of desire.' The novel features a language called Gliglico. A subplot involves Juan's suspicions that Frau Marta is a vampire preying on English tourists. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Julio Cortázar is an Argentine writer and key figure of the Latin American Boom, overwhelmingly identified in quizbowl by the nonlinear novel Hopscotch and his numerous short stories.

Hopscotch (Rayuela) is his most frequently clued novel. It contains 155 chapters, with the last 99 described as 'expendable.' A 'Table of Instructions' advises the reader to start at chapter 73 and read in various nonlinear orders. The novel follows Horacio Oliveira and his lover La Maga, a member of the bohemian Serpent Club in Paris. Horacio later moves to Buenos Aires, where he confuses Talita (the wife of Traveler) with La Maga and works at an insane asylum with Traveler. A mysterious writer called Morelli contributes literary criticism throughout; Morelli's musings in the 62nd chapter were expanded into the novel 62: A Model Kit. Other details include the language Gliglico, the 'kibbutz of desire,' Osip Gregorovius nearly seducing La Maga, and a scene where everyone knows a baby is dead except the mother.

'Axolotl' is the most frequently clued short story. The narrator visits the Jardin des Plantes in Paris, becomes obsessed with axolotls (comparing them to 'Chinese figurines of milky glass,' noting 'larva means disguise and also phantom'), and eventually transforms into one.

'Blow-Up' features the photographer Roberto Michel, who while walking along the Seine and translating a treatise by José Norberto Allende, photographs a blonde woman propositioning a teenage boy. He enlarges the photograph and becomes tormented by it. Michelangelo Antonioni adapted it into a film in which the photographer discovers a dead body.

'Bestiary' features a tiger that wanders freely through a summer house. The narrator misinforms the Kid about the tiger's location, and the Kid is mauled to death, motivated by the Kid's abusive attitude towards Rema.

'House Taken Over' likely allegorizes Perón's government: strange noises force the narrator and his knitting-obsessed sister Irene into progressively smaller areas of their home. The narrator buys Irene wool and French literature. They flee and he throws the key in the sewer.

'The Continuity of Parks' is a metafictional story: a man in a green velvet armchair reads a novel about two lovers who plan to murder a man reading in a green velvet armchair.

'The Night Face Up' features a motorcyclist hospitalized after an accident who dreams of being an Aztec sacrificial victim in the 'War of the Blossom.' The twist: the motorcycle accident is the dream and the Aztec sacrifice is real.

'Letter to a Young Lady in Paris' features a narrator who vomits rabbits after moving into his friend Andrea's apartment and is driven to suicide by jumping out a window.

'The Pursuer' is dedicated 'In memoriam Ch. P.' (Charlie Parker). Bruno follows the heroin-addicted jazz saxophonist Johnny Carter, who annotates Dylan Thomas's 'The Mask' and dies after wanting to give up jazz.

Other clued stories include 'The Idol of the Cyclades' (archaeologist Morand kills Somoza with a stone hatchet), 'End of the Game' (girls play 'Statues and Attitudes' by train tracks; Ariel leaves notes), 'The Southern Highway' (months-long traffic jam, inspired Godard's Week End), 'The Distances' (Alina Reyes's diary entries, connection to a Budapest beggar), 'Secret Weapons,' 'Reunion' (Che Guevara narrator), and 'Instructions for John Howell.'

Other works include A Manual for Manuel (VIP kidnapped by 'the Screwery'), Cronopios and Famas (fictional creatures), Los Autonautas de la cosmopista (VW trip with Carol Dunlop), and Fantomas versus the Multinational Vampires. Cortázar translated Poe's complete prose works into Spanish. He was pressured to resign his professorship in 1946 and self-exiled to Paris. Alejandra Pizarnik lost a manuscript of his; after her suicide he wrote a poem placing her in 'the garden where Alice was waiting.'
