Summary of Facts

Joseph Kosuth is an American conceptual artist born in 1945, widely regarded as one of the pioneers of the conceptual art movement. He is overwhelmingly asked about through his signature work One and Three Chairs (1965), which consists of a real folding chair, a photograph of that chair, and an enlarged dictionary definition of the word "chair." This work appears in quiz bowl questions with extraordinary frequency, both as a direct answer and as a clue pointing to chairs, text, conceptual art, and related topics. The work is sometimes connected to Plato's Republic Book X and its discussion of the three levels of reality.

Kosuth's essay "Art After Philosophy" (1969) is his most important theoretical text, in which he controversially argued that "actual works of art are little more than historical curiosities" and that art's function is to make assertions "beyond physics." The essay traces the origins of conceptual art to Marcel Duchamp's readymades. Kosuth also created the Art as Idea as Idea series, consisting of photostats presenting dictionary definitions of words in white text. He responded to criticism from Benjamin Buchloh with the essay "The Artist as Anthropologist." He co-edited Art-Language: The Journal of Conceptual Art from 1969 to 1985. Two lesser-known works feature red text and reference Wittgenstein's ideas about the indescribability of color.
