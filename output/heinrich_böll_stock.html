Summary of Facts

Heinrich Böll (1917-1985) was the foremost German author of 'rubble literature' (Trümmerliteratur), the sparse postwar German literary movement depicting the destruction of Germany. He was a member of Group 47 and won the Nobel Prize in 1972.

Billiards at Half-Past Nine is set on a single day and chronicles three generations of the Faehmel family of architects. Robert Faehmel designs St. Anthony's Abbey and later orders its destruction. He plays billiards for 90 minutes daily at the Prince Heinrich Hotel. The novel uses the motif of beast vs. lamb. Johanna attempts to shoot a government minister, and Hugo is adopted.

The Lost Honour of Katharina Blum follows a housekeeper whose life is ruined by tabloid journalism after she sleeps with a bank robber. She shoots the reporter Werner Tötges after he asks her for sex. Böll was himself attacked by the tabloid Bild-Zeitung.

The short story 'Stranger, Bear Word to the Spartans We...' features a wounded soldier who realizes his hospital is his old school after seeing the truncated Spartan phrase on a blackboard. He has lost three limbs and whispers 'Milk...' to a janitor-turned-fireman.
