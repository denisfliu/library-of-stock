Franz Schubert (1797–1828) was an Austrian Romantic composer who died at age 31 after composing over 600 lieder, nine symphonies, and a large body of chamber and piano music. His best-known works include the 'Unfinished' Symphony, the 'Great' C major Symphony, the Trout Quintet, the Death and the Maiden String Quartet, and the song cycles Winterreise and Die schöne Müllerin. His works were catalogued by Otto Deutsch (D. numbers).
Song Cycles (Winterreise, Die schöne Müllerin, Schwanengesang)

Schubert's three major song cycles set Wilhelm Müller's poetry (for Winterreise and Die schöne Müllerin) and Heinrich Heine/others (for Schwanengesang). Winterreise ('Winter Journey,' 24 songs) follows a wanderer in winter: it includes a linden tree offering rest (E-major triplets), a lullaby of the brook, and ends with the wanderer encountering a hurdy-gurdy player ('Der Leiermann,' using repeated open fifths). Die schöne Müllerin (20 songs) follows a miller's apprentice who falls in love unsuccessfully and drowns himself in the brook — the brook turns out to be the villain; the cycle ends with a brook lullaby in E major. Schwanengesang (14 songs, posthumously published) includes 'Der Doppelgänger' (B-A#-D-C# ostinato, through-composed, sets Heine's poem about a man passing his lost love's house), 'Der Atlas,' and 'Die Taubenpost' (often thought to be Schubert's last lied). Both Die schöne Müllerin and Winterreise use poetry by Wilhelm Müller.

Der Erlkönig

Schubert's most famous single lied, setting Goethe's poem about a father riding on horseback with his sick child being beckoned by the supernatural Erlking. The piano right hand plays repeated G octave triplets representing the galloping horse. The vocalist sings separate vocal ranges for the narrator, father, boy, and the Erlking; the boy's pitch rises in each successive appearance to show mounting fear. The song ends with the child dead ('war tot') after a perfect authentic cadence. It was arranged as a 'grand caprice' for solo violin by Heinrich Ernst. It is sometimes transposed down to F or E minor.

Symphony No. 8 in B minor, 'Unfinished'

Schubert's Symphony No. 8 in B minor (D. 759) is called 'Unfinished' because only two movements were completed (plus a fragmentary scherzo). The first movement opens with cellos and basses playing B–C#–D–B before an oboe and clarinet theme. The second movement (Andante con moto) features a solo clarinet in E major/C-sharp minor, with the theme passing to oboe. Brian Newbould proposed using the B minor entr'acte from Schubert's Rosamunde incidental music as a finale. The score was discovered in the possession of Schubert's brother. Susan McClary gave a queer reading of the E major second movement in Queering the Pitch. Robert Schumann praised its 'heavenly length.' Columbia Records ran a 1928 completion competition.

Symphony No. 9 in C major, 'Great'

The 'Great' C major Symphony (D. 944) is Schubert's ninth and final symphony, praised by Robert Schumann for its 'heavenly length.' Its introductory theme is stated by two unison French horns playing long C–D–E–A–B–C. A chorale for cellos and violas follows. The A minor slow movement begins with seven measures of staccato string eighth notes followed by an extended oboe solo. The finale's development begins with an E-flat major quotation of the 'Ode to Joy'; its second theme features violins using ricochet in an ostinato of a quarter note plus three eighth triplets. The symphony was revived by Mendelssohn after Schumann brought him the score. Liszt planned but never completed a transcription. The 'Little' C major Symphony is No. 6; there is no standard No. 7.

Piano Quintet in A major, 'Trout' (D. 667)

The Trout Quintet replaces the second violin with a double bass (inspired by Hummel's Op. 87, which uses the same unusual instrumentation). It was commissioned by Sylvester Paumgartner in Steyr, where Schubert vacationed with baritone Johann Michael Vogl (who premiered many of Schubert's works). The fourth of five movements is a set of variations in D major on the lied 'Die Forelle' (originally in D-flat major). A 1969 documentary featured Zubin Mehta as performer rather than conductor.

String Quartet No. 14 in D minor, 'Death and the Maiden'

The 'Death and the Maiden' quartet (D. 810) is named for the second movement, a theme and variations based on Schubert's earlier lied 'Der Tod und das Mädchen' (D. 531). The first movement opens with unison D, then fortissimo triplets, before modulating to F major. The finale is a D minor Presto tarantella (marked Prestissimo). It is preceded by the 'Rosamunde' Quartet (No. 13 in A minor, which reuses the incidental music to Helmina von Chézy's Rosamunde). The first movement of No. 14 opens with a 'long D in octaves, after which the second violin and viola play a triplet down to G.'

Wanderer Fantasy (D. 760)

The Wanderer Fantasy in C major (D. 760, Op. 15) is based on the lied 'Der Wanderer' — specifically the line 'The sun seems so cold to me here.' Its main rhythmic cell is 'long, short short, long, short short' on the tonic. Schubert said 'the devil may play it' in reference to its difficulty. It was dedicated to Carl Emanuel Liebenberg. Liszt orchestrated it (after Schubert's comment). Sviatoslav Richter recorded a famously slow performance of a G major sonata nicknamed 'Fantasy' but the Wanderer Fantasy itself opens with thundering C major chords in 'long, short-short' rhythm.

String Quintet in C major (D. 956)

Schubert's only string quintet (D. 956) adds a second cello rather than a second viola (unlike Mozart's quintets). It is in C major and ends with the whole ensemble playing an unusual D-flat grace note resolving down to C. The second movement is Schubert's most famous adagio. The first movement features one cello holding a G while another chromatically descends to transition to an E-flat major second theme. David Finckel rejoined the Emerson String Quartet to perform it at the ensemble's farewell concerts. Schubert said after hearing Beethoven's 14th String Quartet: 'After this, what is left for us to write?'

Last Piano Sonatas (D. 958–960) →

Schubert's last three Piano Sonatas (D. 958 in C minor, D. 959 in A major, D. 960 in B-flat major) are often performed as a trilogy. The B-flat major Sonata (No. 21, D. 960) is the final one and opens with a mysterious low G-flat trill eight measures in (Alex Ross called it the 'Trill of Doom'); its C-sharp minor slow movement unusually ends in major; the rondo finale begins with a loud G octave in the left hand. Andras Schiff gave an interview titled 'The Trill of Doom' analyzing the trill. Sviatoslav Richter recorded a famously slow performance of a G major sonata nicknamed 'Fantasy.' The C major sonata (Reliquie) was mistakenly thought to be Schubert's last.

General / Biographical

Austrian composer (1797–1828) who died at age 31 from typhoid fever. He wrote over 600 lieder, nine symphonies, and vast chamber and piano music. His works were catalogued by Otto Deutsch using 'D.' numbers. He was possibly gay — Maynard Solomon identified a close circle of male intimates including Eduard von Bauernfeld (the 'peacocks of Benvenuto Cellini' theory). He expanded the impromptu form (invented by Jan Václav Vořišek/Vorisek) and wrote 8 impromptus in two sets and 6 Moments Musicaux. He added a second violin to Beethoven's Septet to create his Octet in F (commissioned by Ferdinand Troyer for the Schuppanzigh quartet). After hearing Beethoven's 14th String Quartet, he asked 'After this, what is left for us to write?'

Summary of Facts

Franz Schubert (1797–1828) was an Austrian Romantic composer who died at age 31 yet produced over 600 lieder, nine symphonies, and a vast body of chamber and piano music. His works are catalogued by Otto Deutsch using 'D.' numbers.

Schubert's song cycles are central to quizbowl. Winterreise (24 songs on Wilhelm Müller poems) follows a wanderer in winter; key moments include the linden tree song (E-major triplets in the accompaniment), and the cycle ends with the narrator meeting a hurdy-gurdy player ('Der Leiermann') whose instrument is depicted by repeated open fifths. Die schöne Müllerin (20 songs, also on Müller) follows a miller's apprentice who falls for a woman and drowns himself in the brook — the brook is revealed as the villain, and it sings the cycle's final lullaby in E major. Schwanengesang (14 songs, posthumously published) includes 'Der Doppelgänger' (B–A#–D–C# ostinato, setting Heine) and 'Die Taubenpost' (often called Schubert's last lied, with an accompaniment quoting the pigeon's call).

Der Erlkönig (D. 328), setting Goethe, features the piano's right hand repeating rapid G octave triplets to portray a galloping horse; the vocalist must sing four distinct registers for narrator, father, child, and the Erlking; the child's pitches rise with each terrified plea. The child is dead at the end.

The Symphony No. 8 in B minor ('Unfinished,' D. 759) has only two completed movements (plus a fragmentary scherzo). Its first movement opens with cellos and basses on B–C#–D–B before an oboe/clarinet theme. Brian Newbould proposed using the B minor entr'acte from Rosamunde as a finale. The score was recovered from Schubert's brother. The Symphony No. 9 in C major ('Great,' D. 944) is praised by Robert Schumann for 'heavenly length.' Its introduction is stated by two unison French horns on C–D–E–A–B–C; the A minor slow movement begins with staccato string eighths before an oboe solo; the finale's development quotes the 'Ode to Joy' in E-flat major. Mendelssohn revived it after Schumann brought him the score.

The Piano Quintet in A major ('Trout,' D. 667) replaces the second violin with a double bass, following Hummel's Op. 87. Commissioned by Sylvester Paumgartner in Steyr during a vacation with baritone Johann Michael Vogl, its fourth movement is a set of D-major variations on the lied 'Die Forelle.' The String Quartet No. 14 in D minor ('Death and the Maiden,' D. 810) quotes the earlier lied in its second-movement variations; its finale is a Presto tarantella; it is preceded in composition by the 'Rosamunde' Quartet (No. 13, which reuses Rosamunde incidental music). The String Quintet in C major (D. 956) adds a second cello (unusual for the genre), ends with a D-flat grace note resolving to C, and its second movement is considered Schubert's finest adagio.

The Wanderer Fantasy in C major (D. 760) is based on the lied 'Der Wanderer' (the line 'The sun seems so cold to me here' generates its main rhythmic cell: long-short-short). Schubert said 'the devil may play it'; Liszt later orchestrated it. The last three Piano Sonatas (D. 958–960 in C minor, A major, B-flat major) are often performed as a trilogy; the B-flat major final sonata has a mysterious G-flat bass trill eight measures in, which Alex Ross called the 'Trill of Doom.' Schubert wrote eight impromptus in two sets and six Moments Musicaux, expanding the form invented by Jan Václav Vořišek. His Octet in F was commissioned by Ferdinand Troyer and adds a second violin to Beethoven's Septet instrumentation.
