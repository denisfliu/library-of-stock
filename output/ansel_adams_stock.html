General / Biographical

Ansel Adams (1902–1984) was an American photographer best known for black-and-white landscape photography of Yosemite and the American West. He was associated with the Sierra Club and used photography to promote conservation. Alfred Stieglitz hosted one of Adams's first exhibitions in 1936. He photographed a rose petal on driftwood after meeting Paul Strand. He created the collection The Parmelian Prints of the High Sierras (with help from Albert Bender). His landscapes include The Tetons and Snake River (Grand Teton National Park), Mount Williamson, Clearing Winter Storm, Moon and Half Dome, and White House Ruin in Canyon de Chelly. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Ansel Adams (1902–1984) was an American photographer celebrated for his black-and-white landscape photography of Yosemite National Park and the American West. He is one of the most recognized names in photography history, strongly associated with both artistic and conservation photography.

His most iconic photograph is Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico, captured after Adams pulled off Route 84 near Hernandez. The moon is placed in Zone VII and Adams progressively increased contrast in the darkroom to make it stand out. Nearly as famous is Monolith, the Face of Half Dome, published in his first commercial collection The Parmelian Prints of the High Sierras (supported by Albert Bender). This image was taken from a perch called the 'Diving Board' using a deep-red filter to create a jet-black sky.

Adams co-developed the Zone System with Fred Archer, a method for standardizing exposure and brightness in photography that rates tonal values from zero to ten on a black-and-white contrast scale. The system was inspired by the Hurter and Driffield actinograph. Adams also co-founded Group f/64, a San Francisco-based photography collective named for the f/64 aperture setting (which produces maximum depth of field and sharpness on large-format cameras). Other members included Edward Weston, Imogen Cunningham, and Willard Van Dyke.

Adams's first published book was Taos Pueblo, co-written with the poet Mary Austin (Mary Hunter Austin). He met Paul Strand during this project documenting Taos Pueblo culture, and Strand inspired Adams to move away from Pictorialism toward straight photography. In 1936, Alfred Stieglitz hosted one of Adams's first major exhibitions in New York. Adams also photographed a rose petal on a piece of driftwood after meeting Strand.

During World War II, Adams was invited by camp director Ralph Merritt to photograph the Manzanar Japanese-American internment camp in California. This work was published as Born Free and Equal (1944), documenting the everyday lives of Japanese-Americans at Manzanar — including images of women doing calisthenics, people playing baseball, the Richard/Tom Kobayashi family farming cabbages, and a white cemetery monument 'outlined against mountains.' Adams's baseball photographs from Manzanar later inspired Mike Mandel to invite Adams and 133 other photographers to pose as baseball players.

Other notable works include The Tetons and Snake River (a Grand Teton National Park landscape), Mount Williamson (included in the Family of Man exhibition), Clearing Winter Storm (Yosemite), and Moon and Half Dome. He also photographed the White House Ruin in Canyon de Chelly National Monument, Arizona. Adams praised Alfred Stieglitz's cloud photograph series ('Equivalents') for their musical quality.
