Czech abstract painter, co-founder of Orphism with Robert and Sonia Delaunay. Known for colorful geometric and disc paintings inspired by Newton's color wheel and stained glass at Chartres Cathedral. A Theosophist influenced by the Thought-Forms book of Besant and Leadbeater. Wrote Creation in the Plastic Arts. Apollinaire coined 'Orphism' upon seeing his work.

Summary of Facts

František Kupka (1871-1957) was a Czech abstract painter who, along with Robert and Sonia Delaunay, co-founded the Orphism movement (an offshoot of Cubism focused on pure abstraction and bright colors). Guillaume Apollinaire coined the term 'Orphism' upon seeing Kupka's work. Orphism derived its concept of 'simultanism' from Michel Eugene Chevreul's color theory. Kupka was also part of Jacques Villon's Section d'Or (Golden Section) group. He was a Theosophist, influenced by Annie Besant and C.W. Leadbeater's Thought-Forms book, and this spiritual involvement partly motivated his abstraction. He wrote the treatise Creation in the Plastic Arts, arguing that different colors provoke different emotions.

His best-known series is Discs of Newton (Study for Fugue in Two Colors), featuring multicolored circular forms and colorful off-center circles that decompose sunlight into seven colored circles, inspired by Newton's 1704 color wheel. His painting The Cathedral, part of a series titled Vertical and Diagonal Planes, was inspired by light shining through stained glass at Chartres Cathedral (the Blue Virgin window and rose windows), using thin blue and red rectilinear shapes and blue parallelograms to evoke stained glass. Piano Keyboard/Lake, depicting a lake melding with a piano, marks his transition away from representational art. The Yellow Scale is an unusual self-portrait dominated by the title color, in which he slumps back in a chair holding a book and raising a cigarette.

Other works include The Wave (a red-haired girl on a rock facing the sea), The Beginning of Life (giant lily pads with two orbs, one containing a fetus), Planes By Colors (an exception to his non-representational style, featuring a reclining female nude), and The Musician Follot (experimenting with color and vertical lines before Amphora: Fugue in Two Colors). He also painted a portrait of his wife 'among verticals.' He enrolled in the Foreign Legion during WWI, and his wife attempted to accompany him on a march to Picardy but was arrested and sent back to Paris.
