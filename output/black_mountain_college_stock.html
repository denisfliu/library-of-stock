Summary of Facts

Black Mountain College was an experimental liberal arts college in North Carolina, founded in 1933 and known for integrating art into everyday life and interdisciplinary studies. Josef and Anni Albers came to the college after the Nazis shut down the Bauhaus, with Philip Johnson helping them secure positions. Josef ran the painting program for a decade, taught a Werklehre class emphasizing 'fingertip touch for materials,' and created his Homage to the Square series, inspired by trips to Mexico to study pre-Columbian art. Tom Wolfe mockingly described Josef's paper exercises where he praised a simple tent over Gothic castles for using 'the soul of paper.' Anni Albers became the first textile designer to have a solo MoMA exhibition in 1949, wrote On Designing and On Weaving, and created Six Prayers to memorialize Holocaust victims. Matiere exercises at the college led to leaf studies, and Pre-Columbian ruins inspired the Variant/Adobe series.

Notable students and faculty included Robert Rauschenberg (known for his combines), who was introduced to photograms by Susan Weil at the college. John Cage was on faculty and created Theater Piece No. 1, a happening, at the school. Merce Cunningham founded his dance company there. Ruth Asawa created looped-wire hangings after watching basket makers in Toluca, Mexico. M.C. Richards translated Erik Satie's The Ruse of Medusa for a performance. The college also attracted Walter Gropius and Willem de Kooning.

The Black Mountain poets, centered at the college, were a significant literary movement. Charles Olson pioneered 'projective verse' and 'composition by field' in his 1950 essay, and Robert Creeley, Denise Levertov, and Robert Duncan were also associated with the group. The movement influenced the British Poetry Revival of the 1960s and 70s.
