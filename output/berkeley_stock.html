Irish bishop and philosopher of subjective idealism (immaterialism), known for the maxim 'to be is to be perceived' (esse est percipi). Author of A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge, Three Dialogues Between Hylas and Philonous, The Analyst (attacking calculus), and Alciphron. Developed the 'master argument' against mind-independent objects and collapsed Locke's distinction between primary and secondary qualities.
Subjective Idealism and the Master Argument

Berkeley's central philosophical position is subjective idealism (immaterialism): reality exists only in the mind, summarized as 'esse est percipi' (to be is to be perceived). The 'master argument' (name coined by Andre Gallois, called 'The Worst Argument in the World' by David Stove) argues that mind-independent objects cannot exist because one cannot conceive of them without conceiving. Berkeley used examples of trees in a park and books in a closet. Samuel Johnson famously refuted it by kicking a stone and declaring 'I refute it thus.' Berkeley collapsed Locke's distinction between primary and secondary qualities by arguing that heat and color are experienced as pleasure and pain. Ronald Knox parodied the view in two limericks about God 'in the Quad.'

Three Dialogues Between Hylas and Philonous

Berkeley's 1713 work presents his idealism through a dialogue between Philonous ('lover of mind,' representing Berkeley) and Hylas ('matter,' usually interpreted as representing Locke). Philonous argues that observable qualities like heat are not different from pleasure and pain, and that mind-independent material substance does not exist. The dialogues use the hot/cold hand plunged into water to demonstrate the absurdity of materialism. The work establishes mind-independent existence as consistent with God's creation by tying all existence to God's perceptions.

The Analyst and Critique of Calculus

Berkeley's The Analyst (1734) attacks the foundations of infinitesimal calculus, ridiculing derivatives as 'ghosts of departed quantities.' Addressed to an 'infidel mathematician' (possibly Halley or Newton), it questions whether it is 'an effect of thinking' that mathematicians admire Newton's fluxions but deride his religion. James Jurin's Geometry no Friend to Infidelity attacked Berkeley for 'horrid calumny' against mathematicians.

Other Works and General / Biographical

Berkeley's other works include De Motu (argued inanimate objects should not be attributed solicitation/conation; physical forces like gravity are 'mathematical hypotheses'), An Essay Towards a New Theory of Vision (the 'heterogeneity thesis' that sight and touch detect distinct objects), Alciphron (dialogue attacking freethinkers like Mandeville and Shaftesbury; includes theological argument for God from vision), The Querist (392 pointed questions about Irish economics), and Siris (advocated tar-water). He tried to found a college in Bermuda and defended Anglicanism in Rhode Island. Lenin argued that Ernst Mach 'merely repeats' Berkeley. His idealism partially inspired Ernst Mach's empirio-criticism.

Summary of Facts

George Berkeley (1685-1753) is a consistently clued philosopher whose central idea — subjective idealism or immaterialism — dominates virtually every question. The giveaway is always 'to be is to be perceived' (esse est percipi), usually followed by identification as an Irish bishop.

Berkeley's 'master argument' (named by Andre Gallois, called 'The Worst Argument in the World' by David Stove) contends that mind-independent objects cannot exist because one cannot conceive of an unconceived object. He used examples of trees in a park and books in a closet. Samuel Johnson's famous stone-kicking refutation is the most common secondary clue. Berkeley collapsed Locke's primary/secondary quality distinction by arguing that qualities like heat and color are experienced as pleasure and pain.

Three Dialogues Between Hylas and Philonous presents the debate between Philonous (Berkeley, 'lover of mind') and Hylas (representing Locke/matter). A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge (1710) first argued that matter does not exist.

The Analyst attacked calculus, calling derivatives 'ghosts of departed quantities' and addressing an 'infidel mathematician.' De Motu argued physical forces are merely 'mathematical hypotheses.' An Essay Towards a New Theory of Vision proposed the 'heterogeneity thesis' that sight and touch detect incommensurable objects. Alciphron attacked freethinkers like Mandeville and Shaftesbury.

Berkeley is connected to several bonus-level facts: Hume said his ideas 'admit of no answer and produce no conviction'; G. E. Moore's 'Refutation of Idealism' targeted esse est percipi; Lenin argued Mach 'merely repeats' Berkeley; and Berkeley advocated tar-water in Siris and tried to found a college in Bermuda.
