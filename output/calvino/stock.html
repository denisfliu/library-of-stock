Summary of Facts

Italo Calvino (Italo Giovanni Calvino Mameli) was an Italian postmodernist author associated with the Oulipo literary group, known for their strict mathematical writing constraints. He used mathematics to sequence many of his works.

Cosmicomics is his most frequently clued collection, narrated by the immortal primordial being Qfwfq (spelled Q-F-W-F-Q). "The Distance of the Moon" is the opening and most clued story, set during a time when the moon was so close to Earth that people climbed ladders to it to collect moon-milk. The narrator's deaf cousin (the Deaf One) is the best at climbing and extracting milk from the moon's scales. Captain Vhd Vhd's wife (Mrs. Vhd Vhd) gets trapped on the moon; she stays because she loves the Deaf One, who prods the moon away with a pole. The story ends with Mrs. Vhd Vhd playing the harp on the Moon. A love triangle develops between Qfwfq, the Captain's wife, and the deaf cousin. In "Without Colors," the world gains color for the first time, and Qfwfq's love interest Ayl retreats into the earth. In "All at One Point," life was "packed like sardines" in a singularity before the Big Bang, which happens when Mrs. Ph(i)Nk0 offers to make noodles for everyone. In "The Form of Space," Qfwfq and Lieutenant Fenimore both love Ursula as they travel on parallel paths. In "The Light-Years," the narrator responds "WHAT OF IT?" to a sign saying "I SAW YOU" and creates signs to draw attention to good and bad actions. In "How Much Shall We Bet?," characters bet atoms on far-future events. The collection's stories open with scientific epigraphs. The companion collection t zero includes the title story about a hunter frozen in the moment his arrow flies toward a pouncing lion.

If on a winter's night a traveler is Calvino's celebrated second-person metafictional novel. It opens "You are about to begin reading Italo Calvino's new novel." The protagonist ("you, the Reader") encounters Ludmilla at a bookstore after buying a misprinted copy of Calvino's book. The odd-numbered chapters are written in second person about the Reader's Experience, while the even-numbered chapters present excerpts from various novels that cut off at the climax. The writer Silas Flannery is translated by the chaos-inducing Ermes Marana, who introduces bizarre errors. The Organization of Apocryphal Power is interested in Flannery's works. The fictional countries Cimmeria (near the Gulf of Bothnia) and the Cimbrian People's Republic appear. Professor Uzzi-Tuzii teaches Cimmerian literature. The Reader eventually marries Ludmilla (whose sister is Lotaria). "Zeno of Elea" is used as a codeword during a luggage exchange in the first chapter. A character accidentally has sex with Madame Miyagi while pursuing her daughter Makiko.

Invisible Cities is framed as a conversation between Marco Polo and Kublai Khan, in which Polo describes 55 cities named after women (Isidora, Diomira, Dorothea, Ersilia, Despina, Isaura). The cities are described as entities that "like dreams, are made of desires and fears." Khan wonders if his empire is "nothing but a zodiac of the mind's phantasm" while playing chess. Polo uses chess pieces to describe the cities. The novel ends with the line about the "inferno of the living" being "what is already here." Section titles follow a sine wave pattern. Calvino patterned this after Oulipo's mathematical constraints.

The Our Ancestors trilogy consists of: The Cloven Viscount (Medardo of Terralba is split into good and evil halves by a cannonball), The Baron in the Trees (Cosimo refuses to eat a dinner of snails prepared by his sister Battista, flees society by living in trees, hunts with his dachshund Ottimo Massimo; he hangs on a hot-air balloon at the end), and The Nonexistent Knight (the crusader Agilulf is only a set of floating armor serving as Charlemagne's paladin alongside Rambaldo).

Mr. Palomar opens with the title character trying to fully "see" a wave from a beach; he is later overcome realizing his front lawn is infinite. It is a philosophical novel divided into three sections, each with three subsections, each with three chapters.

The Castle of Crossed Destinies has its plot created by characters communicating with tarot cards.

Six Memos for the Next Millennium consists of Harvard lectures (Charles Eliot Norton lectures) about lightness, quickness, exactitude, visibility, and multiplicity. The "Lightness" lecture tells the story of Guido Cavalcanti leaping over his foes. The "Multiplicity" chapter discusses Flaubert's Bouvard and Pecuchet.

"Why Read the Classics?" proposes 14 definitions of classic books, describes them as texts "which constitute a treasured Experience" and as books that "exert a peculiar influence." He praises Candide's "narrative rapidity" and Borges's analysis of Ugolino's cannibalism in Dante.

Other works include "Under the Jaguar Sun" (Olivia obsesses over Aztec sacrifice; represents the sense of taste in an unfinished collection about the five senses), "The King Listens" (a paranoid king listens for rebellion), Italian Folktales (1956 collection of 200 folktales including "The Little Girl Sold with the Pears"), The Path to the Nest of Spiders (debut novel about orphaned cobbler's apprentice Pin and partisans in WWII), Marcovaldo, and "The Burning of the Abominable House" (Oulipo-inspired). He called for books that "take a form equivalent to the universe."
