Summary of Facts

Jules Verne (1828–1905) was a French author widely recognized as a pioneer of science fiction, best known for his prolific series Voyages Extraordinaires. In quizbowl, he is consistently identified through his major works: Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea, featuring the enigmatic Captain Nemo (revealed in The Mysterious Island as Prince Dakkar) and his submarine the Nautilus; Around the World in Eighty Days, in which the impeccably precise Phileas Fogg travels the globe with his valet Passepartout, rescues the Indian princess Aouda, and outwits Detective (Inspector) Fix while winning his bet at the Reform Club; Journey to the Center of the Earth, in which Axel and Professor Otto Lidenbrock follow a cryptic message from Arne Saknussemm into an Icelandic volcano; and From the Earth to the Moon, in which the Baltimore Gun Club fires a projectile to the moon from a cannon called the Columbiad, located at Stone's Hill in Florida. The Mysterious Island serves as a sequel to both Twenty Thousand Leagues and (by inspiration) to Edgar Allan Poe's work — Verne also wrote An Antarctic Mystery (The Sphinx of the Ice Fields), a direct sequel to Poe's Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym, narrated by Jeorling aboard the ship Halbrane. Verne's connection to film is frequently clued: Georges Méliès made A Trip to the Moon and The Impossible Voyage inspired by his works, and Czech filmmaker Karel Zeman's Invention for Destruction adapted his novels. Journalist Nellie Bly visited Verne in Amiens during her 1889 record-breaking circumnavigation. Verne's works were primarily aimed at adolescent readers, and he set several novels in the United States (Florida in particular) despite never visiting the American South.
