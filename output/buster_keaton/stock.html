American silent film actor and director (1895–1966), known as the 'Great Stone Face' for his deadpan expression. Major films include The General, Steamboat Bill Jr., and Sherlock Jr. Known for performing his own dangerous stunts. Gilles Deleuze analyzed his use of 'trajectories' and 'machines.' Made his debut alongside Fatty Arbuckle in The Butcher Boy.
Steamboat Bill Jr. (1928)

The most famous scene: a cyclone causes the facade of a house to fall around Keaton's character, who miraculously survives because of the placement of an open window — his shoes were supposedly nailed to the ground so he wouldn't be tempted to move. His character (William 'Steamboat Bill' Canfield, owner of the Stonewall Jackson) wakes up to find his hospital bed exposed to the elements by a windstorm just before the scene. After the house facade, the protagonist unsuccessfully tries to cross a street and has boxes fall on his head. Dana Stevens's 2022 biography Cameraman connects the stunt metaphorically to the hurricane going through Keaton's career. Steve McQueen's 1997 film Deadpan re-creates the stunt. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Buster Keaton (1895–1966), known as the 'Great Stone Face' for his deadpan expression. Always did his own stunts. Debut role was in The Butcher Boy alongside Fatty Arbuckle. Coached Lucille Ball in a scene for a TV pilot (instrumental in the creation of I Love Lucy). Summary of Facts

Buster Keaton (1895–1966) was an American silent film actor and director known as the 'Great Stone Face' for his trademark deadpan expression; he always performed his own stunts. He made his debut in The Butcher Boy alongside Fatty Arbuckle. Gilles Deleuze analyzed his films, arguing that his frequent use of 'trajectories' and 'machines' creates a unique link between a small hero and a giant situation. Dana Stevens wrote a 2022 biography titled Cameraman ('and the invention of the twentieth century'). He also coached Lucille Ball in a scene for an unaired TV pilot that was instrumental in the creation of I Love Lucy.

Steamboat Bill Jr. (1928) is home to his most iconic stunt: a cyclone causes the facade of a house to fall around his character, who survives because of the placement of an open window — his shoes were supposedly nailed to the ground so he wouldn't be tempted to move. Just before the house scene, his character (the owner of the Stonewall Jackson) wakes up in a hospital to find his bed exposed to the elements. After the house falls, the protagonist unsuccessfully tries to cross a street and has boxes fall on his head. Dana Stevens connects this stunt metaphorically to the hurricane going through Keaton's career. Steve McQueen's 1997 film Deadpan re-creates the stunt (the title 'Deadpan' references Keaton's persona).

The General (1926) features Keaton as Johnnie Gray, a Civil War engineer who pursues Union army spies (who stole his locomotive The General) in the locomotive The Texas. He smuggles his girlfriend Annabelle Lee in a burlap sack full of shoes. The film ends with Johnnie embracing Annabelle with his left hand while saluting passing soldiers with his right. A famous scene shows a bridge collapsing under the weight of The Texas.

Sherlock Jr. (1924) features Keaton as a film projectionist; a villain called 'the local sheik' blames the theft of a pocket watch on him. In a dream sequence, the protagonist falls asleep and enters the movie he's projecting. He uses trick pool shots to avoid an explosive number 13 ball (having already replaced it).
