The Greatest Happiness Principle & Core Doctrine

The foundational principle of utilitarianism, which holds that actions are right insofar as they promote the greatest happiness for the greatest number. Developed by Jeremy Bentham in An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation and refined by John Stuart Mill in his essay Utilitarianism. Bentham's felicific calculus attempted to quantify pleasure and pain, while Mill distinguished between higher and lower pleasures. The doctrine has been traced to Cesare Beccaria's On Crimes and Punishments.

Act vs. Rule Utilitarianism

The distinction between act utilitarianism (judging each individual action by its consequences) and rule utilitarianism (judging actions by whether they conform to rules that maximize utility). J.J.C. Smart advocated 'extreme' act utilitarianism, while R.M. Hare argued rule utilitarianism was preferable for ordinary people. John Rawls's 'Two Concepts of Rules' defended utilitarianism by distinguishing justifying a practice from justifying an act within it. Smart criticized rule utilitarianism as 'restricted.'

Bernard Williams's Critiques

Bernard Williams was the most prominent critic of utilitarianism in the quizbowl corpus. He debated J.J.C. Smart in the volume Utilitarianism: For and Against, attacking the doctrine of 'negative responsibility' through thought experiments like 'Jim and the Indians' and 'George and the chemical warfare researcher.' Williams coined the term 'moral luck' in a paper about Gauguin's abandonment of his family, and introduced the phrase 'one thought too many' to critique utilitarian prescriptions.

Derek Parfit & the Repugnant Conclusion

Derek Parfit's 'repugnant conclusion' (from the mere addition paradox) is a major counterexample to utilitarianism. It shows that for any population, there exists a larger population whose total existence is better even though individuals have lives barely worth living. Parfit explored this in Reasons and Persons, which also uses the teletransporter thought experiment. Parfit later attempted to show that rule utilitarianism, Kantian deontology, and Scanlon's contractualism make similar recommendations in On What Matters.

Peter Singer & Effective Altruism

Peter Singer is a major modern utilitarian, known for 'Famine, Affluence, and Morality' (1971/1972), which uses the drowning child analogy to argue that wealthy people are morally obligated to donate to humanitarian causes. Singer coined the term 'speciesism' and argued that the capability to suffer should be the basis for rights. He is closely associated with the effective altruism movement and has shifted from preference utilitarianism to hedonistic utilitarianism.

Henry Sidgwick & The Methods of Ethics

Henry Sidgwick's The Methods of Ethics (1874) analyzes three approaches to ethics: egoism, intuitionism, and utilitarianism. Sidgwick is commended by G.E. Moore for not committing the naturalistic fallacy. Sidgwick identified the paradox of hedonism and formulated the principle of 'pure equality' (each counts for one). He is frequently clued as a key figure bridging classical utilitarianism and modern moral philosophy.

Critiques and Thought Experiments

Utilitarianism faces numerous thought-experiment-based critiques. Robert Nozick's 'utility monster' shows that a being who derives vastly more pleasure could justify taking from others. The trolley problem (Philippa Foot) was originally an argument against utilitarianism. The 'demandingness objection' holds that utilitarianism demands too much of individuals. 'Roko's Basilisk' imagines an AI punishing those who did not help build it. The 'no rest' objection (Louis Pojman) argues strict utilitarianism freezes adherents into inaction.

Summary of Facts

Utilitarianism is the most heavily clued ethical theory in quizbowl. It is a consequentialist system that holds the morality of an action depends on its outcomes, specifically whether it maximizes happiness for the greatest number. The theory was founded by Jeremy Bentham, who developed the felicific calculus to quantify pleasure and pain in An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation, and who described humanity as governed by 'two sovereign masters, pain and pleasure.' John Stuart Mill refined the doctrine in his 1861 essay Utilitarianism, distinguishing between higher and lower pleasures and famously claiming 'better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied.' The phrase 'greatest happiness of the greatest number' may trace back to Cesare Beccaria's On Crimes and Punishments.

The theory comes in 'act' and 'rule' forms. Act utilitarianism judges each individual action by its consequences, while rule utilitarianism judges actions by whether they conform to rules that maximize utility. J.J.C. Smart advocated extreme act utilitarianism and criticized rule utilitarianism as 'restricted.' John Rawls defended utilitarianism from objections in 'Two Concepts of Rules.' Peter Singer pioneered 'preference' utilitarianism and is closely associated with the effective altruism movement.

Bernard Williams is the most prominent critic in the quizbowl corpus. He debated Smart in Utilitarianism: For and Against, introducing the 'Jim and the Indians' and 'George and the chemical warfare researcher' thought experiments to challenge the doctrine of 'negative responsibility.' Williams coined the term 'moral luck' and the phrase 'one thought too many.' Derek Parfit's 'repugnant conclusion' (from the mere addition paradox in Reasons and Persons) is another major counterexample, showing that utilitarianism could endorse a vastly overpopulated world of miserable people. Robert Nozick's 'utility monster' and Philippa Foot's trolley problem also serve as critiques. The 'no rest' objection holds that strict utilitarianism freezes adherents into inaction, and the 'demandingness objection' argues it asks too much.

Henry Sidgwick's The Methods of Ethics is a key text that analyzes egoism, intuitionism, and utilitarianism as three methods of ethics. G.E. Moore praised Sidgwick for not committing the naturalistic fallacy. In modern times, Peter Singer's drowning child analogy and effective altruism have made utilitarianism prominent in applied ethics.
