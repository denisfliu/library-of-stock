African-American author and anthropologist of the Harlem Renaissance. Known for her novel Their Eyes Were Watching God, her anthropological work Mules and Men, and her study of voodoo in Tell My Horse. Studied under Franz Boas at Columbia/Barnard, and was the first African-American graduate of Barnard College. Based much of her work in her hometown of Eatonville, Florida.
Their Eyes Were Watching God

Hurston's best-known novel, set in Florida, following Janie Crawford through her three marriages to Logan Killicks, Jody Starks (mayor of Eatonville), and Vergible 'Tea Cake' Woods. The novel's title comes from a passage where characters stare into darkness after a hurricane with 'triple fury' knocks out their lights. The climax involves a hurricane devastating Lake Okeechobee; Janie shoots Tea Cake in self-defense after he is bitten by a rabid dog. An epigraph from the novel ('If you are silent about your pain, they'll kill you and say you enjoyed it') appears in Carmen Maria Machado's In the Dream House.

Mules and Men

Hurston's 1935 ethnography of African-American folklore, compiled from trips to Eatonville and Polk County, Florida, as well as New Orleans. The first section has Hurston lying about being a bootlegger to sawmill employees to collect folklore, and includes 'toe-parties' and stories like a man mistaking a frog for a bogeyman. The second part, 'Hoodoo,' describes her experiences with spiritual doctors in New Orleans, including lying on a snakeskin for 69 hours. The book includes appendices on 'Prescriptions of Root Doctors' and 'Paraphernalia of Conjure.' Illustrated by Miguel Covarrubias.

Short Stories

Hurston's short fiction includes several frequently clued stories. 'Sweat' features the washerwoman Delia Jones whose abusive husband Sykes takes up with Bertha and brings home a snake to scare Delia, but the snake bites him instead. 'The Gilded Six-Bits' follows Missie May who sleeps with Otis D. Slemmons for his fake gold coins; her husband Joe later slips the coins under her pillow. 'Spunk' appeared in Alain Locke's anthology The New Negro and features Joe Kanty killed for threatening his wife's lover, who then falls onto a moving saw.

General / Biographical / Anthropological

Hurston was a central figure of the Harlem Renaissance and a trained anthropologist who studied under Franz Boas at Columbia/Barnard College. She was the first African-American graduate of Barnard. She compared anthropology to a 'spyglass' and coined the phrase 'spyglass of anthropology.' Her work was repopularized by Alice Walker's 1975 essay 'In Search of Zora Neale Hurston' in Ms. magazine, which describes searching for her unmarked grave in Fort Pierce, Florida; Walker placed a tombstone reading 'A Genius of the South' (borrowing the phrase from Jean Toomer). Hurston wrote 'How It Feels To Be Colored Me,' which describes feeling the title attribute most 'when I am thrown against a sharp white background' and compares people to bags of 'miscellany' filled by 'the Great Stuffer of Bags.' Her hometown Eatonville, Florida features in much of her work. She interviewed Cudjoe Lewis, last survivor of the Middle Passage, for the posthumously published Barracoon. She wrote Tell My Horse about Jamaican and Haitian voodoo. She was controversial for endorsing Robert Taft and opposing Brown v. Board of Education.

Summary of Facts

Zora Neale Hurston is one of the most frequently clued American authors in quizbowl, appearing across both literature and social science categories due to her dual role as a fiction writer and anthropologist. A central figure of the Harlem Renaissance, she studied under Franz Boas at Columbia University/Barnard College, where she was the first African-American graduate.

Her best-known novel, Their Eyes Were Watching God, follows Janie Crawford through three marriages in Florida: to Logan Killicks, to Jody Starks (mayor of Eatonville), and to Vergible 'Tea Cake' Woods. The climax involves a devastating hurricane at Lake Okeechobee, after which Janie shoots Tea Cake when he turns rabid from a dog bite. The novel's title derives from characters staring into darkness as the hurricane kills their lights.

Hurston's anthropological work is equally important in the clue corpus. Mules and Men (1935) compiles folklore from Eatonville, Polk County Florida, and New Orleans, including her lying about being a bootlegger to collect stories and her hoodoo initiation involving lying on a snakeskin for 69 hours. Tell My Horse documents Jamaican and Haitian voodoo, and features her meeting the first photographically documented 'zombie,' Felicia Felix-Mentor. Her posthumously published Barracoon (2018) preserves her interviews with Cudjoe Lewis, the last survivor of the Middle Passage.

Her short stories are frequently clued: 'Sweat' features the washerwoman Delia whose husband Sykes brings home a snake that kills him instead; 'The Gilded Six-Bits' follows Missie May sleeping with Otis Slemmons for fake gold coins; 'Spunk' appeared in Alain Locke's The New Negro anthology. She co-wrote the play Mule Bone with Langston Hughes, which led to their falling-out (documented in Hughes's memoir The Big Sea).

Hurston's reputation was revived by Alice Walker's 1975 essay 'In Search of Zora Neale Hurston,' which describes finding her unmarked grave in Fort Pierce, Florida and placing a tombstone reading 'A Genius of the South.' Her essay 'How It Feels To Be Colored Me' describes feeling the title attribute most 'when thrown against a sharp white background' and compares people to bags of miscellany filled by 'the Great Stuffer of Bags.' Her hometown Eatonville, Florida features prominently across her fiction and nonfiction.
