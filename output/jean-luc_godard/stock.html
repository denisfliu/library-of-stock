Pioneering French-Swiss director and central figure of the French New Wave (Nouvelle Vague). Best known for Breathless (À bout de souffle, 1960), which popularized jump cuts and features a Humphrey Bogart-obsessed criminal named Michel. Frequent collaborator with actress Anna Karina. Later films reflect increasing Marxist politics. Died in Switzerland via assisted suicide in 2022.
Breathless (À bout de souffle, 1960) →

Godard's debut feature and most iconic film. Follows Michel Poiccard (Jean-Paul Belmondo), a Humphrey Bogart-obsessed car thief and cop killer, and his American girlfriend Patricia Franchini (Jean Seberg), who sells the New York Herald Tribune. Ends with Patricia betraying Michel to police; Michel is shot in the street. Famous for pioneering jump cuts. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Jean-Luc Godard is the preeminent director of the French New Wave (Nouvelle Vague), best known for his 1960 debut feature Breathless (À bout de souffle). Breathless follows Michel Poiccard (Jean-Paul Belmondo), a Humphrey Bogart-obsessed car thief who kills a policeman after stealing a car; his girlfriend Patricia Franchini (Jean Seberg) sells the New York Herald Tribune and ultimately betrays him to the police, leading to his death in a street shooting. The film ends with the famously ambiguous word 'dégueulasse' ('disgusting' or 'puke') as Michel dies and Patricia imitates his habit of rubbing his thumb across his lips — itself an imitation of Bogart. Breathless pioneered the jump cut technique, in which cuts between shots do not preserve continuity, including a famous sequence of Jean Seberg in sunglasses riding in a convertible. The soundtrack was composed by Martial Solal.

Among Godard's other major works, Bande à part (Band of Outsiders, 1964) features Odile, Franz, and Arthur, who attempt to steal money from a rich uncle. The film is famous for two sequences: the 'Madison Dance' in a crowded cafe (the soundtrack goes silent for 36 seconds), and the trio's record-breaking run through the Louvre, during which the camera lingers on The Oath of the Horatii. The Madison dance inspired the disco scene in Pulp Fiction, and the film inspired the name of Quentin Tarantino's production company A Band Apart Films. Its title sequence shifts between three faces as white letters appear one by one, beginning with an accented 'a.'

Pierrot le Fou (1965), starring Anna Karina, ends with the protagonist painting his face blue and strapping red and yellow dynamite to his head and blowing himself up. Before this, a pen changes a word meaning 'art' to a word meaning 'death.' Sam Fuller appears in the film to describe cinema as 'a battleground — love, hate, action, violence, death — in one word: emotions.' Weekend (1967) features a notorious long traveling shot along an epic traffic jam and ends with the murderous couple Corinne and Roland captured by hippie cannibals.

Vivre sa Vie (My Life to Live, 1962) is split into twelve chapters about Nana (Karina), an aspiring actress who resorts to prostitution and dies under Raoul. The film uses a recurring slow two-bar organ theme and includes a scene where Nana in a sailor suit performs a striptease directly into the camera at a table with red-and-white tablecloths. Alphaville (1965) opens with a continuous four-minute take of Lemmy Caution taking an elevator to his hotel room and ends with Natacha von Braun declaring her love after Caution destroys a supercomputer. Two or Three Things I Know About Her (1967) is known for a long close-up of swirling coffee accompanied by Godard's whispered monologue, with the narrator claiming 'when I speak, I limit the world.'

Masculin Féminin (1966) centers on communist Paul and pop singer Madeleine, with the intertitle 'This film could be called The Children of Marx and Coca-Cola.' Le Mépris (Contempt, 1963) features Fritz Lang playing himself and was famously altered when producers forced Godard to include a nude scene with Brigitte Bardot; the film is about the making of an adaptation of The Odyssey, and includes a Michel Mourlet quote misattributed to André Bazin. La Chinoise (1967) shows copies of Mao's Little Red Book thrown at a toy tank and features the song 'Mao Mao' by Claude Channes; a character is expelled from a group for liking Johnny Guitar, and the group attempts to assassinate Mikhail Sholokhov but confuses his room.

Biographically, Godard was a central figure of the French New Wave and wrote for Cahiers du Cinéma, where he described his 'taste for quotations.' He was a frequent collaborator with Anna Karina, his then-wife, who starred in many of his 1960s films (though she reportedly rejected a minor role in Breathless). His long-time cinematographer was Raoul Coutard. His 1960s films feature extensive use of red, white, and blue intertitles. Later in his career Godard became increasingly Marxist and was a member of the Dziga Vertov Group. He married Anne Wiazemsky a year after her appearance in Robert Bresson's Au Hasard Balthazar. Isabelle Huppert starred in his 1980 film Every Man for Himself. Godard died in Switzerland via assisted suicide in 2022.
