Hungarian composer and ethnomusicologist (1881–1945). Pioneer in field recording of folk music alongside Zoltán Kodály. Known for integrating folk idioms with modernist techniques, including his 'night music' style and arch form. Major works include the Concerto for Orchestra, six String Quartets, Duke Bluebeard's Castle, The Miraculous Mandarin, Mikrokosmos, and Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta. Died of leukemia in New York.
Concerto for Orchestra

Bartók's most frequently performed work (Sz. 116, BB 123, 1943). Commissioned by Serge Koussevitzky and premiered by the Boston Symphony Orchestra. Five movements: 1. Introduzione; 2. 'Game of Pairs' (Giuoco delle coppie); 3. 'Elegia'; 4. 'Intermezzo interrotto'; 5. Finale. 'Game of Pairs' opens with a solo side drum rhythm, followed by pairs of instruments playing the same melody in parallel intervals (bassoons in sixths, oboes in thirds, etc.). 'Intermezzo interrotto' (fourth movement) quotes Franz Lehár's The Merry Widow and parodies Shostakovich's 'invasion theme' from the Leningrad Symphony; it also includes a B–F glissando for bass trombone that is unplayable on modern B-flat bass trombones (requiring a custom instrument or an F bass trombone). Title is 'paradoxical' — each instrument is treated as a soloist. Based partly on Croatian part-singing. Wikipedia

String Quartets – Overview →

Bartók wrote six String Quartets spanning his career. They are among the most important contributions to the genre in the 20th century. Key features: arch form (used in quartets 2, 4, 5); 'Bartók pizzicato' (snap pizzicato) — strings snapped against the fingerboard, especially prominent in String Quartet No. 4's finale; aksak/Bulgarian rhythms in the fifth quartet's scherzo (4+2+3)/8 time 'alla bulgarese'; each movement of the sixth quartet marked Mesto (melancholy); String Quartet No. 1's first movement is a 'funeral dirge' Lento fugue referencing Bartók's unrequited love for violinist Stefi Geyer; String Quartet No. 3 structured as Prima Parte / Seconda Parte / recapitulation / coda; fourth and fifth quartets premiered by the Kolisch Quartet. The third and fourth quartets heavily inspired Ligeti's first string quartet Metamorphoses nocturnes (sometimes called Bartók's 'seventh quartet'). Wikipedia

Duke Bluebeard's Castle

Bartók's only opera (Kékszakállú herceg vára, 1911). Two characters only: Bluebeard and his new wife Judith. Behind the seven doors of the title location, Judith discovers a bloody torture chamber, a lake of tears, and ultimately Bluebeard's three previous wives. The prologue opens with a pentatonic 'Darkness' motif (F#–A–B–C#–E). The minor second interval is known as the 'blood motif.' The leitmotif for Bluebeard is a modal form of the theme Bartók used to represent Stefi Geyer in his first Violin Concerto. Wikipedia

The Miraculous Mandarin

A one-act pantomime ballet (A csodálatos mandarin, 1918–1924). Thieves force a girl to lure passing men with 'lure dances' (lockspiels). Three men arrive; after the mandarin arrives, the thieves try to kill him (stabbing, hanging from a lamp hook) but he refuses to die until the girl accepts his love. The title character's love/desire is represented by trombone glissandi playing a descending minor third. The death scene features an offstage women's chorus singing a wordless minor third. Wikipedia

Mikrokosmos

A pedagogical piano collection of 153 pieces arranged in order of increasing difficulty (Sz. 107, BB 105, 1926–1939), dedicated to Bartók's son Péter. Six volumes; Volume VI concludes with 'Six Dances in Bulgarian Rhythm.' The first book contains 'Six Unison Melodies' and 'Parallel Motion' pieces. Square noteheads indicate harmonics. Volume IV includes a piece with three staves: two interspersed with chromatic tone clusters and one slipping in and out of C-sharp Dorian mode. Inspired George Crumb's Makrokosmos. Also inspired Anna Thorvaldsdóttir's Metacosmos. Wikipedia

Piano Concertos – Overview

Bartók wrote three piano concertos. Piano Concerto No. 1 (1926, 'piano year'): begins with low B-naturals pounded out by the soloist, then by horns and trombones at the bottom of their range, with timpani playing alternating Bs. Piano Concerto No. 2 (1930–31): begins with soloist playing a quick scale up to a trill under a trumpet fanfare; slow movement features outer Adagio for piano, muted strings, and timpani interrupted by a dizzying Presto; built in arch form; described as 'finger-breaking' and 'the single most difficult piece' by András Schiff. Piano Concerto No. 3 (1945): includes a chorale-like 'Adagio religioso' central movement; last 17 measures finished by student Tibor Serly after Bartók died; uses polymodal chromaticism; first movement analyzed as a mixture of E-Mixolydian and E-Dorian modes. General / Biographical

Béla Bartók (1881–1945) was a Hungarian composer and pioneering ethnomusicologist. He used an Edison machine to collect field recordings of folk music alongside Zoltán Kodály, including 'rain-begging' songs in Turkey; he later brought this collection to Columbia University as a professor. Called the 'father of ethnomusicology.' He wrote Contrasts for clarinet trio (for Benny Goodman) — its first movement is a verbunkos dance. Died of leukemia in New York, leaving an unfinished viola concerto (completed by student Tibor Serly; another version by son Péter). 'Night music' style: spooky ambient textures in slow movements evoking natural sounds — notably in Out of Doors fourth movement, Music for Strings third movement. 'Arch form' (ABCBA): used in String Quartets 2, 4, 5 and Piano Concerto No. 2. 'Bartók pizzicato' (snap pizzicato): strings snapped against fingerboard. 'Piano year' of 1926: Piano Sonata, Out of Doors, Nine Little Piano Pieces, Piano Concerto No. 1. Romanian Folk Dances: six short pieces based on authentic tunes collected near the Hungarian-Transylvanian border. Rhapsody No. 1: includes a cimbalom in its violin/orchestra version. Allegro barbaro uses Hungarian scales.

Summary of Facts

Béla Bartók (1881–1945) was a Hungarian composer and pioneering ethnomusicologist, considered one of the most important composers of the 20th century. Known as the 'father of ethnomusicology,' he used an Edison machine alongside his compatriot Zoltán Kodály to collect field recordings of folk music throughout Eastern Europe, the Balkans, Turkey, and North Africa; he later brought these collections to Columbia University when he emigrated to the United States. His music integrates folk idioms with modernist techniques, including his distinctive 'night music' style — spooky, ambient dissonances in slow movements that evoke natural sounds — and 'arch form' (ABCBA), used in his Piano Concerto No. 2 and String Quartets Nos. 2, 4, and 5. He also pioneered 'snap pizzicato' ('Bartók pizzicato'), in which strings are snapped against the fingerboard.

His best-known work is the Concerto for Orchestra (1943), commissioned by Sergei Koussevitzky and premiered by the Boston Symphony Orchestra. Its five movements include: the 'Game of Pairs' (second movement), which opens with a solo side drum rhythm followed by pairs of instruments playing parallel intervals (bassoons in sixths, oboes in thirds, etc.); the 'Elegia' (third movement) in his night music style; and the 'Intermezzo interrotto' (fourth movement), which quotes and parodies Shostakovich's 'invasion theme' from the Leningrad Symphony alongside a quote from Lehár's The Merry Widow. The Intermezzo also contains a B–F glissando written for an F bass trombone that is unplayable on modern B-flat bass trombones, requiring special equipment.

Bartók's six String Quartets are landmarks of the genre. The first quartet's opening Lento fugue is described as a 'funeral dirge' in Bartók's letters to violinist Stefi Geyer (his unrequited love). The fourth quartet's finale is characterized by snap pizzicato. The fifth quartet's scherzo is in (4+2+3)/8 time 'alla bulgarese,' drawing on Bulgarian aksak rhythms. All four movements of the sixth (final) quartet begin with a Mesto introduction. Bartók's third and fourth quartets heavily influenced György Ligeti's Metamorphoses nocturnes, sometimes called 'Bartók's seventh quartet.'

Duke Bluebeard's Castle is Bartók's only opera (1911), featuring just two roles: Bluebeard and his new wife Judith. Judith discovers seven doors; behind them lie a torture chamber, a lake of tears, and three previous wives. The prologue opens with the pentatonic 'Darkness' motif (F#–A–B–C#–E); the minor second interval is the 'blood motif.' The Miraculous Mandarin is a one-act pantomime ballet (1918–24) in which thieves force a girl to lure men; the title mandarin refuses to die despite being stabbed and hanged until the girl accepts his love. The mandarin's desire is represented by trombone glissandi descending a minor third.

Mikrokosmos (1926–1939) is a pedagogical collection of 153 piano pieces in order of increasing difficulty, dedicated to Bartók's son Péter. Volume VI ends with 'Six Dances in Bulgarian Rhythm.' It inspired George Crumb's Makrokosmos. Bartók's 'piano year' of 1926 produced the Piano Sonata, Out of Doors, Nine Little Piano Pieces, and Piano Concerto No. 1 — all in a percussive style. Piano Concerto No. 1 opens with low B-naturals from the soloist and timpani. Piano Concerto No. 2, built in arch form, is notoriously difficult. Piano Concerto No. 3 includes an 'Adagio religioso' central movement and was left unfinished (last 17 bars completed by his student Tibor Serly); Bartók also left an unfinished viola concerto (for William Primrose, also completed by Serly) when he died of leukemia. Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta places two string groups on opposite sides of the stage and uses xylophone and timpani glissandi for night music effects in the third movement. His Contrasts trio for clarinet, violin, and piano was written for Benny Goodman and begins with a verbunkos (Hungarian recruiting dance).
