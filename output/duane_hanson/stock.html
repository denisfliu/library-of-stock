American hyperrealist sculptor known for lifelike depictions of ordinary Americans. Used resin, fiberglass, and lifecasting techniques to create uncannily realistic figures. Major works include Tourists, Young Shopper, Supermarket Shopper, and Queenie II. Part of the photorealism/hyperrealism movement alongside Chuck Close and Richard Estes.

Summary of Facts

Duane Hanson was an American hyperrealist sculptor best known for creating uncannily lifelike sculptures of everyday Americans. He used materials like resin and fiberglass, along with lifecasting (casting directly from live models), to produce figures that blur the line between art and reality. His most frequently referenced work is Tourists (1970), depicting a camera-wielding man in a Hawaiian shirt standing beside his wife — a commentary on American middle-class culture. Supermarket Shopper (also referenced as Young Shopper) shows an overweight woman with hair curlers and varicose veins pushing a shopping cart, and is often cited in connection with the lifecasting technique. Queenie II portrays a black janitorial worker. Hanson is consistently grouped within the hyperrealism/photorealism movement, alongside painters Chuck Close and Richard Estes. He is sometimes contrasted with George Segal, who created white plaster figures, whereas Hanson's sculptures are painted and clothed to appear fully realistic.
