Arthur Schopenhauer

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
German pessimistic philosopher who wrote The World as Will and Representation, drawing on the Upanishads and Kant's thing-in-itself to argue that all reality is a manifestation of an irrational, amoral 'will to life.' Known for placing music at the top of an arts hierarchy, his compassion-based ethics in On the Basis of Morality, and his critiques of Kant and Hegel.
The Will and the Thing-in-Itself

The central idea of Schopenhauer's philosophy is that the 'will' (Wille zum Leben, or will-to-life) is the thing-in-itself, the fundamental reality underlying all phenomena. He identifies the will with Kant's thing-in-itself but rejects Kant's claim of a causal relationship between sensations and things-in-themselves, arguing they are aspects of the same reality. The will is a 'blind incessant impulse' that drives human experience, and the world of phenomena (including the human body) is the will 'objectified.' He uses the Australian bull-ant, which fights with itself after being severed in half, to illustrate the will's blind striving. Philipp Mainlander later argued only individual will exists, contra Schopenhauer's cosmic version.

Aesthetics and Music

Schopenhauer's aesthetics place music at the top of a hierarchy of the arts, arguing it is the only art form that directly copies the will itself rather than merely imitating Platonic Ideas. He calls listening to music 'an unconscious exercise in metaphysics' and extends harmonies to the animal world and melodies to the human world. Architecture and landscaping are the lowest art forms. Aesthetic contemplation releases one from the will's blind striving, and the sublime bears a hostile relation to the will. Arthur Danto built on Schopenhauer's ideas, and Schopenhauer's aesthetics heavily influenced Nietzsche's Birth of Tragedy (Apollonian/Dionysian).

Critique of Kant →

Schopenhauer engaged extensively with Kant's philosophy, praising Kant for showing how the perceiving mind separates the thing-in-itself from appearances, but criticizing many aspects. He rejected eleven of Kant's twelve categories as redundant (retaining only causality), attacked Kant's schemata, criticized proofs of Euclid's parallel postulate, and argued Kant failed to distinguish abstract from intuitive knowledge. His appendix criticizing Kant had a Voltaire quote attached. Neo-Kantians were particularly interested in his critique.

On the Basis of Morality (Compassion Ethics)

On the Basis of Morality is Schopenhauer's essay on ethics arguing that compassion (mitleid) is the foundation of morality and the only true non-egoistic moral motive. He argues all moral principles express the Latin dictum 'Neminem laede' (harm no one). The essay was the only submission to a Royal Danish Society contest but was rejected by an angry Hegelian. He also wrote On the Freedom of the Will for the Royal Norwegian Society of Sciences, outlining 'physical,' 'intellectual,' and 'moral' types of freedom and taking the compatibilist position 'man can do what he wills but cannot will what he wills.'

On the Fourfold Root of the Principle of Sufficient Reason

Schopenhauer's doctoral dissertation argues that the Leibnizian principle of sufficient reason (everything must have a cause) divides into four roots: becoming, knowing, being, and willing. These are the irreducible subject-object relations that explain all phenomena. He argued coexistence is only possible through the union of time and space.

General / Biographical

Schopenhauer is identified as a German pessimist, heavily influenced by the Upanishads and Indian thought (quoting 'tat tvam asi' from the Chandogya Upanishad), a student of Fichte whose lectures he found boring, and a bitter rival of Hegel. He scheduled his lectures at the same time as Hegel's but could never enroll enough students. Thomas Mann edited an abridged version of his magnum opus. Nietzsche praised him 'as Educator' in the Untimely Meditations, and his ideas about will and representation correspond closely to the Apollonian/Dionysian. He owned a succession of poodles all named Atman/Atma, and argued about the suffering of animals.

Summary of Facts

Arthur Schopenhauer is one of the most frequently clued philosophers in quizbowl, appearing with 20 tossups and 19 bonuses in the dataset. His universal giveaway is as the German pessimist who wrote The World as Will and Representation.

His central philosophical idea is that the 'will' (Wille zum Leben, will-to-life) is the thing-in-itself, the fundamental reality underlying all phenomena. He identifies it with Kant's thing-in-itself but rejects Kant's causal account, arguing sensations and things-in-themselves are aspects of the same reality. The entire phenomenal world, including the human body, is the will 'objectified.' Natural forces like gravity and electricity represent the will's lowest grade of objectification. He illustrates the will's blind striving with the Australian bull-ant that fights itself after being cut in half, and uses the Scholastic term 'principle of individuation' (principium individuationis) for space and time. Asceticism, denying the will-to-life, is the only escape from suffering.

His aesthetics place music at the top of a hierarchy of arts because it directly copies the will itself rather than imitating Platonic Ideas. He calls music 'an unconscious exercise in metaphysics.' Aesthetic contemplation releases one from the will. The sublime bears a hostile relation to the will, and tragedy draws out its highest degree. Richard Wagner changed the Ring Cycle ending after reading Schopenhauer.

On the Basis of Morality argues compassion (mitleid) is the only non-egoistic moral motive and the foundation of ethics. Despite being the only entry, it was rejected from a Royal Danish Society contest by an angry Hegelian. He also argues all moral imperatives are hypothetical, not categorical. On the Freedom of the Will takes the compatibilist position: 'man can do what he wills but cannot will what he wills.'

His doctoral dissertation, On the Fourfold Root of the Principle of Sufficient Reason, divides the principle into four roots: becoming, knowing, being, and willing. He critiqued Kant extensively, rejecting 11 of 12 categories and retaining only causality.

Parerga and Paralipomena contains the hedgehog dilemma (intimacy), the 'Essay on Spirit-Seeing' (dream organ), and the misogynistic 'On Women.' The Art of Being Right lists 38 sarcastic debate tactics.

Biographically, he drew heavily on the Upanishads (quoting 'tat tvam asi' from the Chandogya), was a student of Fichte, despised Hegel, owned poodles named Atman, and argued for animal rights. Nietzsche praised him 'as Educator' in the Untimely Meditations.
