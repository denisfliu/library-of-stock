Summary of Facts

Robert Bridges was an English poet who served as Poet Laureate from 1913. He is best known in quizbowl for his critical study Milton's Prosody (1921), which evolved from an introduction to Paradise Lost requested by Henry Beeching. The book argues that Paradise Lost was not meant to be read exclusively in iambic pentameter, with its third part examining 'obsolete mannerisms' like recession of accent. Its first part defends the use of the term 'elision' to account for 'supernumerary syllables,' demonstrated with the phrase 'of man's first disobedience' that opens Book I of Paradise Lost. Chapter II examines the 'accentual verse' in Milton's closet drama Samson Agonistes. Bridges's 'Neo-Miltonian syllabics' — a form of free verse — contrasted with the 'sprung rhythm' of his close friend Gerard Manley Hopkins. Bridges claimed to have been 'shamefully worsted' in a 'frontal assault' on Hopkins's 'The Wreck of the Deutschland.'

Bridges and Hopkins were lifelong friends who met at Oxford and maintained an extensive correspondence. Hopkins described his 'Terrible Sonnets' (or 'sonnets of desolation') as 'the thin gleanings of a long weary while' to Bridges, though the sonnets themselves were never actually sent to him. Bridges posthumously published Hopkins's poems in a 1918 collection, bringing Hopkins wide recognition and greatly expanding his readership. This role as Hopkins's literary executor is by far the most frequently clued connection.

Bridges wrote the long poem The Testament of Beauty, which opens with the speaker stating that 'Man's reason is in such deep insolvency to sense' that we can easily be led down the 'sad pathways of Hell.' He also wrote the most popular English version of the hymn 'Jesu, Joy of Man's Desiring,' the poem 'Poor Poll' employing his syllabic verse system, and the essay 'Humdrum and Harum-Scarum' on the limitations of free verse. Bridges founded the Society for Pure English, which published dozens of reformist tracts. The Critic Yvor Winters championed Bridges, controversially claiming he and Fulke Greville were better Poets than T.S. Eliot and Edmund Spenser. Bridges was also a cousin of The Poet Digby Mackworth Dolben, whose posthumous collection Poems was published when Bridges became Poet Laureate.
