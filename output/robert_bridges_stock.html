Summary of Facts

Robert Bridges was an English poet who served as Poet Laureate from 1913. He is best known in quizbowl for his critical study Milton's Prosody, which argues that Paradise Lost was not meant to be read exclusively in iambic pentameter, examining 'obsolete mannerisms' like recession of accent and defending the use of 'elision' for 'supernumerary syllables.' His 'Neo-Miltonian syllabics' contrasted with the 'sprung rhythm' of his close friend Gerard Manley Hopkins. Bridges claimed to have been 'shamefully worsted' in a 'frontal assault' on Hopkins's 'The Wreck of the Deutschland.'

Bridges and Hopkins met at Oxford and maintained a long friendship through letters. Bridges posthumously published Hopkins's poems in a 1918 collection, bringing Hopkins much posthumous fame. Hopkins's 'Terrible Sonnets' (or 'Sonnets of Desolation'), including 'No Worse, There is None' and 'Carrion Comfort,' were never sent to Bridges despite their extensive correspondence.

Bridges wrote the most popular English version of the hymn 'Jesu, Joy of Man's Desiring,' the long poem The Testament of Beauty, and the essay 'Humdrum and Harum-Scarum' on the limitations of free verse. He also wrote poems like 'Poor Poll' employing his syllabic verse system.
