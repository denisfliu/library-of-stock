Summary of Facts

I.M. Pei (Ieoh Ming Pei, 1917–2019) was a Chinese-American architect who trained at the Harvard University Graduate School of Design and became one of the twentieth century's most celebrated modernist architects, dying at age 102. His work is characterized by bold geometric forms — particularly pyramids, triangles, and cylinders — often using glass and concrete.

His most famous work is the Louvre Pyramid in Paris, completed in 1989, which serves as the main public entrance to the Louvre Museum. Located in the Cour Napoleon, it sits between the Sully, Denon, and Richelieu wings. An urban legend holds that the pyramid has exactly 666 panes of glass, which is false. A smaller inverted pyramid also designed by Pei appears inside the museum.

The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in Cleveland, Ohio sits on the shores of Lake Erie and consists of a cylindrical tower — the lakeshore side — attached to a roughly cylindrical segment supported by a single column that rises from the water, combined with a truncated glass pyramid or 'glass tent' of two triangles leaning against a white rectangular tower. The building includes a first-floor stage and third-floor theater.

The East Building of the National Gallery of Art in Washington DC is another key work. John Russell Pope designed the West Building (main gallery), and Pei was commissioned to design the angular modernist East Building, which is connected to the West Building by Leo Villareal's light sculpture Multiverse. The building's roof once displayed Katharina Frisch's Hahn/Cock sculpture.

Other major works include the Museum of Islamic Art in Doha, Qatar, built on an offshore island; the Bank of China Tower in Hong Kong; the John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum in Boston, which houses an enormous American flag; the Fragrant Hills Hotel outside Beijing (designed low-slung with white stucco walls and grey tiles to avoid building high-rise there); Place Ville Marie in Montreal; the NCAR Mesa Laboratory in Boulder, Colorado (Pueblo-inspired, initially criticized as 'just a bunch of towers'); the John Hancock Tower in Boston (Henry N. Cobb of Pei's firm designed it; notoriously suffered falling windowpanes in strong winds); and the Christian Science Mother Church complex in Boston. He also designed a library in Columbus, Indiana with Henry Moore's Large Arch outside, built to mirror a nearby brick church's 166-foot tower. Pei drew up plans to redesign Oklahoma City in the 1960s, including the Myriad Botanical Gardens and Convention Center.
