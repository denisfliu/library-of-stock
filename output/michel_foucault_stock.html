French post-structuralist philosopher known for Discipline and Punish (panopticon, docile bodies), The History of Sexuality (biopower, the repressive hypothesis), The Order of Things (epistemes, Las Meninas analysis), and Madness and Civilization (the 'Great Confinement'). Developed the 'archaeological' and 'genealogical' methods for analyzing power, knowledge, and discourse.
Discipline and Punish

Foucault's 1975 genealogical account of Western penal systems. It begins with the public execution of Robert-Francois Damiens and observes how schools and hospitals resemble prisons like Jeremy Bentham's Panopticon. The book examines how the carceral shift coincided with a push to create 'docile bodies' — those that 'may be subjected, used, transformed and improved.' It analyzes power through hierarchical observation, normalization, and examination. Contrasts Damiens's execution with Leon Faucher's regimented timetable at Mettray. Sandra Bartky drew on Foucault to critique beauty practices as instruments of self-imposed patriarchy.

The History of Sexuality and Biopower

Foucault's multi-volume work examining how states control populations by managing bodies. The first volume, 'The Will to Knowledge,' introduces the concept of biopower (the state's control over bodies of subjects) and criticizes the 'repressive hypothesis' (that modern society represses sexuality). The second volume is 'The Use of Pleasure.' The third, 'The Care of the Self,' analyzes Artemidorus's Oneirocritica. Achille Mbembe expanded biopower into 'necropolitics' (the ability to determine who lives or dies). While researching the History of Sexuality, Foucault discovered and republished memoirs by Herculine Barbin, an intersex person. Judith Butler criticized his treatment of Barbin in Gender Trouble. Lectures like Security, Territory, Population further developed 'governmentality.'

The Order of Things and Epistemes

Subtitled 'An Archaeology of the Human Sciences,' this work opens with a 'forensic analysis' of Velazquez's Las Meninas, examining the superimposition in the model's gaze. It lays out the concept of 'epistemes' — historical groundings of knowledge within a particular epoch. It claims the 'Classical age' field of natural history has more in common with general grammar than biology (a 'great discontinuity'). Before Kant, representation was associated with thought; the Renaissance is characterized by similitude. It argues that the 'birth of man' came with 'the return of language.' The concept of 'heterotopia' first appears in its preface. Lacan's analysis of Las Meninas disagreed with Foucault's.

Madness and Civilization

Foucault's first major work, subtitled 'A History of Insanity in the Age of Reason.' It argues that the dawn of the Age of Reason led to the 'Great Confinement' of the insane, who had previously been treated with kindness during the Renaissance. It begins with an analysis of the 'Ship of Fools' (Stultifera Navis) and the Sebastian Brandt engraving. It describes how leprosy's decline made the 'ship of fools' motif commonplace. The opening of the Hopital General initiated the Great Confinement. Derrida wrote an essay pairing the cogito with the title concept, to which Foucault added an appendix 'My Body, This Paper, This Fire.' Aims to write a 'history of silence.'

The Archaeology of Knowledge and Methodology

Foucault's 1969 methodological work developing the 'archaeological method' used in The Order of Things. It examines how epistemes and 'discursive formations' are governed by subconscious rules, distinguishes between general statements and individual 'enunciations,' and argues syntax and semantics alone cannot specify meaning. The method was extended into a Foucauldian Discourse Analysis. The introduction cites Bachelard and Canguilhem (Foucault's doctoral advisor). He also used a 'genealogical' method (critiqued by Richard Rorty as fundamentally negative).

General / Biographical

French post-structuralist philosopher. Wrote The Birth of the Clinic (defining the 'medical gaze'). Influenced by Bachelard and Canguilhem (his doctoral advisor). Debated Noam Chomsky on Dutch TV in 1971 (Dutch host tried to make him wear a red wig; partly paid in hashish). Rejected label of postmodernism. Wrote 'What is an Author?' essay. Coined 'dispositif.' Borrowed Bataille's 'limit-experience.' Outlined Raymond Roussel's 'thresholds' in Death and the Labyrinth. Used parrhesia (truthful speech) in Fearless Speech, citing Apollo denying his rape of Creusa. Judith Butler drew on his methods; Wendy Brown drew on 'regulative discourses.' Habermas called him a 'crypto-normativist.' Nancy Fraser found 'empirical insights and normative confusions.' Fictionalized in several novels (Duncker's Hallucinating Foucault, Guibert's To the Friend Who Did Not Save My Life, Binet's The Seventh Function of Language). Didier Eribon wrote his biography. 1990 postscript (Deleuze) argued Foucault's analysis replaced by 'society of control.'

Summary of Facts

Michel Foucault is a French post-structuralist philosopher known for his analyses of power, knowledge, and discourse through works including Discipline and Punish, The History of Sexuality, The Order of Things, and Madness and Civilization.

Discipline and Punish (1975) provides a genealogical account of Western penal systems. It begins with the public execution of Robert-Francois Damiens and observes how modern institutions like schools and hospitals resemble prisons, particularly Jeremy Bentham's Panopticon. The book examines how the carceral shift coincided with a push to create 'docile bodies' — those that 'may be subjected, used, transformed and improved.' It analyzes power through hierarchical observation, normalization, and examination, and contrasts Damiens's execution with Leon Faucher's regimented timetable at Mettray.

The History of Sexuality is Foucault's multi-volume work examining state control of populations through managing bodies. The first volume, 'The Will to Knowledge,' introduces the concept of biopower and criticizes the 'repressive hypothesis.' Achille Mbembe expanded biopower into 'necropolitics.' While researching this work, Foucault discovered and republished memoirs by the intersex writer Herculine Barbin; Judith Butler critiqued his treatment of Barbin in Gender Trouble. Lectures like Security, Territory, Population further developed 'governmentality.'

The Order of Things, subtitled 'An Archaeology of the Human Sciences,' opens with a celebrated analysis of Velazquez's Las Meninas. It introduces the concept of epistemes — historical groundings of knowledge within particular epochs — and claims that Classical-age natural history has more in common with general grammar than biology. The concept of 'heterotopia' first appears in its preface.

Madness and Civilization argues that the Age of Reason led to the 'Great Confinement' of the insane, who had previously been treated with kindness. It begins with the 'Ship of Fools' motif and describes how the decline of leprosy made this symbol commonplace. Derrida responded with an essay pairing the cogito with madness, to which Foucault added the appendix 'My Body, This Paper, This Fire.'

The Archaeology of Knowledge develops Foucault's archaeological method, examining how discursive formations are governed by subconscious rules. He also used a genealogical method, critiqued by Richard Rorty as fundamentally negative. Habermas called him a 'crypto-normativist.'

The Birth of the Clinic defined the 'medical gaze.' He debated Chomsky in 1971 (the Dutch host tried to make him wear a red wig; he was partly paid in hashish). He rejected the label of postmodernism. He was fictionalized in multiple novels including Duncker's Hallucinating Foucault and Binet's The Seventh Function of Language.
