Carol Ann Duffy

Scottish poet. First female, first Scot, and first openly LGBT British Poet Laureate (2009-2019), succeeded by Simon Armitage. Best known for The World's Wife, a collection of dramatic monologues from the perspectives of female counterparts to famous men (Mrs. Tiresias, Anne Hathaway, Queen Herod, Elvis's Twin Sister, Little Red-Cap, Mrs. Faust, Mrs. Quasimodo, Medusa). Other major works include the collections Standing Female Nude and the T.S. Eliot Prize-winning Rapture. Notable individual poems include 'Havisham' (inspired by Great Expectations), 'Valentine' (offering an onion), 'Education for Leisure' (censored for knife crime references), and 'Last Post' (inverting Owen's 'Dulce et Decorum Est'). She adapted Everyman for the National Theatre in 2015. She was a member of the 'New Generation' school succeeding the Martian poets and had a decade-long relationship with Liverpool Poet Adrian Henri.

Summary of Facts

Carol Ann Duffy is a Scottish poet who served as British Poet Laureate from 2009 to 2019, the first woman, first Scot, and first openly LGBT person to hold the position. She was succeeded by Simon Armitage. She funded the Ted Hughes Award. In her laureate role, she wrote poems for national occasions including 'Rings' for the 2011 Royal Wedding and 'Silver Lining' about the 2010 Eyjafjallajokull eruption, describing 'the music silence summons, that Shakespeare heard.'

Her most frequently clued work is The World's Wife (1999), a collection of dramatic monologues from the perspectives of female counterparts to famous men. It opens with 'Little Red-Cap,' in which the speaker lets a wolf spot her for the sake of 'poetry' before killing the wolf with an axe ('sweet sixteen, never been, babe'). 'Anne Hathaway' features Shakespeare's wife holding 'him in the casket of my widow's head,' with the marriage bed likened to 'seas where he would dive for pearls' (alluding to Ariel's 'Full Fathom Five') and an heirloom described as 'a spinning world of forests, castles, torchlight, [and] clifftops.' 'Elvis's Twin Sister' presents a nun who thinks of her convent as 'a land of grace' and prays for 'the immortal soul of rock 'n' roll.' 'Mrs. Tiresias' describes how her husband 'went out for his walk a man / and came home female.' 'Queen Herod' features three female Magi. 'Mrs. Quasimodo' portrays a wife who 'sawed and pulled and hacked/I wanted silence back' after her husband loses interest, with a campanologist who 'fucked her underneath the gaping, stricken bells.' 'Mrs. Faust' declares 'I keep his secret still- / the clever, cunning, callous bastard / didn't have a soul to sell.' 'Medusa' describes turning a bee into 'a dull grey pebble.' 'Mrs. Rip Van Winkle' narrates finding hobbies during her husband's absence. Pontius Pilate's wife notes his fingernails 'like shells from Galilee.'

Her poem 'Havisham,' inspired by Dickens's Great Expectations, opens 'Beloved sweetheart bastard' and describes 'Love's hate behind a white veil' and 'the dress / yellowing,' ending 'Don't think it's only the heart that b-b-b-breaks.'

In 2015, she adapted the medieval morality play Everyman for the National Theatre, featuring a birthday party with the rap 'What's God Like? You're God Like.'

Her first collection Standing Female Nude includes the title poem written from the perspective of an artist's model, with the narrator imagining 'the bourgeoisie will coo // at such an image of a river-whore' and ending 'It does not look like me.' The same collection contains 'Dear Norman,' which begins 'I have turned the newspaper boy into a diver for pearls.' 'Education for Leisure' was censored in schools in 2008 for knife-crime references and begins 'Today I am going to kill something. Anything.' In response to the censorship, Duffy wrote 'Mrs. Schofield's GCSE,' referencing Romeo and Juliet's 'star-cross'd lovers' and 'Tybalt's death.' 'Valentine' offers an onion instead of 'a red rose or satin heart,' describing it as 'a moon wrapped in brown paper' whose 'platinum loops shrink to a wedding ring / if you like.' 'Last Post' inverts Wilfred Owen's 'Dulce et Decorum Est.' 'John Barleycorn' repeatedly calls the figure a 'Green Man.' 'Campaign' anthropomorphizes the UK after Brexit. The collection Rapture won the T.S. Eliot Prize.

Duffy was a member of the 'New Generation' school succeeding the Martian poets, wrote 'Warming Her Pearls,' and had a decade-long relationship with Liverpool Poet Adrian Henri.
