Summary of Facts

Rudyard Kipling is clued as an Anglo-Indian author and poet, born in India and associated with British imperialism. He is most frequently identified through his poems "If--" (ending "you'll be a Man, my son!"), "Gunga Din" (the Indian water-bearer who is "a better man than I am"), and "Recessional" (with its refrain "Lest we forget"), as well as The Jungle Book and the novel Kim.

In poetry, Kipling's Barrack-Room Ballads are a major source of clues. "If--" was written to honor Leander Starr Jameson and advises on moral character, calling Triumph and Disaster "two impostors." "Gunga Din" tells of a water-bearer abused by soldiers who nonetheless saves the narrator's life and dies doing so. "Recessional" was composed for Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee and warns against imperial hubris with lines about Nineveh and Tyre. "The White Man's Burden" advocated American colonization of the Philippines and inspired a racist Pears' Soap ad. "Mandalay" features a soldier remembering a Burmese girl by the "old Moulmein Pagoda" where "dawn comes up like thunder." "The Ballad of East and West" begins with the famous line about the twain never meeting and features the Colonel's son and the horse-thief Kamal. "Danny Deever" describes a soldier's execution. "Tommy" laments how soldiers are mistreated in peacetime. George Orwell famously called Kipling a "good bad poet."

In prose, "The Man Who Would Be King" is his most frequently clued story: two ex-soldiers, Dravot and Carnehan, try to rule Kafiristan, where Dravot is worshipped until his bride bites him and reveals he bleeds. Carnehan survives crucifixion and brings back Dravot's head. "Rikki-Tikki-Tavi" features a mongoose defeating the cobras Nag and Nagaina while Darzee's wife feigns injury. Kim follows an Irish soldier's son on the streets of Lahore who travels with a Tibetan lama and assists British intelligence in the "Great Game." Captains Courageous is about the spoiled Harvey Cheyne learning to work on Disko Troop's fishing schooner off the Grand Banks. Puck of Pook's Hill has Dan and Una meeting Puck after performing A Midsummer Night's Dream, and includes "Weland's Sword." The Just So Stories include "How the Camel Got His Hump" and "The Cat That Walked by Himself." Plain Tales from the Hills introduced recurring soldiers Learoyd, Otheris, and Mulvaney. Kipling also wrote the boarding school stories Stalky & Co. based on his time at the United Services College.
