14th-century English Franciscan friar and Scholastic philosopher, author of Summa Logicae. Best known for Ockham's razor ('entities should not be multiplied without necessity'), his nominalism about universals, his reduction of Aristotle's ten categories to substance and quality, and his theory of mental language.
Ockham's Razor (Principle of Parsimony)

Ockham's razor, also called the principle of parsimony or lex parsimoniae, is the statement that 'entities should not be multiplied without necessity.' Though widely attributed to Ockham, the exact phrasing was coined by Irish theologian John Punch. The razor has been discussed by Quine (whose 'Plato's beard' problem shows how nonbeing 'dulls' it), Paul Grice (who used a modified version to defend conversational implicature), and Walter Chatton (who created an 'anti-razor'). It frequently appears as its own answerline.

Nominalism and the Problem of Universals

Ockham is the most famous nominalist philosopher, arguing that universals exist only in the mind and are not real entities. He transitioned from a 'fictum theory' (universals as mental fictions with objective being in the soul) to an 'intellectio theory' (universals are acts of intellection themselves). He accepted abstractions like whiteness but denied they were universals. His position opposed Duns Scotus's realism.

Summa Logicae and Mental Language

Ockham's Summa Logicae (Summa of Logic) is his major work on logic, in which he develops a theory of mental language using 'natural signs' analogous to spoken language. He distinguished between intuitive and abstractive cognition, and developed a theory of supposition (personal, simple, and material). The work subordinates written and spoken language to mental language. David Chalmers wrote a paper on Ockham's 'simple connotative terms' and 'synonymy.'

General / Biographical

English Franciscan friar of the 14th century. His idealization of poverty fueled a dispute with Pope John XXII, whom he called a heretic for rejecting Apostolic Poverty. He argued against papal infallibility in Eight Questions on the Power of the Pope. He is the foremost representative of the via moderna, contrasted with the earlier via antiqua. Marilyn McCord Adams wrote a two-volume study of him. Anselm is considered the 'father' of Scholasticism, of which Ockham was a leading late figure.

Summary of Facts

William of Ockham is a 14th-century English Franciscan friar and the most famous nominalist philosopher. He is best known for Ockham's razor, the principle of parsimony commonly stated as 'entities should not be multiplied without necessity,' though the exact phrase was coined by Irish theologian John Punch. The razor has been discussed by many later thinkers: Quine's 'Plato's beard' problem shows how nonbeing 'dulls' it, Walter Chatton created an 'anti-razor,' Paul Grice used a modified version to defend conversational implicature, and Ernst Mach adapted it to physics.

Ockham's nominalism held that universals exist only in the mind and are not real entities. He developed this position in his Summa Logicae (Summa of Logic), transitioning from a 'fictum theory' (universals as mental fictions) to an 'intellectio theory' (universals as acts of intellection). He accepted abstractions like whiteness but denied they were universals. His position opposed Duns Scotus's realism. Richard Weaver later lamented Ockham's role in changing 'man's conception of reality' in Ideas Have Consequences.

Ockham reduced Aristotle's ten categories of being to just two: substance and quality (sometimes three, adding relation for Trinitarian thought). He used the example 'Socrates is similar to Plato' to demonstrate that seven of the ten categories were unnecessary. Walter Burley disagreed with this reduction.

The Summa Logicae develops a theory of mental language, building on Aristotle's idea to analyze concepts as 'natural signs' and subordinating written and spoken language to thought. Ockham distinguished between intuitive and abstractive cognition and developed a theory of supposition with personal, simple, and material types. David Chalmers's only history-of-philosophy paper addresses Ockham's theory of 'simple connotative terms' and 'synonymy.'

Biographically, Ockham's idealization of poverty fueled a major dispute with Pope John XXII, whom he called a heretic for rejecting the doctrine of Apostolic Poverty. He argued that no property rights existed in the Garden of Eden. He is the foremost representative of the via moderna. Marilyn McCord Adams wrote a two-volume study of him, and he is often considered a leading figure of Scholasticism alongside Anselm (its 'father'), Aquinas, and Scotus.
