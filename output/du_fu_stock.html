Tang Dynasty Chinese poet, known as the 'Poet-Sage' or 'Poet-Saint.' A close friend and younger contemporary of Li Bai. Best known for 'Song of the Wagons' (Ballad of the Army Carts), 'Spring View' (Spring Scene), and poems about his thatched cottage in Chengdu. Together with Li Bai, they form the 'Great Li-Du.' He had the most poems (39) in the standard Three Hundred Tang Poems anthology. Master of the lushi (regulated verse, eight-line) style. Wrote about the An Lushan Rebellion.
Song of the Wagons (Ballad of the Army Carts)

Du Fu's most frequently clued poem, depicting conscripts marching to war. Opens with wagons that 'rumble and roll' and horses that 'whinny and neigh.' Declares that 'having girls is best' because 'our sons are merely buried amid the grass.' Describes dust stirred up and hiding a bridge across the Wei river. Contains imagery of 'shed blood becoming a sea' and 'new ghosts complain and the old ghosts weep.' States 'The martial emperor's dream of expansion has no end.' Written during the An Lushan Rebellion. Wikipedia

Spring View / Spring Scene

One of Du Fu's most famous regulated-verse poems, written while he was held captive by rebels during the An Lushan Rebellion. Opens with 'the country is broken/shattered, but mountains and rivers remain.' The speaker laments his thinning white hair, now 'too sparse even to hold a hat pin/hairpin.' Contains the line 'one letter from home is worth ten thousand pieces of gold.' Greatly admired by Matsuo Basho. Wikipedia

Thatched Cottage Poems / My Cottage Unroofed by Autumn Gales

A poem describing how the autumn wind 'angrily howls' and tears the roof off his thatched cottage/hut. Du Fu had a 'thatched hut period' in Chengdu, and a museum honoring him includes a painting of the cottage. He wrote many poems from and about the cottage, including lines like 'my window contains peaks with a thousand years of ice.' The 'Autumn Meditations' were also written during this period. Wikipedia

Poems about Li Bai (Thinking of Li Bai / Dreaming of Li Bai) →

Du Fu wrote several poems about and to his friend Li Bai. 'Thinking of Li Bai at the End of the Sky' asks 'What time will the wild goose come?' and declares 'My friend, I cannot vouch for your intent.' 'Dreaming of Li Bai' begins 'Parting from the dead, I've stifled my sobs, / But this parting from the living brings me constant pain' and asks 'Caught in a net, how is it you still have wings?' In one poem, Li declares 'I'm the god of wine, Your Majesty!' when the emperor summons him.

Autumn Day in Kui Prefecture / Autumn Meditations

A poem (or series of poems) in 100 rhyming couplets. The 'third call' of an ape moves the speaker to tears. A later poet (Su Shi, after the Crow Terrace Poetry Trial) borrowed the rhymes from this poem. Also known as 'Autumn Meditations.'

Other Poems

Other Du Fu poems that appear less frequently. A poem describes coming home to see 'the little boy we've spoiled all his life... turn away in tears.' He asks drowned poets 'whether freezing water is their element.' He criticized an artist for 'only painting the flesh of horses' but elsewhere praised the 'white flesh rich like snow.' He asked 'what ten cups could make me as drunk as I always am with your love in my heart?' in 'To My Retired Friend Wei.' A hawk hovering in the air reminds the speaker he stands 'alone with a thousand sorrows.'

Summary of Facts

Du Fu is a Tang Dynasty Chinese poet known as the 'Poet-Sage' or 'Poet-Saint,' and a close friend and younger contemporary of Li Bai. Together they form the 'Great Li-Du.' He is overwhelmingly clued for 'Song of the Wagons' (Ballad of the Army Carts), which opens with wagons that 'rumble and roll' and horses that 'whinny and neigh.' The poem's most distinctive clue is 'having girls is best' because 'our sons are merely buried amid the grass.' It depicts conscripts marching to war during the An Lushan Rebellion, with imagery of dust hiding a bridge, new and old ghosts weeping, and the martial emperor's endless dream of expansion.

'Spring View' (Spring Scene) is his second most clued poem, opening with 'the country is broken/shattered, but mountains and rivers remain.' The speaker's white hair is now too sparse to hold a hairpin. It was written while Du Fu was held captive during the An Lushan Rebellion and was greatly admired by Matsuo Basho.

Du Fu's thatched cottage poems are another major cluster. The autumn wind 'angrily howls' and tears off the roof. A museum in Chengdu honors the cottage. His 'Autumn Meditations' (Autumn Day in Kui Prefecture) is a 100-couplet poem whose rhymes were later borrowed by Su Shi.

Du Fu wrote several poems to and about Li Bai: 'Thinking of Li Bai at the End of the Sky' asks 'What time will the wild goose come?'; 'Dreaming of Li Bai' asks 'Caught in a net, how is it you still have wings?'

Biographically, Du Fu had the most poems (39) in the standard Three Hundred Tang Poems anthology. He was a master of the lushi (eight-line regulated verse) form. He is also known as the 'saint of poetry' in Japan.
