Jacobean-era English playwright. Most commonly identified by Women Beware Women, A Chaste Maid in Cheapside, The Changeling (with William Rowley), and The Revenger's Tragedy (now attributed to Middleton rather than Cyril Tourneur). Collaborated with Shakespeare on Timon of Athens and possibly Macbeth and All's Well That Ends Well. Also co-wrote The Roaring Girl and The Honest Whore with Thomas Dekker.

Summary of Facts

Thomas Middleton was a Jacobean-era English playwright, most commonly identified by the plays Women Beware Women and A Chaste Maid in Cheapside, and by his collaborations with William Rowley on The Changeling. He is now generally attributed as the author of The Revenger's Tragedy, previously credited to Cyril Tourneur.

The Revenger's Tragedy is Middleton's most independently clued work (3 tossups, 7 bonuses as its own answerline). It is set in an unnamed Italian court and follows the protagonist Vindice (whose name means "revenge" in Italian). Vindice's central scheme involves tricking the sex-crazed Duke into kissing the poisoned skull of Gloriana, Vindice's dead fiancee, whom he bedecks and dresses "in tires" for the tryst. The Duke's lips are eaten away, and before dying he watches his bastard son Spurio cuckold him. Vindice disguises himself as the pander Piato to serve Lussurioso, the Duke's heir, and secures his sister Castiza for Lussurioso while discovering that his mother is subject to "frailty." He ultimately kills Lussurioso with the aid of his brother Hippolito. The brothers Ambitioso and Supervacuo accidentally kill their own brother Junior by delivering a signet ring. Vindice invokes thunder while stabbing, crying "Is there no thunder left" and "Dost know thy cue, thy big-voiced crier." The play also contains a bed trick. Its authorship dispute — long attributed to Cyril Tourneur, now attributed to Middleton — is among the most frequent giveaway-level clues. T.S. Eliot quoted from the play in "Tradition and the Individual Talent" to illustrate how the poet combines human emotions into a purer artistic emotion. The play belongs to the revenge tragedy genre influenced by Seneca, related to Kyd's Spanish Tragedy (in which the ghost of Don Andrea and a personification of Revenge form the chorus, and the mad Hieronimo seeks justice).

The Changeling, co-written with William Rowley, is set in the Spanish city of Alicante. Middleton wrote the main tragic plot set in Vermandero's castle, while Rowley wrote the comic subplot in a madhouse. In the main plot, Beatrice-Joanna induces the servant De Flores — who is obsessed with her despite her revulsion — to murder her fiance Alonzo de Piracquo by leading him through secret passages, so she can marry the Valencian nobleman Alsemero instead. De Flores cuts off Alonzo's finger to retrieve a diamond ring; in a dumbshow before the fourth act, Alonzo's ghost shows his missing finger to the murderer. De Flores rapes Beatrice as payment for the murder. To pass Alsemero's virginity test (in which she drinks from a glass inscribed with the letter "M" and yawns, sneezes, and bursts out laughing), Beatrice pays her servant Diaphanta to perform a bed trick on the wedding night. De Flores then starts a fire to murder Diaphanta and conceal the deception. Beatrice-Joanna and De Flores ultimately die together after their affair is discovered by Alsemero, while Tomazo seeks revenge for his brother Alonzo. In their first encounter, De Flores returns a dropped glove to Beatrice, who immediately takes off her other glove to signify her repulsion. De Flores later tells her: "you forget yourself; a woman dipped in blood, and talk of modesty?" In the comic madhouse subplot, Alibius confines his wife Isabella while Lollio tries to seduce her; Antonio and Franciscus feign foolishness and madness to gain access to Isabella, and are originally accused of the central murder. An insane Welshman memorably cries "Cat-whore, cat-whore! Her Parmesan, her Parmesan!" about a cat that stole his cheese.

A Chaste Maid in Cheapside is a city comedy set in Cheapside, a London thoroughfare whose bawdy reputation contributes to the play's humor (Cheapside was also the site of the Mermaid Tavern and St Mary-le-Bow with its Bow bells). The title character is Moll Yellowhammer, a goldsmith's daughter betrothed to the lecherous, heavily indebted Sir Walter Whorehound. Moll tries to escape across the Thames and gets sick in the attempt. The virile Touchwood Senior must leave his wife for fear of having another child; under the guise of administering "fertility water" (almond milk), he sleeps with the elderly Lady Kix and gets her pregnant. Touchwood Senior's brother Touchwood Junior eventually marries Moll after they stage a double funeral and spring from their coffins to be wed. Allwit is cuckolded by Sir Walter Whorehound, and later Allwit and his wife expel Sir Walter when news comes that he slew Touchwood. A goldsmith is tricked into making a wedding ring intended for his own daughter's elopement. The foolish university graduate Tim engages in a lengthy fourth-act exchange in broken Latin over whether a fool is a rational animal, speaks to his fiancee in Latin, and mistakes her Welsh for Hebrew. Tim marries a supposed heiress of "nineteen mountains." In another subplot, a woman hides a baby under mutton to trick the promoters into taking her child, and a woman escapes a locked room through a hole to the gutter, prompting the remark "A wise man for love will seek every hole."

Women Beware Women centers on Bianca and Leantio in a love quadrangle that ends with most characters dead. The play opens with Leantio bragging to his mother of his "unvalued'st purchase" of a wife. A famous scene features a chess match between Mother and Livia that mirrors a rape carried out above and offstage. The fool Ward swears not to eat "eggs in moonshine" as they give him erections. The play ends with a bloody masque in which Hippolito and Isabella's incest is punished, Leantio's death is avenged, and the Duke and Bianca's marriage ends in poisoned death.

A Game of Chess is a satirical play allegorically themed around chess, depicting the relationship between Spain and Great Britain. Middleton had significant connections to Shakespeare: he may have co-written Timon of Athens (about an overly generous Greek who becomes a bitter misanthrope after realizing his friends are parasites); scholar Gary Taylor concluded that Middleton wrote approximately eleven percent of Macbeth, particularly the Hecate scenes (Act 3 Scene 5 and Act 4 Scene 1); and he may have written part of All's Well That Ends Well. He co-wrote The Roaring Girl with Thomas Dekker, dramatizing the life of Mary Firth as Moll (both authors shared the first name Thomas), and co-wrote The Honest Whore with Dekker. His city comedy A Trick to Catch the Old One, in which Witgood brings his courtesan to London pretending she's a rich widow to set off schemes by rival usurers Lucre and Hoard, was used by Philip Massinger as the basis for A New Way to Pay Old Debts. Middleton's plays The Second Maiden's Tragedy and The Duke of Milan also feature the motif of tyrants dying after kissing poisoned objects.
