American Postmodern architect (1925–2018), known for the quip 'Less is a bore.' Authored Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture and co-authored Learning from Las Vegas with his wife Denise Scott Brown and Steven Izenour. Key buildings include the Vanna House, the Sainsbury Wing of London's National Gallery, and the Seattle Art Museum. Won the 1991 Pritzker Prize.
Vanna House (Vanna Venturi House)

House built for Venturi's mother Vanna in Philadelphia. Features an exposed chimney, a wrap-around staircase, a gently sloping roof, and a large monumental facade with a split gable. Described by critic Frederick Schwartz as 'the first Postmodern anything.' Located near the Esherick House by Louis Kahn. Venturi lived in the house after marrying Denise Scott Brown. Wikipedia

Learning from Las Vegas

1972 book co-authored by Venturi, his wife Denise Scott Brown, and Steven Izenour. Full title: 'A Significance for A&P Parking Lots, or Learning from Las Vegas.' Celebrated roadside decor and the Las Vegas strip. Divided buildings into two types: 'ducks' (where the shape represents the building's function) and 'decorated sheds.' The genesis for this work came from a study of A&P Parking Lots; Venturi led Yale students on helicopter tours dubbed the 'Great Proletarian Cultural Locomotive.' Its movement on Las Vegas neon signs was described as 'quicker than mosaic glitter.' Includes a section titled 'Space as God.' La Tourette Monastery used as an example. Led to the development of two opposing factions: the 'Greys' (who followed LfLV's principles) and the 'Whites' (who dissented — including Richard Meier and Michael Graves of the New York Five). Aron Vinegar's I am a Monument is a critical examination of this book. Wikipedia

Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture

1966 book by Venturi that attacked modernism with a call for 'messy vitality' and an eclectic style. Defined his Postmodern philosophy in contrast to Mies van der Rohe's minimalism. Its 'nonstraightforward' introduction presents a 'gentle manifesto' citing Gödel's incompleteness theorem and T.S. Eliot's analysis of 'difficult' poetry as precedents. Analyzes 'double-functioning' and 'both-and' elements of buildings. Praises Philip Johnson's Wiley House but criticizes the 'bland architecture' of his Glass House. Praises Alvar Aalto for combining 'standardization with irrationality' in his Cultural Center in Wolfsburg. Argues buildings should rely on a technique called 'poche' instead of transparency. Yale historian Vincent Scully wrote the introduction. Wikipedia

Financed by J. Irwin Miller, who championed the city (nicknamed 'Athens on the Prairie') as an international center of modernist architecture. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Robert Venturi (1925–2018) was the foremost American Postmodern architect, best known for the quip 'Less is a bore,' a pointed rebuke of Mies van der Rohe's Modernist dictum 'Less is more.' He won the Pritzker Prize in 1991, though his long-time partner and wife Denise Scott Brown refused to attend the ceremony because the prize was awarded to him alone for projects on which they had collaborated, including the Sainsbury Wing of London's National Gallery. Louis Kahn was Venturi's teacher; both worked in Philadelphia, and Venturi's most celebrated building sits near Kahn's Esherick House.

His most celebrated building is the Vanna House (Vanna Venturi House) in Philadelphia, designed for his mother Vanna. It features an exposed chimney, a wrap-around staircase, a gently sloping roof, and a monumental split-gable facade. Critic Frederick Schwartz called it 'the first Postmodern anything.' Venturi himself later lived in the house after marrying Denise Scott Brown.

Venturi co-authored two foundational texts of Postmodern architecture. Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture (1966) called for an eclectic 'messy vitality' against Modernist purity. Its 'nonstraightforward' introduction presents a 'gentle manifesto' citing Gödel's incompleteness theorem and T.S. Eliot as precedents. The book analyzes 'double-functioning' and 'both-and' elements of buildings, argues for 'poche' over transparency, praises Philip Johnson's Wiley House while criticizing his Glass House, and praises Alvar Aalto for combining 'standardization with irrationality.' Vincent Scully, the Yale architectural historian, wrote the introduction.

Learning from Las Vegas (1972, full title: A Significance for A&P Parking Lots, or Learning from Las Vegas), written with Denise Scott Brown and Steven Izenour, celebrated Las Vegas roadside commercial architecture and introduced the distinction between 'ducks' (buildings whose form represents their function) and 'decorated sheds.' The genesis came from a study of A&P parking lots; Venturi led Yale students on helicopter tours of the city (jokingly dubbed the 'Great Proletarian Cultural Locomotive'), describing the movement of neon signs as 'quicker than mosaic glitter.' The book includes a section titled 'Space as God' and uses La Tourette Monastery as an example. Its publication split the architectural world into 'Greys' (who followed its principles) and 'Whites' (who dissented, including New York Five architects Richard Meier and Michael Graves). Aron Vinegar's I am a Monument is a critical examination of that book.

Other notable projects include Fire Station No. 4 in Columbus, Indiana — financed by J. Irwin Miller, the patron who turned Columbus ('Athens on the Prairie') into a center of modernist architecture. The fire station has a giant gold '4' making the structure look like an overturned number four. The Lieb House featured a 'sailboat' semicircle window, the number nine beside the door, and had to be floated up the East River to escape New Jersey. Franklin Court in Philadelphia used two steel-tube 'ghost structures' in the shape of homes. Venturi also designed a bank in the Disney-sponsored planned town of Celebration, Florida alongside Cesar Pelli. His favorite building in America was H.H. Richardson's Sever Hall at Harvard.
