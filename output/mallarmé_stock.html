Stéphane Mallarmé

Leading French Symbolist poet, known for dense, multilayered poetry and typographical experimentation. Most commonly identified by 'The Afternoon of a Faun' (which inspired Debussy's Prelude), 'A Throw of the Dice Will Never Abolish Chance' (a visually revolutionary poem with varied typefaces and spacing), and his Tomb poems (for Poe, Baudelaire, and Gautier). Hosted the Tuesday Salons (Mardistes) and mentored Paul Valéry.

Summary of Facts

Stéphane Mallarmé was a leading French Symbolist poet known for dense, multilayered poetry and typographical experimentation. He hosted the Tuesday Salons (Mardistes) in Paris and mentored Paul Valéry, whose 'Great Silence' was catalyzed by Mallarmé's death.

'The Afternoon of a Faun' (L'Après-midi d'un faune) is his most frequently identified poem. It opens 'These nymphs, I would perpetuate them' and features a half-goat faun speaker who lusts after nymphs upon waking from a daytime nap. The speaker waits to 'see the shade you became' and asks if 'the women of your glosses are the phantoms of your fabulous desires.' It envisions Venus on the 'lava-flesh' of Mount Etna. This poem inspired Debussy's famous Prelude (with its opening flute melody C-sharp to G and back). Ravel wrote three art songs based on Mallarmé's poems, and Boulez's Pli selon pli contains three improvisations on his poems.

'A Throw of the Dice Will Never Abolish Chance' is a visually revolutionary 20-page poem using varied typefaces, font sizes, and liberal white space. It describes 'eternal circumstances / from the depth of a shipwreck' and states 'All Thought emits' the title event. It contains a constellation 'cold with neglect and disuse' near the end. Quentin Meillassoux determined the poem's 'unique Number' is 707 (also the number of words) in The Number and the Siren. Marcel Broodthaers turned it into an artist's book.

The Tomb poems (tombeaux) are sonnets of homage: 'The Tomb of Edgar Allan Poe' describes the 'Poet with a naked sword' and 'black flights of Blasphemy'; 'The Tomb of Charles Baudelaire' describes 'the buried temple through the sewer's dark' and an 'abominable statue of Anubis'; 'Funeral Toast' for Théophile Gautier calls him the 'emblem fatal.' The 'Sonnet in -yx' has six lines ending in '-yx,' opening 'pure nails raised to consecrate their onyx,' and alludes to the Pleiades as a 'septet of scintillations.'

Other works include the unfinished Herodiade (34-year poetic retelling of Salome), the conceptual project The Book (30+ years), and notes for 'A Tomb for Anatole' (his son who died age 8 of rheumatism). 'Sea Breeze' opens 'Sad flesh. And I've read all the books.' 'Azure' ends 'I am haunted. Azure! Azure! Azure! Azure!' His prose work 'Crisis in Poetry' includes 'I say: a flower' and a flower 'absent from every bouquet.'
