August Wilson

African-American playwright, author of the ten-play Pittsburgh Cycle (Century Cycle) depicting Black life in each decade of the 20th century. Most commonly identified by Fences (Troy Maxson, garbage collector, Rose, Cory, Gabriel's trumpet) and The Piano Lesson (Boy Willie, Berniece, Ghosts of the Yellow Dog, Sutter's ghost). Other plays include Ma Rainey's Black Bottom, Joe Turner's Come and Gone, Two Trains Running, Jitney, Seven Guitars, King Hedley II, Gem of the Ocean, and Radio Golf.
Fences

Wilson's most famous play, set in 1950s Pittsburgh. Troy Maxson is a garbage collector and former baseball player who couldn't break into the MLB due to the color barrier. He prevents his son Cory from playing college football, forcing him to work at A&P. Troy has an affair with Alberta, who dies in childbirth; Rose adopts their daughter Raynell. Troy's brother Gabriel has a metal plate in his head from WWII and believes he's an archangel. The play ends at Troy's funeral, where Gabriel blows his broken trumpet and the 'gates of Heaven' open as he dances. Troy tells Rose he 'planted a seed' but 'the soil was hard and rocky.' Troy refers to death as 'nothing but a fastball on the outside corner.' Bono buys a fridge after Troy finishes building the fence.

The Piano Lesson

Boy Willie wants to sell the family piano (which has the family's history carved into it by their slave ancestors) to buy Sutter's land. His sister Berniece refuses. Sutter's ghost appears and is exorcised when Berniece plays the piano. The Ghosts of the Yellow Dog (Boy Charles and four hobos burned alive in a boxcar by a lynch mob) are blamed for mysterious deaths including Sutter falling down a well. Wining Boy sells clothes to Lymon. The play features a 'magic suit.'

Ma Rainey's Black Bottom

The only Pittsburgh Cycle play not set in Pittsburgh (set in Chicago). Blues musicians wait for Ma Rainey to start a recording session; she refuses until she gets a Coca-Cola. The trumpeter Levee stabs Toledo for stepping on his Florsheim shoes. Stuttering Sylvester appears. Slow Drag. Adapted into a 2020 film starring Viola Davis and Chadwick Boseman.

Joe Turner's Come and Gone

Set in the 1910s at Seth Holly's boarding house. Herald Loomis searches for his wife Martha Pentecost after being captured and enslaved by Joe Turner for seven years. The conjure man Bynum sings a song about Joe Turner. The 'People Finder' Selig is a sheet metal seller. Herald slashes his chest and rubs blood on his face, finding his 'song of self-sufficiency.' Bynum describes following a 'shiny man.' Reuben and Zonia look at pigeons. Working title was Mill Hand's Lunch Bucket, inspired by Romare Bearden painting.

Summary of Facts

August Wilson is the most frequently clued African-American playwright in quizbowl, known for his ten-play Pittsburgh Cycle depicting Black life in each decade of the 20th century. Fences is by far the most heavily clued play: Troy Maxson is a garbage collector and former baseball player who couldn't enter the MLB due to the color barrier, prevents his son Cory from playing football, and has an affair with Alberta. Troy's brother Gabriel, brain-damaged from WWII, blows his broken trumpet at Troy's funeral and the 'gates of Heaven' open.

The Piano Lesson is the second most clued play, centering on the conflict between Boy Willie (who wants to sell the family piano to buy land) and Berniece (who refuses). The Ghosts of the Yellow Dog — Boy Charles and four hobos burned alive in a boxcar by a lynch mob — are blamed for mysterious deaths. Berniece ultimately plays the piano to exorcise Sutter's ghost.

Joe Turner's Come and Gone features Herald Loomis searching for his wife after seven years of enslavement by Joe Turner; he slashes his chest finding his 'song of self-sufficiency.' Ma Rainey's Black Bottom, uniquely set in Chicago rather than Pittsburgh, features the trumpeter Levee stabbing Toledo over Florsheim shoes. Two Trains Running features Hambone and Sterling. Seven Guitars and King Hedley II explore generational violence. Gem of the Ocean features Aunt Ester, the recurring clairvoyant who lives at 1839 Wylie Avenue (address inspired by the Amistad Revolt).
