Czech playwright and novelist, best known for R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots), the play that coined the word 'robot' (attributed to his brother Josef). Also wrote War with the Newts, The Makropulos Affair/Secret, The White Disease, The Absolute at Large, and collaborated with brother Josef on The Insect Play (Pictures from the Insects' Life). His works frequently explore themes of artificial life, rebellion of exploited servant races, immortality, and anti-war satire.
R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots)

Capek's most famous play, published in 1920, which coined the word 'robot' (the word itself credited to brother Josef, from Czech 'robota' meaning forced labor). Harry Domin manages the company that produces the title artificial people. Helena Glory, representing the Humanity League, arrives and advocates for freeing the robots. Her nurse Nana watches as Helena burns Old Rossum's formula/manuscript for making robots. The super-intelligent robot Radius is sent by Helena to the library but concludes robots are superior and leads the robot rebellion that destroys humanity. All humans are killed except Alquist, who is spared because of his hands. At the end, Alquist discovers that robots Primus and Helena have developed human-like love, and he calls them the new Adam and Eve. The robots include Sulla, Marius, Radius, Proteus, Damon, and Helena. Other characters include Fabry and Dr. Gall. Wikipedia

War with the Newts

A 1936 satirical science-fiction novel. Captain van Toch discovers intelligent newts (salamanders) on the island of Tana Masa / near Sumatra / Devil Bay. The industrialist G.H. Bondy (Gussie H. Bondy) exploits them for pearl farming and forms the Salamander Syndicate. The newts learn English (one named Andrew Scheuchzer at the London Zoo reads newspapers), build dams, invent 'lebensraum,' and eventually wage an apocalyptic war against humanity, destroying continents. The central chapter 'Up the Ladder of Civilization' uses Mr. Povondra's newspaper clippings, which include celebrities like Mae West and George Bernard Shaw commenting on whether newts have souls. The novel ends with a metafictional conversation between the Author and the Writer imagining a war against the Lemurians. An appendix satirizes academic writing by detailing the sex life of the newts. Multiple editions have introductions by Ivan Klima. Wikipedia

The Insect Play (Pictures from the Insects' Life)

A satirical play co-written with brother Josef Capek. A tramp dreams that human vices are represented by insects — dung beetles, ants, and butterflies. The tramp meets Iris and Felix, who are butterflies. Like Animal Farm, it allegorized post-World War I society. E.M. Forster compared the British fascist vs. communist conflict to a scene from this play. Adapted into Jan Svankmajer's last film. Victor Pelevin referenced it in his novel The Life of Insects. General / Biographical

Karel Capek (1890-1938) was a Czech author. His brother Josef Capek is credited with coining the word 'robot,' which Karel popularized in R.U.R. The brothers collaborated on several plays including The Insect Play and Adam the Creator. Josef designed the cover of the first edition of R.U.R. Karel also wrote the novel Hordubal, the story collection Nine Fair Tales, and The Life and Work of the Composer Foltyn (about a hack plagiarist). Romain Rolland attended a performance of R.U.R., and Capek asked him whether he had patience for his 'stupid, boring' play.

Summary of Facts

Karel Capek (1890-1938) is a Czech playwright and novelist whose works are asked about primarily through his play R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots), which coined the word 'robot,' and his satirical novel War with the Newts.

R.U.R. is Capek's most frequently clued work. The word 'robot' itself is credited to his brother Josef Capek, derived from the Czech word for forced labor. In the play, Harry Domin manages the company that produces robots. Helena Glory arrives as a representative of the Humanity League advocating for robot freedom. Her nurse Nana watches as Helena burns Old Rossum's formula for making robots. The super-intelligent robot Radius, sent by Helena to the library to learn equality, instead concludes robots are superior and leads a rebellion that destroys all of humanity except Alquist, who is spared because of his hands. At the end, Alquist discovers that robots Primus and Helena have developed human-like love, and he calls them the new Adam and Eve. Named robots include Sulla, Marius, Radius, Proteus, Damon, and Helena.

War with the Newts (1936) is a satirical science-fiction novel. Captain van Toch discovers intelligent newts near Sumatra (on Tana Masa, in Devil Bay). The industrialist G.H. Bondy exploits them for pearl farming and forms the Salamander Syndicate. One newt, Andrew Scheuchzer at the London Zoo, learns English by reading newspapers. The central chapter 'Up the Ladder of Civilization' consists of Mr. Povondra's newspaper clippings, in which celebrities like Mae West and George Bernard Shaw comment on whether newts have souls. The newts eventually destroy continents and wage apocalyptic war against humanity. The novel ends with a metafictional conversation between the Author and the Writer. An appendix satirizing academic writing details the sex life of the newts. Ivan Klima (Czech author of Love and Garbage) has written introductions for multiple editions.

The Makropulos Affair features the 300-year-old opera singer Emilia Marty (who uses pseudonyms with initials 'E.M.'), granted immortality by a parchment from Holy Roman Emperor Rudolf II. Her arrival complicates the longstanding lawsuit Gregor v. Prus. Bernard Williams wrote his famous essay on 'the tedium of immortality' in reference to this play.

The White Disease (1937) is an anti-war play satirizing Nazi Germany. A leprosy-like disease kills everyone over 45, but the Marshal doesn't care because the military is young. Doctor Galen has the only cure but refuses to share it until world peace is declared. Galen works for Sigelius at the Lilienthal Clinic and is killed by a crowd shouting 'We want war!'

The Insect Play (Pictures from the Insects' Life) was co-written with brother Josef. A tramp dreams that human vices are represented as insects — dung beetles, ants, and butterflies (including characters Iris and Felix). E.M. Forster compared the British fascist-communist conflict to a scene from it. It was adapted into Jan Svankmajer's last film and referenced by Victor Pelevin in The Life of Insects.

The Absolute at Large features engineer Rudolph Marek's invention of the Karburator, which generates residue-less energy but produces 'the absolute' as a spiritual byproduct causing worldwide strife.

Capek also wrote Hordubal, the story collection Nine Fair Tales, and The Life and Work of the Composer Foltyn. At a performance of R.U.R., he asked Romain Rolland if he had patience for his 'stupid, boring' play.
