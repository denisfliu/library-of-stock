German philosopher who wrote Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Beyond Good and Evil, On the Genealogy of Morals, The Birth of Tragedy, and The Gay Science. On the Genealogy of Morals and Master-Slave Morality

On the Genealogy of Morals is arguably Nietzsche's most analytically important work. It traces a 'revolt of the slaves' in which ressentiment inverted noble values, relabeling nobility as evil. The first essay contrasts master morality (the 'pathos of distance,' giving names) with slave morality (based on ressentiment). The second essay traces the internalization of debt into guilt. The third essay asks 'what do ascetic ideals mean?' and describes the 'ascetic priest' cultivating ressentiment among his 'sick sheep.' The work attacks Paul Ree as an 'English psychologist' and inspired both Bernard Williams's genealogical method and Foucault's 'genealogical method.' Brian Leiter wrote Nietzsche on Morality defending 'methodological naturalism.' The example of lambs complaining about birds of prey illustrates how morality separates strength from its expression.

The Birth of Tragedy and Apollonian-Dionysian

The Birth of Tragedy (1872) is Nietzsche's first published book. It contrasts the Apollonian (order, form, differentiation) with the Dionysian (chaos, ecstasy, undifferentiation) as two 'artistic impulses.' The Greeks achieved the highest art through this dichotomy. Tragedy's downfall was caused by Socratic rationalism. Midas is told by Silenus that the best thing for man is never to be born. The work promoted Wagner's operas. Nietzsche praised Aeschylus and Sophocles for combining the two impulses, and condemned Euripides for reducing the chorus and eliminating music.

Eternal Recurrence and Amor Fati

Eternal recurrence (or eternal return) is the idea that the universe keeps reforming itself. It is introduced by a demon 'stealing into your loneliest existence' in The Gay Science, where it is called 'the most burdensome thought.' Alfred Baeumler (the Nazi interpreter) elided it; Maurice Blanchot called it a 'local vertigo'; Heidegger called it 'the supreme triumph of metaphysics.' Amor fati ('love of fate') is Nietzsche's formula for human greatness and the antidote to eternal recurrence's nihilism; it means embracing anything that occurs as good or necessary. Kundera's Unbearable Lightness of Being opens by naming Nietzsche.

Thus Spoke Zarathustra and the Ubermensch

Thus Spoke Zarathustra is a philosophical novel about an Iranian prophet living in a cave with a snake and an eagle. It contains Dionysian hymns (dithyrambs), the concept of the Ubermensch (superman/overman), and the will to power. Zarathustra dreams of a child with a mirror. The first English translation by Alexander Tille predated Thomas Common's controversial 'Superman' rendering by 13 years. Walter Kaufmann translated untergehen as 'go under.' Richard Strauss wrote a famous tone poem inspired by it, with the 'C, G, C' motif used in 2001: A Space Odyssey.

Beyond Good and Evil

Beyond Good and Evil (subtitled 'Prelude to a Philosophy of the Future') opens with 'Suppose truth is a woman - what then?' It contains the chapter 'The Natural History of Morals,' calls Kant 'the great Chinaman of Konigsberg,' describes 'good Europeans,' and discusses the 'pathos of distance.' Nietzsche uses perspectivism (from Teichmuller) and argues the 'noble soul has reverence for itself.' Famous aphorisms include 'he who fights with monsters' and 'when you gaze into an abyss.' The section 'Maxims and Arrows' was expanded in Twilight of the Idols.

Untimely Meditations and Other Works

The Untimely Meditations include 'Schopenhauer as Educator' (the third), an attack on David Strauss's 'New Faith' (the first), and 'On the Use and Abuse of History for Life' (the second), which distinguishes monumental, antiquarian, and critical approaches to history. The Gay Science declares 'God is dead' via the parable of the madman with a lantern. Twilight of the Idols has 'Four Great Errors,' 'The Problem of Socrates,' 'Morality as the Enemy of Nature,' and the 'Maxims and Arrows' section. Ecce Homo has chapters like 'Why I Am So Clever' and 'Why I Am a Destiny/Fatality,' ending with Voltaire's 'ecrasez l'infame.' Daybreak contains 'thoughts on the prejudices of morality.' Human, All Too Human began Nietzsche's aphoristic style.

General / Biographical and Reception

Nietzsche is identified as a German philosopher who went insane (reportedly after seeing a horse whipped in Turin). His sister Elisabeth Forster-Nietzsche (who co-founded the Paraguayan colony Nueva Germania) posthumously edited his works to be more anti-Semitic and fascistic; Mazzino Montinari and Giorgio Colli called The Will to Power a 'historical forgery.' Walter Kaufmann rehabilitated his reputation with his book 'Philosopher, Psychologist, Antichrist,' though it has been criticized for its existentialist portrayal. Paul Ricoeur named Nietzsche, Marx, and Freud as 'masters of suspicion.' Gilles Deleuze rehabilitated Nietzsche via 'active' and 'reactive' forces. Derrida used Nietzsche's thinking as the alternative to 'Rousseauist freeplay.' He used the Tschandala concept from the Laws of Manu.

Summary of Facts

Friedrich Nietzsche is the most frequently clued philosopher in the dataset, with 22 tossups and 30 bonuses. His giveaways include authorship of Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Beyond Good and Evil, and The Gay Science, and the declaration 'God is dead.'

On the Genealogy of Morals is his most analytically rich work. Its three essays trace: (1) master morality (the 'pathos of distance') vs. slave morality (from ressentiment), using the etymology of 'schlecht'/'schlicht' and the lambs-vs-birds-of-prey image; (2) the internalization of debt into guilt; (3) the meaning of ascetic ideals, with the 'ascetic priest' cultivating ressentiment among 'sick sheep.' It attacks Paul Ree and inspired Foucault's genealogical method and Williams's Truth and Truthfulness.

The Birth of Tragedy (1872) contrasts the Apollonian (order) with the Dionysian (chaos), arguing the Greeks achieved the highest art through their combination. Tragedy declined through Socratic rationalism. Silenus tells Midas the best thing is never to be born.

Eternal recurrence (introduced by a demon in The Gay Science) is 'the most burdensome thought.' Amor fati ('love of fate'), 'the formula for human greatness,' is its antidote. Heidegger called eternal recurrence 'the supreme triumph of metaphysics.'

Thus Spoke Zarathustra features an Iranian prophet with a snake and eagle, the Ubermensch, and the will to power. The first English translation (Tille's 'Beyond-man') preceded Thomas Common's controversial 'Superman.'

Beyond Good and Evil opens with 'Suppose truth is a woman,' calls Kant 'the great Chinaman of Konigsberg,' and contains the famous abyss aphorism. The Untimely Meditations include Schopenhauer as Educator, an attack on Strauss, and the Use/Abuse of History (monumental, antiquarian, critical). Twilight of the Idols has the 'Four Great Errors' and 'Problem of Socrates.' Ecce Homo has 'Why I Am a Destiny.'

His sister Elisabeth edited his works to be more fascistic; Montinari/Colli called The Will to Power a forgery. Walter Kaufmann rehabilitated his reputation. Ricoeur named him one of three 'masters of suspicion' with Marx and Freud. Deleuze and Derrida were key French interpreters.
