German-British Baroque composer (1685–1759), born in the same year as J.S. Bach and Domenico Scarlatti. Worked as kapellmeister to the Elector of Hanover before settling in England. Composed prolifically in opera seria, oratorio, and orchestral genres. His oratorio Messiah, with its famous Hallelujah chorus, is his best-known work. Also composed Water Music, Music for the Royal Fireworks, and the coronation anthem Zadok the Priest.
Messiah

Handel's most famous work, an oratorio on the life of Jesus Christ with a libretto by Charles Jennens (HWV 56). Premiered in 1742. Divided into three parts; Part II closes with the Hallelujah chorus, during which audiences traditionally stand — a custom attributed to King George II attending a London performance. Mozart later re-orchestrated it with substantial wind additions. Marin Alsop created a gospel-inspired version in 1992. Wikipedia

Water Music

A set of three orchestral suites (HWV 348–350) written for King George I's barge trip down the River Thames. The first suite is in F major and D minor and may be the first English music to feature the French horn. The second suite (D major, trumpet-heavy) contains the famous Alla Hornpipe movement. The third suite is in G major and is flute-heavy. First recorded in original orchestration by Charles Mackerras in 1959. Wikipedia

Music for the Royal Fireworks

An outdoor suite (HWV 351) composed to celebrate the 1748 Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (end of the War of Austrian Succession). Originally for wind ensemble only (no strings), calling for 24 oboes, 12 bassoons, and 9 trumpets and 9 horns. Later rescored for full orchestra. Movements include 'La Paix' (Largo alla siciliana) and 'La Rejouissance.' The premiere structure (built by Giovanni Servandoni) caught fire. Handel later rescored it for a Foundling Hospital concert. The Harmonious Blacksmith

The nickname for the Air and Variations finale of Handel's Suite No. 5 in E major for harpsichord (HWV 430). Consists of a theme with five variations that increase in speed. The nickname may derive from the repeated 'B' pedal tone in the first variation, likened to a blacksmith's hammer. Percy Grainger was inspired by the theme to write 'Handel in the Strand.' The theme begins with the notes E, G-sharp, F-sharp, high-B. The G minor Passacaglia and D minor Sarabande from Handel's keyboard suites were arranged for violin and viola by Johan Halvorsen. General / Biographical

George Frideric Handel (1685–1759) was born in the same year as J.S. Bach and Domenico Scarlatti (the 'Class of 1685'). He worked as kapellmeister to the Elector of Hanover before settling in England. He went blind in his last years, with John Christopher Smith serving as his amanuensis. He was a pioneer of the English oratorio, which led him to abandon Italian opera after Messiah's success. His rival was Giovanni Bononcini (they were compared in John Byram's 'Tweedledum and Tweedledee' epigram). His 105-volume collected edition was mostly produced by Friedrich Chrysander in the late 19th century. He met Corelli through concerts organized by Pietro Ottoboni.

Summary of Facts

George Frideric Handel (1685–1759) was a German-born Baroque composer who spent most of his career in England. He was born in the same year as J.S. Bach and Domenico Scarlatti — a cohort sometimes called the 'Class of 1685.' Before settling in England, he worked as kapellmeister to the Elector of Hanover. He went blind in his final years, with John Christopher Smith serving as his amanuensis. In the late 19th century, his complete works were collected in a 105-volume edition largely produced by Friedrich Chrysander. His rival was the Italian composer Giovanni Bononcini; the two were famously compared in John Byram's epigram that coined the phrase 'Tweedledum and Tweedledee.' He met Arcangelo Corelli through concerts organized by Pietro Ottoboni, and modeled his own Opus 6 set of 12 concerti grossi on Corelli's.

Handel's most famous work is the oratorio Messiah (HWV 56, 1742), with a libretto by Charles Jennens. It is divided into three parts; Part II closes with the Hallelujah chorus, during which audiences traditionally stand — a custom traced to King George II at a London performance. Purely instrumental segments include the opening E minor French overture ('Sinfony') and the shepherd-inspired 'Pifa' (Pastoral Symphony) in 12/8 time, with violins ascending C–D–E–F–G–A over a bass drone. Part I contains the arias 'Comfort ye my people' and 'Ev'ry valley shall be exalted,' while the bass aria 'The trumpet shall sound' features a solo trumpet imitating the voice. Musicologist Rudolf Steglich identified the ascending fourth as a unifying motif throughout. In 1789, Mozart re-orchestrated Messiah with substantial wind additions. In 1992, Marin Alsop created a gospel-inspired version.

His orchestral output includes Water Music (three suites for King George I's barge trip down the Thames) and Music for the Royal Fireworks (commemorating the 1748 Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle, originally for wind ensemble with 24 oboes and 12 bassoons). The Royal Fireworks suite's premiere structure, built by Giovanni Servandoni, caught fire. Both works remain among the most performed pieces of the Baroque repertoire. The Water Music's D-major suite contains the famous Alla Hornpipe movement; the first suite may be the earliest English music to feature the French horn. The Royal Fireworks suite includes 'La Paix' (Largo alla siciliana) and 'La Rejouissance.'

Handel composed extensively in opera seria. His Giulio Cesare in Egitto (1724) stars Julius Caesar (premiered by castrato Senesino), who sings the hunting aria 'Va tacito e nascosto' with a natural horn obbligato; Cleopatra seduces Caesar in 'V'adoro pupille,' with a second orchestra playing offstage; and Sesto swears revenge on Tolomeo in 'Svegliatevi nel core.' His opera Serse opens with 'Ombra mai fu,' in which Xerxes addresses the shade of a plane tree — though marked larghetto, it became known as 'Handel's Largo.' Rinaldo (his first London opera) contains the aria 'Lascia ch'io pianga' for the captive Almirena, and may include the first piccolo part ever written. Other notable operas include Ariodante, Rodelinda, and Deidamia (his last Italian opera).

Among his oratorios, Israel in Egypt features extreme word painting: dotted rhythms depict frogs leaping and demisemiquavers depict flies buzzing in the plagues sequences; a bass rage aria breaks off as a two-octave descending scale depicts spear-hurling. Solomon features the sprightly 'Arrival of the Queen of Sheba' sinfonia for oboes and strings at the opening of Act 3, and the 'Nightingale Chorus' with two obbligato flutes. Judas Maccabaeus includes Simon's 'Arm, arm ye brave' and the chorus 'See, the conqu'ring hero comes.' Saul contains a 'Dead March' depicting a funeral procession. Jephtha was his last oratorio, written while going blind. His coronation anthem Zadok the Priest, written for George II, has been performed at every British coronation since.

Handel's keyboard music includes 'The Harmonious Blacksmith' — the nickname for the Air and Variations finale of his Suite No. 5 in E major, whose repeated B pedal tone was likened to a blacksmith's hammer. The G minor Passacaglia and D minor Sarabande from his keyboard suites were arranged for violin and viola by Johan Halvorsen. His Opus 6 concerti grossi (12 works) modeled on Corelli include a G minor sixth concerto with an E-flat major musette central movement, and one arranged by Arnold Schoenberg as a Concerto for String Quartet. His Opus 4 organ concertos, performed as interludes during oratorio concerts, are considered the earliest chamber organ concertos — an inventive form he pioneered incidentally through oratorio production.
