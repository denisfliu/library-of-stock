Nigerian playwright, poet, and Nobel laureate (first African to win the Nobel Prize in Literature). Best known for Death and the King's Horseman and The Lion and the Jewel. Yoruba author whose works explore ritual, colonialism, and dictatorship. Also wrote Madmen and Specialists, The Strong Breed, Kongi's Harvest, A Dance of the Forests, The Trials of Brother Jero, and the poem 'Telephone Conversation.' Translated D.O. Fagunwa's Forest of a Thousand Daemons into English. Imprisoned for 22 months during the Nigerian Civil War; wrote memoir The Man Died and poetry collection A Shuttle in the Crypt. Adapted Euripides' Bacchae with a chorus of slaves.
Death and the King's Horseman

Soyinka's most frequently clued play. Based on a real 1946 incident in Oyo. Elesin Oba, the King's Horseman, is expected to commit ritual suicide following the king's death. He tells the Praise-Singer the story of the 'Not-I Bird' (a bird that augurs death, to which everyone responds 'Not I') to prove his readiness. The 'mother of the market' Iyaloja allows him to marry and sleep with her son's fiancee before the ritual. District Officer Simon Pilkings (a British colonial officer) intervenes to stop the suicide. Pilkings and his wife horrify Amusa by dancing while wearing egungun masks. Elesin's medical-student son Olunde believes his father has died when the sound of drums changes, but discovers his father was stopped. Olunde commits suicide instead. Iyaloja reveals Olunde's body rolled in a mat to Elesin, prompting the Praise-Singer to say 'Our world is tumbling in the void of strangers.' Elesin then strangles himself with his prison chains as his unnamed Bride watches. The author's note attacks the 'facile tag' of 'clash of cultures' and highlights the 'threnodic essence' of the action. Duro Ladipo earlier dramatized the same event in Oba ko so (The King Did Not Hang). Wikipedia

The Lion and the Jewel

A play in which the chieftain Baroka ('the Lion,' the fox) and the Westernized schoolteacher Lakunle compete for the hand of the beautiful Sidi ('the Jewel') in the town of Ilujinle. Lakunle calls Sidi 'Ruth, Rachel, Esther, and Bathsheba' and refuses to pay the bride-price. Baroka feigns impotence to trick Sidi into marrying him; he ultimately seduces her. Baroka is 62 with 63 children; his main wife is Sadiku; he has his armpit hairs plucked one by one. Lakunle plays a photographer during the 'Dance of the Lost Traveller.' Four girls pretend to be wheels of a car that breaks down. A man throws a flask at a bathing girl during a performance. Women chant 'little children are our champions' after their daughters chase away a policeman. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Wole Soyinka (Akinwande Oluwole Babatunde Soyinka, b. 1934), Nigerian Yoruba playwright and poet. First African to win the Nobel Prize in Literature. Nobel lecture titled 'This Past Must Address Its Present,' discussing Nelson Mandela and criticizing Leo Frobenius's racist attribution of Yoruba art to an ancient Greek colony. Imprisoned for 22 months during Nigerian Civil War for trying to mediate peace; wrote memoir The Man Died and poetry collection A Shuttle in the Crypt (also A Big Airplane Crashed Into the Earth). Scribbled memoir on toilet paper. Edited Poems of Black Africa anthology. Translated D.O. Fagunwa's Forest of a Thousand Daemons. Adapted Euripides' Bacchae (subtitled 'A Communion Rite') with a chorus of slaves. Linked 'restorative' deity and Nietzsche's Apollonian-Dionysian dichotomy to origin of tragedy in essay on the 'Fourth Stage.' Wrote 'Neo-Tarzanism' and book about 'Myth, Literature, and the African World.' Also wrote Kongi's Harvest (dictator President Kongi demands Oba Danlola's yams; Segi presents her father's head at the Festival of the New Yam), A Dance of the Forests (official play of Nigerian independence; prostitute Rola learns she was a queen in past life; Emperor Mata Kharibu and Lady Tortoise), The Trials of Brother Jero / Jero's Metamorphosis, The Interpreters (novel), Chronicles from the Land of the Happiest People on Earth (2021 novel; Dr. Menka; 'Gong of Four'; human body parts), Requiem for a Futurologist, and A Play of Giants (satirizes Idi Amin as Dr. Kamini). Poem 'Telephone Conversation' — landlady asks 'ARE YOU DARK? OR VERY LIGHT?' in all caps; speaker says 'I hate a wasted journey - I am African'; ends with 'Wouldn't you rather see for yourself?' Also wrote the oriki poem 'Dawn' praising the orisha Ogun. Assembled the 1960 Masks acting troupe for Nigerian independence. Published in the journal Black Orpheus.

Summary of Facts

Wole Soyinka (b. 1934) is a Nigerian Yoruba playwright and poet, the first African to win the Nobel Prize in Literature. He is asked about primarily through his plays Death and the King's Horseman and The Lion and the Jewel.

Death and the King's Horseman, based on a real 1946 incident in Oyo, is Soyinka's most clued work. Elesin Oba, the King's Horseman, must commit ritual suicide following the king's death. He tells the Praise-Singer the story of the 'Not-I Bird' to prove his readiness. The 'mother of the market' Iyaloja allows him to marry her son's fiancee before the ritual. District Officer Simon Pilkings intervenes to stop the suicide. Elesin's medical-student son Olunde commits suicide instead; Iyaloja reveals Olunde's body. Elesin then strangles himself with his prison chains. The author's note attacks the 'facile tag' of 'clash of cultures.'

The Lion and the Jewel follows the competition between chieftain Baroka ('the Lion,' who feigns impotence) and the Westernized teacher Lakunle for the beautiful Sidi ('the Jewel') in Ilujinle. Lakunle refuses to pay the bride-price and calls Sidi 'Ruth, Rachel, Esther, and Bathsheba.' The play features the 'Dance of the Lost Traveller' in which Lakunle plays a photographer.

Other major works include Madmen and Specialists (Dr. Bero imprisons his cannibalistic father, The Old Man, and the cult of As), The Strong Breed (Eman replaces a mute boy as the village 'carrier' and is killed), Kongi's Harvest (dictator President Kongi demands Oba Danlola's yams; Segi presents her father's head), A Dance of the Forests (official play of Nigerian independence; Rola was a queen in past life), and the Brother Jero plays.

His poem 'Telephone Conversation' features a landlady asking 'ARE YOU DARK? OR VERY LIGHT?' in all caps. He translated D.O. Fagunwa's Forest of a Thousand Daemons. He adapted Euripides' Bacchae with a chorus of slaves ('A Communion Rite'). His Nobel lecture 'This Past Must Address Its Present' discussed Mandela and criticized Frobenius. He was imprisoned for 22 months during the Nigerian Civil War, writing the memoir The Man Died on toilet paper. His essay on the 'Fourth Stage' links Nietzsche's Apollonian-Dionysian dichotomy to African tragedy. His 2021 novel Chronicles from the Land of the Happiest People on Earth was his first novel in 48 years.
