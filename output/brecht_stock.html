Summary of Facts

Bertolt Brecht was a German playwright and the leading practitioner of 'epic theatre,' a theatrical paradigm he developed in opposition to Aristotelian drama. Central to his theory is the 'alienation effect' (also called the 'distancing effect' or 'estrangement effect'), which he coined after studying Chinese theater and the performances of Mei Lanfang. He elaborated this concept in the essay 'On Chinese Acting' (also called 'Alienation Effects in Chinese Acting'), noting how Chinese theater's lack of a fourth wall and strict conventionality created the effect he sought. He described the gestus technique, where characters embody social commentary through gestures and express attitudes physically. In 'A Short Organum for the Theatre,' he described epic theatre's principles. His 'non-Aristotelian drama' eliminated catharsis and the purging of emotions through empathy, creating 'untragic heroes' instead. Frederic Jameson noted that Brecht stripped back the 'well-made production' by forcing actors to question their decisions through verbalizing alternative choices. Walter Benjamin wrote the essay 'What is Epic Theater?' discussing Brecht's theories. Roland Barthes contrasted epic theatre with the 'Zhdanovian impasse' of propaganda.

Brecht's most frequently clued work is Mother Courage and Her Children, set during the Thirty Years' War. The title character, Anna Fierling, sells mouldy bread to soldiers and loses her children Kattrin, Eilif, and Swiss Cheese during the war. Helene Weigel, Brecht's second wife, originated the title role and delivered a famous 'silent scream' accompanied by increasingly loud bass and treble pitch after refusing to acknowledge the body of Swiss Cheese. The play ends with Mother Courage singing a lullaby and hitching herself to a wagon. The prostitute Yvette sings the 'Fraternization Song' in this play. George Steiner praised Weigel's performance for 'making the audience bow their heads as if they had been hit by a blast of wind.' Margarete Steffin was a collaborator on this play.

The Threepenny Opera, co-written with Kurt Weill and Elisabeth Hauptmann, is based on John Gay's The Beggar's Opera. It features 'Mack the Knife' (about 'old Macheath' who wears 'fancy gloves' and keeps a 'jack-knife' 'out of sight') and 'Pirate Jenny,' whose singer is bribed by Polly Peachum to turn over Polly's husband. Brecht fought bitterly with director G.W. Pabst over the screenplay of a 1931 film adaptation starring Rudolf Forster and Lotte Lenya. The Threepenny Novel features the one-legged George Fewkoombey, who murders William Coax with his own prosthetic leg before being falsely convicted and hanged for the drowning of Mary Sawyer. Elisabeth Hauptmann is sometimes purported to have written the lion's share of The Threepenny Opera.

The Caucasian Chalk Circle was inspired by a Chinese zaju gong'an drama by Li Xingdao in which Judge Bao Zheng identifies a child's mother using a chalk circle. Brecht adapted this into a play-within-a-play set during a conflict between two communes. The maid Grusha refuses to pull the child Michael out of the title chalk circle, and the judge Azdak makes a Solomonic ruling in her favor over Natella. A Singer performs works about 'The Four Generals' and 'The Rotten Bridge.' An earlier version, 'The Augsburg Chalk Circle,' is a short story set in Augsburg where Frau Zingli spends so much time packing her clothes she abandons her son. The play ends by declaring 'There must be happy endings, must, must, must!' and at one point an actor laments 'There must, there must, be some end that would fit.' Athol Fugard's Circle Players were named for this play.

The Good Person of Szechwan features Shen Teh, who is given funds by three gods to buy a tobacco shop. She puts on a mask and impersonates her male cousin Shui Ta, singing in a man's voice 'without trampling down twelve others / You cannot help one poor man.' A water-carrier Goes to Shui Ta to get lodging for some gods. At the end, three gods sing, smile, and wave as they float away on a cloud, ignoring cries for help. It was adapted in Ghana by Mohammed ben Abdallah as Land of a Million Magicians, and John Ruganda translated it into Swahili.

The Resistible Rise of Arturo Ui is a 'parable play' allegorizing the rise of the Nazis, set among Chicago gangsters expanding their corrupt cauliflower empire into Cicero, Illinois. The central character seduces Ignatius Dullfeet's wife Betty in a scene paralleling Richard III. The protagonist frames Fish for a warehouse fire while working with the Cauliflower Trust. The epilogue declares 'don't rejoice too soon at your escape- / The womb he crawled from still is going strong.'

Saint Joan of the Stockyards features the title character as a lieutenant in a group called Black Straw Hats who organizes with meat packers against their boss, the meatpacking tycoon Pierpont Mauler, who becomes disaffected with capitalism after meeting her. A song declares 'you folk that are poor / are an army vast and grand' sung to seventy-thousand workers outside the Lennox packing plant.

Life of Galileo was revised in collaboration with actor Charles Laughton in the months after the atomic bombing of Japan. The title character's daughter Virginia dies of dysentery in a convent after being unable to find a husband. The play ends with a copy of the Discorsi being smuggled out of Florence by Andrea Sarti.

Brecht also developed 'learning-plays' (Lehrstucke), including The Baden-Baden Lesson on Consent, which caused a riot after a scene where the clown Smith is dismembered by friends to ease his pain, and The Measures Taken, where four Communist agents explain their decision to murder a comrade during a Chinese revolution. In the Jungle of Cities is set in 1912 Chicago and depicts Shlink and Garga engaging in a metaphorical boxing match. Mr Puntila and His Man Matti, set in Finland, was developed with Margarete Steffin from a translation of the Finnish play The Sawdust Princess; the chauffeur Matti holds up a landowner's plan to marry his daughter Eva to a diplomat.

Brecht's poetry includes 'Alabama Song' ('show me the way / to the next whiskey bar,' later recorded by The Doors), 'Reminiscence of Marie A.' (about a kiss under a plum tree while a white cloud passed overhead), 'Children's Hymn' (a response to Germany's national anthem), and 'How Fortunate the Man with None' (observing that 'sagacious Solomon,' 'courageous Caesar,' and 'honest Socrates' all met ignominious fates). He also suggested the idea for The Cradle Will Rock by Marc Blitzstein and was its dedicatee. His only Hollywood screenplay was Hangmen Also Die, directed by a fellow German expat. He wrote the slapstick film Mysteries of a Barbershop starring Karl Valentin, a main influence on his work. The minimalist staging of Lars von Trier's Dogville was influenced by Brecht. Brecht's work has been widely adapted in Africa, with Rose Mbowa creating Mother Uganda and Her Children, Wole Soyinka adapting The Threepenny Opera as Opera Wonyosi, and Augusto Boal developing the Theater of the Oppressed as an alternative to Brecht's didacticism.
