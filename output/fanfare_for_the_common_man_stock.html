Summary of Facts

Fanfare for the Common Man is a short orchestral fanfare composed in 1942 by Aaron Copland. It was commissioned by Cincinnati Symphony Orchestra conductor Eugene Goossens as part of a wartime project in which 18 American composers each wrote a fanfare for the orchestra. Copland drew his title and inspiration from a speech by Vice President Henry Wallace titled 'The Price of Free World Victory,' which proclaimed the dawning of the 'Century of the Common Man.' The piece is scored exclusively for brass and percussion: four French horns, three trumpets, three trombones, tuba, timpani, bass drum, and tam-tam. It opens with five bars of solemn percussion (the tam-tam or gong, followed by repeated bass drum and timpani strokes), which are then followed by the three trumpets entering in unison on the now-iconic ascending motif of F–B-flat–F. The work premiered in Cincinnati on Tax Day 1943. The distinctive F–B-flat–F motif culminates in a D major chord. Copland later incorporated the fanfare's melody into the finale of his Third Symphony (1946), where Leonard Bernstein later cut 12 bars of the movement. The piece also acquired a notable rock adaptation: Copland himself confessed that he preferred his own version to the cover recorded by Emerson, Lake and Palmer. Joan Tower composed a series of six fanfares collectively titled Fanfare for the Uncommon Woman over roughly 30 years in direct response to Copland's work, using the same instrumentation. Tower's series is dedicated to conductor Marin Alsop (then of the Baltimore Symphony Orchestra), who became the first woman to conduct the Last Night of the Proms in 2013. The piece is widely considered emblematic of Copland's 'Americana' style.
