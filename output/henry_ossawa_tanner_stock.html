African-American painter, student of Thomas Eakins, known for genre scenes of Black life and religious paintings. Best known for The Banjo Lesson (elderly man teaching a child), The Thankful Poor (father and son praying), and The Annunciation (Mary gazes at Gabriel depicted as a beam of light). Studied in Paris under Jean-Paul Laurens; patronized by Rodman Wanamaker.

                Summary of Facts

                Henry Ossawa Tanner (1859-1937) was an African-American painter born in Philadelphia, best known for genre scenes of Black life and later religious paintings. He studied under Thomas Eakins in Philadelphia before moving to Paris, where he studied under Jean-Paul Laurens. A Cincinnati bishop, Joseph Hartzell, bought his entire exhibition, enabling his move to France. He was later patronized by businessman Rodman Wanamaker.

                Tanner's most famous painting is The Banjo Lesson (1893), depicting an elderly man teaching a young barefooted boy on his lap to play the banjo, illuminated by yellow firelight on the right and blue window light on the left, with a frying pan and hat on the wooden floor. Its composition resembles an illustration he made for Ruth McEnery Stuart's story 'Uncle Tim's Compromise on Christmas,' and it may have been inspired by Paul Dunbar's literature or a trip to the Blue Ridge Mountains.

                The Thankful Poor depicts the same pair (father/grandfather and son/child) praying before a meal, with light from a single curtained window highlighting empty plates, a pitcher, and a bare wall. Both paintings draw on Tanner's faith and African-American identity.

                After returning to Paris and traveling to the Middle East (Palestine, the Levant), Tanner focused on religious scenes. His Annunciation (1898) innovatively depicts Gabriel as a sliver of golden light rather than a traditional figure, with Mary sitting on a rumpled bed. It hangs in the Philadelphia Museum of Art next to Eakins's Gross Clinic. His Resurrection of Lazarus won a third-place medal at the Paris Salon in 1897, innovatively emphasizing Lazarus's response. Other religious works include Daniel in the Lions' Den (a figure cast in a rectangular beam of light), The Flight into Egypt (a faceless man in white holding a lantern), and Nicodemus and Jesus.

                Later works display his namesake palette of 'Tanner blues,' seen in The Arch (1919), The Seine (1902), The Disciples See Christ Walking on the Water, and Sand Dunes at Sunset (which hangs in the White House). He also painted a portrait of Booker T. Washington for the National Portrait Gallery.
