Philosophical Letters (Letters on the English)

The Philosophical Letters, also known as Letters on the English or Letters Concerning the English Nation, discuss topics including the Quakers, Isaac Newton, and the trial of Charles I. They praise Francis Bacon as the 'father of experimental philosophy' and also address the problem of evil, including a rejection of Pascal's theological approach. Voltaire had to flee Paris after publishing them, as they praised inoculation and attacked French religion.

Philosophical Dictionary

The Philosophical Dictionary (Dictionnaire philosophique portatif) was published anonymously in Geneva by Gabriel Grasset. It has a similar form to a work by Bayle, and outlines virtue via a dialogue between 'The Honest Man' and 'The Excrement' after telling the story of Brutus committing suicide. In the Dictionary, Voltaire argues against the Judeo-Christian conception of God but affirms an impersonal deity, thus espousing deism.

Treatise on Tolerance

Voltaire's Treatise on Tolerance (Traite sur la Tolerance) uses the case of Jean Calas, a Calvinist who was innocent but killed on the wheel, as evidence that all men should be brothers. This work is frequently clued alongside his broader attacks on religious dogmatism.

Summary of Facts

Voltaire (Francois-Marie Arouet) appears in quizbowl as one of the central figures of the French Enlightenment. His most common giveaway is as the author of Candide, a satire of Leibniz's Theodicy via the character Pangloss, who maintains that we live in the 'best of all possible worlds' despite catastrophes like syphilis and the Lisbon earthquake. The novel concludes with the injunction to 'cultivate our garden.'

His Philosophical Letters (Letters on the English) are the next most frequently clued work. They discuss the Quakers, praise Isaac Newton's rational method (popularizing the apple myth) over Descartes, address the problem of evil (rejecting Pascal's approach in the 25th letter), and praise Francis Bacon as the 'father of experimental philosophy.' Their publication forced Voltaire to flee Paris because they praised inoculation and attacked French religion.

The Philosophical Dictionary, published anonymously in Geneva by Gabriel Grasset, is structured similarly to Bayle's dictionary and argues for deism against the Judeo-Christian God. It contains the dialogue between 'The Honest Man' and 'The Excrement.' The Treatise on Tolerance uses the case of Jean Calas, an innocent Calvinist killed on the wheel, to argue for religious tolerance.

Key biographical identifiers include: the famous quote 'if God did not exist, it would be necessary to invent him' (from his commentary on The Three Imposters, inverted by Bakunin); the rallying cry 'crush the infamous' (ecrasez l'infame); his relationship with Emilie du Chatelet, who translated Newton's Principia; his correspondence with Frederick the Great; his imprisonment in the Bastille; and his argument that China had 'perfected moral science.' David Friedrich Strauss published lectures on Voltaire, and Voltaire's essay on universal history is credited with founding social history.
