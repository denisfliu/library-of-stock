Summary of Facts

Christina Rossetti was a Victorian English poet and a member of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood, to which she contributed through the journal The Germ. She was the sister of Dante Gabriel Rossetti, who wrote "The Blessed Damozel" and the sonnet sequence The House of Life.

Her most frequently clued work is "Goblin Market," a long narrative poem about the sisters Laura and Lizzie. The goblin merchants cry "Come buy, come buy" as they peddle their orchard fruits, including "Apples and quinces, / Lemons and oranges, / Plump unpeck'd cherries, / Melons and raspberries." Laura purchases the forbidden fruit using "a golden curl" and "a tear more rare than pearl," and afterward "knew not was it night or day / As she turned home alone." The goblins are described as animal-like — "cat-like and rat-like, ratel- and wombat-like" — possibly referencing Dante Gabriel Rossetti's unusual pet wombat. The poem includes the proverbial line "One may lead a horse to water, twenty cannot make him drink." When the goblins try to force fruit into Lizzie's mouth (kicking and pinching her), she resists and then tells Laura, "Did you miss me? / Come and kiss me" and "Eat me, drink me, love me" — language noted for its sexual charge. A cautionary figure, Jeanie, "fell sick and died" and lies in "her grave, who should have been a bride." The poem ends with the moral "for there is no friend like a sister." Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar's The Madwoman in the Attic compares Laura's desire for fruit to Keats's "La Belle Dame Sans Merci." The poem is also referenced in Jeanette Winterson's Oranges Are Not the Only Fruit, in which Elsie Norris reads it to the narrator during a hospital stay.

"In the Bleak Midwinter" is a poem adapted into a well-known Christmas carol, originally titled "A Christmas Carol." It describes a time when "frosty wind make moan" and "Earth stood hard as iron," with the repeated image of "snow on snow, snow on snow." It includes the line "Our God, heaven cannot hold Him / Nor earth sustain" and "If I were a Shepherd, I would bring a lamb."

"Remember" is a Petrarchan sonnet in which the speaker imagines herself "Gone far away into the silent land" and counsels her beloved that it is "Better by far you should forget and smile" than to remember and be sad, fearing that "darkness and corruption" would leave only "A vestige of the thoughts that once I had."

"The Convent Threshold" opens with the guilty speaker addressing a former lover: "There's blood between us, love," recalling "You sinned with me a pleasant sin." The speaker dreams of a "fire-footed" "spirit with transfigured face" that screams "Give me light," and of a spirit that "drank a sea" in search of knowledge only "to learn that all is small / Save love, for love is all in all." The speaker exhorts her love to "look up" to Heaven.

"Up-Hill" is a short dialogue poem consisting entirely of one-line questions and answers, including "Does the road wind uphill all the way?" and "Will there be beds for me and all who seek?"

"A Birthday" compares the speaker's heart to "a singing bird," "an apple tree," and "a rainbow shell," concluding it is "gladder than all these" because "my love has come to me." "Amor Mundi" describes a couple who "went together in glowing August weather" to "escape...by never turning back," while the narrator sees "a scaled and hooded worm" lurking in flowers. "Maude Clare" features a love triangle involving Nell, Sir Thomas, and the title character, with the exchange "Take my share of a fickle heart / Mine of a paltry love" and the bride's reply "I'll love him till he loves me best."

Other less frequently clued poems include "The One Certainty" ("there is nothing new under the sun" / "All things are vanity"), "Symbols" (crushing three "failed" eggs; God "waiteth for thy fruits in vain"), "De Profundis" (not Oscar Wilde's — "I strain my heart, I stretch my hands, / and catch at hope"), "The Skylark" ("A singing speck above the corn"), and "The P.R.B.," a sonnet comparing the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood's decline to "luscious fruit [that] must fall when over-ripe." Rossetti was reportedly gifted a pickled mouse, a fact mentioned in Winterson's novel.
