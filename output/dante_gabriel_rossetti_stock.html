English Pre-Raphaelite poet and painter. Most identified by the poem 'The Blessed Damozel,' the sonnet sequence The House of Life, the painting Beata Beatrix (modeled on his wife Elizabeth Siddal), and Proserpine. Brother of Christina Rossetti, who wrote 'Goblin Market.' Translated Francois Villon's 'Where are the snows of yesteryear?' Discovered a one-penny clearance copy of the Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam.
The Blessed Damozel (poem and painting)

Rossetti's most famous poem and a later painting based on it. The title figure leans out 'from the gold bar of Heaven' waiting for her lover. She has 'three lilies in her hand' and 'the stars in her hair were seven.' Hair 'yellow like ripe corn.' Inspired by the death of Elizabeth Siddal; intended as a reversal of Poe's 'The Raven.' Published in The Germ (initially titled Songs of the Art Catholic).

Summary of Facts

Dante Gabriel Rossetti was an English poet and painter who co-founded the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood with John Everett Millais and William Holman Hunt, publishing their work in the magazine The Germ. He is the brother of Christina Rossetti, author of 'Goblin Market,' and illustrated her poem's frontispiece with wombats (he also kept a pet wombat). William Michael Rossetti, another brother, edited The Germ.

Rossetti's most famous poem, 'The Blessed Damozel,' describes a woman who 'lean'd out / From the gold bar of Heaven' with 'three lilies in her hand' and 'the stars in her hair were seven,' with hair 'yellow like ripe corn.' It was intended as a reversal of Poe's 'The Raven' and was first published in Songs of the Art Catholic (later reprinted in The Germ), where italics were replaced with parentheticals. The last line reads 'I heard her tears.' Friend Thomas Gordon Hake objected to its calling Earth a 'fretful midge.' The 1877 painting, modeled on Alexa Wilding and commissioned by William Graham, includes a predella showing a black-cloaked man leaning back with clasped hands. Five handmaidens 'whose names are five sweet symphonies' — Cecily, Gertrude, Magdalen, Margaret, Rosalys — surround the lady Mary.

His wife Elizabeth Siddal (who caught pneumonia modeling in a cold bathtub for Millais's Ophelia) died of a laudanum overdose. Rossetti buried his poems in her coffin, then had it exhumed seven years later to retrieve them; her hair had reportedly grown. Her death inspired Beata Beatrix, depicting a green-clad figure with eyes clenched shut, a sundial casting its shadow on 9, and a red dove with a white poppy in its mouth, inspired by Dante's La Vita Nuova.

The House of Life is his sonnet sequence, opening with 'A sonnet is a moment's monument' and comparing a sonnet to 'a coin' whose 'face reveals / The soul.' It contains 'Willowwood,' is bookended by 'Love Enthroned' and 'The One Hope,' and includes 'A Superscription' ('Look into my face; my name is Might-Have-Been'). 'Nuptial Sleep' was omitted from the 1881 edition after Robert Buchanan coined 'the fleshly school of poetry'; Rossetti responded with 'The Stealthy School of Criticism.'

'Jenny' is a dramatic monologue about a 'lazy laughing languid' prostitute 'fond of a kiss and fond of a guinea' (based on mistress Fanny Cornforth), in which the speaker places gold coins in her hair. Lady Lilith depicts a woman brushing 'enchanted hair' in a mirror with white roses, poppies, and foxgloves; Alexa Wilding's face was repainted onto the canvas. It contrasts with Sibylla Palmifera as 'Body's Beauty' vs 'Soul's Beauty,' and was part of six 'stunners' for Frederick Leyland's drawing room. Proserpine shows a woman biting a pomegranate in a blue-green robe, modeled on Jane Morris.

Ecce Ancilla Domini! is an Annunciation where lily-bearing Gabriel wakes a sleepy Mary, both in plain white robes. Other paintings include The Beloved (bride's red hair, four women and Black child), La Ghirlandata (red-haired harpist flanked by red-haired angels), Monna Vanna, The Bower Meadow, The Day Dream (Jane Morris in a sycamore tree), Veronica Veronese, and the unfinished Found. He was obsessed with painting red-haired women and designed stained glass of Tristram and Isolde with William Morris.

Rossetti translated Francois Villon, coining 'yesteryear' in 'Where are the snows of yesteryear?' He discovered a one-penny clearance copy of Fitzgerald's Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam and gave a copy to Robert Browning. Max Beerbohm drew 23 caricatures about him. Evelyn Waugh wrote his biography. He transcribed William Blake's notebook (containing the first draft of 'The Tyger'). He appears in John Fowles's The French Lieutenant's Woman employing Sarah Woodruff as an assistant in Chelsea. Pound's 'Hugh Selwyn Mauberley' mentions Siddal's 'topaz eyes' in its 'Yeux Glacques' section.
