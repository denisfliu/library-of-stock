Dante Gabriel Rossetti

English Pre-Raphaelite poet and painter. Most identified by the poem 'The Blessed Damozel,' the sonnet sequence The House of Life, the painting Beata Beatrix (modeled on his wife Elizabeth Siddal), and Proserpine. Brother of Christina Rossetti, who wrote 'Goblin Market.' Translated Francois Villon's 'Where are the snows of yesteryear?'
The Blessed Damozel (poem and painting)

Rossetti's most famous poem and a later painting based on it. The title figure leans out 'from the gold bar of Heaven' waiting for her lover. She has 'three lilies in her hand' and 'the stars in her hair were seven.' Hair 'yellow like ripe corn.' Inspired by the death of Elizabeth Siddal; intended as a reversal of Poe's 'The Raven.' Published in The Germ.

Summary of Facts

Dante Gabriel Rossetti was an English poet and painter who co-founded the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood with John Everett Millais and William Holman Hunt. He is the brother of Christina Rossetti, author of 'Goblin Market,' and illustrated her poem's frontispiece with wombats (he also kept a pet wombat and wrote parody poems about it). William Michael Rossetti, another brother, edited the Pre-Raphaelite magazine The Germ.

Rossetti's most famous poem, 'The Blessed Damozel,' describes a woman who 'lean'd out / From the gold bar of Heaven' with 'three lilies in her hand' and 'the stars in her hair were seven,' with hair 'yellow like ripe corn.' It was intended as a reversal of Poe's 'The Raven' and was published in The Germ. Parenthesized stanzas reveal the earthly speaker's thoughts, and the poem ends with him hearing her tears. Rossetti later painted the same subject, with Alexa Wilding as model and a predella commissioned by William Graham.

His wife Elizabeth Siddal (who caught pneumonia modeling in a cold bathtub for Millais's Ophelia) died of a laudanum overdose. Rossetti buried his poems in her coffin, then had it exhumed seven years later to retrieve them; her hair had reportedly grown. Her death inspired Beata Beatrix, which shows Siddal in Paradise (inspired by Dante's La Vita Nuova) with a sundial and a red bird holding a yellow flower.

The House of Life is Rossetti's sonnet sequence, opening with 'A sonnet is a moment's monument.' It contains the 'Willowwood' poems and is bookended by 'Love Enthroned' and 'The One Hope.' The sexually frank 'Nuptial Sleep' was omitted from the 1881 edition after Robert Buchanan coined 'the fleshly school of poetry' to criticize Rossetti and Swinburne; Rossetti responded with 'The Stealthy School of Criticism.'

'Jenny' is a dramatic monologue about a 'lazy laughing languid' prostitute (based on mistress Fanny Cornforth), in which the speaker places gold coins in her hair. Lady Lilith depicts a woman brushing 'enchanted hair' in a mirror, with white roses, poppies, and foxgloves; Alexa Wilding's face was repainted onto the canvas. It was paired with Veronica Veronese (red-haired woman playing violin strings, yellow bird in cage) as part of six 'stunners' for Frederick Leyland's drawing room. Proserpine shows a woman biting a pomegranate, modeled on Jane Morris, with a sonnet inscribed at the top right.

Ecce Ancilla Domini! is an Annunciation where both Gabriel and Mary wear plain white robes, with Mary on her bed as if just woken. Other works include The Day Dream (Jane Morris in a sycamore tree), Monna Rosa (patron's wife arranging pink roses), The Tune of the Seven Towers, and the unfinished Found. Rossetti translated Francois Villon, coining 'yesteryear' in 'Where are the snows of yesteryear?' He frequently employed models Alexa Wilding and Jane Morris, and Max Beerbohm drew 23 caricatures about him.
