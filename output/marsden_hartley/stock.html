American modernist painter (1877–1943), born Edmund Hartley, nicknamed 'the painter of Maine.' Known for Portrait of a German Officer — an abstract painting featuring military flags, an iron cross, and the initials 'K.v.F.' (Karl von Freyburg, his German lover killed in WWI). Also known for many paintings of Mount Katahdin and the Maine coast. His painting The Warriors was used as the backdrop for Stieglitz's famous photograph of Duchamp's Fountain.
Portrait of a German Officer (1914)

Abstract memorial painting for Hartley's German lover Karl von Freyburg (initials 'K.v.F.'), who was killed in WWI at age 24. Features German military flags, an iron cross, an Iron Cross, and a helmet inscribed with the numbers 8 and 9 (von Freyburg's regiment numbers). Hartley used 'object abstraction' to express his grief without depicting the human form. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Marsden Hartley (1877–1943), born Edmund Hartley, was an American modernist painter often nicknamed 'the painter of Maine.' He was part of the circle of artists around Alfred Stieglitz's Gallery 291, alongside Georgia O'Keeffe, Arthur Dove, and John Marin.

His most celebrated work is Portrait of a German Officer (1914), an abstract memorial painting for his German lover Karl von Freyburg, who was killed in the First World War at age 24. Using what he called 'object abstraction,' Hartley translated his grief into a visual arrangement of German military symbols — flags, an iron cross, and a helmet inscribed with the numbers 8 and 9 (von Freyburg's regiment) — along with the initials 'K.v.F.' displayed prominently. The painting never mentions the human body but is one of the first openly elegiac works about a same-sex relationship in American art history. Another of his abstract paintings from the same German period, The Warriors, was used by Stieglitz as the backdrop for his famous photograph of Marcel Duchamp's Fountain (1917).

Hartley spent much of his late career painting landscapes of Maine, particularly multiple views of Mount Katahdin, which he depicted as a brooding black mass rising over a red landscape. Other Maine works include The Ice Hole — showing a large 'A'-shaped opening in a frozen lake — Lobster Fishermen, and Fisherman's Last Supper. He also produced a series of works set in Dogtown (an abandoned Massachusetts landscape of granite boulders) and painted New Mexico landscapes. He was openly gay and counted many queer artists and writers among his friends.
