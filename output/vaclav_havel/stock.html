Czech playwright and politician who became the last president of Czechoslovakia and the first president of the Czech Republic. Known for absurdist plays satirizing bureaucracy (The Garden Party, The Memorandum) and political essays (The Power of the Powerless). A founding signatory of Charter 77 and key figure in the Velvet Revolution. Also wrote Largo Desolato, the Vanek plays, and Letters to Olga.
The Garden Party

A satirical absurdist play in which Hugo Pludek attends the title event at the Liquidation Office. He plays chess with himself, disguises himself with a papier-mache nose to mimic the bureaucratic Maxy Falk, and eventually learns to speak in bureaucratic cliches and jargon, becoming head of the Liquidation Office. His parents send his brother Peter into a pantry for being a 'bourgeois intellectual.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Vaclav Havel was a Czech playwright and politician who became the last president of Czechoslovakia and the first president of the Czech Republic. He was a founding signatory of Charter 77, participated in the Prague Spring (after which his plays were blacklisted), led the Civic Forum movement, and was a key figure in the Velvet Revolution and Velvet Divorce. He succeeded Gustav Husak as president. Many of his writings circulated as samizdat.

His most frequently clued play is The Garden Party, in which Hugo Pludek attends the title event at the Liquidation Office. Hugo plays chess with himself at the play's opening, disguises himself with a papier-mache nose to mimic the bureaucratic inaugurator Maxy Falk, and proposes swapping Small Dance Floor C and Large Dance Floor A. He learns to speak in bureaucratic cliches and jargon, eventually gaining control of the Liquidation Office and pitting it against the Inauguration Service. His parents send his brother Peter into a pantry for being a 'bourgeois intellectual.' The Pludek family uses nonsensical proverbs like 'lentils are lentils and oats are oats.' Hugo yells 'Checkmate!' at the end of a convoluted speech questioning his own identity. A man stutters 'magnet - telephone - magnet' to support his claim that 'we live in a technical age.'

The Memorandum follows Josef Gross, who cannot read a note written in the fictional bureaucratic language Ptydepe. Gross fights the official adoption of Ptydepe, a 'strictly scientific' language with 'maximum rationality.' Secretary Maria insists she cannot translate it without permission. Havel applied Shannon entropy (learned from his computer scientist brother) to design Ptydepe's structure. Ptydepe's shortest word is 'gh' (meaning 'whatever') and its longest word (319 letters) means 'wombat.' The language Chorukor replaces Ptydepe at the end because of its complexity. George watches characters through cracks in the walls and temporarily replaces Gross. Gross is tricked into signing a document by a man who spreads a rumor about him taking a rubber stamp home. He incorrectly uses the Ptydepe phrase 'hutput zexdohyt.' At the end, Gross urges Maria not to lose hope.

The Power of the Powerless is a 1978 essay that opens 'A specter is haunting Eastern Europe' (echoing Karl Marx). It describes a greengrocer who displays 'Workers of the world, unite!' in his shop window out of fear, not belief, arguing that 'I am afraid and therefore unquestioningly obedient' would be more accurate. The essay discusses 'post-totalitarian' systems where ideology transcends physical power, citizens must 'live within a lie,' and change can come through 'small-scale work.'

Largo Desolato is about the neurotic Leopold Nettles, a dissident driven to distraction by constant state surveillance. The student Marguerite boasts about reading books titled Ontology of the Human Self, Phenomenology of Responsibility, and Love and Nothingness by a professor who sits in only a dressing gown.

The Ferdinand Vanek plays (a quartet including Audience and Protest) feature a thinly-veiled autobiographical dissident writer forced to work at a brewery and offered the task of writing reports for the secret police.

Other works include Temptation (drawing from Goethe's Faust, featuring the wizard Fistula who smells like cheese), Leaving (about former Chancellor Vilem Rieger), and Letters to Olga (prison letters to his wife containing an early formulation of 'existential revolution' and citing Saul Bellow's Herzog). He also wrote experimental concrete poems called 'anticodes.'

Havel was a fan of the Plastic People of the Universe and helped draft Charter 77 after the band's arrest. He claimed to be inspired to run for president by the Velvet Underground. Tom Stoppard's play Rock 'n' Roll reflects Czech political turmoils with connections to Havel's world. Czech philosopher Jan Patocka, a Husserlian phenomenologist, was another Charter 77 intellectual.
