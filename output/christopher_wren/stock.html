English Baroque architect who rebuilt 52 London churches after the Great Fire of 1666, most notably St. Paul's Cathedral on Ludgate Hill. Also designed the Monument to the Great Fire, the Royal Observatory at Greenwich, the Trinity College Cambridge library, portions of Hampton Court Palace, and the Wren Building at William & Mary. Before architecture, illustrated Thomas Willis's Cerebri Anatome, introducing the word 'neurology.'
St. Paul's Cathedral

St. Paul's Cathedral on Ludgate Hill is Wren's most celebrated work, rebuilt after the Great Fire of 1666. Its dome — considered 'the most perfect in the world' by Nikolaus Pevsner — rises above a whispering gallery and features woodwork by Grinling Gibbons, ironwork by Jean Tijou, and a facade with paired Corinthian columns. Wren's tomb is inside, with the inscription 'Reader, if you seek his monument, look around you.' John Donne is also interred within. The dome was inspired by Mansart's Val-de-Grace and by the Romanesque lantern at Ely Cathedral, where Wren's uncle was bishop. Its eastern limewood paneling lists the names of 28,000 American soldiers. Herbert Mason's 1940 Blitz photograph of the cathedral surviving became an iconic image of wartime resilience.

Other Works

Other Wren works include the library of Trinity College Cambridge; the Sheldonian Theatre in Oxford; portions of Hampton Court Palace (which William and Mary tasked him to remodel to rival Versailles, a project abandoned in 1694 leaving a mix of Baroque and Tudor architecture); the Old Royal Naval College in Greenwich; the Wren Building at the College of William and Mary (the oldest collegiate building in the US); and repairs to Salisbury Cathedral, where he installed hidden beams to support the tower.

Polymath and Other Contributions

Before his architectural career, Wren created the illustrations for Thomas Willis's Cerebri Anatome, the book that introduced the word 'neurology.' He worked with Robert Hooke to rebuild London after the Great Fire and took on Nicholas Hawksmoor as an apprentice. He also served as MP for Weymouth, through which he controlled Portland stone quarries used for St. Paul's. John Dryden's poem Annus Mirabilis commemorates his works. Peter Ackroyd's novel Hawksmoor follows a detective solving murders at churches tied to a Satanist associate of Wren.

Summary of Facts

Sir Christopher Wren was an English Baroque architect who became the preeminent builder of his era following the Great Fire of London in 1666. He was commissioned to redesign 52 London churches, with St. Paul's Cathedral — his masterpiece on Ludgate Hill — standing as his greatest achievement. Nikolaus Pevsner dubbed its dome 'the most perfect in the world.' Wren's tomb is contained within St. Paul's, and its Latin inscription reads: 'Reader, if you seek his monument, look around you.' The cathedral is an English Baroque edifice featuring woodwork by Grinling Gibbons and ironwork by Jean Tijou, domes inspired by Mansart's Val-de-Grace and also the Romanesque lantern at Ely (where Wren's uncle was bishop), a facade with paired Corinthian columns, and a whispering gallery below its three-shelled double dome. Its dome is also flanked by two spires and surrounded by a Stone Gallery (above which lies a Golden Gallery). The cathedral's eastern limewood paneling incorporates a rocket and the names of 28,000 American soldiers who died in Britain during World War II. Also interred within is John Donne. Herbert Mason's famous 1940 photograph of St. Paul's surrounded by Blitz smoke became an iconic image of resilience. Bill Viola's Martyrs and William Holman Hunt's The Light of the World are among the artworks housed inside.

Wren also designed the Monument to the Great Fire — a fluted Doric column 202 feet tall with an interior staircase allowing visitors to reach a golden urn of fire at the top. Wren served as MP for Weymouth and used his access to Portland stone from the local quarries for St. Paul's.

The Royal Observatory at Greenwich was another Wren commission, built to house John Flamsteed's astronomical work; Flamsteed became its first resident. Tall windows were installed in the Octagon Room for the work. Greenwich also marks the prime meridian. Wren worked with Robert Hooke to reconstruct London after the Great Fire, and he also took on Nicholas Hawksmoor as an apprentice. He contributed illustrations to Thomas Willis's Cerebri Anatome, the book that introduced the word 'neurology.' Wren is sometimes credited with being a model for the Provincetown Heritage Museum, which appeared in Charles Demuth's watercolor After Sir Christopher Wren. He also designed the Sheldonian Theatre in Oxford, the library at Trinity College Cambridge, portions of Hampton Court Palace (where William and Mary hoped to create an English rival of Versailles, a project abandoned in 1694 leaving a mix of Baroque and Tudor architecture), the Old Royal Naval College in Greenwich, and the Wren Building — the oldest collegiate building in the US — at the College of William and Mary. A false ceiling in Salisbury Cathedral conceals beams installed by Wren to help support the weight of its tower. John Dryden's poem Annus Mirabilis commemorates Wren's works. Peter Ackroyd's novel Hawksmoor follows a detective solving murders at churches associated with a Satanist who worked alongside Wren.
