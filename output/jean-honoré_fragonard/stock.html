French Rococo painter (1732–1806) best known for The Swing (1767), commissioned by the Baron de Saint-Julien after Gabriel-François Doyen refused the commission. Also known for The Progress of Love series (commissioned by Madame du Barry, now at the Frick), The Bolt, Blind Man's Bluff, The Souvenir, and his Fantasy Figures portraits. Trained under François Boucher. Wife Marie-Anne was also a painter; anatomist cousin Honoré Fragonard made flayed écorchés.
The Swing (1767)

Fragonard's most famous work. A woman on a swing kicks off her shoe as a reclining man below looks up her dress, while a stone statue of Cupid holds a finger to its lips. Originally commissioned for a bishop, but Fragonard painted a layman instead. Contains Falconet's Menacing Love sculpture on the left side.

Summary of Facts

Jean-Honoré Fragonard (1732–1806) was a major French Rococo painter of the 18th century. He studied under François Boucher, who claimed that nature was 'too green and badly lit'; Boucher initially passed the young Fragonard to Jean-Baptiste-Siméon Chardin early in his training. Fragonard is best known for The Swing (1767), also titled The Happy Accidents of the Swing. This work was commissioned by the Baron de Saint-Julien — though it was originally offered to Gabriel-François Doyen, who refused due to its frivolous nature and passed it along. The common name comes from a contemporary engraving by Nicolas de Launay that mentions 'Happy Accidents.' In the painting, a woman's pink slipper flies off as she swings; a reclining man in green crouches in the bushes below and looks up her pink dress while a stone statue of Cupid holds a finger to its lips. The left side features Falconet's Menacing Love sculpture and a beehive with two babies on top of it; a dog on the lower right rears on its hind legs and yaps at the scene; one figure holds a black three-cornered hat in his outstretched left hand. Originally Fragonard painted a layman instead of the commissioned bishop. The Wallace Collection restoration revealed the upholstered red fabric on the central figure's seat. Yinka Shonibare recreated the work as a headless mannequin sculpture dressed in Dutch wax/Ankara fabric. A companion landscape painting of the same name, made to accompany Blind Man's Bluff, is housed in the National Gallery.

The Progress of Love (1771–1773), whose full title is The Progress of Love in the Heart of a Young Woman, is a cycle of four paintings commissioned by Madame du Barry for her villa at Louveciennes, now housed in the Frick Collection. Falconet's sculptures including Mischievous Cupid provided the basis for watchful statues in the series. The paintings include: The Pursuit (putti on a dolphin watch the lovers), The Meeting (a man in red climbs a garden balcony), The Lover Crowned (a woman places a floral wreath on her lover's head amid a lute, tambourine, and sheet music), and Love Letters (concludes with a couple courting next to letters). A separate panel, The Oath of Love, shows a man kissing a woman while holding his right hand on a statue of cupids holding a book with the title inscribed in it.

Other notable works include The Bolt (a man reaches for the title bolt while his lover restrains him, using chiaroscuro and a red curtain to symbolize profane love), Blind Man's Bluff (a woman peeks from under a cloth to cheat while a baby grasps at a dangling toy), Young Girl Reading (a woman in a white-collared yellow dress with an open book), and his Fantasy Figures (portraits de fantaisie) — quickly executed genre portraits in vaguely Spanish clothing; one shows a woman holding a white dog with a blue-ribboned leash, and 14 sketches were unveiled at a 2012 Paris auction. Fragonard also painted The Stolen Kiss (a woman's outstretched arm holds a garment as a young man enters from an open door and kisses her cheek) and The Souvenir (a small dog watches as a woman carves her lover's initials into a tree; in the Wallace Collection; titles a pair of Joanna Hogg films). He also painted The Birth of Venus, inspired by Boucher's Triumph of Venus, and Rinaldo in the Enchanted Forest (a knight chopping down a tree to break Armida's spell).

His wife Marie-Anne was also an artist, argued by Pierre Rosenberg to have painted miniatures including a boy in pale clothing with a black-feathered hat. His anatomist cousin Honoré Fragonard created a flayed horse and rider (écorché) for a veterinary school in Maisons-Alfort. Kerry James Marshall cited Fragonard and Boucher as inspirations for his Vignettes series.
