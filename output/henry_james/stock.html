American-born novelist and critic, brother of psychologist William James. Most commonly identified by The Portrait of a Lady (Isabel Archer, Gilbert Osmond), The Turn of the Screw (ghosts, governess), The Ambassadors (Lambert Strether), Daisy Miller, and The Aspern Papers. Known for his essay 'The Art of Fiction,' the New York Edition prefaces, and his 'late period' of increasingly complex prose. Colm Toibin's novel The Master and Leon Edel's biography are frequently referenced.
The Portrait of a Lady

James's most frequently clued novel, about the American heiress Isabel Archer who travels through Europe. Clues center on her marriage to the egotistical Gilbert Osmond, her rejection of suitors Lord Warburton and Caspar Goodwood, the revelation that Pansy is the daughter of Madame Merle, and the famous fireside vigil scene. The preface introduces the celebrated 'house of fiction' metaphor.

Summary of Facts

Henry James is one of the most heavily clued American authors in quizbowl, appearing across difficulty levels with a vast range of works and critical concepts. The Portrait of a Lady is by far the most frequently referenced novel, with clues covering Isabel Archer's marriage to Gilbert Osmond, her rejection of Lord Warburton and Caspar Goodwood, the Madame Merle-Pansy revelation, and the famous fireside vigil scene. The preface to this novel introduces the 'house of fiction' metaphor, one of James's most iconic critical formulations.

The Turn of the Screw is the second most commonly clued work, functioning as a giveaway identifier. Clues focus on the ghosts of Peter Quint and Miss Jessel haunting the children Miles and Flora at Bly, with Mrs. Grose as the housekeeper. Miles's death in the governess's arms and his cry of 'Peter Quint — you devil!' are standard giveaway material. The 1961 film adaptation The Innocents, co-written by Truman Capote, appears in Fine Arts crossover questions.

The Ambassadors receives substantial coverage as a late-period masterwork, especially Lambert Strether's 'Live all you can' speech in Gloriani's garden and the discovery of Chad Newsome's affair with Marie de Vionnet. The Aspern Papers is frequently clued through the narrator's scheme to steal Jeffrey Aspern's letters from Juliana Bordereau in Venice, ending with Tita burning the papers. Daisy Miller appears as a standard giveaway, identified through Winterbourne, Giovanelli, the Colosseum, and Roman fever.

James's critical output is heavily represented. 'The Art of Fiction,' responding to Walter Besant, appears often, as do the New York Edition prefaces. Wayne Booth's The Rhetoric of Fiction credits James with the 'showing vs. telling' distinction. His preface's dismissal of Victorian novels as 'loose, baggy monsters' is a recurring power clue. The late period — marked by the failure of Guy Domville, the employment of stenographer William MacAlpine, and Philip Guedalla's 'Old Pretender' quip — forms its own distinct clue cluster.

Biographical clues include the Leon Edel five-volume biography, James's friendship with Constance Woolson (the dress-drowning incident), homoerotic letters to Hendrik Christian Andersen, and his relationship with brother William James. Colm Toibin's The Master and Alan Hollinghurst's The Line of Beauty are frequently referenced in connection to James. Less common works that receive specific clues include 'The Beast in the Jungle' (John Marcher, May Bartram, Sedgwick's queer reading), Washington Square (Catherine Sloper, Morris Townsend), 'The Figure in the Carpet' (Hugh Vereker's secret), and 'The Jolly Corner' (Spencer Brydon's alter ego).
