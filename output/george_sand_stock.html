George Sand

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
French novelist who wrote under a male pseudonym (real name Amantine Lucile Aurore Dupin de Francueil). Most commonly identified by the novels Indiana and Consuelo, and the autobiographical travelogue A Winter in Majorca describing her stay with lover Frederic Chopin. Also known for pastoral novels (The Devil's Pool, La Petite Fadette) and Mauprat. Cross-dressing. Corresponded with Gustave Flaubert (who addressed her as 'Master'). Fictionalized by Alfred de Musset in Confession of a Child of the Century. Four of her pastoral novels appear in the opening of Proust's Swann's Way.

Summary of Facts

George Sand (real name Amantine Lucile Aurore Dupin de Francueil) was a nineteenth-century French novelist who wrote under a male pseudonym and was known for cross-dressing. She is most commonly identified as the lover of Frederic Chopin and as the author of the novels Indiana and Consuelo. Her relationship with Chopin is the single most frequent giveaway clue, followed by mentions of Indiana, Consuelo, and the travelogue A Winter in Majorca.

Indiana (1832), Sand's debut novel, follows a woman trapped in a loveless marriage with Colonel Delmare on the island of Reunion (a French colony whose descriptions Sand sourced from Jules Neraud's travel diary, as she had never visited). Indiana falls for the rake Raymon de Ramiere, who first seduces her Creole maid Noun — Noun drowns herself after becoming pregnant with Raymon's child. Indiana is eventually rescued by her cousin Sir Ralph, and the novel ends with the two of them nearly killing themselves by jumping into a waterfall. In one scene, Indiana cruelly presents Raymon with locks of hair she claims are hers but which belonged to the dead Noun. The novel also touches on the July Revolution of 1830.

Consuelo is about a Spanish orphan opera singer modeled on the real soprano Pauline Viardot. The title zingarella's talent for singing is discovered by maestro Porpora, who sends her to Bohemia. She rejects Count Zustiniani, lives in the "Castle of the Giants" owned by the Rudolstadt family, and at one point meets Haydn while fleeing to Vienna. The novel was first published in the Revue Independante, a periodical Sand co-founded. Its sequel, The Countess of Rudolstadt, directly influenced Walt Whitman.

A Winter in Majorca is Sand's autobiographical travelogue describing her stay with the tubercular Chopin at the Carthusian monastery of Valldemossa on the island of Majorca (Balearic Islands). It describes Chopin composing music during their stay and remarks on the "incredible slowness" of local agriculture. Robert Graves later translated the book, appending a "Refutation" by Jose Quadrado that calls Sand "the most immoral of writers" and "the most obscene of women."

Sand's pastoral novels are also frequently clued. La Petite Fadette (Little Fadette) involves the title character making the farmer boy Landry dance with her in exchange for helping him find his sickly twin brother Sylvinet, whom she cures by the novel's end; Sylvinet later joins the army after falling in love with his twin brother's wife. The Devil's Pool (La Mare au Diable) follows a widower who travels to a farm to find a new wife but falls for a shepherdess traveling with him; its first appendix describes supernatural stories told by the village hemp-dresser on autumn nights, and it inspired the painting Ploughing in the Nivernais. Four of Sand's pastoral novels are gifted to the narrator by his mother in an early scene of Proust's Swann's Way.

Mauprat tells of the titular nobleman Bernard de Mauprat, who helps his cousin Edmee escape from his savage relatives but extracts a promise of marriage by threatening rape. Edmee eventually civilizes Bernard, basing his education on Rousseau's Emile.

Sand had notable relationships with several literary and musical figures. Beyond Chopin, she had affairs with Jules Sandeau, Prosper Merimee, and Alfred de Musset. Musset fictionalized Sand as Brigitte in his autobiographical novel Confession of a Child of the Century, and Sand's own unflattering depiction of Musset in She and He prompted his brother to write an angry response novel. Sand corresponded extensively with Gustave Flaubert, who addressed her as "Master" in letters. Elizabeth Barrett Browning wrote two poems about Sand subtitled "A Desire" and "A Recognition," describing her as a "large-brained woman and large-hearted man." George Eliot defended Sand in a letter, writing that although she would never go to Sand's writings as a moral code, "it is given to her to delineate human passion." The Goncourt brothers described Sand smoking cigars "like an automaton" and claimed she only worked from 1 to 4 a.m. Sand also appears as a reference point in Harold Frederic's The Damnation of Theron Ware, where Celia Madden likens herself to Sand. Jaroslaw Iwaszkiewicz depicted Sand in his play A Summer in Nohant. Louis Hachette, founder of the publishing house Hachette, sold Sand's books.
