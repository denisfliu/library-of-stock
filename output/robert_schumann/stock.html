General / Biographical

Robert Schumann (1810–1856) was a German Romantic composer, pianist, and critic. He married Clara Wieck in 1840 (the year of his famous 'Year of Song'). He ruined his right hand by using a finger-strengthening mechanism, ending his concert career. He co-founded the music journal Neue Zeitschrift für Musik, in which he wrote under the fictional personas of Florestan (fiery) and Eusebius (lyrical), along with Master Raro (representing his father-in-law Friedrich Wieck). He used these alter egos throughout his music, most famously in Carnaval and Davidsbündlertänze. He published 'New Paths' in 1853, championing the young Johannes Brahms. He suffered a mental breakdown, attempted suicide by jumping into the Rhine, and died in the asylum at Endenich. His Ghost Variations were his final composition; he thought the melody was dictated to him by ghosts (it was actually from his own D minor Violin Concerto). Wikipedia

Carnaval (Op. 9)

Schumann's 1835 set of 21 piano miniatures (Op. 9) depicting a masked pre-Lent carnival. Uses permutations of the ASCH cryptogram (A-Eb-C-B or A-C-B, the letters of a town in Bohemia) throughout. Depicts commedia dell'arte characters (Pierrot, Arlequin, Pantaloon, Columbine) alongside real persons (Chiarina = Clara Wieck, Estrella = Ernestine von Fricken, Paganini, Chopin). Includes a section called 'Sphinxes' (three bars with no key signature or tempo — never meant to be played) and ends with a 'March of the Davidsbündler against the Philistines.' Florestan and Eusebius are both represented. Wikipedia

Dichterliebe (Op. 48)

Schumann's 1840 song cycle of 16 songs setting poems from Heinrich Heine's Lyrisches Intermezzo. Composed during his 'Year of Song.' The cycle opens with 'Im wunderschönen Monat Mai' (ending with an unresolved dominant 7th chord in F-sharp minor). Includes 'Ich grolle nicht' ('I bear no grudge'). The last song ('Die alten, bösen Lieder') has the poet imagining putting his songs and dreams in a huge coffin to be cast into the sea. Wikipedia

Piano Quintet in E-flat major (Op. 44)

Schumann's 1842 Piano Quintet in E-flat major, his most innovative chamber work. Premiered by Clara at the Leipzig Gewandhaus. First movement opens with the piano leaping up a minor seventh from E-flat to D-flat. Second movement is a funeral march in C minor with rapid descending scales. Last movement contains a double fugue integrating the opening theme of the first movement. Its use of two violins instead of a cello was innovative; Brahms's Piano Quintet was influenced by it. Summary of Facts

Robert Schumann (1810–1856) was a German Romantic composer, pianist, and music critic. He married the pianist Clara Wieck in 1840, the same year he composed over 130 songs during his famous 'Year of Song' (Liederjahr). His performing career was ended when a finger-strengthening mechanism permanently injured his right hand. He co-founded the Neue Zeitschrift für Musik, writing criticism under the fictional personas of Florestan (fiery) and Eusebius (lyrical), with Master Raro representing his father-in-law Friedrich Wieck. He famously championed Chopin (Eusebius exclaims 'Hats off, gentlemen, a genius!' in a review of Chopin's variations on 'Là ci darem la mano') and later Brahms (in the 1853 essay 'New Paths'). He suffered from mental illness, attempted suicide by jumping into the Rhine, and died in the asylum at Endenich in 1856.

His piano music includes: Carnaval (Op. 9, 1835) — 21 miniatures on an ASCH cryptogram, depicting commedia dell'arte characters alongside 'Chiarina' (Clara), 'Estrella' (Ernestine von Fricken), Paganini, and Chopin; a 'Sphinxes' section (three bars with no markings, never played); ending with a 'March of the Davidsbündler'; Kinderszenen (Op. 15, 1838) — 13 children's pieces, most famous for 'Träumerei'; Fantasie in C (Op. 17, 1838) — three movements, 'Im Legenden-ton' section, coda quoting Beethoven's An die ferne Geliebte, originally titled 'Ruins'; Kreisleriana (Op. 16) — 8 pieces after E.T.A. Hoffmann's Kreisler character; the Toccata in C (called 'the hardest piece ever written'); and Ghost Variations (his last composition, melody unknowingly transposed from his own D minor Violin Concerto).

His Dichterliebe (Op. 48, 1840) sets 16 Heine poems: the opening 'Im wunderschönen Monat Mai' ends on an unresolved dominant 7th; 'Ich grolle nicht' has the singer expressing 'I bear no grudge' over nonstop C major chords; the final song imagines a giant coffin thrown into the sea. His two Liederkreis cycles set Heine (Op. 24) and Eichendorff (Op. 39) poems. A Woman's Love and Life (Op. 42) sets Chamisso poems.

His Piano Concerto in A minor (Op. 54) was premiered by Clara; it began as a Concert Phantasie, begins with a distinctive tutti-chord/solo piano/tutti-chord/woodwind-chorale opening, and contains a cipher for 'Chiara' in the main theme. It is routinely paired with Grieg's A minor concerto. His Piano Quintet in E-flat (Op. 44, 1842) — premiered by Clara at the Leipzig Gewandhaus — opens with a minor-seventh leap (E-flat to D-flat), has a funeral march second movement, and a finale with a double fugue; it influenced Brahms's Piano Quintet.

Among his four symphonies: the 'Spring' Symphony No. 1 (B-flat, modeled on Beethoven's 4th); Symphony No. 2 (C major — the only homotonal 19th-century symphony, Mendelssohn conducted the premiere, unusual scherzo with two trios); the 'Rhenish' Symphony No. 3 (E-flat, five movements, inspired by Cologne Cathedral, trombone chorale in E-flat minor in the fourth movement); and Symphony No. 4 (D minor, actually written earlier but numbered later). He also wrote the Album for the Young (Op. 68, 'The Happy Farmer,' 'Knecht Ruprecht') and the Manfred Overture (first piece at Bernstein's 1943 Carnegie Hall debut).
