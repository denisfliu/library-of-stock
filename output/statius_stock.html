Summary of Facts

Statius (Publius Papinius Statius) was a Silver Age Roman poet and court poet of Domitian, contemporary of Martial and Suetonius. He is most commonly identified by three works: the Thebaid, the Achilleid, and the Silvae.

The Thebaid is an epic poem about the war between Eteocles and Polynices for control of Thebes, featuring the Seven Against Thebes led by Adrastus. It opens with Oedipus's curse — the Fury Tisiphone answers a prayer to bring strife between the brothers. Among its most memorable episodes: Tydeus has Melanippus's head ripped off and eats his brains; Amphiaraus along with his chariot is swallowed by the earth; the divine horse Arion saves Adrastus, the only survivor. In Book 4, Manto summons a spirit from the underworld, and Hypsipyle leaves baby Opheltes to be crushed by a snake. Book 6 contains a double ekphrasis of a bowl and cloak. The poem's envoi asks it to 'follow' the Aeneid 'at a distance and always adore their traces.' Characters from the Thebaid (Capaneus and Amphiaraus) appear in Dante's Inferno.

The Achilleid is an unfinished epic about Achilles's early life, of which only 1,127 lines survive. Achilles trains with the centaur Chiron, then disguises himself as a woman in the court of King Lycomedes on Scyros to escape Odysseus. He conceives his son Neoptolemus with Deidamia, Lycomedes's daughter.

The Silvae is a collection of 32 occasional poems in five books, seeking favor with Domitian. Book 1 opens with an ekphrasis praising an equestrian statue of Domitian. The fifth book includes an 'Ode to Sleep.' Other topics include the oddly bent tree and parrot of Atedius Melior, a lament for a slave boy, and a praise of an imperial freedman for enduring Caligula 'in icy Germany.' One poem commemorates the death of Lucan.

Statius appears as a character in Dante's Purgatorio, where he claims to have secretly converted to Christianity after reading Virgil's fourth Eclogue. He travels through the last three terraces with Dante and Virgil after an earthquake signals his completion of 500 years' penance for prodigality. He embraces Virgil's feet and gives a lecture about how blood turns into sperm to explain how bodiless souls can look malnourished. Juvenal's seventh satire attacks Statius as needing to prostitute his lost play Agave.
