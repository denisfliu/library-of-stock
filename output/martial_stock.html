Summary of Facts

Martial (Marcus Valerius Martialis) was a Roman epigrammatist born in Hispania who lived during the reigns of Domitian, Nerva, and Trajan. He is best known for his twelve books of Epigrams — over 1,500 brief, witty, satirical poems in elegiac couplets about Roman daily life. His signature line is 'my poems are naughty, but my life is pure' (vita proba est). He claims to be 'known over the whole world for his humorous books.'

Among the individual poems clued: he accuses Tongilianus of burning his house for insurance; doctor Symmachus gives him a cold with 'frigid hands'; he addresses a patron whose guests applaud food rather than companionship; he wrote about the gladiators Priscus and Verus; he declared 'Let all girls read Sulpicia if they want to please their husband alone'; and he describes the lapdog Issa as 'naughtier than Catullus's sparrow.' Tom Brown famously evaded expulsion by translating one of his poems as 'I do not love thee, Dr. Fell.' In his retirement, he wrote gloatingly that Juvenal might be 'wandering restless in the noisy Suburra.' Lord Byron mistranslated 'mentitur' in one of his poems. The word 'plagiarism' etymologically originates from his usage — his poem insults Fidentius for what he calls 'plagium,' which before Martial meant 'to kidnap.' Catullus's poetry served as the model for his work.

His earliest published work, On the Spectacles (Liber Spectaculorum), describes the shows at the newly constructed Colosseum, including fights between humans and polar bears from Caledonia and an epigram about a naumachia (mock naval battle). He also wrote the Xenia and Apophoreta, two sets of poems accompanying gifts during Saturnalia. He wrote controversial poems in praise of Domitian.
