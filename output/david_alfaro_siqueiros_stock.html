Summary of Facts

David Alfaro Siqueiros was a Mexican muralist and one of 'los tres grandes' alongside Diego Rivera and Jose Clemente Orozco. Their work was profiled in the Whitney Museum's 2020 exhibit Vida Americana and was opposed by the Rupture Generation. Siqueiros was taught by the pseudonymous artist Dr. Atl, who convinced him to join a revolutionary group. Sergei Eisenstein published an essay titled 'Prometheus of [Mexico's] Painting' comparing his films to the Mexican painting style.

His largest and most frequently clued work is The March of Humanity, the largest mural in the world, housed at his namesake dodecagonal Polyforum Cultural Center in Mexico City. The mural has both indoor and outdoor panels and is viewable from a rotating platform that holds up to a thousand visitors. It begins with an erupting volcano and is divided into sections including 'Bourgeois Democratic Revolution,' 'Science and Technology,' and 'to the Revolution of the Future.' It depicts the space race, revolutions, enslaved pregnant workers at the South entrance, a man emerging from an amate tree, three men mourning a dead worker on a conveyor belt, and Scenes of Ecology and the Atom on the outside of the building.

Echo of a Scream (1937) depicts a red-robed crying baby emerging from the mouth of an identical, larger baby above a field of metal scraps, with piles of rubble, a lone tree, and smokestacks in the background. Tropical America (1932) was painted at the Plaza Art Center in Los Angeles and shows a crucifixion scene with a gold-brown eagle atop a double cross in the jungle around a Mayan ruin; inspired by Masaccio frescoes, it was restored by the Getty in 2012. Portrait of the Bourgeoisie features generals and businessmen in gas masks, a metal falcon, a machine feeding blood to an octopus, and a temple inscribed 'Liberté, Egalité, Fraternité' on fire. It was created using pyroxylin automobile paint and spearheaded by Joseph Renau.

Other works include The Future Triumph of Medical Science over Cancer (depicting a crowd cheering as a giant monster is obliterated, painted for the Social Security Oncology Hospital), The Devil in the Church (a monster crashing through the roof), La nueva democracia (bare-breasted woman holding chains), Cain in the United States (naked men with distorted faces carrying a black body with a cracked skull), Collective Suicide and Landscape with Steer (both created at the Experimental Workshop using 'controlled accidents'). His self-portrait shows him in a red toga throwing a fist forward with smeared paint on his fingers. He painted a portrait of Hart Crane after discussing modernism with Crane and Sergei Eisenstein.

Siqueiros was a committed Stalinist who participated in a failed assassination attempt on Trotsky in 1940, strafing the Blue House with machine gun fire. Pablo Neruda later helped arrange a visa for Siqueiros after the accusations. He hosted an Experimental Workshop in Union Square where 'accidental painting' techniques of letting paint freely mix on a horizontal canvas influenced a young Jackson Pollock's drip technique, Abstract Expressionists, and even fluid dynamicists. He toured Italy with Diego Rivera in the early 1920s, and Morris Louis also studied under him.
