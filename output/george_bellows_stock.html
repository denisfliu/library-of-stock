American Ashcan School painter, student of Robert Henri, known for boxing scenes (Stag at Sharkey's, Both Members of This Club, Dempsey and Firpo), urban New York street scenes (Cliff Dwellers, 42 Kids), and WWI atrocity paintings (The Barricade). Editor/illustrator for the socialist magazine The Masses.

            Summary of Facts

            George Bellows (1882-1925) was an American painter and a leading figure in the Ashcan School, a movement of largely socialist realists who sought to capture gritty, realistic scenes of daily urban life in New York. He was a student of Robert Henri and also served as an illustrator and editor for the socialist magazine The Masses, where Art Young was the first critic to apply the term 'Ashcan' to painting. The Ashcan School had its origins in Philadelphia, where members studied under Thomas Anshutz.

            Bellows is most famous for his boxing paintings: Stag at Sharkey's (1909) depicts a match attended by patrons of the title club, featuring a blue-shirted referee behind two bloodied fighters; Both Members of This Club satirizes racial exclusion at private clubs and depicts the black boxer Joe Gans; and Dempsey and Firpo shows the dramatic moment when Luis Angel Firpo knocks Jack Dempsey over the ropes.

            His urban New York scenes include Cliff Dwellers, showing a crowded tenement street with clotheslines and a passing streetcar (with a woman breastfeeding in the summer heat), and 42 Kids, depicting the title number of skinny-dipping children on a wooden pier known as 'Splinter Beach' on the East River.

            Bellows also created a controversial series of WWI atrocity paintings, including The Barricade (German soldiers using naked Belgians as a human shield), The Germans Arrive (an officer holding a boy whose hands were severed), and the Massacre of Dinant. When Joseph Pennell and others criticized him for painting scenes he hadn't witnessed, Bellows famously retorted that Leonardo da Vinci didn't have 'a ticket to paint the Last Supper.'

            Other notable works include seascapes inspired by Winslow Homer from his visits to Monhegan and Matinicus Islands in Maine, The Big Dory (angular men moving a red-and-white boat), a portrait of the buck-toothed Paddy Flannigan, a lithograph of evangelist Billy Sunday, a series on the construction of Pennsylvania Station, a lithograph of a lynching victim made with collaborator Bolton Brown, and the painting Steaming Streets.
