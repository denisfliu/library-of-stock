Summary of Facts

George Bellows (1882–1925) was an American painter and a leading figure in the Ashcan School, a movement of largely socialist realists who sought to capture gritty, realistic scenes of daily urban life in New York. He was a student of Robert Henri and also served as an illustrator and editor for the socialist magazine The Masses, where Art Young was the first critic to apply the term 'Ashcan' to painting. The Ashcan School had its origins in Philadelphia, where members studied under Thomas Anshutz.

Bellows is most famous for his boxing paintings: Stag at Sharkey's (1909) depicts a match attended by patrons of the title club, set across the street from Bellows's studio, featuring a blue-shirted referee behind two bloodied fighters and a man with a cigar cynically glaring back at the viewer. 'Stag' refers to non-members brought into the club to fight. Both Members of This Club satirizes racial exclusion — its title references a trick to skirt the Lewis Law of 1900, and depicts a black boxer (possibly inspired by Jack Johnson or Joe Gans). Dempsey and Firpo shows the dramatic moment when Luis Angel Firpo knocks Jack Dempsey over the ropes into the crowd.

His urban New York scenes include Cliff Dwellers, showing a crowded Lower East Side tenement street with clotheslines and a trolley heading toward Vesey Street (with a woman breastfeeding in the summer heat). This painting used a color system based on musical notes learned from H.G. Maratta. 42 Kids depicts the title number of skinny-dipping children on a wooden pier known as 'Splinter Beach' on the East River.

Bellows also created a controversial series of WWI atrocity paintings, including The Barricade (German soldiers using naked Belgians as a human shield), The Germans Arrive (an officer holding a boy whose hands were severed), and a painting of the execution of nurse Edith Cavell. When Joseph Pennell and others criticized him for painting scenes he hadn't witnessed, Bellows famously retorted that Leonardo da Vinci didn't have 'a ticket to paint The Last Supper.'

Other notable works include seascapes inspired by Winslow Homer from his visits to Monhegan and Matinicus Islands in Maine (including a painting of gulls over Monhegan Island), The Big Dory (angular men moving a red-and-white boat), a portrait of the buck-toothed Paddy Flannigan, a lithograph of evangelist Billy Sunday, a series on the construction of Pennsylvania Station, a lithograph of a lynching victim made with collaborator Bolton Brown, and the painting Steaming Streets.
