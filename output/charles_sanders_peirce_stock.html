The Fixation of Belief

An 1877 essay published in Popular Science Monthly as part of Peirce's 'Illustrations of the Logic of Science' series. It outlines four methods for settling opinion: tenacity (clinging spasmodically to existing views), authority (institutions designed to keep correct doctrines), the a priori method, and the scientific method, which Peirce advocates as the best. The method of tenacity is compared to an ostrich burying its head in the sand.

How to Make Our Ideas Clear

An 1878 essay and early statement of Pragmatism. It proposes three grades of clearness of apprehension and formulates the pragmatic maxim: that the meaning of a concept is determined by its practical effects. The essay discusses the parallelogram of forces example and contrasts distinct and confused conceptions. It defines truth as the opinion 'fated to be ultimately agreed to by all who investigate.'

Semiotics & On a New List of Categories

Peirce's triadic theory of signs divides them into icons, indexes, and symbols. Icons resemble their objects, indexes evidence a real connection (smoke for fire), and symbols are arbitrary. His 'On a New List of Categories' (1867) establishes the central categories of firstness (quality/feeling), secondness (relation/reaction), and thirdness (representation/habit). These map to his 66 classes of signs developed with Victoria, Lady Welby. Peirce also introduced the type-token distinction.

Synechism, Tychism & Evolutionary Love

Peirce developed theories of continuity (synechism) and absolute chance (tychism) in a series of essays for The Monist. Synechism holds that all thought and consciousness must be fully continuous. Tychism argues that properties of the universe evolved through 'absolute chance.' 'The Law of Mind' discusses the human tendency to view the universe as continuous and how ideas tend to spread, gaining generality and losing intensity. 'Evolutionary Love' (agapeism) argues against social Darwinism.

Logic & Mathematics

Peirce was a major logician who introduced abductive reasoning (inference to the best explanation), showed NAND and NOR were Sheffer functions decades before Sheffer, developed three-valued logic (V, L, F), and proposed Peirce's law (if 'P then Q' implies P, then P must be true). He expanded on De Morgan's work in logic, coined the term 'likelihood functions,' and used the 'ampliative' for arguments whose conclusions go beyond premises. He introduced phaneroscopy (phenomenology) and attacked Cartesian methods of doubt.

General / Biographical

Peirce is identified as the 'father of Pragmatism' and an American philosopher. He renamed his philosophy 'pragmaticism' after breaking with William James. He published extensively in Popular Science Monthly and The Monist. He was banned from Harvard, taught at Johns Hopkins under Daniel Coit Gilman, and his bad temper and serial adultery ruined his academic prospects. His father was a mathematician who claimed mathematics is the 'science that draws necessary conclusions.' Umberto Eco focused on Peirce's reading of Kant in Kant and the Platypus.

Summary of Facts

Charles Sanders Peirce is the American philosopher known as the 'father of Pragmatism.' His foundational essays 'How to Make Our Ideas Clear' (1878) and 'The Fixation of Belief' (1877), published in Popular Science Monthly, establish the pragmatic maxim and outline four methods for settling opinion: tenacity, authority, the a priori method, and the scientific method, which Peirce advocates as best.

Peirce's triadic semiotics divides signs into icons, indexes, and symbols. His 'On a New List of Categories' (1867) introduces the central concepts of firstness (quality/feeling), secondness (relation/reaction), and thirdness (representation/habit). He developed 66 classes of signs with Victoria, Lady Welby, and introduced the type-token distinction.

In logic and mathematics, Peirce introduced abductive reasoning (a third type of inference alongside deduction and induction), demonstrated that NAND and NOR were Sheffer functions decades before Sheffer, developed three-valued logic, and formulated Peirce's law. He expanded on De Morgan's work, coined 'phaneroscopy' to replace 'phenomenology,' and attacked Cartesian methods of doubt.

Peirce developed theories of continuity (synechism) and absolute chance (tychism) in essays for The Monist. 'The Law of Mind' discusses how ideas naturally spread, and 'Evolutionary Love' introduces agapeism as an alternative to social Darwinism. He also wrote 'A Neglected Argument for the Reality of God.' He renamed his philosophy 'pragmaticism' after breaking with William James. His academic career was ruined by his bad temper and serial adultery, leading to his ban from Harvard, though he taught at Johns Hopkins under Daniel Coit Gilman.
