17th-century French playwright, a leading figure of French Classical drama alongside Molière and Jean Racine. Best known for the tragicomedy Le Cid, which caused a major controversy (the 'Querelle du Cid') over its violation of the classical unities. Also wrote The Comic Illusion (L'Illusion Comique), Polyeucte, The Liar, Cinna, and Horace. Rival of Jean Racine. Collaborated with Molière and Philippe Quinault on the comedy Psyché.
Le Cid

Corneille's most famous work, a tragicomedy based on a Spanish play by Guillén de Castro about the legendary Castilian hero El Cid. Don Rodrigue kills Don Gormas (the Comte de Gormas / Don Gomes), the father of his lover Chimène, in a duel to avenge his own father Don Diègue, who was insulted by Gormas. Rodrigue then fights off a Moorish invasion and earns the title 'Le Cid.' Chimène forces Rodrigue to duel her champion Don Sanche, but Rodrigue spares him. Upon seeing a bloody sword, Chimène confesses her love and they reconcile. The play caused a massive controversy — Cardinal Richelieu and the French Academy (via Jean Chapelain and Georges de Scudéry's pamphlet war) condemned it for violating the classical unities. Corneille briefly withdrew from public life / began a three-year creative silence after the scandal. The play was also politicized in the Quarrel of the Ancients and Moderns. Wikipedia

The Comic Illusion (L'Illusion Comique)

A meta-theatrical comedy in which the magician Alcandre shows Pridamant visions of his estranged son Clindor's exploits using a device in a cave. Pridamant is shocked to witness his son apparently killed, only to learn that Clindor has become an actor and Pridamant has been watching a play-within-a-play. Ends with actors divvying up money and a monologue calling theatre 'a fief whose income is good.' Adapted by Tony Kushner in 2002 (as The Illusion), in which character names switch to Calisto and Theogenes between acts. Richard Wilbur translated it as The Theatre of Illusion. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Pierre Corneille is a 17th-century French playwright, one of the three great figures of French Classical drama alongside Molière and Jean Racine. He is overwhelmingly identified by his most famous work, Le Cid, which serves as the giveaway in virtually every tossup.

Le Cid is the most heavily clued work by far. It is a tragicomedy based on a Spanish play by Guillén de Castro about the legendary Castilian hero El Cid. The central conflict: Don Rodrigue must avenge his father Don Diègue after the Comte de Gormas (Don Gomes) insults him, which means killing Gormas — who is the father of his lover Chimène. After the duel, Rodrigue fights off a Moorish invasion and earns the honorary title 'Le Cid.' Chimène forces him to duel her champion Don Sanche, but Rodrigue spares him. A champion brings a bloody sword to Chimène, who breaks down and confesses her love. They reconcile and marry. The most frequently clued aspect beyond the plot is the controversy: Le Cid violated the classical (Aristotelian) unities of time, place, and action, angering Cardinal Richelieu. Georges de Scudéry led a 'war of pamphlets' against the play, and Jean Chapelain of the French Academy formally condemned it. Corneille briefly withdrew from public life or entered a three-year creative silence. The play was politicized in the broader Quarrel of the Ancients and Moderns. A notable detail: the soliloquy 'My father or my betrothed? Love or honor?' was originally performed by the actor Montdory, and audiences reportedly chanted along.

The Comic Illusion (L'Illusion Comique) is the second most frequently clued work. It is a meta-theatrical comedy featuring the magician Alcandre, who shows Pridamant visions of his estranged son Clindor's exploits from a cave. The twist: Pridamant believes he watches his son being killed, but discovers Clindor is actually an actor and the death scene was a play-within-a-play. The comedy ends with actors divvying up money and a monologue calling theatre 'a fief whose income is good.' Tony Kushner adapted it in 2002 as The Illusion, in which the main character's name switches between Calisto and Theogenes across acts. Richard Wilbur translated it as The Theatre of Illusion.

Polyeucte is a tragedy about an Armenian nobleman who converts to Christianity. Felix and Pauline try to discourage his conversion but ultimately convert themselves after Polyeucte is martyred. This appears across three separate clues.

The Liar (Le Menteur) is a comedy adapted from Juan Ruiz de Alarcón in which Dorante pretends to be a military hero to court Clarice but mixes up the names of Clarice and Lucrèce, causing Geronte's matchmaking plans to fail.

Other works mentioned include Cinna (about a conspiracy to assassinate Augustus Caesar), Horace (dedicated to Cardinal Richelieu), Mélite (an early comedy featuring forged love letters and delusions of hell), and Titus et Bérénice (commissioned in competition with Racine's Bérénice). Corneille also produced a verse translation of The Imitation of Christ and wrote Three Discourses on Dramatic Poetry, arguing against rigid adherence to the unities. He collaborated with Molière and Philippe Quinault on the comedy Psyché.
