General / Biographical

Richard Avedon (1923–2004) was an American fashion and portrait photographer known for minimalist portraits against white/plain backgrounds. He was a staff photographer for Vogue and later The New Yorker. He photographed Andy Warhol and members of The Factory (including naked 'superstars') in a 1969 series. He documented his father Jacob Israel Avedon's final years (1969–1973) in Sarasota, Florida — a series described as 'anatomies of aging' by Janet Malcolm in a 1974 MoMA exhibition curated by Marvin Israel. He collaborated with Marvin Israel on a 1967 'Master Class' and 1970 'Minneapolis Portfolio.' He photographed Marilyn Monroe (1957 and 1958). He also photographed Willem de Kooning with his shirt unbuttoned. He provided both inspiration and actual photos for the film Funny Face. He photographed Ringo Starr with a laurel wreath (Daily Mail: 'Hail, Ringo'). He created the album cover for Teddy Pendergrass. His style typically posed subjects head-on against white backgrounds. Summary of Facts

Richard Avedon (1923–2004) was an American fashion and portrait photographer, staff photographer for Vogue (1945–1988) and later The New Yorker (1992–2004). He is known for his minimalist style — posing subjects head-on against plain white or light backgrounds to achieve psychologically revealing results.

His most iconic image is Dovima with Elephants (1955), showing model Dovima in a Dior evening gown (designed by Yves Saint-Laurent, then an apprentice to Christian Dior) standing between two circus elephants at the Cirque d'Hiver in Paris, with her hand appearing to touch one trunk. A major documentary project was In the American West, commissioned by the Amon Carter Museum in Fort Worth, which depicted working-class and rough subjects — cowboys, drifters, a girl in overalls (on the cover), a hairless man covered in bees (Boyd Fortin, a 13-year-old rattlesnake skinner), and a blond boy holding a gutted snake.

In 1967, Avedon created four solarized, psychedelic portraits of the Beatles, including one with John Lennon wearing swirling glasses and another of Ringo Starr holding a dove. He also photographed the nude Nastassja Kinski with a python coiled around her body — one of his most recognized portrait photographs. He collaborated with his childhood friend James Baldwin on Nothing Personal (1964), a critique of American society during the Civil Rights era.

Avedon documented his father Jacob Israel Avedon's final years (1969–1973) in Sarasota, Florida — a series described as 'anatomies of aging' by New Yorker columnist Janet Malcolm at a 1974 MoMA exhibition curated by Marvin Israel. He collaborated repeatedly with Marvin Israel on a 1967 'Master Class' and a 1970 'Minneapolis Portfolio.' He also photographed Andy Warhol and his Factory superstars (including some naked) in a 1969 series, and photographed Marilyn Monroe in 1957 and 1958. He provided both inspiration and actual photographs for the 1957 film Funny Face. A signature trick: to capture a dour couple portrait, he told them his taxi had just run over a pug.
