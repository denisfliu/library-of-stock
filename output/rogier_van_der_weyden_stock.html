Early Netherlandish/Flemish painter, student of Robert Campin (Master of Flemalle). Best known for The Descent from the Cross (commissioned by the Crossbowmen's/Archers' Guild in Leuven), the Braque Triptych, the Beaune Altarpiece (Last Judgement), and St. Luke Drawing the Virgin.

Panel painting in the Museo del Prado, commissioned by the Crossbowmen's (Archers') Guild of Leuven. The most frequently clued work.

Summary of Facts

Rogier van der Weyden was an early Netherlandish painter and student of Robert Campin (the Master of Flemalle), active at the Burgundian court of Philip the Good. He was a contemporary of Jan van Eyck and the teacher of Hans Memling. His most frequently clued work is The Descent from the Cross, a panel painting now in the Museo del Prado, commissioned by the Crossbowmen's (Archers') Guild of Leuven. The painting features Christ's body splayed in the shape of a crossbow, a blue-clad Virgin Mary swooning in a pose that mirrors Christ's, Joseph of Arimathea and Nicodemus lowering the body, the skull of Adam on the ground, and crossbow imagery in the spandrels. Erwin Panofsky called it a 'painted critique' of a work by van der Weyden's teacher.

The Braque Triptych, a memorial for Jehan of Tournai, shows Jesus holding a globus cruciger with a bright orange halo in the center, flanked by John the Baptist and Mary Magdalene, with five floating Latin inscriptions and a continuous landscape. When closed, it shows a skull on a brick and a cross. The Beaune Altarpiece (Last Judgement), a massive nine-panel work commissioned by Chancellor Nicolas Rolin for the Hospices de Beaune, shows Jesus on a rainbow above the Archangel Michael weighing souls, and was likely painted in response to the Ghent Altarpiece.

St. Luke Drawing the Virgin depicts a saint in red robes using silverpoint to draw Mary breastfeeding, with a triple-arched view onto a garden, loggia, and river that resembles van Eyck's Madonna of Chancellor Rolin. The Miraflores Altarpiece contains scenes of Christ's birth, death (with a central Pieta where Mary holds Jesus diagonally), and resurrection. The Seven Sacraments Altarpiece features angels in progressively darker clothing accompanying each sacrament, with a central crucifixion in dramatic linear perspective within a cathedral interior. The Crucifixion Diptych unites two panels with a continuous gray wall and recurring red cloth. Other notable works include portraits of Charles the Bold (with Order of the Golden Fleece), Francesco d'Este, Lady Wearing a Gauze Headdress, The Magdalen Reading, and the Thyssen Madonna.
