Chilean Nobel Prize-winning poet, born Ricardo Reyes Basoalto, who took his pen name from Czech poet Jan Neruda. Known for Twenty Love Poems and a Song of Despair, Canto General (including 'The Heights of Macchu Picchu'), and the Elemental Odes. Also a diplomat and Communist. His work ranges from love poetry to political verse inspired by the Spanish Civil War and Latin American history. Always wrote in green ink.
Twenty Love Poems and a Song of Despair

Neruda's early collection of love poems, published when he was 19. Translated by W. S. Merwin. Includes poems like 'Body of a Woman,' 'Tonight I Can Write the Saddest Lines,' 'White Bee,' 'In My Sky at Twilight,' 'Ah Vastness of Pines,' and the final 'Song of Despair.' Summary of Facts

Pablo Neruda was a Chilean Nobel Prize-winning poet, born Ricardo Reyes Basoalto, who took his pen name from Czech poet Jan Neruda. He always wrote in green ink, which he considered the color of hope. A Communist who served as a senator, he was also a diplomat with consulships throughout Asia and Spain.

His most frequently clued collection is Twenty Love Poems and a Song of Despair, published when he was 19 and translated by W. S. Merwin. It includes 'Tonight I Can Write the Saddest Lines' ('Love is so short, forgetting is so long'; 'The night is starry and the stars are blue and shiver in the distance'), 'Body of a Woman' ('white hills, white thighs'), 'White Bee,' 'Ah Vastness of Pines,' and the final 'Song of Despair' (refrains 'in you everything sank!' and 'it is the hour of departure'; opens 'The memory of you emerges from the night around me'). Vicente Huidobro accused Neruda of plagiarizing Tagore's The Gardener for the poem 'In My Sky at Twilight.'

Canto General is his magnum opus, a vast poetic sequence about the Americas. It includes 'The Heights of Macchu Picchu,' a 12-part poem that begins 'From breeze to breeze, like an empty net' and commands 'Arise to birth with me, my brother' and 'Speak through my words and my blood.' Sections include 'A Lamp on Earth,' 'The Conquistadors,' 'The Sand Betrayed,' 'The Fugitive,' and 'America, I Do Not Invoke Your Name in Vain.' The collection also includes 'The Invisible Man' ('I laugh / I smile / at the old poets'), poems attacking the United Fruit Company and 'Celestial Poets,' and 'The Earth's Name is Juan.' Neruda's election as senator overlapped with completing 'The Heights of Machu Picchu.'

The Elemental Odes are addressed to everyday objects: socks (knitted by Maru Mori; 'beauty is twice beauty'; socks 'as soft as rabbits'; feet look like 'two long sharks'), a large tuna ('bullet from the ocean depths'; 'catafalqued king'), an artichoke ('dressed up like a warrior'), tomatoes ('star of earth'), conger chowder, salt ('you won't believe me but this thing sings'), maize ('America grew from' it), a watermelon ('green whale of summer'), a seashore cactus, and an atom ('crazy spark').

'Walking Around' opens with 'It so happens I am sick of being a man' and describes 'hideous intestines hanging over doors'; Federico Garcia Lorca drew images to accompany it. 'I Explain a Few Things' ends 'Come and see the blood in the streets!' and was inspired by the Spanish Civil War. Both appear in Residence on Earth, written during his diplomatic postings.

One Hundred Love Sonnets includes Sonnet XVII ('I don't love you as if you were a rose of salt, topaz'; 'I love you as one loves certain obscure things, secretly, between the shadow and the soul'). The Captain's Verses includes 'Your Laughter' and 'If You Forget Me,' likely about his wife Matilde Urrutia. He anonymously published Los versos del capitan.

Neruda appears as a character in Antonio Skarmeta's The Postman / Burning Patience, where he provides advice to Mario on Isla Negra, and in Roberto Bolano's By Night in Chile. In his Nobel lecture, he rejected Huidobro's creationist slogan 'the poet is a little god' and quoted Rimbaud about conquering 'the splendid city' through 'burning patience.' He was criticized for failing to condemn the USSR forcing Pasternak to reject the 1958 Nobel Prize, and the Congress for Cultural Freedom tried to discredit him by publicizing pro-Stalin sections of Grapes and the Wind. Gabriela Mistral read his poems after moving to his hometown of Temuco.
