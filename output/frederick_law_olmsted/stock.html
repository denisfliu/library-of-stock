American landscape architect (1822–1903), co-designer of Central Park and Prospect Park with Calvert Vaux. Known for the Greensward Plan, the Emerald Necklace in Boston, the Midway Plaisance for the 1893 World's Fair, and the Biltmore Estate grounds. Mentor Andrew Jackson Downing introduced him to Vaux. Also authored The Cotton Kingdom about antebellum Southern poverty. First executive secretary of the U.S. Sanitary Commission during the Civil War. His seven design principles are Scenery, Suitability, Style, Subordination, Separation, Sanitation, and Service.
Central Park

Designed with Calvert Vaux, who became his partner after Olmsted's mentor Andrew Jackson Downing introduced them. Their design, the Greensward Plan, beat out engineer Ebert Ludovicus Viele's existing plan. Landmarks include the Bethesda Fountain, the Sheep Meadow, the Bow Bridge, the Great Lawn, a zoo, and an obelisk known as Cleopatra's Needle. The park is the setting of Edward Albee's play The Zoo Story. Robert Smithson's 'Dialectical Landscape' article centered on Olmsted's Greensward Plan. Christo and Jeanne-Claude installed The Gates (7,503 bright orange gates) there in 2005. Diane Arbus photographed a boy holding a toy hand grenade there. Charles Ives wrote a composition titled for Central Park 'In the Dark.' The park was later restored by Robert Moses. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Frederick Law Olmsted (1822–1903) was a landscape architect, author, conservationist, and public servant. Before his design career he was a merchant, apprentice seaman, experimental farmer, and goldmine manager. He was co-founder of The Nation magazine and the first executive secretary of the United States Sanitary Commission (USSC), a Civil War era relief organization led by Henry Whitney Bellows. His mentor Andrew Jackson Downing (who died in a steamboat explosion on the Hudson River, and was also the first American exponent of landscape architecture) introduced him to partner Calvert Vaux. He served as chairman of the first Yosemite Commission. His design philosophy (the seven S's): Scenery, Suitability, Style, Subordination, Separation, Sanitation, and Service — with a focus on preserving existing landscape contours and heightening natural beauty. He authored The Cotton Kingdom (a.k.a. Journeys and Explorations in the Cotton Kingdom), written after the success of his earlier travelogue Walks and Talks of an American Farmer in England; The Cotton Kingdom argued that slavery made the South economically inefficient. Edmund Wilson discussed the book in Chapter 6 of Patriotic Gore. John Singer Sargent painted a portrait of Olmsted holding a walking stick in the woods. Near the end of his life he was committed to McLean Hospital in Waverly, Massachusetts—whose grounds he had himself designed.

Summary of Facts

Frederick Law Olmsted (1822–1903) was the founding figure of American landscape architecture, best known for co-designing Central Park in Manhattan with his English-born partner Calvert Vaux. Their winning submission, the Greensward Plan, beat out a design by engineer Ebert Ludovicus Viele. The park's landmarks include the Bethesda Fountain, the Sheep Meadow, the Bow Bridge, the Great Lawn, a zoo, and an obelisk known as Cleopatra's Needle. It is the setting of Edward Albee's play The Zoo Story, and Robert Smithson's essay 'Dialectical Landscape' centers on Olmsted's Greensward Plan. Christo and Jeanne-Claude installed The Gates (7,503 bright orange gates) in the park in 2005. Diane Arbus photographed an exasperated boy holding a toy hand grenade in the park. Charles Ives composed a piece about the sounds of nature heard sitting on a Central Park bench 'In the Dark.' Ai Weiwei's 2017 Gilded Cage protest was installed there. The park was later restored by Robert Moses. Olmsted and Vaux also co-designed Prospect Park in Brooklyn.

Olmsted's Boston work produced the Emerald Necklace, a chain of parks and parkways linking several green spaces across the city. For the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago, he designed the grounds including the Midway Plaisance (an entertainment corridor) and a Wooded Island at its center, though he originally wanted to cut a canal through the Midway. The Exposition contributed to the City Beautiful movement; the McMillan Plan subsequently brought Olmsted to Washington D.C. as part of that movement. He also designed the campus of Stanford University, the University of Chicago and Cornell campuses, the U.S. Capitol Grounds, the planned city of Riverside, Illinois, and the Buffalo park system and the grounds of the Buffalo State Hospital alongside his friend H.H. Richardson. The Olmsted Brothers firm (his sons) designed the Cleveland Metroparks System and collaborated with the Bartholomew firm on a never-implemented plan for Los Angeles.

Olmsted designed the landscape for the Biltmore Estate in North Carolina, the buildings of which were by Richard Morris Hunt. His care for the estate's surrounding forests inadvertently helped lay the groundwork for American forestry; Carl A. Schenck ran the nation's first forest school there.

His design philosophy was governed by the 'seven S's': Scenery, Suitability, Style, Subordination, Separation, Sanitation, and Service. He focused on preserving the existing contours of the landscape and heightening natural beauty.

Before his design career, Olmsted had been a merchant, apprentice seaman, experimental farmer, and goldmine manager. He was co-founder of The Nation magazine and the first executive secretary of the United States Sanitary Commission (USSC), a Civil War-era relief organization led by Henry Whitney Bellows. He served as chairman of the first Yosemite Commission. His mentor, Andrew Jackson Downing — the first American exponent of landscape architecture, who died in a steamboat explosion on the Hudson River — introduced him to Calvert Vaux. Olmsted also wrote The Cotton Kingdom (formally Journeys and Explorations in the Cotton Kingdom), commissioned after the success of his earlier travelogue Walks and Talks of an American Farmer in England; the book argued that slavery made the antebellum South economically inefficient, and is discussed in Chapter 6 of Edmund Wilson's Patriotic Gore. John Singer Sargent painted a portrait of Olmsted holding a walking stick in the woods. Near the end of his life he was committed to McLean Hospital in Waverly, Massachusetts — whose grounds he had himself designed.
