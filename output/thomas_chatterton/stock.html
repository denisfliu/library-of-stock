Summary of Facts

Thomas Chatterton (1752-1770) is an 18th-century Bristol poet who forged medieval poems attributed to the fictional 15th-century monk Thomas Rowley, 'discovering' such faux-medieval works as Aella, a Tragical Interlude ('the budding floweret blushes at the light' opens its Minstrel's Marriage-Song), 'An Excellent Ballad of Charitie' (a pilgrim refused entry to the Seyncte Godwynes convent), 'The Parliament of Sprites' (Queen Mab summons ghosts in the graveyard of St. Mary Redcliffe), and 'The Battle of Hastings.'

Chatterton committed suicide by arsenic poisoning at age 17, becoming a major icon for the Romantic poets. Before leaving Bristol, he wrote a satirical 'Last Will and Testament.' Wordsworth called him a 'marvellous Boy' in 'Resolution and Independence,' which also laments the early death of Robert Burns. Keats dedicated his four-book poem Endymion ('A thing of beauty is a joy forever') to Chatterton. Shelley names him alongside Sidney and Lucan as 'inheritors of unfulfill'd renown' in Adonais. Dante Gabriel Rossetti attributed to him 'Shakespeare's manhood at a boy's wild heart' in 'Five English Poets.'

Henry Wallis painted The Death of Chatterton using George Meredith as the model; the painting hangs in the Tate. Peter Ackroyd wrote a novel about Chatterton, with the Wallis painting on its cover. Robert Browning adopted the term 'slug-horn' from Chatterton's pseudo-medieval language.
