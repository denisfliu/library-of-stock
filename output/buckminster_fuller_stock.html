American architect, designer, and futurist (1895–1983). Pioneered the geodesic dome; designed the Montreal Biosphere for Expo 67. Created the Dymaxion House (aluminum, circular, O-volving shelf, fog gun), Dymaxion Car (three-wheeled), and Dymaxion Map (icosahedron projection). Coined 'Spaceship Earth' and 'ephemeralization.' Namesake of the carbon-60 buckyball molecule.
Geodesic Dome / Montreal Biosphere (Expo 67)

Fuller's most celebrated design: a spherical structure distributing stress over polyhedra made solely of triangular faces. His most famous example is the US Pavilion at the 1967 Montreal Expo (Expo 67), built on St. Helen's Island. After being damaged by fire, the steel skeleton became the environmental museum 'Biosphere.' The structure embodies his principle of 'doing more with less' via interlocking triangles. His Geoscope at Expo 67 was also notable. The Eden Project in Cornwall, England (by Nicholas Grimshaw) was inspired by Fuller's geodesic domes. Epcot at Disney World emulates his style.

Dymaxion House

A circular, factory-made aluminum house suspended from a central mast with 'bicycle-wheel' spokes and hexagonal walls. Prototypes 'Barwise' and 'Danbury' built with Wichita's Beech Aircraft. Features included an 'O-volving shelf' (revolving closet) and a 'fog gun' instead of a shower. Shape compared to a Hershey's Kiss. One surviving prototype purchased by the Henry Ford Museum in 2011 (in Dearborn, Michigan). The Wichita House was a slightly modified round version. Later designs included the Growth Home of 1952 (which included a proto-vertical indoor farm). Fuller described his design philosophy as 'doing more with less' / 'ephemeralization.'

Dymaxion Map / Dymaxion Car / Dymaxion concept

Fuller coined 'Dymaxion' (portmanteau of dynamic, maximum, tension) as a prefix for many projects. The Dymaxion Map projects Earth's surface onto an icosahedron, which unfolds without directional distortion; profiled in Life magazine and precedes the Mercator-Robinson projections for area accuracy. The Dymaxion Car was a three-wheeled vehicle (funded by Henry Kaiser), intended to 'hop off the road'; documented as having fatal accidents. The Dymaxion Chronofile recorded Fuller's activities every 15 minutes for 63 years.

General / Biographical

Richard Buckminster Fuller (1895–1983): American architect, designer, and systems thinker. Coined 'Spaceship Earth.' Developed Synergetics: a design philosophy exploring intrinsic properties of shapes using 60-degree coordination. Defined 'ephemeralization' as doing everything with nothing through technological efficiency. Promoted 'design science.' Second World President of Mensa. Epitaph: 'Call me Trim Tab' — derived from a 1972 Playboy interview where he used the metaphor of a trim tab building low pressure to explain how individuals can change the world. Named the carbon-60 molecule 'buckminsterfullerene' (buckyball). Proposed Old Man River City (a giant dome over a crater for a new East St. Louis). Proposed floating spherical habitats 'Cloud Nine' held together by tensegrity. Professor at Southern Illinois University; collaborated with John McHale on the World Design Science Decade (1965). Collaborated with Shoji Sadao on the Dymaxion Map. Jasper Johns created a painting of twenty-two triangular canvas panels inspired by Fuller. Isamu Noguchi created a portrait bust of Fuller early in his career.

Summary of Facts

Richard Buckminster Fuller (1895–1983) was an American architect, designer, and systems thinker celebrated for pioneering the geodesic dome and coining the term 'Spaceship Earth.' His ethos of 'doing more with less' — which he called 'ephemeralization' — pervaded every aspect of his work, from architecture to cartography.

Fuller's most iconic building is the geodesic dome: a spherical structure that distributes stress across polyhedra made solely of triangular faces. His most famous example is the US Pavilion at Expo 67, the 1967 Montreal World's Fair, built on St. Helen's Island. After being damaged by fire, its steel skeleton became the environmental museum 'Biosphere.' Fuller also designed a Geoscope for the same event. His geodesic ideas were emulated at Epcot in Disney World and inspired the Eden Project in Cornwall by Nicholas Grimshaw. WWII-era circular steel shelters Fuller designed were inspired by the sight of metal grain silos.

Fuller's domestic design work centered on the Dymaxion House: a circular aluminum structure suspended from a central mast with 'bicycle-wheel' spokes and hexagonal walls. Prototypes named 'Barwise' and 'Danbury' were built in partnership with Wichita's Beech Aircraft. The house featured an 'O-volving shelf' (a revolving closet) and a 'fog gun' instead of a conventional shower or bath. Its shape has been compared to a Hershey's Kiss. One prototype was purchased in 2011 by the Henry Ford Museum in Dearborn, Michigan — the only surviving example. A Wichita House was a slightly modified round variant; the 1952 Growth Home included a proto-form of a vertical indoor farm.

Fuller coined the portmanteau 'Dymaxion' (dynamic + maximum + tension) to brand his most forward-looking projects. The Dymaxion Map projects Earth's surface onto an icosahedron, which can be unfolded to two dimensions with minimal directional distortion, outperforming the Mercator and Robinson projections in area accuracy. The Dymaxion Car was a three-wheeled vehicle funded by Henry Kaiser, intended to 'hop off the road at will'; it was documented to have caused fatal accidents. The Dymaxion Chronofile recorded Fuller's activities every 15 minutes for 63 years. He developed Synergetics, a design philosophy that used 60-degree rather than 90-degree coordination to explore the intrinsic properties of shapes.

Fuller proposed Cloud Nine: giant floating spherical habitats held together by tensegrity (with Shoji Sadao), which would remain airborne by keeping internal temperature one degree higher than the surrounding atmosphere. He proposed Old Man River City, a massive new East St. Louis covered by a giant umbrella over a crater. Collaborating with John McHale, he inaugurated the World Design Science Decade in 1965 while a professor at Southern Illinois University. His 1968 book was framed as an 'operating manual' for 'Spaceship Earth.' Fuller was the second World President of Mensa. His epitaph reads 'Call me Trim Tab,' derived from a 1972 Playboy interview in which he used the metaphor of a trim tab building low pressure to illustrate individual empowerment. The carbon-60 molecule — buckminsterfullerene or the 'buckyball' — is named for him. Isamu Noguchi created a portrait bust of Fuller early in his career.
