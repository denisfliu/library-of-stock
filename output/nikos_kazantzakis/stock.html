Greek novelist and poet. Best known for Zorba the Greek (set on Crete, featuring a lignite mine and Madame Hortense) and The Last Temptation of Christ (Satan disguises as a guardian angel to tempt Jesus away from the crucifixion). Also wrote the epic poem The Odyssey: A Modern Sequel (33,333 lines), Freedom or Death (about Captain Michalis), The Greek Passion (Manolios chosen to play Christ in a village play), and the spiritual work The Saviors of God. Frequent translator was Kimon Friar.
Zorba the Greek

Kazantzakis's most famous novel. The narrator travels to Crete with the vivacious title character to reopen a lignite mine. Zorba seduces the hotel-owner Madame Hortense, whom he calls 'Bouboulina.' The narrator and Zorba give each other the pen-names Canavaro and Bouboulina. The narrator is inspired by Zorba's love of life to sleep with a widow who is consequently beheaded. Zorba cut off his finger because it got in the way of his pottery wheel. The narrator is reminded of the Buddha by his 'internal no.' The novel opens with the narrator reading Dante in a cafe in Piraeus. Zorba is described as a santuri player. Wikipedia

The Odyssey: A Modern Sequel

A 33,333-line epic poem billing itself as a continuation of Homer's Odyssey. The protagonist meets Don Quixote and Jesus before his sailboat collides with an Antarctic iceberg, killing him. The soul separates from his body. The protagonist becomes an ascetic and meets characters named Motherth and Kapetan Enas. Kazantzakis's frequent translator Kimon Friar argued that the philosophy of The Saviors of God was echoed in this poem. Wikipedia

The Greek Passion

A novel in which Manolios, a shepherd, is chosen to play Christ in a passion play staged every seven years in the village of Lycovrissi. He falls in love with Katerina, who plays Mary Magdalene. A priest refuses to help Turkish refugees and excommunicates Manolios for trying to help them. Manolios is ultimately murdered by his fellow villagers. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Nikos Kazantzakis is a Greek novelist and poet whose most frequently clued work is Zorba the Greek. In that novel, the narrator travels to Crete with the vivacious title character (a santuri player) to reopen a lignite mine. Zorba seduces the hotel-owner Madame Hortense, whom he calls 'Bouboulina.' The narrator and Zorba use the pen-names Canavaro and Bouboulina. The novel opens with the narrator reading Dante in a cafe in Piraeus. Zorba's love of life inspires the narrator to sleep with a widow who is then beheaded. Zorba cut off his finger because it interfered with his pottery wheel, and the narrator is reminded of the Buddha by his 'internal no.'

The Last Temptation of Christ is Kazantzakis's second most frequently clued work. Satan (a black angel) disguises himself as a guardian angel and tempts Jesus to live a pleasure-filled life rather than be crucified. The title character constructs crosses for crucifixions. The novel ends with Christ being called a 'coward, deserter, traitor' before dying with the cry 'IT IS ACCOMPLISHED!'

The Odyssey: A Modern Sequel is a 33,333-line epic poem continuing Homer's Odyssey. The protagonist meets Don Quixote and Jesus before being killed by an iceberg in Antarctica. His soul separates from his body. Kazantzakis's translator Kimon Friar linked the poem's philosophy to The Saviors of God.

The Greek Passion features Manolios, a shepherd chosen to play Christ in a passion play staged every seven years in Lycovrissi. He falls in love with Katerina (playing Mary Magdalene) and is ultimately murdered by fellow villagers after a priest excommunicates him for helping Turkish refugees.

Freedom or Death (also called Captain Michalis) is about a Cretan rebellion against the Ottomans. Emine, the Muslim wife of Nuri, is Captain Michalis's beloved. A man shouts the revolutionary slogan while clutching the severed head of his nephew.

Kazantzakis also wrote The Saviors of God (a spiritual work with sections 'The Preparation' and 'The Silence'), and minor works including The Fratricides, Serpent and Lily, and a one-act tragedy ironically called Comedy.
