      Summary of Facts

      Hugo van der Goes (c. 1440-1482) was an early Netherlandish painter from Ghent, in the Flemish/Dutch-speaking region of Belgium. He was a member of the painter's guild of Ghent, where Agnes van den Bossche studied under him.

      His best-known work is the Portinari Altarpiece, a triptych depicting the Adoration of the Shepherds, commissioned by Tommaso Portinari. It was the first oil-on-panel work in Florence and the largest Flemish work there, installed in the hospital of Santa Maria Nuova. It took eight years to deliver, requiring 'sixteen burly porters' to travel up the Arno. Its off-kilter, expressive shepherds had a major influence on Italian artists, particularly inspiring Domenico Ghirlandaio's Adoration of the Shepherds in the Sassetti Chapel. The exterior panels display the Annunciation in grisaille. Portinari may have commissioned it after seeing Hans Memling's Last Judgment. Some scholars once credited Andrea del Castagno with the work.

      His other major work is the Monforte Altarpiece, a richly colored depiction of the Adoration of the Magi.

      Van der Goes suffered a famous late-career mental breakdown at the Red Cloisters monastery, recorded by Gaspar Ofhuys, in which he believed his soul was lost. He was said to have gone mad after his Portinari Altarpiece failed to exceed the van Eycks' Ghent Altarpiece. The Belgian artist Emile Wauters painted the best-known depiction of his breakdown, showing him sitting on a monastery chair. Vincent van Gogh compared himself to van der Goes in letters to his brother Theo.
