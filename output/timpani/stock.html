The timpani (kettledrums) is the most important percussion instrument of the Classical-era orchestra. Large copper-bodied drums tuned by a foot pedal, developed from the Arabic naker. Most clued as the subject of iconic solos: the opening of Beethoven's Violin Concerto (four soft D's), the opening of Grieg's Piano Concerto (crescendo roll), and Haydn's Symphony No. 103 ('Drumroll') opening solo. Carl Nielsen's 'Inextinguishable' Symphony features a famous timpani duel.
The Timpani Duel in Nielsen's 'Inextinguishable' Symphony

Carl Nielsen's Symphony No. 4 (Det Uudslukkelige, 'The Inextinguishable') features two sets of timpani placed on opposite sides of the orchestra, which 'duel' by playing tritones in the finale before a 'glorioso' coda. The timpani are told to play with 'a certain menacing character' throughout. Nielsen wrote that the symphony depicts the 'elemental will to live.' Wikipedia

Beethoven's Violin Concerto – Timpani Opening →

Beethoven's Violin Concerto in D major (Op. 61) opens with five soft D's played by the timpani (sometimes described as 'four D's' or 'four solo D's'), followed by the rest of the orchestra. This is one of the most famous timpani solos in the repertoire. Wikipedia

Haydn's Symphony No. 103 – 'Drumroll' Opening

Haydn's Symphony No. 103 in E-flat major is nicknamed the 'Drumroll' for its opening: a long roll (E-flat) on the timpani that immediately precedes the main theme. After the roll, the lower strings play a slow 3/4-time rendition of the Dies Irae theme. The second movement features a violin solo written for the virtuoso Giovanni Battista Viotti. Johann Christian Fischer wrote an unusual symphony for eight obbligato timpani. Wikipedia

Haydn's Mass in Time of War – Paukenmesse

Haydn's Mass in B-flat major, Hob. XXII:9 (1796), nicknamed the 'Paukenmesse' (German for 'Kettledrum Mass') because of an ominous, foreboding timpani solo in the Agnus Dei. The solo introduces a C-minor section that follows a Benedictus reflecting the 'Time of War.' Wikipedia

Haydn's 'Surprise' Symphony – Mid-Performance Retuning

Haydn's Symphony No. 94 in G major ('Surprise') features a notable timpani episode in its first movement: the timpanist is instructed to retune the G-drum up to A (in the first movement, three measures provided for retuning). The 'Surprise' Symphony is the earliest major work in which the timpani are tuned mid-performance; the German nickname ('Paukenschlag') references the fortissimo chord in the second movement. General / Instrument Description

The timpani (kettledrums, Italian pl. from singular timpano) consist of copper hemispherical shells with a membrane stretched over them, tuned using a sliding foot pedal (the Dresden pedal system was developed in the late 1800s, enabling mid-performance retuning). In the Classical era, timpani were primarily used to double trumpets on tonic and dominant notes. Berlioz's Requiem calls for 16 timpani. Carl Nielsen's 'Inextinguishable' features the famous duel. Other notable uses: opens Grieg's Piano Concerto (crescendo roll); four D's open Beethoven's Violin Concerto; opens Sibelius's Symphony No. 1 under a clarinet solo and introduces the scherzo theme; plays triplets on C and G in Also sprach Zarathustra; Prokofiev's Romeo and Juliet uses 15 straight notes for Tybalt's death. Beethoven's Eighth Symphony requires them tuned to octave F's. Developed from the Arabic naker.

Summary of Facts

The timpani (kettledrums) is the most commonly clued percussion instrument in quizbowl. These large, copper-bodied drums are tuned using a sliding foot pedal; the Dresden pedal system developed in the late 1800s enabled rapid mid-performance retuning. In the Classical era, timpani were primarily used to double trumpets on tonic and dominant pitches, and were the only percussion instrument in Beethoven's first eight symphonies. They are also used to represent thunder (Berlioz's Symphonie fantastique), hunters' gunshots (Prokofiev's Peter and the Wolf), and various dramatic effects. Berlioz's Requiem unusually calls for 16 timpani. The instrument descended from the Arabic naker.

The single most frequently clued timpani passage is the duel in the finale of Carl Nielsen's Symphony No. 4 ('The Inextinguishable'): two sets of timpani placed on opposite sides of the orchestra play tritones (F/B and D-flat/G) against each other before a 'glorioso' coda, with a 'certain menacing character' throughout. Nielsen wrote the symphony to depict the 'elemental will to live.'

Other iconic timpani solos: Beethoven's Violin Concerto in D major opens with four or five soft D's on solo timpani before the orchestra enters — one of the most famous timpani moments in the repertoire. Grieg's Piano Concerto in A minor opens with a crescendo roll on timpani before the soloist's entry. Haydn's Symphony No. 103 is nicknamed the 'Drumroll' for its opening timpani roll, after which the lower strings play a slow 3/4-time rendition of the Dies Irae. Haydn's Mass in Time of War is nicknamed the 'Paukenmesse' for its ominous Agnus Dei timpani solo. Haydn's 'Surprise' Symphony (No. 94) is the earliest major work in which the timpani are retuned mid-performance (German nickname 'Paukenschlag' references the fortissimo chord in the second movement). In Sibelius's Symphony No. 1, the timpani plays under the opening clarinet solo and later introduces the scherzo theme. In Also sprach Zarathustra (Strauss), the timpani responds to the trumpet fanfare with loud triplets on C and G. Beethoven's Symphony No. 9 scherzo has the opening motif answered by a brief one-measure timpani solo while the orchestra is silent.

Notable speciality works: Elliott Carter's Eight Pieces for Four Timpani; Philip Glass's Concerto Fantasy for Two Timpanists; Johann Christian Fischer's symphony for eight obbligato timpani; Leonard Bernstein called for timpani played with maracas. In Prokofiev's Romeo and Juliet, 15 straight timpani notes signify the death of Tybalt.
