Summary of Facts

M.C. Escher (1898-1972) was a Dutch graphic artist renowned for his impossible constructions, tessellations, and visual paradoxes. His work is frequently linked to Douglas Hofstadter's Pulitzer Prize-winning book Gödel, Escher, Bach, which describes his art as depicting "strange loops" — structures that exhibit self-reference.

His most commonly clued works include Relativity (1953), a lithograph depicting faceless people in a world with multiple sources of gravity, with staircases forming a Penrose triangle shape, inspired by Piranesi's Imaginary Prisons. His tessellation works (including Reptiles and Verbum) were inspired by Islamic tile art at the Alhambra Palace in Spain, which he sketched multiple times. He was obsessed with the "regular division of the plane," creating works contrasting black-and-white tessellations of knights and birds.

Drawing Hands is his famous lithograph of two hands drawing each other into existence, exemplifying Hofstadter's "strange loop" concept. Hand with Reflecting Sphere (1935) is a self-portrait in his Roman studio where he looks directly into a reflective sphere. Ascending and Descending depicts Penrose stairs, while Waterfall uses the Penrose tribar (which Roger Penrose created after being inspired by Escher's work) to depict a perpetual motion machine.

Other works include Another World (a mezzotint with bird-humans inspired by a simurgh statue), the Metamorphosis series (featuring the Italian town of Atrani), Belvedere (referencing Bosch's Garden of Earthly Delights), Snakes (his final print, with rotational symmetry), and a self-portrait inspired by Parmigianino's Self-Portrait in a Convex Mirror with an Indonesian shadow puppet. He studied under Samuel Jessurun de Mesquita and was popularized by Martin Gardner in Scientific American.
