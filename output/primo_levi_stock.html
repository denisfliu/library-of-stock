Summary of Facts

Primo Levi is an Italian Jewish chemist and author, best known for his Holocaust memoir If This Is a Man (also titled Survival in Auschwitz) and his short story collection The Periodic Table.

If This Is a Man describes Levi's experiences as a prisoner in Auschwitz. In a pivotal chapter, the narrator attempts to recite Dante's 'Canto of Ulysses' from memory to the 'messenger boy' Jean (Pikolo) while fetching soup rations, comparing hearing the poetry to 'the blast of a trumpet, the voice of God.' The narrator recites the line 'you were made men / To follow after knowledge and excellence' and uses the line 'and over our heads the sea closed up' to illustrate his notion of the 'drowned' and 'saved.' The narrator is told 'there is no why here' after having an icicle confiscated. After being beaten in an electrical warehouse, a French girl tells the narrator 'bite your lip, little brother.' The 'Lager' is used throughout to describe the concentration camp, and the 'Ka-Be' is the infirmary where nurses confiscate spoons from the ill and sell them for profit. A bricklayer named Lorenzo smuggled food to Levi. The book includes chapters titled 'The Work' and 'The Drowned and the Saved.' The opening poem addressed to 'you who live safe in your warm houses' asks the reader to consider a man 'who works in the mud' and a woman 'without hair and without name,' commanding 'carve them into your hearts' or have 'your children turn their faces from you.' The memoir closes with a meditation paralleling the Jewish V'ahavta. Attempts are described to cut handkerchiefs out of shirts at an Exchange Market where Mahorca tobacco is traded for bread. Ann Goldstein completed a three-volume translation in 2015.

The Periodic Table is a short story collection in which each story is named for a chemical element. 'Argon' analogizes autobiographical episodes to chemical properties. 'Cerium' describes Levi's theft of iron-cerium from a chemical factory to make cigarette lighters, buying two months of bread. 'Vanadium' describes Levi's surprise at recognizing his former Auschwitz colleague Dr. Muller through his characteristic spelling of 'naphthenate' in a letter about defective varnish; Muller asks 'Why are you so perturbed?' 'Silver' is set on the fictional island of Desolation, where Corporal Abrahams gave features Welsh place names (Holywell Cave, Mt. Snowdon, Weeping Forest). 'Nitrogen' contains the line 'matter is matter, neither noble nor vile, infinitely transformable.' 'Carbon' narrates the cyclical journey of a single carbon atom and is the final story. In one story, a researcher replied 'Follow me' (from the Gospel) after Levi was rejected by professors; Levi handles a 'half pea' of a substance 'like a holy relic.'

The Drowned and the Saved is Levi's last work, an essay collection sharing its title with a chapter of If This Is a Man. It includes 'Letters from Germans' (reprinting and analyzing letters sent in response to his first book), 'The Memory of the Offense,' and 'The Gray Zone,' which places Chaim Rumkowski of Lodz in the titular moral area.

Levi's poem 'Buna' recounts the 'monotonous horror' of mud in Auschwitz. His poem 'Crescenzago' suggests that surprisingly the sun rises in the title location. He also wrote the novel If Not Now, When?, about a watchmaker named Mendel who fights alongside the violinist Gedalah behind enemy lines in Russia. Levi's difficulty finding a thesis advisor due to anti-Semitic laws is described in his works.
