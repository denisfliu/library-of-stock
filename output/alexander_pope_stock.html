Alexander Pope

18th-century English poet, master of the heroic couplet. Most commonly identified by The Rape of the Lock (Belinda's hair, Baron, sylphs), The Dunciad (goddess Dulness, Colley Cibber/Lewis Theobald), "An Essay on Criticism" ('To err is human; to forgive, divine,' 'Fools rush in,' 'A little learning is a dang'rous thing'), "An Essay on Man" ('Whatever is, is right,' Great Chain of Being), "Epistle to Dr. Arbuthnot" ('damn with faint praise,' 'who breaks a butterfly upon a wheel'), "Eloisa to Abelard" ('Eternal sunshine of the spotless mind'), and his translation of Homer's Iliad.

Summary of Facts

Alexander Pope was an 18th-century English poet, the master of the heroic couplet and a major satirist of the Augustan era.

The Rape of the Lock is a mock-epic inspired by a real incident between Lord Petre and Arabella Fermor, in which a Baron uses Clarissa's 'two-edged weapon' (scissors) to cut a lock of Belinda's hair. The poem declares 'What mighty contests rise from trivial things.' A guardian sylph is sliced in half trying to protect the lock. A vial full of 'soft sorrows, melting griefs, and flowing tears' appears, and a bag of 'sighs, sobs and passions' from the Cave of Spleen triggers fury. The lock's history is traced through seal rings, a belt buckle, and a baby's whistle before being set in the stars. The poem mentions 'coffee, which makes the politician wise.' Anne Finch criticized it, prompting Pope's 'Impromptu.'

The Dunciad is a mock-heroic satire in which the goddess Dulness crowns either Lewis Theobald (three-book version) or Colley Cibber (four-book version) as her new king. The bookseller Edmund Curll competes in humiliating games including a pissing contest and slips in excrement, saved by the sewer goddess Cloacina. Theobald's Shakespeare Restored had attacked Pope's Shakespeare edition. Pope walked London with a Great Dane and two loaded pistols after publication.

'An Essay on Criticism' is a Horace-inspired poem that originated many famous maxims: 'To err is human; to forgive, divine,' 'Fools rush in where angels fear to tread,' 'A little learning is a dang'rous thing,' and 'Drink deep, or taste not the Pierian Spring.' It advises 'First follow NATURE' and notes 'Nature and Homer were, he found, the same.'

'An Essay on Man' is a didactic philosophical poem addressed to Lord Bolingbroke about the Great Chain of Being ('draws all to agree'; 'upheld by God, or Thee?'). It declares 'Whatever is, is right' and places man on 'this isthmus of a middle state / A being darkly wise, and rudely great.' Voltaire called it 'the most sublime didactic poem ever.' Nabokov's Pale Fire references Zembla from this poem.

'Epistle to Dr. Arbuthnot' is a satirical verse letter containing the phrases 'damn with faint praise' and 'Who breaks a butterfly upon a wheel?' (about John Hervey as 'Sporus'). It opens 'Shut, shut the door, good John!' and compares a bad writer to a spider.

'Eloisa to Abelard' coined the phrase 'Eternal sunshine of the spotless mind' and yearns 'The world forgetting by the world forgot.' Pope was inspired by heartbreak over Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, whom he later attacked as 'Sappho.'

Pope's translation of Homer's Iliad was praised by Samuel Johnson as 'a performance which no age or nation can pretend to equal.' It opens 'Achilles' wrath, to Greece the direful spring' and uses 'wat'ry waste' where modern translations use 'wine-dark sea.' Half of the Odyssey sequel was ghostwritten by Fenton and Broome. He also wrote 'Peri Bathous' (parody of Longinus's On the Sublime), Newton's famous epitaph, 'Ode on Solitude,' and a Guardian essay 'Against Barbarity to Animals.' He was a member of the Scriblerus Club with Swift, Gay, and Arbuthnot.
