18th-century English poet, master of the heroic couplet. Most commonly identified by The Rape of the Lock (Belinda's hair, Baron, sylphs), The Dunciad (goddess Dulness, Colley Cibber/Lewis Theobald), "An Essay on Criticism" ('To err is human; to forgive, divine,' 'Fools rush in,' 'A little learning is a dang'rous thing'), "An Essay on Man" ('Whatever is, is right,' Great Chain of Being, 'Hope springs eternal'), "Epistle to Dr. Arbuthnot" ('damn with faint praise,' 'who breaks a butterfly upon a wheel'), "Eloisa to Abelard" ('Eternal sunshine of the spotless mind'), and his translation of Homer's Iliad.

Summary of Facts

Alexander Pope was an 18th-century English poet, the master of the heroic couplet and a major satirist of the Augustan era. He was a member of the Scriblerus Club with Jonathan Swift, John Gay, and John Arbuthnot.

The Rape of the Lock is a mock-epic inspired by a real incident between Lord Petre and Arabella Fermor, opening with 'What dire offence from am'rous causes springs, / What mighty contests rise from trivial things.' Its dedication names the occult book Le Comte de Gabalis as the source for its supernatural 'machinery.' The Baron builds an altar of billet-doux, then uses a 'forfex' (Clarissa's 'two-edged weapon' / scissors) to cut Belinda's lock of hair after a game of ombre at Hampton Court. Sylphs led by Ariel guard Belinda — fifty guard her petticoat, Ariel guards her spaniel Shock — but a guardian sylph is sliced in half trying to protect the lock. Ariel threatens the punishment of Ixion on the wheel of a chocolate Mill. A thousand sprites use their wings to 'twitch' the diamond in a character's ear to aid Crispissa. The 'dusky melancholy' gnome Umbriel visits the Cave of Spleen, ruled by a Queen served by handmaids 'Ill-Nature' and 'Affection,' who gives him a vial of 'fainting fears, soft sorrows, melting griefs, and flowing tears' and a bag of 'Force of Female Lungs' and 'War of Tongues.' Bodies are 'chang'd to various Forms' including living teapots, jars, bottles, and a pipkin. The lock's history traces through seal rings, a belt buckle, and a baby's whistle before the lock shoots 'thro' liquid air' and becomes a constellation to 'add new glory to the shining sphere.' In later editions, Clarissa gives a speech ('charms strike the sight, but merit wins the soul') parodying Sarpedon's speech to Glaucus. The poem mentions 'coffee, which makes the politician wise.' Anne Finch criticized it, prompting Pope's poem 'Impromptu.' Catullus's translation of a Callimachus poem from the Aetia was taken as inspiration.

The Dunciad is a mock-heroic satire in which the goddess Dulness crowns either Lewis Theobald (three-book version, as successor to Elkanah Settle) or Colley Cibber (four-book version, represented as 'Bays') as her new king. The bookseller Edmund Curll competes in humiliating games including a pissing contest (whose winner receives a copy of the works of 'Eliza Heywood'), slips in excrement, and is saved by the sewer goddess Cloacina; he also races to capture the ghost of The Poet Moore. Theobald's Shakespeare Restored had attacked Pope's edition of Shakespeare. Pope walked London with a Great Dane and two loaded pistols after publication. The poem was inspired by Dryden's Mac Flecknoe.

'An Essay on Criticism' is a Horace-inspired poem that originated many famous maxims: 'Fools rush in where angels fear to tread' (source of E.M. Forster's title Where Angels Fear to Tread), 'A little learning is a dang'rous thing,' 'Drink deep, or taste not the Pierian Spring,' and 'To err is human; to forgive, divine.' It advises 'First follow NATURE' and notes 'Nature and Homer were, he found, the same.' It claims 'words are like leaves; and where they most abound, much fruit of sense beneath is rarely found,' describes how 'a needless Alexandrine ends the song,' and says 'a perfect judge will read each work of wit with the same spirit that its author writ.' John Dennis is satirized as 'a certain bard.'

'An Essay on Man' is a didactic philosophical poem in four epistles addressed to Lord Bolingbroke about the Great Chain of Being, which 'draws all to agree' and is described as 'All are but parts of one stupendous whole, whose body Nature is, and God the soul.' It declares 'Whatever is, is right,' 'Hope springs eternal in the human breast,' and 'The proper study of mankind is man.' The second epistle advises 'Know then thyself, presume not God to scan.' Man is placed on 'this isthmus of a middle state / A being darkly wise, and rudely great.' It describes a 'poor Indian, whose untutor'd mind / Sees God in clouds, or hears him in the wind' whose 'faithful dog' guides him to heaven — an oft-invoked example of the noble savage. It imagines men with preternatural senses who would 'die of a rose in aromatic pain,' calls Bacon 'wisest, brightest, meanest of mankind,' Cromwell 'damned to everlasting fame,' and sums up Tory Politics: 'For forms of government let fools contest.' Voltaire called it 'the most sublime didactic poem ever.' Nabokov's Pale Fire takes the name 'Zembla' from this poem. Lady Montagu countered it: 'though he preaches ne'er so long / That all is right, his head is wrong.'

'Epistle to Dr. Arbuthnot' is a satirical verse letter containing the phrases 'damn with faint praise' and 'Who breaks a butterfly upon a wheel?' (about John Hervey satirized as 'Sporus,' 'a painted child of Dirt that stinks and stings,' 'a mere white curd of ass's milk'). It opens 'Shut, shut The Door, good John!' because 'All Bedlam, or Parnassus, is let out.' It compares a bad writer to a spider who 'spins the slight, self-pleasing thread anew.' Addison is satirized as 'Atticus,' 'blessed with each talent and each art to please' but 'too fond to rule alone, / Bear, like the Turk, no brother near the throne.' Pope thanks Arbuthnot for helping 'through this long disease, my life.' He flatters himself that people say he 'cough[s] like Horace' and has 'Ovid's nose.' G.K. Chesterton noted the poem's 'illuminating' hatred.

'Eloisa to Abelard' coined the phrase 'Eternal sunshine of the spotless mind' and yearns 'The world forgetting by the world forgot.' It was based on a translation by Pope's friend John Hughes, inspired by Pope's heartbreak over Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, whom he later attacked as 'Sappho.'

Pope's translation of Homer's Iliad was praised by Samuel Johnson as 'a performance which no age or nation can pretend to equal.' It opens 'Achilles' wrath, to Greece the direful spring' and uses 'wat'ry waste' for what is now 'wine-dark sea.' Half of the Odyssey sequel was ghostwritten by Fenton and Broome. He also wrote 'Peri Bathous' (parody of Longinus), Newton's famous epitaph, 'Windsor Forest' (condemning the Normans, composed for the Peace of Utrecht), 'Ode on Solitude,' and a Guardian essay 'Against Barbarity to Animals.'
