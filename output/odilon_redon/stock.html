French Symbolist painter and printmaker. Best known for The Cyclops. Early career devoted to charcoal lithographs ('noirs'), later shifted to colorful oils and pastels. Associated with Gustave Moreau. Works include The Cyclops, Guardian Spirit of the Waters, The Eye like a Strange Balloon, and depictions of Buddha, Ophelia, and The Birth of Venus.

Summary of Facts

Odilon Redon was a French Symbolist painter and printmaker, best known for The Cyclops (c. 1914), in which the one-eyed Polyphemus peers over a hill covered in colorful flowers at the sleeping nude nymph Galatea. He was a member of the Symbolist school alongside Gustave Moreau and Fernand Khnopff, and was taught by the draftsman Rodolphe Bresdin.

The first half of Redon's career was devoted to charcoal lithographs known as his 'noirs,' which he abandoned by 1900 in favor of colorful oils and pastels. Key noirs include: The Eye like a Strange Balloon Mounts toward Infinity (an eyeball in a hot-air balloon, from his lithograph series For Edgar Allan Poe), Guardian Spirit of the Waters (a giant floating head supported by a single wing), The Crying Spider (a humanoid spider), Cactus Man (spines jutting from a head on a man in a box), The Marsh Flower (a 'sad and human head,' homage to Goya's Los caprichos), and the series Dans le Reve (11 lithographs including The Gnome and Limbo). His odd charcoal drawings were included in the final Impressionist exhibition.

After turning to color, Redon painted several versions of The Birth of Venus (always with a conch shell, not scallop or oyster, with forest animals watching from shore in one version), multiple depictions of Buddha (Walking Among the Flowers, The Death of the Buddha, one in mosaic robes with a staff), several Ophelia works showing Nabis influence (Among the Flowers, with a Blue Cape, dissolving into yellow flowers and blue water), and the atmospheric Roger and Angelica (winged horse obscured by mist). He painted seventeen decorative panels for the Chateau de Domecy-sur-le-Vault, the Oannes (a snake-like figure inspired by Flaubert's Temptation of St. Anthony), and an Evocation of Butterflies.

Redon co-founded the Salon des Indépendants with Seurat and Signac, was a friend of Stéphane Mallarmé, and kept a journal called A Soi-même. He stands at the far left of Maurice Denis' Homage to Cézanne. The Barnes Foundation holds his St. George and the Dragon.
