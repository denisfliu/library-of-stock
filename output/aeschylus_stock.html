Summary of Facts

Aeschylus (~525–456 BCE) was an ancient Greek tragedian, often called the 'father of tragedy.' He is most commonly identified by the Oresteia trilogy, Prometheus Bound, The Persians, Seven Against Thebes, and The Suppliant Women.

The Oresteia consists of three plays. In Agamemnon, Clytemnestra lays out a purple carpet for the returning king and murders him (with Aegisthus's help) in revenge for his sacrifice of Iphigenia. Cassandra enters the house praying for revenge, crying that it 'stinks of murder.' In The Libation Bearers, Electra finds a lock of hair and footprints resembling her own at her father's tomb; Orestes and Pylades disguise themselves as travelers from Phocis to carry out their revenge on Clytemnestra. In The Eumenides, the Furies pursue Orestes for matricide. Athena breaks a tie to acquit him at the Areopagus, arguing the father is the more important parent since she herself sprang from Zeus's head. The Furies are renamed 'Eumenides' ('Kindly Ones') at the end. This play centers on the invention of the jury trial.

Prometheus Bound depicts Prometheus chained to a mountain by Kratos and Bia, struck by lightning, and having his liver eaten. He tells the Oceanids about a marriage that could threaten Zeus's downfall. It opens 'O divine air Breezes on swift bird-wings... The multitudinous laughter of Mother Earth!' Some scholars (Martin West, Mark Griffith) argue it was composed by Aeschylus's son Euphorion.

The Persians is the earliest surviving play, uniquely set in contemporary events: Atossa mourns the defeat of her son Xerxes at Salamis. Pericles served as choregos. Atossa dreams of two women fighting before her son yokes them to his chariot.

In Aristophanes' The Frogs, Aeschylus defeats Euripides in a poetry contest, using the phrase 'lost his little oil flask' to interrupt Euripides' prologues. Aeschylus apocryphally died when an eagle dropped a turtle on his bald head.
