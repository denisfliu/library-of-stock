American photographer (1946–1989) known for stark, stylized black-and-white photographs of BDSM culture, male nudes (especially African-American men), flowers, and celebrities. Close collaborator and friend of Patti Smith — photographed the cover of her album Horses. His posthumous exhibition The Perfect Moment was controversially canceled by the Corcoran Gallery in Washington, D.C., sparking a major debate over public arts funding and obscenity. Died of AIDS in 1989.
The Perfect Moment (Exhibition, 1988–1990)

Retrospective exhibition that became the center of a major controversy over public arts funding and obscenity. Planned for Washington D.C.'s Corcoran Gallery, which canceled it under Congressional pressure. It included explicitly homoerotic and BDSM photographs, including Man in a Polyester Suit, Jim and Tom Sausalito, and an image of five-year-old Jesse McBride. The Cincinnati Contemporary Arts Center was subsequently prosecuted for obscenity for showing it. The NEA placed restrictions on grants after the controversy. Wikipedia

Black Book / African-American Male Nudes

Mapplethorpe's Black Book (1986) focuses on nude African-American men. It has been criticized for its exploitative fetishization of Black men. The photograph Derrick Cross shows a Black man with his back to the viewer and his ankles grasped in outstretched hands. Man in a Polyester Suit is another image in the series. Glenn Ligon paired quotes describing their 'degradation of blackness' with photographs from this collection. Wikipedia

Self-Portraits

Mapplethorpe made several notable self-portraits. One shows him holding a rifle in front of a black five-pointed star (referencing Patty Hearst/SLA). Another taken a year before his death shows him in a black turtleneck grasping a cane topped with an ornamental skull — a memento mori. Another self-portrait shows him inserting a whip into his anus. Patti Smith Photographs

He also portrayed her nude, sitting on a wooden floor with her knees to her chest, holding a radiator pipe. Smith described their relationship in her 2010 memoir Just Kids. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Robert Mapplethorpe (1946–1989) was one of the most controversial and celebrated American photographers of the late twentieth century. Known for his stark, formally composed black-and-white images, he explored BDSM culture, male nudity, flowers, and celebrity portraiture with equal aesthetic rigor. He claimed his approach was sculptural: 'I see things like they were sculptures.'

His long-running friendship and creative partnership with the rock musician Patti Smith was central to his early career. He photographed the cover of her debut album Horses (1975) and she described their bohemian New York life together in her acclaimed 2010 memoir Just Kids — including her period of supporting him financially while he made altars from found objects. He also photographed Smith nude, and shot the covers of several of her later albums.

Mapplethorpe organized his most explicit photographs into the X, Y, and Z Portfolios, dividing 39 works into three groups (X for BDSM, Y for flowers, Z for Black male nudes). His 1986 Black Book, a collection of nude African-American men including actor Derrick Cross, was criticized by scholar Kobena Mercer for its 'fantasy of absolute authority' and exploitative fetishization. Glenn Ligon later paired photographs from the collection with 78 quotes describing their 'degradation of blackness.' His photograph Jim and Tom Sausalito depicts a hooded man urinating into another man's mouth; Man in a Polyester Suit shows a Black man's clothed body with his genitals exposed.

His posthumous exhibition The Perfect Moment became the center of a landmark culture war over public arts funding and obscenity. The Corcoran Gallery in Washington, D.C. canceled the show under Congressional pressure in 1989, the same year Mapplethorpe died of AIDS. The National Endowment for the Arts subsequently placed restrictions on its grants. The controversy sparked a national debate about censorship and government funding of the arts.

Other key works include several self-portraits: one shows him holding a rifle in front of a five-pointed star (referencing Patty Hearst's SLA mug shot); a late self-portrait taken a year before his death depicts him in a black turtleneck grasping a cane topped with an ornamental skull. He also photographed his friend Louise Bourgeois holding her phallic sculpture Fillette in a long black coat — one of the most famous photographs in contemporary art history. He photographed bodybuilder Lisa Lyon for the 1983 book Lady, Lisa Lyon. The most expensive Mapplethorpe photograph ever sold was a platinum print portrait of Andy Warhol. After his death, his estate was donated to the Getty Research Institute in Los Angeles.
