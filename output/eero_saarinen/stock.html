Finnish-American modernist architect and furniture designer. Son of architect Eliel Saarinen. Best known for the Gateway Arch in St. Louis, the TWA Flight Center at JFK Airport, and Dulles International Airport. Also designed the MIT Chapel and Kresge Auditorium, the CBS Building ('Black Rock'), the Miller House in Columbus, Indiana (famous for its conversation pit), and the Ingalls Rink at Yale ('the whale'). Designed iconic furniture for Knoll including the Tulip, Womb, and Grasshopper chairs. The Tulip chair famously appeared on the set of Star Trek and was designed to eliminate the 'slum of legs.'
Gateway Arch (1965)

The most frequently clued Saarinen work. A stainless steel catenary arch in St. Louis, Missouri — the world's tallest arch, 630 feet tall — built as the Jefferson Expansion Memorial. Described as a 'weighted inverted catenary.' The arch design inspired his Dulles Airport roof. Wikipedia

Miller House, Columbus, Indiana (1957)

A landmark modernist residence in Columbus, Indiana, credited with popularizing the 'conversation pit' — a sunken seating area. The house features a bas relief by Jacques Lipchitz. The house was based on Villa Rotunda with four wings stemming from a central conversation pit, with interiors designed by Alexander Girard and gardens by Dan Kiley. Columbus, Indiana became famous for its architecture through J. Irwin Miller's patronage; a 2017 film Columbus by Kogonada was set there. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Eero Saarinen was a Finnish-American modernist architect and furniture designer, son of the Finnish architect Eliel Saarinen. He finished his studies at Yale. He is most famous in quizbowl for two structures: the Gateway Arch in St. Louis — the world's tallest arch at 630 feet, built as the Jefferson Expansion Memorial using a 'weighted inverted catenary' design — and the TWA Flight Center at JFK International Airport, now converted into the TWA Hotel. The TWA terminal's thin-shell exterior was 'shaped like a bird,' its 486 nontempered glass panels all had different shapes, and writer Vanessa R. Schwartz argues it was inspired by the concrete shell at Disney's Tomorrowland. The terminal was described by critic Vincent Scully as exemplifying 'paramilitary dandyism.' The TWA head house now forms part of JetBlue's Terminal 5.

Saarinen's CBS Building in New York City was nicknamed 'The Black Rock' for its dark granite pier facade, which he designed to be 'the simplest skyscraper in New York City.' He also designed Washington Dulles International Airport, featuring an airfoil-like concrete roof incorporating catenary curves (linking it to the Gateway Arch concept); for Dulles, he also designed a special 'mobile lounge' vehicle to transport passengers.

At MIT, Saarinen designed two adjacent buildings in Cambridge, Massachusetts: the nondenominational red-brick cylindrical chapel, which features an aluminum bell tower and spire by Theodore Roszak, and a Harry Bertoia sculpture that creates a cascade of light from a skylight to a white marble altar; and the Kresge Auditorium, shaped like a leaf — a perfect eighth of a sphere — whose roof of reinforced concrete premiered Aaron Copland's Canticle of Freedom. At Yale in New Haven, he designed the Ingalls Rink, nicknamed 'the whale' for its dramatic sloping roof; one building from that project was previously used as an all-girls dormitory with a drawbridge to keep men out.

Saarinen's Miller House in Columbus, Indiana is credited with popularizing the 'conversation pit' — a sunken seating area based on the Villa Rotunda plan, with four wings stemming from a central conversation pit, interiors by Alexander Girard, and gardens by Dan Kiley. The house contains a bas relief by sculptor Jacques Lipchitz. Columbus, Indiana became renowned for its modernist architecture through J. Irwin Miller's patronage; the 2017 film Columbus by Kogonada was set there among buildings including Henry Moore's Large Arch, I.M. Pei's library, and Robert Venturi's Fire Station No. 4. Saarinen designed multiple Columbus buildings including the Miller House, the North Christian Church (hexagonal base, central metal spire), and the Irwin Union Bank. Eero collaborated with his father Eliel on the First Christian Church in Columbus and the Kleinhans Music Hall in Buffalo, New York (which contains the Mary Seaton room).

As a furniture designer, Saarinen created three iconic chairs for Knoll: the Grasshopper, the Womb, and the Tulip. The Tulip Chair features a fiberglass shell attached to a single aluminum stem 'designed to look like they are the same material,' and was created to eliminate the 'slum of legs.' It gained mainstream exposure from its use on the original Star Trek set and is housed in the Vitra Design Museum. Florence Knoll (after whose married surname the company is named) collaborated with Saarinen to produce the Tulip Chair. The Saarinen pedestal tables appeared in 2001: A Space Odyssey.

Saarinen also collaborated with Charles Eames on the Entenza House (a Case Study House adjacent to the famous Eames House); designed the Vivian Beaumont Theater at Lincoln Center; and designed the IBM egg-shaped pavilion at the 1964 World's Fair (with a film by Charles and Ray Eames). He built a War Memorial in Milwaukee, which now houses the Milwaukee Art Museum's Quadracci Pavilion by Roche-Dinkeloo. He designed dormitories at Penn (Hill House, with alternating portrait and landscape windows) and at Vassar College (Noyes dormitory).

Eliel Saarinen's 1922 runner-up design for the Chicago Tribune Tower competition influenced the American Radiator Building in New York. Eliel also directed the Cranbrook Academy of Art, strongly influenced by the Bauhaus, and designed Christ Church Lutheran in Minneapolis as his last building. Lillian Swann Saarinen was a sculptor who created Night at the 1939 World's Fair, depicting the panther Bagheera, and designed a reflecting pool surrounded by buffalo originally paired with Eliel's catenary curve design for the Jefferson Expansion Memorial.
