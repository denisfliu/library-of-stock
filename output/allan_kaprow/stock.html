American performance artist and critic (1927-2006) who pioneered the 'Happening' as an art form. Studied under John Cage; taught at Rutgers and later Claes Oldenburg. Coined the term 'environments' for transformed gallery spaces — a precursor to installation art — and coined 'happenings' to describe participatory, improvisatory performance events. His essay 'The Legacy of Jackson Pollock' laid out the theoretical basis for happenings and called for 'concrete art' made of everyday materials. Member of Fluxus. Called his practice 'un-art.'
Happenings (as a form) / 18 Happenings in 6 Parts (1959)

The art form Kaprow coined: participatory, improvisatory performances in which audiences act as participants in a temporary event. Kaprow introduced the term in his essay 'The Legacy of Jackson Pollock.' His seminal work '18 Happenings in 6 Parts' was presented at the Reuben Gallery in 1959 and documented by Michael Kirby. The form discards the hierarchy between audience and artist; Yoko Ono's Cut Piece is sometimes classified as a happening. Wikipedia

"The Legacy of Jackson Pollock" (essay)

Kaprow's foundational essay, written while teaching at Rutgers, that laid out the theory behind happenings. It argues that after Pollock, artists should pursue a more spontaneous 'new concrete art' rather than creating 'near-paintings' mimicking Pollock's scale and form. It called for art made of 'paint, chairs, food, electric and neon lights, smoke, water, old socks, a dog, and movies.' The essay introduced the concepts of 'environments' and 'happenings.' Collected in Kaprow's 2001 essay anthology The Blurring of Art and Life. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Allan Kaprow (1927-2006) was an American performance artist, critic, and theorist. He studied under composer John Cage and later taught at Rutgers. He is credited as a teacher of Claes Oldenburg. He coined both 'happenings' and 'environments,' was a member of Fluxus, and described his own practice as 'un-art.' His essay 'Art Which Can't Be Art' explained how everyday acts like brushing one's teeth can be artistic practice. He influenced the development of socially engaged art in the United States.

Summary of Facts

Allan Kaprow (1927-2006) was the American artist-theorist who invented the 'Happening' and transformed mid-century ideas about what art could be. Trained as a painter, Kaprow studied with composer John Cage in the 1950s and channeled Cage's ideas about chance and audience participation into a new visual practice. While teaching at Rutgers, he wrote 'The Legacy of Jackson Pollock,' a landmark essay arguing that after Pollock's radical act-of-painting, artists should abandon 'near-paintings' mimicking Pollock's scale and instead make spontaneous 'concrete art' from everyday materials — 'paint, chairs, food, electric and neon lights, smoke, water, old socks, a dog, and movies.' That essay introduced both the concept of 'environments' (transformed gallery or real-world spaces that preceded installation art) and the 'happening' (a participatory, improvisatory event in which viewers become performers). Kaprow's landmark 1959 work 18 Happenings in 6 Parts, staged at the Reuben Gallery and documented by Michael Kirby, broke the event into concurrent compartments and drew participants into the art rather than keeping them as observers. His happening Eat took place in a cave in the Bronx, where a girl fried bananas while visitors were offered red or white wine, and Kaprow served boiled potatoes. His happening Household, photographed by Sol Goldberg at Cornell, began with the construction of a wooden tower and a nest of saplings before culminating in women licking strawberry jam off the hood of a burning car — an image that recurs across Kaprow's work as a signature motif of bodies, vehicles, and condiments. His Environment Yard filled the Martha Jackson Gallery with car tires. Kaprow also taught Claes Oldenburg, and was a member of Fluxus alongside Yoko Ono and Nam June Paik. He described his own practice as 'un-art' and in his essay 'Art Which Can't Be Art' argued that everyday activities like brushing one's teeth could constitute artistic practice. His work is collected in the 2001 anthology The Blurring of Art and Life. Kaprow's influence extends to socially engaged art, relational aesthetics, and installation art, and his happenings are credited as a direct precursor to performance art broadly.
