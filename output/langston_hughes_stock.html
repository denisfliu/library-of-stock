Central figure of the Harlem Renaissance. His most iconic poems are 'The Weary Blues' (musician playing a 'drowsy syncopated tune' on Lenox Avenue), 'The Negro Speaks of Rivers' ('My soul has grown deep like the rivers,' composed on a train to Mexico), 'Harlem' / 'A Dream Deferred' ('Does it dry up / like A Raisin in the Sun?,' inspiring Hansberry's play title), 'I, Too' (responds to Whitman, 'I, too, sing America'), 'Mother to Son' ('Life for me ain't been no crystal stair'), and 'Theme for English B' ('the only colored student in my class'). Wrote the essay 'The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain,' the novel Not Without Laughter, the Jesse B. Semple stories, and translated Gabriela Mistral, Nicolas Guillen, and Garcia Lorca. Collaborated with Zora Neale Hurston on the play Mule Bone. Wrote libretti for Troubled Island (with William Grant Still) and Street Scene (with Kurt Weill).
The Weary Blues

A poem about a musician playing a 'drowsy syncopated tune' 'down on Lenox Avenue' 'by the pale dull pallor of an old gas light.' After playing, 'The stars went out and so did the moon' and the man sleeps 'like a rock or a man that's dead.' Also the title of the collection containing it. Countee Cullen admonished Hughes not to become a 'racial artist' in a review of this collection. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Hughes was a central figure of the Harlem Renaissance. He wrote the essay 'The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain' about the 'urge within the race towards whiteness' (in response to George Schuyler's 'The Negro-Art Hokum'). He created the character Jesse B. Semple (Simple) for satirical stories told to a writer alter-ego named Boyd. His autobiography The Big Sea recounts meeting Zora Neale Hurston and their collaboration on the play Mule Bone. He wrote the novel Not Without Laughter (set in 1910s Kansas, with bluesman W.C. Handy adored by characters Hagar and Sandy). He wrote the libretto for the opera Troubled Island (about the Haitian Revolution, with William Grant Still) and Street Scene (with Kurt Weill). He translated Gabriela Mistral, Nicolas Guillen ('Sensemaya,' 'Songoro Cosongo'), and Federico Garcia Lorca (Gypsy Ballads including 'Sleepwalking Ballad'). Vachel Lindsay claimed to have 'discovered' him while he was working as a busboy. Jessie Fauset, literary editor of The Crisis, helped launch his career. Carl Van Vechten patronized and photographed him. He also produced the photobook The Sweet Flypaper of Life with Roy DeCarava.

Summary of Facts

Langston Hughes is the most prominent poet of the Harlem Renaissance. His poems are among the most frequently asked-about in quizbowl.

'The Weary Blues' describes a musician playing a 'drowsy syncopated tune' 'down on Lenox Avenue' by 'the pale dull pallor of an old gas light.' The musician 'did a lazy sway' and played with 'his ebony hands on each ivory key,' making the piano 'moan with melody.' Afterward, 'the stars went out and so did the moon' and he sleeps 'like a rock or a man that's dead.' The collection of the same name was reviewed by Countee Cullen, who admonished Hughes not to become a 'racial artist.'

'The Negro Speaks of Rivers' declares 'My soul has grown deep like the rivers' while invoking the Euphrates, Congo, Nile, and Mississippi. The speaker 'heard the singing of the Mississippi when Abe Lincoln went down to New Orleans.' Hughes composed it on a train trip to Mexico, as recounted in his autobiography The Big Sea.

'Harlem' (also known as 'A Dream Deferred') asks 'What happens to a dream deferred?' with answers including 'Does it dry up / like A Raisin in the Sun?', 'Does it stink like rotten meat?', 'crust and sugar over like a syrupy sweet?', and 'Or does it explode?' This poem inspired the title of Lorraine Hansberry's play A Raisin in the Sun. It appears in the collection Montage of a Dream Deferred, which also contains 'Dream Boogie' (opening 'Good morning, daddy! / Ain't you heard?').

'I, Too' (or 'I, Too, Sing America') responds to Walt Whitman's 'I Hear America Singing.' The speaker, 'the darker brother,' is sent to 'eat in the kitchen when company comes' but says 'Tomorrow, I'll be at the table' and 'They'll see how beautiful I am / And be ashamed.' It was chosen as the 'Epilogue' to The Weary Blues collection.

'Mother to Son' features a mother telling her son 'Life for me ain't been no crystal stair' — it has 'had tacks in it / And splinters / And boards torn up.' She urges 'Don't you fall now' and 'I'se still climbin'.' It was included in the collection The Dream Keeper.

'Theme for English B' presents 'the only colored student in my class' who likes 'a pipe for a Christmas present, / or records — Bessie, bop, or Bach.' 'Let America Be America Again' asks 'Say, who are you that mumbles in the dark?' and 'Who said the free? Not me?' Other poems include 'Harlem Sweeties' (describing 'luscious, delicious, fine Sugar Hill'), 'The Cat and the Saxophone' (set at 2AM), and 'Good Morning Revolution' (addressing revolution as 'the very best friend I ever had').

Hughes wrote the essay 'The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain' about the 'urge within the race towards whiteness,' responding to George Schuyler's 'The Negro-Art Hokum.' He created the character Jesse B. Semple (Simple) for satirical stories. His novel Not Without Laughter, set in 1910s Kansas, features the bluesman W.C. Handy adored by characters Hagar and Sandy, with the title referencing Hughes's definition of the blues as 'hopeless weariness' mixed with laughter.

He collaborated with Zora Neale Hurston on the play Mule Bone before their famous falling-out (described in The Big Sea). He wrote libretti for the opera Troubled Island (about the Haitian Revolution, with William Grant Still) and Street Scene (with Kurt Weill). He translated Gabriela Mistral, Nicolas Guillen ('Sensemaya,' 'Songoro Cosongo'), and Federico Garcia Lorca (Gypsy Ballads including 'Sleepwalking Ballad'; his translation of Blood Wedding has been lost). He also produced the photobook The Sweet Flypaper of Life with photographer Roy DeCarava.

Vachel Lindsay claimed to have 'discovered' him while he was working as a busboy. Jessie Fauset, literary editor of The Crisis, helped launch his career. Carl Van Vechten patronized and photographed him. He wrote for The Defender, a major African American newspaper in Chicago. Lynn Nottage's Crumbs from the Table of Joy also borrows its title from Hughes.
