Major Harlem Renaissance poet known for 'The Weary Blues,' 'The Negro Speaks of Rivers,' 'Harlem' ('A Dream Deferred'), 'I, Too,' 'Mother to Son,' and 'Theme for English B.' Also wrote prose, plays, and translations. His work celebrates Black life, jazz, and blues music.
The Weary Blues

A poem about a musician playing a 'drowsy syncopated tune' 'down on Lenox Avenue' 'by the pale dull pallor of an old gas light.' The musician 'did a lazy sway' and plays on a piano, making it 'moan with melody' with 'his ebony hands on each ivory key.' After playing, 'The stars went out and so did the moon' and the man sleeps 'like a rock or a man that's dead.'

The Negro Speaks of Rivers

A poem in which the speaker has 'known rivers ancient as the world and older than the flow of human blood in human veins.' The speaker 'bathed in the Euphrates when dawns were young,' 'heard the singing of the Mississippi when Abe Lincoln went down to New Orleans,' and declares 'My soul has grown deep like the rivers.' Hughes composed it on a train trip to Mexico, as recounted in The Big Sea.

Harlem (A Dream Deferred)

The poem asks 'What happens to a dream deferred?' and offers answers including 'Does it dry up / like a raisin in the sun?' and 'Or does it explode?' The title of Lorraine Hansberry's A Raisin in the Sun comes from this poem. It appears in Montage of a Dream Deferred.

I, Too (I, Too, Sing America)

A poem responding to Walt Whitman's 'I Hear America Singing.' The speaker is sent 'to eat in the kitchen / when company comes' but says 'Tomorrow, I'll be at the table.' The poem ends 'I, too, am America' and declares 'They'll see how beautiful I am / And be ashamed.'

Mother to Son

A dramatic monologue in which a mother tells her son 'Life for me ain't been no crystal stair.' Instead, her life 'has had tacks in it / And splinters / And boards torn up.' She urges 'Don't you fall now' and 'I'se still climbin'.'

Theme for English B

The speaker is 'the only colored student in my class' at the Harlem Branch Y, writing an assignment after an instructor tells him 'Go home and write / a page tonight.' The speaker says 'will my page be colored that I write?' and describes walking from the Y through Harlem.

General / Biographical

Hughes was a central figure of the Harlem Renaissance. He wrote the essay 'The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain' about the 'urge within the race towards whiteness.' He created the character Jesse B. Semple (Simple) for satirical stories told to a writer alter-ego named Boyd. His autobiography The Big Sea recounts meeting Zora Neale Hurston and their collaboration on the play Mule Bone. He wrote the libretto for the opera Troubled Island and Street Scene (with Kurt Weill). He translated Gabriela Mistral and Federico Garcia Lorca. Vachel Lindsay claimed to have 'discovered' him while he was working as a busboy.

Summary of Facts

Langston Hughes is the most prominent poet of the Harlem Renaissance. 'The Weary Blues' describes a musician playing a 'drowsy syncopated tune' 'down on Lenox Avenue' by 'the pale dull pallor of an old gas light,' who afterward sleeps 'like a rock or a man that's dead.' 'The Negro Speaks of Rivers' declares 'My soul has grown deep like the rivers' while invoking the Euphrates, Congo, Nile, and Mississippi, and was composed on a train to Mexico.

'Harlem' asks 'What happens to a dream deferred?' with answers including 'Does it dry up / like a raisin in the sun?' -- inspiring Lorraine Hansberry's play title. 'I, Too' responds to Whitman, with the speaker sent to 'eat in the kitchen when company comes' but declaring 'Tomorrow, I'll be at the table.' 'Mother to Son' features a mother telling her son 'Life for me ain't been no crystal stair.' 'Theme for English B' presents 'the only colored student in my class' writing an assignment.

Hughes wrote the essay 'The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain,' created the character Jesse B. Semple for satirical stories, and collaborated with Zora Neale Hurston on Mule Bone before their famous falling-out. His autobiography The Big Sea recounts his early career. He translated Gabriela Mistral and Garcia Lorca, and wrote libretti including Street Scene with Kurt Weill. Vachel Lindsay claimed to have 'discovered' him as a busboy.
