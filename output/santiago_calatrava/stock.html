Spanish architect and engineer (b. 1951, Valencia) known for curvilinear structures, cantilever-spar cable-stayed bridges, and organic forms. Major works: Turning Torso (Malmo), Milwaukee Art Museum Quadracci Pavilion (Burke Brise Soleil), World Trade Center Transportation Hub / Oculus (NYC), City of Arts and Sciences (Valencia). Also known for bridges including the Puente del Alamillo (Seville) and Zubizuri (Bilbao).
Turning Torso (Malmo, Sweden)

Skyscraper in Malmo, Sweden (tallest building in Sweden at completion; world's second twisting skyscraper). Constructed of nine twisted pentagonal segments; the top floor is rotated 90 degrees from the base. Based on Calatrava's own marble sculpture of a twisting human form. Adjacent to the Oresund Strait separating Malmo from Copenhagen.

Quadracci Pavilion, Milwaukee Art Museum

White ship-like addition to the Milwaukee Art Museum (which is housed in an Eero Saarinen War Memorial). The Quadracci Pavilion features Windhover Hall, whose chancel is shaped like the prow of a ship. The Burke Brise Soleil — a movable brise soleil of 72 white steel fin-like wings — opens and closes like a bird's wings. Part of an art complex also including the Kahler Building and Eero Saarinen's War Memorial Center. A large mast is used for the bridge connecting it.

City of Arts and Sciences (Valencia)

Cultural complex in Valencia, Spain, designed by Calatrava and Felix Candela. Built at the end of a sunken park in the former bed of the Turia River. Contains an IMAX theater whose outer concrete 'eyelid' can open to reveal a pool, an opera house named for Queen Sofia (sued for tile mosaics blown off — nicknamed 'white elephant'), and curvy aluminum brise soleils shading the buildings.

General / Biographical

Santiago Calatrava Valls (b. 1951, Valencia, Spain). Spanish architect and structural engineer known for curvilinear, organic forms and bridges. Designed Bilbao Airport's main terminal; Stadelhofen Station (underground arcade in Zurich, one of his early works); Gare de Lyon Saint-Exupery in Lyon (bird-in-flight form); Gare do Oriente in Lisbon ('forest' of steel trees above); Athens Olympic Velodrome and stadium with retractable glass roof (2004); communications tower on Montjuic (Barcelona, 1992 Olympics, doubles as sundial); Tenerife Auditorio (massive wave-like cantilevered arc). Born same country as Antoni Gaudi. Designed a swimming pool hanging from the cupola of the ETH Zurich library.

Summary of Facts

Santiago Calatrava Valls (b. 1951, Valencia, Spain) is a Spanish architect and structural engineer celebrated for his curvilinear, organic forms and cantilever-spar cable-stayed bridges. He was born in the same country as Antoni Gaudi.

Calatrava's most recognizable skyscraper is the Turning Torso in Malmo, Sweden — the country's tallest building and the world's second twisting skyscraper — which is constructed of nine twisted pentagonal segments such that the top floor is rotated 90 degrees from the base. It was based on Calatrava's own marble sculpture of a twisting human form. At the Milwaukee Art Museum, the Quadracci Pavilion takes the form of a ship: Windhover Hall's chancel is shaped like the prow of a ship, and the Burke Brise Soleil — 72 movable white steel fin-like wings — open and close like a bird. The pavilion is part of a complex also including the Kahler Building and Eero Saarinen's War Memorial Center.

At the World Trade Center site in New York, Calatrava designed the Transportation Hub (the 'Oculus'), whose white steel ribs form a pair of wings meant to evoke a bird released from a child's hands. He also designed the St. Nicholas Greek Orthodox Church at the same site. The Oculus was criticized for its poor functionality. For his hometown of Valencia, Calatrava constructed the City of Arts and Sciences with Felix Candela, sitting at the end of a sunken park in the former bed of the Turia River. The complex includes an IMAX theater whose outer concrete 'eyelid' can open to reveal a pool, and an opera house named for Queen Sofia, which Calatrava was later sued over after tile mosaics were blown off (the opera house was nicknamed 'the white elephant'). Curvy aluminum brise soleils shade many of the buildings.

Calatrava is celebrated for his bridges. The Puente del Alamillo in Seville (built for the 1992 World's Fair on the Isla de la Cartuja) is harp-like and asymmetric. The Zubizuri in Bilbao is a white tied-arch footbridge. The Chords Bridge (Bridge of Strings) is in Jerusalem, and the Sundial Bridge at Turtle Bay is in Redding, California. The Samuel Beckett Bridge in Dublin over the Liffey resembles a harp laid on its side, with 31 cables representing the strings; it rotates 90 degrees to let boats pass. The Constitution Bridge across Venice's Grand Canal was criticized for being out of place and was retrofitted with a spherical wheelchair lift that overheated and was later scrapped.

Among Calatrava's other notable works: the Stadelhofen Station (underground arcade, Zurich, an early work), the Gare de Lyon Saint-Exupery in Lyon (bird-in-flight form), the Gare do Oriente in Lisbon (a 'forest' of steel trees), a communications tower on Montjuic for the 1992 Barcelona Olympics that doubles as a sundial, an Olympic Velodrome and a stadium with a retractable glass roof for the 2004 Athens Olympics, the Tenerife Auditorio (massive wave-like cantilevered arc), and the Bilbao Airport terminal.
