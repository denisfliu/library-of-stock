Iraqi-British architect, Pritzker Prize winner (2004 — the first woman to receive it). Known as the 'queen of the curve' for her fluid, parametricist designs. Major works include the Guangzhou Opera House, the London Aquatics Centre (2012 Olympics), Rome's MAXXI Museum, the 2022 FIFA World Cup stadium (Al-Wakrah/Al-Janoub) in Qatar, the Vitra Fire Station, the Dongdaemun Design Plaza in Seoul, and the Phaeno Science Centre in Wolfsburg. A planned 2020 Tokyo Olympic Stadium was scrapped. Died in 2016.

Summary of Facts

Zaha Hadid was an Iraqi-born British architect who died in 2016. She was the first woman to win the Pritzker Prize, receiving it in 2004. She studied mathematics before moving to London to study architecture, where she studied under Rem Koolhaas and Elia Zenghelis; Koolhaas later co-founded the Office of Metropolitan Architecture. Her work is associated with Deconstructivism and Parametricism — the latter term coined by her firm's partner Patrik Schumacher, who also titled his book The Autopoiesis of Architecture using a term for self-generating systems. She became known as the 'queen of the curve.'

Her most frequently clued work is the Guangzhou Opera House in China, designed around the concept of two rocks being smoothed by the Pearl River (the 'twin boulders' design), largely made of glass. For the 2012 Summer Olympics she designed the London Aquatics Centre — notable for its wave-shaped/undulating roof, a concrete ceiling sloping toward the pool between two walls of windows, and spectator wings that were later removed. Rome's MAXXI (Italy's National Museum of 21st Century Arts) is built on a former military barracks and features floating/weaving staircases, flowing concrete beams, louvered skylights, and a sharply angular concrete facade overhang inspired by Constructivist art; Hadid described it as moving away from 'the museum as an object' toward 'a field of buildings.'

Hadid designed the Al-Wakrah / Al-Janoub Stadium for the 2022 FIFA World Cup in Qatar, inspired by the shape of a dhow fishing boat. She sued the New York Review of Books for defamation after criticism of her dismissal of concerns over dying migrant construction workers during its construction. She won the commission for the 2020 Tokyo Olympic Stadium, but the design — described by Arata Isozaki as 'a turtle waiting for Japan to sink so that it can swim away' and criticized as looking like a bicycle helmet — was scrapped. For the Dongdaemun Design Plaza in Seoul, she used the concept of a 'Metonymic Landscape,' and this building is often cited in connection with Schumacher's parametricism.

The Phaeno Science Centre in Wolfsburg rests on huge concrete stilts allowing visitors and vehicles to pass underneath, and inspired her Z.Scape furniture collection. Her first constructed building was the Vitra Fire Station, built after a factory fire on the Vitra campus in Germany. Her other works include the Cardiff Bay Opera House (unrealized), the BMW Central Building in Leipzig (bringing blue- and white-collar workers together), the Rosenthal Center / Contemporary Arts Center in Cincinnati (once described as her only US work), the Innovation Tower in Hong Kong, the Broad Art Museum at Michigan State University, a ski jump in Innsbruck, and the Evelyn Grace Academy (Z-shaped secondary school in London). She designed the first Serpentine Gallery annual pavilion in 2000, initiating the yearly tradition. As a furniture/product designer she created the Aqua Table (a single piece of polyurethane with three wave-like undulations), 3D-printed shoes for Rem Koolhaas's company United Nude, and boots for Lacoste. She mentored Ma Yansong at Yale. Before architecture she created paintings including The Peak Project, depicting an unrealized project for the hills of Kowloon.
