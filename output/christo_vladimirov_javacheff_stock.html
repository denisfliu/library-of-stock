Summary of Facts

Christo Vladimirov Javacheff (1935–2020), known mononymously as Christo, was a Bulgarian-born environmental and installation artist who became famous for large-scale, temporary projects that wrapped, draped, or altered famous landmarks and natural landscapes. He worked in close partnership with his wife Jeanne-Claude Denat de Guillebon throughout his career, and they were both founding members of the Nouveau réalisme movement led by Yves Klein.

Christo's most iconic project was the Wrapped Reichstag (1995), in which he and Jeanne-Claude draped the entire Berlin Reichstag building in over 100,000 square meters of silvery polypropylene fabric, held in place with blue rope. The project required decades of bureaucratic negotiation before receiving permission. Another celebrated work was The Gates (2005), a temporary installation of 7,503 saffron-colored fabric panels hung from gates along 23 miles of footpaths in New York City's Central Park. The photographer Annie Leibovitz famously photographed Christo himself wrapped tightly in fabric in Central Park — a visual pun on his artistic obsession.

Other major projects include Running Fence (1976), an 18-foot-high fence of white nylon fabric running 24.5 miles through the California landscape, documented in a Maysles Brothers film featuring a rancher who slept beside the artwork; Surrounded Islands (1983), in which 11 islands in Biscayne Bay, Miami were encircled with bright pink polypropylene floating on the water; and The Umbrellas (1991), a simultaneous installation of yellow umbrellas in California and blue umbrellas in Japan. The Umbrellas had a tragic outcome: high winds dislodged one umbrella in California, killing a bystander, and a Japanese worker was electrocuted during the dismantling. For much of his life, Christo sought permission from the United Arab Emirates to build the Mastaba, an enormous stacked-oil-barrel sculpture.

Christo's earliest works included Dockside Packages at Cologne Harbor and inflatable indoor Air Packages. His practice of wrapping figures was a consistent early motif — his work Wrapped Woman is believed to have inspired David Lynch's imagery of the wrapped body of Laura Palmer in Twin Peaks.
