Mexican muralist and one of "los tres grandes" of the Mexican Mural Renaissance, alongside Diego Rivera and David Alfaro Siqueiros. Best known for The Epic of American Civilization, a twenty-panel mural cycle at Dartmouth College's Baker Memorial Library. Also painted the Prometheus mural at Pomona College's Frary Dining Hall, and decorated the Cabañas Hospice in Guadalajara with 340 murals. Early career included the House of Tears watercolor series and cartoons for the newspaper The Vanguard. Taught by Dr. Atl; influenced Jackson Pollock, who painted Bald Woman with Skeleton after seeing an Orozco mural. Shares surname with contemporary conceptual artist Gabriel Orozco.
The Epic of American Civilization

Twenty-panel mural cycle at Dartmouth College's Baker Memorial Library (commissioned with support from Ernest Hopkins). Chronicles migration of native peoples, arrival of Europeans, and rise of industry. Contains sections on Quetzalcoatl, Cortez, Gods of the Modern World, Modern Industrial Man, and more. Hood Museum maintains a digital version. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

José Clemente Orozco was a Mexican muralist and one of 'los tres grandes' of the Mexican Mural Renaissance alongside Diego Rivera and David Alfaro Siqueiros. He was taught by the pseudonymous artist Dr. Atl, who introduced him to revolutionary circles. Early in his career he compiled a watercolor series called House of Tears depicting prostitutes and drew cartoons for the newspaper The Vanguard. The Whitney Museum's 2020 exhibit Vida Americana profiled los tres grandes and their influence on American art.

Orozco's most frequently clued work is The Epic of American Civilization, a twenty-panel mural cycle commissioned (with support from Ernest Hopkins) for Dartmouth College's Baker Memorial Library, where the Hood Museum maintains a digital version. The mural chronicles the migrations of native peoples, the arrival of Europeans, and the rise of industry. Key sections include: 'Gods of the Modern World,' in which skeletons in academic gowns and mortarboards watch another skeleton give birth atop a pile of books — mortarboard-wearing fetus skeletons in glass jars represent the birth of useless knowledge; the 'Departure of Quetzalcoatl,' showing black serpents rising from the sea around a white-robed, golden-bearded deity who points to the right toward a red pyramid; 'Cortez and the Cross,' depicting conquistadors wielding a cross like a weapon with an ornately armored rearing horse; 'The Machine,' a giant black pillar that devours a pile of bodies while a gray-cloaked figure hugs a cross behind an armored swordsman; scenes contrasting a blue-clad woman among rural children with the industrial Machine; a horde of stern identical-looking blonde children near a barnhouse; sections on Modern Industrial Man; a decorated general preparing to stab Emiliano Zapata in the back; and panels with golden wheat fields contrasted against gold coins and an eternal flame under a figure buried in wreaths. The mural also contains a man gorging himself on gold coins and a section depicting ancient and modern human sacrifice. Scholar Jacquelynn Baas posited that Orozco's depiction of Christ destroying his cross symbolizes his denial of the sacrificial destiny assigned to Mexican citizens. Jackson Pollock visited this mural and afterward painted his figurative work Bald Woman with Skeleton.

Also frequently clued is the Prometheus mural in Frary Dining Hall at Pomona College, which depicts the Titan on his knees reaching beyond the top of the painting to claim fire; a side panel shows black-and-white centaurs watching Prometheus steal fire. Sergei Eisenstein wrote an essay titled 'Prometheus of Mexican Painting,' comparing his own films to Orozco's style.

Orozco also decorated the former Cabañas Hospice (orphanage) in Guadalajara — Mexico's second largest city — with 340 murals; the hospice stands opposite the Degollado Theatre on Tapatia Plaza. He made murals at the Mexican National Preparatory School. His painting Christ Destroying His Cross shows Christ with an axe amid a Greek column, a statue of Buddha, and a pile of guns. The Trench depicts a blonde man surrounded by waves pointing angrily out to sea as gray people look on, and shirtless men with machine gun rounds smashed against rocks. He also painted Cain in the United States, depicting naked men with distorted faces carrying a black body with a cracked skull.

Orozco shares his surname with contemporary conceptual artist Gabriel Orozco, who made the skull sculpture Black Kites (covered in black-and-white checkers), the chess piece installation Horses Running Endlessly, and suspended a whale skeleton in Mobile Matrix and Dark Wave. The Ecuadorian muralist Oswaldo Guayasamin studied alongside Orozco, and the two met at a gallery hosted by the Rockefeller family.
