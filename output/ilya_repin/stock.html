Ilya Repin (1844–1930) was the most prominent Russian Realist painter and leader of The Wanderers (Peredvizhniki). He is best known for Barge Haulers on the Volga (eleven laborers pulling a barge up the river), Reply of the Zaporozhian Cossacks (raucous Cossacks drafting an insulting letter to Mehmed IV), Ivan the Terrible and His Son Ivan (tsar clutching his dying son on a blood-red carpet), and Religious Procession in Kursk Province (Orthodox crowd carrying an icon while a hunchbacked boy is excluded). His work was famously called 'kitsch' by Clement Greenberg, who argued a Russian peasant would prefer it to Picasso.
Barge Haulers on the Volga (1870-1873)

Eleven laborers struggling to pull a boat up the Volga River. The only person in the central group looking upward is a fair-haired youth in red, contrasting with his despondent comrades. One man tries to refill his pipe from a blue tobacco pouch; a flag's three stripes are inverted; a ship with an unfurled sail appears in the background. This work brought Repin the repute to join The Wanderers. Clement Greenberg contrasted this with Picasso in 'Avant-Garde and Kitsch.' Also subject of a folk song published by Mily Balakirev. Reply of the Zaporozhian Cossacks (1880-1891)

Mustached men surrounding a large table, laughing as they compose an insulting letter to Ottoman Sultan Mehmed IV. A fat man in a white fur cap and red clothes laughs; a shirtless gambler with a kobza (lute-like instrument) sits next to a deck of cards; a man with a quill at the center was modelled on the historian Repin consulted. Insults include 'Goat-fucker of Alexandria,' 'catamite of Tartary,' 'Macedonian mechanic,' and 'Devil shits and your army swallow it.' Alexander III bought it for 35,000 rubles — a record price at the time. A later smaller version was made in which no one smokes a pipe. Repin created it as a tribute to his Ukrainian homeland. Summary of Facts

Ilya Repin (1844–1930) was the most prominent 19th-century Russian Realist painter and leader of The Wanderers (Peredvizhniki), a movement that formed after the Artel of Artists revolted against the Imperial Academy's restrictions on academic painting.

Barge Haulers on the Volga is Repin's most famous work, depicting eleven laborers struggling to pull a boat up the Volga River. The only one in the central group looking upward is a fair-haired youth in red, contrasting with his despondent comrades. One man tries to refill his pipe from a blue tobacco pouch; a flag has its three stripes inverted. This work brought Repin the recognition to join The Wanderers. Clement Greenberg famously contrasted this work with Picasso in 'Avant-Garde and Kitsch,' suggesting a Russian peasant would prefer the kitsch of Repin over the avant-garde of Picasso. The Volga barge haulers (burlaki) were also the subject of a folk song published by Mily Balakirev.

Reply of the Zaporozhian Cossacks shows mustached men at a table laughing uproariously as they compose an insulting letter to Ottoman Sultan Mehmed IV. A fat man in a white fur cap and red clothes laughs; a shirtless gambler with a kobza (a lute-like instrument) sits next to a deck of cards; the man with the quill at the center was modelled on the historian Repin consulted. The letter insults the sultan as 'a Macedonian mechanic,' 'a nasty glob of spit,' 'Goat-fucker of Alexandria,' and 'catamite of Tartary,' and asserts 'the Devil shits and your army swallow it.' It was bought by Alexander III for 35,000 rubles — a record price for a Russian painting. Repin later made a smaller version in which no one smokes a pipe, and wrote that another artist 'knows nothing' of their country's history after that artist compared the Cossacks to restaurant revellers. Repin made the painting as a tribute to his Ukrainian homeland.

Ivan the Terrible and His Son Ivan depicts a crazed monarch clutching his dying son, with blood on a red carpet and a cane/staff lying on the floor. The tsar is wide-eyed, trying to cover the wound on his son's head. The composition was inspired by the 'Sweetness of Revenge' section of Antar. The painting was vandalized in 2018 by Igor Podporin with a metal pole because he believed it was historically inaccurate. A museum curator reportedly jumped in front of a train upon learning of an earlier attack on the painting.

Religious Procession in Kursk Province shows Orthodox priests and peasants carrying a golden shrine with the Root Icon of the Sign from Kursk's Black Hermitage. A red-haired priest and a hunchbacked boy walk at the front; a bearded man with a stick tries to strike a blond boy; two maids in religious garb hold an empty box. An earlier version was set in an oak wood, but Repin reset the final version in a sparse landscape during a drought after returning home to find deforestation. The painting controversially included a drunk peasant.

Other notable works include: They Did Not Expect Him (also known as Unexpected Visitor) — a woman in black sees her gaunt exiled son return, with teenage girls looking toward the man walking in from the hallway; Sadko in the Underwater Kingdom — a mermaid procession and fish (three above Sadko's head) on the left while the legendary merchant Sadko stands on the right; What Freedom! — a gleeful couple holding hands amidst crashing waves; Dragonfly — his young daughter sitting on a branch or fence with her feet dangling; Duchess Sophia in the Convent — malnourished child in background while the central figure looks on angrily; Saint Nicholas Saves Three Innocents — the saint grabs a sword by the blade as a man in chains wonders why he's alive; The Hopak — an elaborately dressed Cossack, reportedly painted just before Repin died. He also painted portraits of art collector Pavel Tretyakov standing in front of his collection and of surgeon Evgeny Pavlov. While studying under Marià Fortuny, he painted a sitting African woman nicknamed 'Ebony' by critics. Repin earned a cult following in the Soviet Union.
