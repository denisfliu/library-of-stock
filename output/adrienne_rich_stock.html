American feminist and lesbian poet known for 'Diving into the Wreck,' Twenty-One Love Poems, and the essay 'Compulsory Heterosexuality and Lesbian Existence.' Her work engages with feminism, lesbian identity, and poetic revision.
Diving into the Wreck

Rich's most iconic poem, in which the speaker descends alone, 'not like Cousteau with his assiduous team,' wearing 'the body-armor of black rubber' and 'the absurd flippers.' The speaker reads a 'book of myths' before the dive and at the poem's end carries 'a knife, a camera / and' that book 'in which our names do not appear.' The speaker comes for 'the thing itself and not the myth' and observes 'the drowned face always staring toward the sun.' The poem also contains the lines 'I am blacking out and yet / my mask is powerful / it pumps my blood with power.'

Twenty-One Love Poems

A collection of poems inspired by Rich's relationship with Jamaican author Michelle Cliff. The opening poem states 'no one has imagined us' in a city where 'screens flicker with pornography.' The sequence includes '(The Floating Poem, Unnumbered)' in which the lover's 'slender fingers' find a 'rose-wet cave.' The collection ends with 'we were two lovers of one gender.' It is addressed to Michelle Cliff and explores lesbian love openly.

Compulsory Heterosexuality and Lesbian Existence

Rich's influential 1980 feminist essay that popularized the term 'compulsory heterosexuality.' It appears in the collection Blood, Bread, and Poetry. The essay lists 'eight characteristics of male power' developed by Kathleen Gough and considers lesbian existence in relation to its 'compulsory' counterpart. Rich's support of Janice Raymond's trans-exclusionary feminism is also clued in connection with this essay.

When We Dead Awaken: Writing as Re-Vision

Rich's influential essay borrowing an Ibsen title. The lecture compares Rich's youthful formalism to 'asbestos gloves' that let her handle materials she 'couldn't pick up barehanded' and casts 'seeing with fresh eyes' as an 'act of survival' for women poets. It discusses the writing of 'Aunt Jennifer's Tigers.'

A Valediction Forbidding Mourning

Rich's poem sharing its title with John Donne's famous metaphysical poem. Rich's version decries the oppression of 'a male-made language' and contains the line 'The grammar turned and attacked me.' The narrator remembers 'a poster in the bus that said: my bleeding is under control.'

Other Poems and Works

Several other poems appear in clues: 'Aunt Jennifer's Tigers' describes 'bright topaz denizens of a world of green' who 'go on prancing, proud and unafraid.' 'Power' reflects on Marie Curie dying 'denying her wounds came from the same source as her power.' 'The Trees' describes 'a forest that was empty all these days.' 'Prospective Immigrants Please Note' states 'The door itself makes no promises. It is only a door.' Rich's essay 'Vesuvius at Home' discusses Emily Dickinson. Her collections include A Change of World, Snapshots of a Daughter-in-Law, and Tonight No Poetry Will Serve. She won a 1974 award split with Allen Ginsberg and invited Audre Lorde and Alice Walker to collectively accept it. Rich wrote Of Woman Born, calling herself 'Kali, Medea...the unwomanly woman in flight from motherhood.' She also wrote ghazals (Ghazals: Homage to Ghalib), which Agha Shahid Ali criticized for not following classic rules.

Summary of Facts

Adrienne Rich is a major American feminist and lesbian poet whose work spans from formalist early collections to radical feminist and lesbian poetry. Her most frequently clued poem is 'Diving into the Wreck,' in which the speaker descends alone -- 'not like Cousteau with his assiduous team' -- wearing 'the body-armor of black rubber' and carrying 'a book of myths in which our names do not appear.' The poem is about seeking 'the thing itself and not the myth.'

Her collection Twenty-One Love Poems, inspired by her relationship with Jamaican author Michelle Cliff, is the second most clued work. It opens with 'no one has imagined us' in a city where 'screens flicker with pornography' and includes '(The Floating Poem, Unnumbered)' with its 'rose-wet cave' imagery. The collection concludes with 'we were two lovers of one gender.'

Rich's poem 'Planetarium,' dedicated to astronomer Caroline Herschel, contains the line 'I am bombarded yet I stand.' Her essay 'Compulsory Heterosexuality and Lesbian Existence' (1980) popularized the term 'compulsory heterosexuality' and appears in Blood, Bread, and Poetry. Her essay 'When We Dead Awaken: Writing as Re-Vision' borrows an Ibsen title and compares her youthful formalism to 'asbestos gloves.'

Other notable works include 'Aunt Jennifer's Tigers' ('bright topaz denizens'), 'A Valediction Forbidding Mourning' (sharing its title with Donne), and 'Power' (about Marie Curie). Rich's essay 'Vesuvius at Home' discusses Emily Dickinson. She wrote Ghazals: Homage to Ghalib, which Agha Shahid Ali criticized for not adhering to classic ghazal rules. Rich shared a 1974 award with Allen Ginsberg and invited Audre Lorde and Alice Walker to collectively accept it for all women 'who go unheard in a patriarchal world.'
