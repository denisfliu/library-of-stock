Summary of Facts

Daniel Chester French was an American sculptor best known for the massive seated statue of Abraham Lincoln at the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, D.C. The statue was carved by the Piccirilli brothers and is located in a Henry Bacon-designed Greek Doric temple on the National Mall next to the Reflecting Pool. An urban legend holds that Lincoln's hands spell his initials in sign language, inspired by French's earlier sculpture of Alice Cogswell at Gallaudet University.

French's career began when, as an unknown 23-year-old vouched for by Louisa May Alcott's sister, he received the commission to create The Minute Man in Concord, Massachusetts, commemorating the Battles of Lexington and Concord. The sculpture, whose pose resembles the Apollo Belvedere, depicts a man standing with a plow and was made from ten melted cannons donated by Congress.

His Alma Mater at Columbia University is a seated Athena with outstretched arms, with an owl hidden in the folds of her robes, located on the steps leading to Low Memorial Library. He allegorized the continents, depicting Africa as a woman between a lion and a sphinx. He created the Statue of the Republic for the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago. A badger and cluster of grapes adorn the helmet of his statue atop the Wisconsin State Capitol, for which Audrey Munson ('America's First Supermodel') modeled.

French collaborated with architect Henry Bacon on the Lincoln Memorial and a Lafayette sculpture in Prospect Park, and with sculptor Edward Clark Potter on the gold-plated quadriga Progress of the State at the Minnesota State Capitol. Potter trained in French's studio and later created the Patience and Fortitude lions at the New York Public Library. French also produced The Sons of God Saw the Daughters of Men That They Were Fair (a surprisingly erotic marble group) and a bronze relief monument to the Milmore brothers. He was a neighbor of Gertrude Vanderbilt Whitney when she created the Titanic memorial.
