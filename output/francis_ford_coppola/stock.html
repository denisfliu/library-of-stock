American director known for the Godfather trilogy, Apocalypse Now, and The Conversation. The Godfather (1972) and its sequel are among the most acclaimed films ever made. Apocalypse Now adapted Heart of Darkness as a Vietnam War film. The Conversation won the Palme d'Or at Cannes 1974. He also co-wrote the screenplay for Patton and co-founded American Zoetrope with George Lucas.

Summary of Facts

Francis Ford Coppola (born 1939) is a major American director of the New Hollywood era, best known for the Godfather trilogy and Apocalypse Now. He was born in Michigan; his father Carmine (a flutist) named him 'Ford' in honor of his employer. He co-founded American Zoetrope with George Lucas and was a frequent collaborator with sound designer Walter Murch.

The Godfather (1972), adapted from Mario Puzo's novel, is one of the most acclaimed films ever made. It opens with a mortician saying 'I believe in America' and begins with the wedding of Connie Corleone. Marlon Brando plays patriarch Vito Corleone, who says 'I'm going to make him an offer he can't refuse.' The film's most famous moments include: Michael (Al Pacino) retrieving a gun from a restaurant bathroom to shoot Officer McCluskey and Sollozzo; a studio director waking up with a horse's severed head; fish delivered with Luca Brasi's vest; Vito putting orange peel in his mouth to chase his grandson in a tomato patch before dying; and the baptism sequence, where shots of Michael renouncing Satan are intercut with the assassination of his enemies. The film ends with a door closing in front of Kay as other men kiss Michael's hand. Scored by Nino Rota, who composed the Love Theme ('Speak Softly, Now'). Sofia Coppola appeared as the baptized baby Michael Rizzi.

Apocalypse Now (1979) adapts Joseph Conrad's Heart of Darkness into a Vietnam War film. Captain Willard (Martin Sheen) is sent to assassinate the rogue Colonel Kurtz (Marlon Brando) — when Kurtz first appears, his face is obscured in shadow so only his ear and bald head are visible. Lieutenant Colonel Kilgore's helicopters attack a beach to the sound of Wagner's 'Ride of the Valkyries.' The Doors' 'The End' plays over the machete killing of a man, intercut with a water buffalo slaughter. The film was shot by Vittorio Storaro with heavy use of dissolves; the opening overlays a man's upside-down face onto a burning treeline. Walter Murch received the first ever credited Sound Designer credit on this film. The troubled production was documented in Hearts of Darkness. The film won the Palme d'Or at Cannes.

The Conversation (1974) is a paranoid surveillance thriller starring Gene Hackman as Harry Caul, who becomes obsessed with the recorded phrase 'he'd kill us if he got the chance.' The film opens with a three-minute slow zoom descending on Union Square in San Francisco, following a mime. It ends with Caul playing saxophone in his completely destroyed apartment after ripping it apart looking for a bug. The film won the Palme d'Or at Cannes in 1974 — it was nominated but lost Best Picture to The Godfather Part II. Coppola credited Blow-Up (Antonioni) rather than Watergate as its inspiration.

The Godfather Part II (1974) interweaves Michael's consolidation of power with flashback sequences of young Vito's rise in New York, including a boy singing to the Statue of Liberty. A sequence has a character traversing rooftops to intercept a man in white during a procession of Christ. Near the end, Michael recites the Hail Mary while fishing, filmed in shadow.

Coppola won two Palme d'Ors (The Conversation and Apocalypse Now), making him one of only eight directors to win it multiple times. He co-wrote the Academy Award-winning screenplay for Patton (1970) but lost the Best Director Oscar that year to Bob Fosse (for Cabaret). He also directed Bram Stoker's Dracula (1992) — which closes with an Annie Lennox song — and the Michael Jackson film Captain EO.
