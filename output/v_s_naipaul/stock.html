Trinidadian Nobel laureate of Indian descent. Best known for A House for Mr. Biswas (about a sign painter who tries to escape the Tulsi family) and A Bend in the River (about the Indian businessman Salim in an African country ruled by the 'Big Man'). Also wrote Miguel Street, The Mystic Masseur, and In a Free State. Controversial for dismissive comments about Jane Austen and female writers. Clashed with C.L.R. James.
A Bend in the River

A novel set in an unnamed African country ruled by a dictator known only as the 'Big Man.' Narrated by Salim, an Indian businessman who runs a shop with his assistant Metty in the Domain. Salim helps Ferdinand, the son of the magician Zabeth, attend school. The Big Man commissions the 'State Domain,' publishes a book of maxims that all citizens are forced to purchase, and sends the Youth Guard to do forced labor after they fail to promote the book. Salim plays squash at the Hellenic Club and beats and spits on a lover before leaving for London. A pamphlet claims 'the ancestors shriek' explaining the Liberation Army's motives. Salim's shop is nationalized and Ferdinand saves him from accusations of selling ivory on the black market. Salim sees a motto on a ruined monument that inverts a quote from Virgil's Aeneid. Summary of Facts

V.S. Naipaul (Vidiadhar Surajprasad Naipaul) is an Indo-Trinidadian Nobel laureate in Literature. His two most frequently referenced works are A Bend in the River and A House for Mr. Biswas.

A Bend in the River is set in an unnamed African country ruled by a dictator known only as the 'Big Man.' It is narrated by Salim, an Indian businessman who runs a shop with his assistant Metty in the Domain. Salim helps Ferdinand, the son of the magician Zabeth, attend school. The Big Man commissions the 'State Domain,' is advised by Raymond, and publishes a book of maxims ('Maximes') that all citizens are forced to purchase. He sends the Youth Guard to do forced labor after they fail to promote the book, then later disbands them, provoking the Liberation Army whose pamphlet claims 'the ancestors shriek.' Salim plays squash at the Hellenic Club, beats and spits on a lover before leaving for London. His shop is nationalized and Ferdinand saves him from accusations of selling ivory on the black market. A ruined monument near the town's dock bears a motto that inverts a quote from Virgil's Aeneid.

A House for Mr. Biswas follows Mohun Biswas, a sign painter who tries to escape the Tulsi family by getting his own home. He meets his wife Shama while painting signs for the Tulsi family and moves into Hanuman House. A pundit predicted at his birth that he was born the wrong way and would 'eat up his mother and father.' His father drowns after Biswas hides following a cow's death. Biswas writes about 'Deserving Destitutes' for The Sentinel and later gets a car while working as a Community Welfare officer. After stealing bananas, Jairam forces him to eat the rest until he develops stomach trouble.

Miguel Street (1959) is a collection of linked stories set on the title street in Trinidad. Residents include the tailor Bogart, the carpenter Popo (who works on a 'thing without a name' and gives up after being imprisoned for theft), the prophet Man-Man, the poet B. Wordsworth, and the gambler Hat. Popo becomes a belligerent drunk after his wife Emelda deserts him. The collection was inspired by a desire to answer an overheard shout of 'What happening there, Bogart?'

In a Free State is a Booker-winning short story collection. In the title story, Bobby and Linda drive through a recently independent African country. In 'One Out of Many,' Santosh marries a Black maid (described with the untranslated word 'hubshi') to gain citizenship after immigrating to Washington, D.C. In 'Tell Me Who to Kill,' a man moves to England to support his brother's engineering studies before learning his brother isn't in school.

The Mystic Masseur features Ganesh Ramsumair, who becomes a successful politician thanks to his ability to cure illness with his hands.

Biographically, Naipaul controversially claimed no female writer was his equal, saying he couldn't share Jane Austen's 'sentimental ambitions.' His clashes with the Afro-Trinidadian Marxist C.L.R. James are described in a biography by Farrukh Dhondy. Derek Walcott wrote a poem (in White Egrets) rebutting Naipaul's criticism of his poetry as 'unpeopled,' opening with 'Here's what that bastard calls the emptiness.'
