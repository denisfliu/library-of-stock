Do not go gentle into that good night

Thomas's most famous poem, a villanelle addressed to his dying father. The poem categorizes men into wise men, good men, wild men, and grave men, each of whom resist death in different ways. The refrain 'Rage, rage against the dying of the light' appears throughout. The speaker asks his father to 'Curse, bless, me now with your fierce tears.' Lines reference 'blind eyes' that 'could blaze like meteors and be gay,' alluding to his father's blindness. Collected in In Country Sleep and also in Deaths and Entrances.

Fern Hill

A poem about Thomas's childhood at his aunt's farm in Wales, recalling a time when he was 'young and easy under the apple boughs.' The poem ends with the famous line 'Time held me green and dying / Though I sang in my chains like the sea.' The speaker describes being 'honoured among the foxes and pheasants' and 'famous among the barns.' The poem uses the color green extensively, with 'green and carefree' and 'happy as the grass was green.' It was the last entry in Deaths and Entrances. The farm setting was Aunt Annie's farm, which also inspired the story 'The Peaches.'

General / Biographical

Welsh poet born in Swansea. His father David John Thomas was an English teacher in Wales, described as a 'clever, disappointed man' who introduced his son to poetry. Thomas addressed his father in the unfinished poem 'Elegy' as a 'cold kind man brave in his narrow pride.' Edith Sitwell supported Thomas and said he behaved impeccably in her presence. Roald Dahl built a replica of Thomas's house to write in. Thomas was notably a heavy drinker. Lawrence Ferlinghetti parodied Thomas in 'Underwear' with lines like 'do not go naked into that good night.' The collection Portrait of the Artist as a Young Dog includes stories set in Swansea. 'A Child's Christmas in Wales' was recited on BBC Radio. Other works include 'Poem in October' (thirtieth birthday poem), 'In my Craft or Sullen Art,' 'Request to Leda,' 'The Orchards,' and 'A Visit to Grandpa's.'

Summary of Facts

Dylan Thomas (1914-1953) was a Welsh poet born in Swansea, recognized in quizbowl as one of the most frequently clued twentieth-century poets. His work is dominated by a handful of major poems and a radio play, all of which appear with remarkable consistency across tournaments.

His most famous poem, 'Do not go gentle into that good night,' is a villanelle addressed to his dying, blind father, David John Thomas, who was an English teacher in Wales. The poem categorizes men into wise, good, wild, and grave types, each resisting death differently, and culminates in the refrain 'Rage, rage against the dying of the light.' The speaker asks his father to 'Curse, bless, me now with your fierce tears,' and describes 'blind eyes' that 'could blaze like meteors and be gay.' Thomas also addressed his father in the unfinished poem 'Elegy,' calling him 'a cold kind man brave in his narrow pride' who was 'too proud to die.'

'Fern Hill' is Thomas's great pastoral poem, recalling his childhood at Aunt Annie's farm where he was 'young and easy under the apple boughs,' 'honoured among the foxes and pheasants,' and 'famous among the barns.' The poem makes extensive use of the color green, with the speaker 'happy as the grass was green' and 'green and carefree,' ending with the celebrated line 'Time held me green and dying / Though I sang in my chains like the sea.' It was the last entry in the collection Deaths and Entrances.

From his first collection 18 Poems, 'The force that through the green fuse drives the flower' is characterized by its repeated phrase 'And I am dumb to tell,' connecting natural forces with human mortality. The speaker observes that 'of my clay is made the hangman's lime' and that the force driving his 'green age' is also his destroyer. This belongs to Thomas's early 'womb-tomb period.'

'And death shall have no dominion,' published in Twenty-Five Poems when Thomas was 21, takes its title from Paul's epistle to the Romans. The title line opens and closes all three stanzas, and the poem declares 'Though lovers be lost love shall not.' It envisions men 'mad and dead as nails' who 'break in the sun til the sun breaks down.'

Thomas wrote several poems inspired by the London Blitz. 'A Refusal to Mourn the Death, by Fire, of a Child in London' depicts the dead 'robed in the long friends, the grains beyond age, the dark veins of her mother' and concludes with 'after the first death, there is no other.' The poem 'Deaths and Entrances' is set 'on almost the incendiary eve' and warns of an enemy who 'will shut the sun.'

Under Milk Wood is a radio play (a 'play for voices') set in the fictional seaside town of Llareggub ('bugger all' reversed), featuring characters like Captain Cat and Mrs. Ogmore-Pritchard. Originally written for BBC Radio, the audience listens to the dreams of sleeping townsfolk.

Thomas was supported by Edith Sitwell, who noted he always behaved impeccably in her presence. Roald Dahl built a replica of Thomas's writing shed. His prose works include Portrait of the Artist as a Young Dog (stories set in Swansea), 'A Child's Christmas in Wales' (recited on BBC Radio), and early esoteric stories like 'The Orchards' and 'A Visit to Grandpa's.' Lawrence Ferlinghetti frequently parodied Thomas, including 'do not go naked into that good night' in 'Underwear.'
