T1 2025 COOT (2025) Diff 8 · Literature
A poem in this collection predicts that “those doomed to die” will be killed by the “sword’s hatred” after noting that “I do not believe that the riches of the world will stand forever”. An unfinished poem near the end of this collection laments that “the work of giants is decaying”. In this book, the narrator of one poem recalls how the “skilful poet” Heorrenda received lands that he was promised; that poem repeats “that passed away, so may this”. This collection contains a poem ending with the single word (*) ‘Amen’, which was translated in 1911 by Ezra Pound. Four major poems in the ubi sunt genre can be found in this collection, in which a key and an onion are described suggestively in two of its over 90 riddles. “The Lament of Deor” and “The Seafarer” appear in, for 10 points, which codex of Old English literature, named for a cathedral in southwestern England?
ANSWER: Exeter Book [or Exeter Codex, or Codex Exoniensis, accept Exeter Cathedral Library MS 3501; anti-prompt on “The Lament of Deor”, “The Ruin”, or “The Seafarer” before read by asking “what collection is that work part of?; do NOT accept or prompt on “Liber Exoniensis”]
T2 2023 IQBT Undergraduate Championship (2023) Diff 8 · Literature
The line “life is [one of these things], give it up” appears in the final couplet of a Hafez ode about being hungover. Arabic examples of these things include muʿammā (“moo-ah-mah”), whose invention is attributed to Abu Nuwas, as well as uhjiyya (“ooh-JEE-yeh”) and lughz (“loogz”). The writer Tatwine (“tah-TWEE-neh”) likely imitated the Epistola ad Acircium (“ay-PEE-stoh-lah ahd ah-KEER-kee-oom”) of Aldhelm, whose poetic examples of these things include De creatura (“day cray-ah-TOO-rah”) and the “Leiden” (“LAY-din”) one. Examples of these things concerning icebergs and onions are among the 94 of them in the (*) Exeter Book, written in Old English. The question “what’s in your pocket?” ends an exchange of these things in the dark between Bilbo and Gollum in The Hobbit. A line about a creature that stands on different numbers of legs is the sphinx’s example of, for 10 points, what quandaries?
ANSWER: riddles [accept chistan; accept Leiden riddle; accept puzzles or quandaries or enigmas before “Exeter Book” and prompt on any of those answers after; prompt on questions]

B1 2024 ACF Winter (2024) Diff 7 · Literature
The speaker of a poem included in this codex describes “the citadels crumbled” and “the work of giants ruined” in a poem that focuses on a “well-crafted ancient-work.” For 10 points each:
[10] Name this codex of Old English poems including “The Seafarer.” “Master artisans” lie buried for “a hundred generations” in “The Ruin,” a poem from this codex.
ANSWER: Exeter Book
[10] “The Ruin” belongs to this genre of Old English poems. “The Wife’s Lament” and “The Husband’s Message,”both of which are in the Exeter Book, are other examples of these poetic laments for the dead.
ANSWER: elegy [or elegies]
[10] Old English words used in “The Ruin,” including enta and orþonc (“or-thonk”), inspired character and place names used by this author. This author likened Beowulf to an elegy in the essay “The Monsters and the Critics.”
ANSWER: J. R. R. Tolkien [or John Ronald Reuel Tolkien]
B2 2018 Oxford Open (2018) Diff 8 · Literature
This language was used to write a poem which, translated, begins: ‘This masonry is wondrous; fates broke it / courtyard pavements were smashed; the work of giants is decaying.’ For 10 points each:
[10] The title character of another poem in this language reminisces that ‘Blithe heart cannot know, / through its happiness, what hardships they suffer / who drive the foam-furrow furthest from land.’
ANSWER: Anglo-Saxon (or Old English)
[10] This collection of Anglo-Saxon verse, the largest still in existence, contains The Wanderer, The Ruin, and The Seafarer, as well as 95 shorter riddles.
ANSWER: Exeter Book
[10] This fictional world takes its name from a line in the poem Christ I, which is included in the Exeter Book. The line in question is preceded by one that declares ‘Hail Earendel brightest of Angels’. This was also the name JRR Tolkien used for the central continent where most of the action of the Lord of Rings takes place.
ANSWER: Middle Earth (Hail Earandel brightest of angels/ over Middle Earth sent to men – Eálá Earendel engla beorhtast / Ofer middangeard monnum sended.)
B3 2015 ACF Nationals (2015) Diff 9 · Literature
In addition to ninety often double-entendre-heavy riddles, this collection’s contents include three allegorical poems about animals, including “The Phoenix”, “The Panther”, and one describing the “Fastitocalon”. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this codex, the source for such anonymous works as “The Whale”, “The Wanderer”, and “The Wife’s Lament”.
ANSWER: the Exeter Book [or the Codex Exoniensis]
[10] The poems in the Exeter Book were written in this language, which was also used to write the poems in the Vercelli Book and the Nowell Codex, the latter of which includes Beowulf.
ANSWER: Old English [or Anglo-Saxon; prompt on “English”]
[10] The fourth major codex of Old English literature is named for this poet, though he almost certainly wrote none of the poems in it. A poem named for him a nine-line hymn describing an illiterate cow-herd singing a song of the creation of the world, and it survives in Bede’s Ecclesiastical History.
ANSWER: Caedmon
