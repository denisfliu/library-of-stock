The Exeter Book is the largest surviving codex of Old English poetry, preserved at Exeter Cathedral (also called the Exeter Codex or Codex Exoniensis). It contains over 90 riddles, elegies, religious verse, and allegorical beast poems. It is one of four major codices of Old English literature, alongside the Vercelli Book, the Nowell Codex (which includes Beowulf), and the Caedmon manuscript. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

The Exeter Book (also called the Exeter Codex or Codex Exoniensis) is the largest surviving codex of Old English poetry, preserved at Exeter Cathedral in southwestern England. It is one of four major codices of Old English literature — the others being the Vercelli Book, the Nowell Codex (containing Beowulf), and the Caedmon manuscript.

The collection contains over 90 riddles (sometimes cited as 94 or 95), many of which are noted for their double-entendre content; riddles about a key and an onion are frequently cited examples. In addition to these riddles, the book contains four major poems in the ubi sunt genre and three allegorical beast poems: 'The Phoenix,' 'The Panther,' and one describing the 'Fastitocalon' (also known as 'The Whale').

Among the most famous elegies in the Exeter Book are 'The Seafarer,' 'The Wanderer,' and 'The Ruin.' 'The Seafarer' is a poem about the hardships of life at sea, whose narrator says 'Blithe heart cannot know, through its happiness, what hardships they suffer / who drive the foam-furrow furthest from land.' It ends with the single word 'Amen' and was famously translated in 1911 by Ezra Pound. 'The Wanderer' contains the passage predicting that 'those doomed to die' will be killed by the 'sword's hatred,' and the narrator notes 'I do not believe that the riches of the world will stand forever.' 'The Ruin' is an unfinished elegy lamenting a decayed city (probably Roman Bath), containing the famous phrase 'the work of giants is decaying'; it notes that 'master artisans' lie buried for 'a hundred generations.' Old English words from 'The Ruin' — including enta (giants) and orþonc — inspired character and place names in J.R.R. Tolkien's writing; Tolkien also likened Beowulf to an elegy in the essay 'The Monsters and the Critics.'

Other elegies include 'The Wife's Lament,' 'The Husband's Message,' and 'The Lament of Deor.' In 'Deor,' the narrator recalls how the 'skilful poet' Heorrenda received lands that were promised to him; the poem's refrain is 'that passed away, so may this.' The religious poem 'Christ I' contains the line 'Hail Earendel brightest of Angels / over Middle Earth sent to men,' which gave Tolkien the name 'Middle-earth' for his fictional world.
