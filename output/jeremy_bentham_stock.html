An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation

Bentham's major work (1789), which lays out the foundations of Utilitarianism. It opens with the famous claim that humanity is governed by 'two sovereign masters, pain and pleasure.' The work introduces the felicific calculus (also called the hedonistic calculus), which uses variables like extent, duration, fecundity, purity, and propinquity to determine the moral status of actions. It also argues against the 'principle of asceticism' and the 'principle of antipathy and sympathy,' rejected natural rights as 'nonsense upon stilts,' and included early arguments for animal rights.

A Fragment on Government

Bentham's early work criticizing William Blackstone's Commentaries on the Laws of England. The Fragment was intended as an introduction to legal reform and opens with a 'fundamental axiom.' It helped establish Bentham's reputation and led to his friendship with Lord Shelburne. Bentham first wrote about his general happiness principle in this text.

Legal Reform & Political Writings

Bentham was a prolific legal reformer who coined the term 'international law,' wrote the Manual of Political Economy, The Book of Fallacies (cataloguing bad arguments used by politicians), The Art of Packing (about libel juries), and A Defense of Usury. His Offences Against Oneself dismantled arguments for criminalizing homosexual sex. He was associated with legal positivism and influenced John Austin's command theory of law.

General / Biographical

Bentham is identified as the English founder of Utilitarianism, associated with the greatest happiness principle, the Panopticon, and his mentoring of John Stuart Mill. His quantity-focused approach to happiness was countered by Mill's distinction of higher and lower pleasures. Marx called him the 'arch-Philistine.' He sent model legislation and locks of hair to Latin American governments.

Summary of Facts

Jeremy Bentham is the English philosopher who founded Utilitarianism, the ethical theory that actions should be judged by whether they maximize happiness for the greatest number of people. His major work, An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation (1789), opens with the famous claim that humanity is governed by 'two sovereign masters, pain and pleasure.' The work introduces the felicific calculus (also called the hedonistic calculus), which quantifies the moral status of actions using variables like extent, duration, fecundity, purity, and propinquity.

Bentham rejected natural rights as 'nonsense upon stilts' and argued against the 'principle of asceticism' and the 'principle of antipathy and sympathy.' He advocated for animal rights with the famous question 'the question is not, Can they reason? nor, Can they talk? but, Can they Suffer?' and dismantled arguments for criminalizing homosexual sex in Offences Against Oneself.

His Panopticon — a circular prison design where inmates can be observed without their knowledge — is one of the most recognizable identifiers for Bentham. A Fragment on Government, his early work criticizing William Blackstone's Commentaries, is where he first discussed his general happiness principle and helped establish his reformist reputation.

Bentham was a prolific legal reformer who coined the term 'international law,' catalogued fifty bad arguments in The Book of Fallacies, wrote satirically about libel juries in The Art of Packing, and advocated laissez-faire economics in A Defense of Usury. He founded the Westminster Review in 1823 and influenced John Austin's command theory of law. He led the Philosophical Radicals alongside James Mill and mentored John Stuart Mill, whose distinction of higher and lower pleasures countered Bentham's purely quantitative approach. He was also associated with Epicureanism for his hedonistic foundations. Marx called him the 'arch-Philistine' in Capital, and he sent model legislation to Latin American governments, earning the title 'Legislator of the World.'
