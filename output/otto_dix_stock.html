Summary of Facts

Otto Dix (1891-1969) was a German painter and leading figure of the New Objectivity movement alongside George Grosz. His work harshly depicted the social inequality and moral decay of Weimar-era Germany, as well as the horrors of World War I.

His Metropolis triptych (1928) shows a nightclub scene in the central panel with Alfred Schulze playing saxophone, a dancing couple, and a woman with pink ostrich feathers, while the side panels depict crippled WWI veterans begging for money, ignored by prostitutes in fur. Seven Deadly Sins (1933) is an allegorical work in which a child-like Envy wears a Hitler mask while riding the back of the old hag Avarice; a skeleton with a scythe completes the composition, and the figures form the shape of a swastika.

His Portrait of the Journalist Sylvia von Harden (1926) is an unflattering depiction of a monocled woman with bobbed hair in a red-and-black checkered dress smoking at a café table; this image was recreated in the film Cabaret. Der Krieg (The War) is a series of 51 war prints modeled on Goya's Disasters of War, including scenes like Shock Troops Advance under Gas and a skull infested with maggots.

Other works include Die Skatspieler (deformed veterans playing cards), The Trench (which sparked national controversy), and a portrait of Maud Arizona/Suleika prominently featuring tattoos. His paintings were labeled 'degenerate art' by the Nazis.
