Modern Japanese composer (1930–1996), largely self-taught, who fused Western avant-garde and traditional Japanese musical styles. Called Debussy his 'great mentor' and Messiaen his 'spiritual mentor.' Composed scores for over 100 films, including multiple collaborations with Akira Kurosawa. Incorporated traditional Japanese instruments (biwa, shakuhachi) into orchestral works. Key concert works include November Steps, Rain Tree Sketch II, Toward the Sea, and Eclipse.
November Steps

Commissioned for the 125th anniversary of the New York Philharmonic. Written for biwa, shakuhachi, and orchestra. Takemitsu secluded himself with Debussy scores to write it. Seiji Ozawa conducted the premiere. Inspired after Leonard Bernstein heard Takemitsu's earlier piece Eclipse and made suggestions. Originally titled 'Water Rings'; renamed on the advice of Jasper Johns. Embraces Buddhist aesthetics (concept of 'ma'). Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Toru Takemitsu (1930–1996) was a largely self-taught modern Japanese composer who blended Western avant-garde techniques with the aesthetics of traditional Japanese music. He called Claude Debussy his 'great mentor' and Olivier Messiaen his 'spiritual mentor.' He studied under John Cage. He is known for his careful use of silence and for incorporating traditional Japanese instruments (biwa, shakuhachi) into concert and film works. He described his music as akin to 'the stillness of grass and flowers.' Initially averse to Japanese musical tradition, he grew to embrace it after a commission for the gagaku ensemble of the Imperial Household. He wrote one novel, featuring a detective. An annual composition award bears his name.

Summary of Facts

Toru Takemitsu (1930–1996) was a largely self-taught modern Japanese composer who became one of the most significant figures in twentieth-century music by synthesizing Western avant-garde techniques with the aesthetics of traditional Japanese music. He called Claude Debussy his 'great mentor' and Olivier Messiaen his 'spiritual mentor.' Rain Tree Sketch II, his last solo piano piece, was written as a memorial to Messiaen following his death; it is subtitled 'in memoriam Olivier Messiaen,' and its episodes marked 'Celestially light' bookend a structure whose many silences exemplify the Japanese concept of ma (negative space). He also studied under John Cage — after a dialogue between them, Cage translated a Basho haiku as 'What mushroom? / What leaf?' — and Cage's influence is visible in Corona, an experimental aleatoric piece consisting of five etudes notated as circles with no traditional musical notation. His early Requiem for Strings earned international recognition when it was heard on Stravinsky's 1958 tour through Tokyo; Stravinsky took Takemitsu out to lunch after hearing it. His masterwork November Steps, commissioned for the 125th anniversary of the New York Philharmonic and premiered by Seiji Ozawa, integrates traditional Japanese instruments — biwa and shakuhachi — with Western orchestra; Takemitsu retreated to a mountain villa with scores of Debussy's Prelude to the Afternoon of a Faun and Jeux to write it. The work was originally titled 'Water Rings' but renamed on the advice of Jasper Johns to reflect when it was premiered; it embraces Buddhist aesthetics. The earlier Eclipse, also for biwa and shakuhachi, included long pauses during which poetry by Rabindranath Tagore was recited; Leonard Bernstein's response to Eclipse inspired him to suggest Takemitsu write November Steps. Toward the Sea (also 'Towards the Sea'), commissioned by Greenpeace and inspired by Moby Dick, uses an S-E-A musical cryptogram and draws on Debussy's La mer; a version for flute, harp, and string orchestra exists. Similarly, Quotation of Dream is subtitled 'Say sea, take me!' referencing Debussy, and was written after Takemitsu met choreographer Jiri Kylian and indigenous dancers in Australia's Northern Territory. The String Around Autumn is his viola concerto, taking its title from a poem by Makota Ooka. He composed film scores for over 100 films, particularly associated with Japanese New Wave cinema and with Akira Kurosawa. His score for Kurosawa's Ran (1985), his second Kurosawa collaboration after Dodes'ka-den, is famous for a moment of abrupt silence when a gunshot rings out during the third castle attack; it also includes the tracks 'Hell's Picture Scroll' and 'Flute of Darkness,' in which a shakuhachi plays piercing high notes as buildings burn. He was a frequent collaborator with director Hiroshi Teshigahara and screenwriter Kobo Abe on films including Pitfall, Woman in the Dunes, The Face of Another, and The Ruined Map; he also scored Teshigahara's documentary on Antoni Gaudi. For Pale Flower (1962) he opened with atonal music over mist swirling around an empty suit of armor, with the climactic murder scored to Purcell's 'Dido's Lament.' His score for Harakiri incorporated the biwa — the climactic duel in rippling grass was set to aggressive biwa strumming — and Donald Richie noted Takemitsu repeatedly used a single chord fading into silence in that film. He used string glissandi to represent shifting sands in Woman in the Dunes. He also scored the film Ginrin. The aleatoric orchestral work Arc II: Textures has groups of orchestral sections repeat short passages until the conductor orders them to move on. In Archipelago S the orchestra is split into five groups on different parts of the stage. For chamber music, he wrote And then I knew 'twas Wind for flute, viola, and harp (the same trio as Debussy's sonata for diverse instruments), and Quatrain II as homage to Messiaen's Quartet for the End of Time after Messiaen played him an excerpt at the piano. He transcribed Summertime for guitar in his 12 Songs for guitar, and also wrote Valeria and Folios for guitar. He also arranged 'The Last Waltz' by Barry Mason and Les Reed for solo guitar. Takemitsu was initially averse to Japanese musical tradition but grew to embrace it following a commission for the gagaku ensemble of the Imperial Household. He wrote one novel, featuring a detective. An annual composition award bears his name; in 2015, Kaija Saariaho became the first woman to judge it. He described his music as akin to the stillness of grass and flowers.
