Summary of Facts

Arthur Rimbaud is a French Symbolist poet best known for 'The Drunken Boat' and the prose poem A Season in Hell. He is one of the most frequently clued Poets in quizbowl, and his turbulent relationship with Paul Verlaine is a constant presence across questions.

'The Drunken Boat' ('Le Bateau ivre') is narrated by an abandoned ship that drifts 'down impassive Rivers' after its crew of haulers are killed by 'gaudy redskins' who nail them to 'colored stakes' or 'totem poles.' The boat describes striking against 'unbelievable Floridas' and seeing 'the low-hanging sun speckled with mystic horrors.' It yearns for 'Europe with its ancient parapets' and laments 'But, truly, I have wept too much! The Dawns are heartbreaking.' The boat is described as 'lighter than a cork' and 'swollen with intoxicating torpor,' and calls upon 'million golden birds, Life Force of the future.' It sees 'fish-traps where a whole Leviathan rots in the rushes' and 'singing phosphorous.' The poem was sent by the 16-year-old Rimbaud to Paul Verlaine and was heavily inspired by Jules Verne's Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea. Ted Berrigan often quotes his own translations of it in The Sonnets.

A Season in Hell ('Une saison en enfer') is a prose poem that opens 'Once, if I Remember correctly, my life was a banquet where every heart revealed itself.' It contains the sections 'Bad Blood,' 'The Impossible,' and two 'Delirium' sections. In 'Delirium I: Foolish Virgin,' a character addresses the narrator as 'Celestial Bridegroom' and says 'O Lord, O Celestial Bridegroom!' -- this represents Verlaine as the 'Foolish Virgin' and Rimbaud as the 'Infernal/Hellish Bridegroom.' In 'Delirium II: Alchemy of the Word,' the speaker declares 'I invented the colour of vowels!' and lists the pairings 'Black A, white E, red I, blue O, green U.' The speaker says he placed 'Beauty on my knee' and found her 'bitter.' The final 'Farewell' section declares 'One must be absolutely modern.' The poem ends with the prediction 'This is the time of the Assassins.' It was written during Rimbaud's relationship with Verlaine, after Verlaine shot Rimbaud in the wrist. Aime Cesaire's play A Season in The Congo references the title. Henry Miller wrote a study of Rimbaud titled The Time of the Assassins.

'Vowels' ('Voyelles') is a sonnet that assigns colors to the five vowels: 'A black, E white, I red, U green, O blue.' It describes A as a 'black velvety jacket of brilliant flies which buzz around cruel smells,' O as the 'Omega, violet ray of [his] eyes' and a 'supreme Clarion full of strange piercing sounds,' and features 'silences crossed by Worlds and by Angels.' It describes 'the peace of the furrows which alchemy prints on broad studious foreheads.'

Illuminations is an incomplete collection of prose poems. It opens with 'After the Flood,' in which 'a hare halted in the clover / as soon as the idea of the flood was finished' and a hare 'said its prayers to the rainbow through the spider's web.' 'Being Beauteous' describes 'whistlings of death and circles of faint music.' 'Common Nocturne' describes 'rotten rooftops.' John Ashbery published a translation of Illuminations in 2011.

The Letters of the Seer ('Lettres du Voyant') were written to Georges Izambard and Paul Demeny. They contain the famous declaration 'I is another' ('Je est un autre') and advocate a 'long, immense, and deliberate derangement of the senses' for The Poet to achieve the state of seer. The letters also state 'the poet is truly the thief of fire.'

Biographically, Rimbaud gave up writing poetry at age 19-21 and spent the final years of his life gun-running in Ethiopia and East Africa, and allegedly slave trading. He was shot in the wrist by his lover Paul Verlaine. Verlaine depicted Rimbaud as an evil angel in the poem 'Crime of Love' and included him in his 1884 anthology Les Poetes maudits. Patti Smith described her 'unrequited love' for Rimbaud in Just Kids. Bruce Duffy's novel Disaster Was My God fictionalizes Rimbaud's time in Cyprus. Bob Dylan cited Rimbaud's influence, referencing Rimbaud's relationship with Verlaine in 'You're Gonna Make Me Lonesome When You Go.' Paul Valery praised Rimbaud, saying all known literature is in the language of common sense except Rimbaud's. Rimbaud was an influence on the surrealists.
