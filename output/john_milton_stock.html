John Milton

English poet and polemicist, blind author of Paradise Lost, Samson Agonistes, Comus, and Lycidas, as well as the prose tract Areopagitica defending free press. One of the most frequently clued authors in quizbowl, spanning literature, philosophy, and art (through illustrations of his works).
Paradise Lost

Milton's magnum opus, an epic poem about the fall of Adam and Eve. Written while blind (he dictated it to his daughters), it opens with the famous invocation to 'justify the ways of God to men.' The poem's preface calls rhyme an 'invention of a barbarous age.' Satan is the poem's most compelling character, declaring 'Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heav'n.' Stanley Fish's Surprised by Sin argues the poem provokes 'fallen responses' from readers, C.S. Lewis wrote a book-length preface, William Empson investigated its moral stance, and Christopher Ricks wrote about Milton's 'grand style.' William Blake claimed Milton was 'of the Devil's party without knowing it.' Blake conceived The Marriage of Heaven and Hell as an inversion of Paradise Lost. A.E. Housman wrote 'malt does more than Milton can / To justify God's ways to man.' Thomas Gray's 'Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard' imagines 'some mute inglorious Milton.'

Sonnets (including 'When I Consider How My Light Is Spent')

Milton's sonnets address his blindness, political topics, and personal relationships. The most frequently clued is 'When I Consider How My Light Is Spent' (also known as 'On His Blindness'), in which the speaker laments 'that one Talent which is death to hide,' asks 'Doth God exact day-labour, light denied?' and is reassured by Patience that 'They also serve who only stand and wait.' Two sonnets are addressed to Cyriack Skinner, one of which describes 'liberty's defense' as 'my noble task.' 'Methought I Saw My Late Espoused Saint' (likely about Katherine Woodcock) ends 'I wak'd, she fled, and day brought back my night.' 'On the Late Massacre in Piedmont' exhorts 'Avenge, O Lord, thy slaughtered saints.' Another sonnet laments turning twenty-three because of 'Time, the subtle thief of youth.'

Lycidas

An elegy mourning the drowning of Milton's Cambridge friend Edward King. The poem contains many famous lines and phrases. It ends 'Tomorrow to fresh woods, and pastures new' (which inspired the title of Thomas Wolfe's Look Homeward, Angel). It describes 'scrannel pipes of wretched straw' and a 'two-handed engine' that will 'smite once, and smite no more.' An 'uncouth swain' tells shepherds to 'weep no more.' Milton's friend Charles Diodati's death is also referenced in the poem's context.

Samson Agonistes

A closet drama (not intended for stage performance) about the biblical Samson, published alongside Paradise Regained. Samson is chained and 'eyeless in Gaza,' laments being his own 'Sepulcher, a moving Grave,' and has his 'race of glory run, and race of shame.' The giant Harapha challenges him (or refuses to fight), and Samson's father Manoa tries to negotiate ransom. Samson ultimately pulls down a temple of Dagon on his captors, and the chorus departs with 'calm of mind all passion spent.' The title 'Eyeless in Gaza' was used by Aldous Huxley, and 'All Passion Spent' by Vita Sackville-West. Milton defended the classical unities in the preface 'Of that sort of Dramatic Poem which is call'd Tragedy.' The play also includes the exchange where Samson says 'commands are no constraints' but then tells his captor 'Masters commands come with a power resistless.'

Comus (A Masque Presented at Ludlow Castle)

A masque first performed at Ludlow Castle in 1634. The Lady (played by Alice Egerton at the premiere) is trapped in a chair by Comus, the son of Bacchus and Circe, who possesses a liquor that turns humans' heads into those of animals. Her two brothers receive the magical plant haemony from the Attendant Spirit (disguised as the shepherd Thyrsis) and the water nymph Sabrina ultimately frees her. The Lady sings to 'Sweet Echo' for help and the famous phrase 'silver lining' originates from clouds turning their 'silver lining to the night.' The play also features the phrase 'light fantastic round.'

Areopagitica

Milton's 1644 prose tract defending the liberty of unlicensed printing and attacking pre-publication censorship. Its most famous line is 'he who destroys a good book, kills reason itself.' It imagines England 'rousing herself like a strong man after sleep' and compares truth to Isis's scattered limbs. It argues that 'he that can apprehend and consider vice with all her baits and seeming pleasures, and yet abstain, is the true wayfaring Christian' and later declares 'let Truth and Falsehood grapple; who ever knew Truth put to the worse in a free and open encounter?' The title comes from an Isocrates oration named for a hill in Athens. The tract complains parliament has been 'bejesuited' and makes an exception for 'Popery.'

L'Allegro and Il Penseroso

A pair of companion poems contrasting a happy man and a serious one. 'L'Allegro' opens 'Hence, loathed Melancholy' and includes 'Come, and trip it as you go / On the light fantastic toe' (origin of the modern idiom 'trip the light fantastic') and ends 'These delights if thou canst give / Mirth, with thee I mean to live.' 'Il Penseroso' opens 'Hence, vain deluding Joys' and invokes Melancholy as a 'pensive nun, devout and pure / Sober, stedfast, and demure' whose 'saintly visage is too bright / To hit the sense of human sight.' Melancholy is also called the goddess 'Of Cerberus, and blackest Midnight born.' Robert Burton's Anatomy of Melancholy was likely an inspiration.

Other Works and General

Milton went blind; his blindness is central to many of his works and references. He was depicted by Delacroix dictating to his daughters and by Fuseli painting him 'dictating to his daughter.' William Blake wrote a two-book epic titled Milton in which Milton's spirit enters Blake's body through his foot in the form of a falling star; that epic opens 'And did those feet in ancient time,' which became the hymn 'Jerusalem.' The skylark Ololon appears in the Blake epic. Milton's best friend was Charles Diodati, whose death inspired a Latin elegy. Milton wrote the Divorce Tracts (Tetrachordon, The Doctrine and Discipline of Divorce) after his wife Mary Powell left him. He defended regicide in Eikonoklastes (response to Eikon Basilike) and The Tenure of Kings and Magistrates (nearly executed for this after the Restoration). He hired amanuenses including Andrew Marvell. He also wrote the masque Arcades ('Such sweet compulsion doth in music lie') and a treatise (De Doctrina Christiana) that defends polygamy and denies the Trinity. Wordsworth addressed him in 'London, 1802.' Tony Harrison's poem 'On Not Being Milton' likens speaking dialect to Luddism. Robert Bridges studied Milton's prosody. Milton coined the word 'pandemonium.' The 'Milton controversy' questions his rhetorical flourishes.

Summary of Facts

John Milton was an English poet and polemicist, best known for his epic Paradise Lost. He is one of the most frequently clued literary figures in quizbowl, with questions spanning literature, philosophy, history, and fine arts.

Paradise Lost is Milton's magnum opus, an epic poem about the fall of Adam and Eve from the Garden of Eden. It opens by declaring its intent to 'justify the ways of God to men.' Satan is the poem's most rhetorically compelling figure, famously declaring 'Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heav'n.' Hell's flames give off 'no light but rather darkness visible.' Book III opens by praising 'holy light, offspring of Heav'n first-born' and describes Milton's own blindness. Satan 'felt how awful goodness is' after Zephon does not recognize him. Eve is described as 'formed for softness and sweet attractive grace' who follows the advice of a serpent 'with burnished neck of verdant gold.' The preface calls rhyme an 'invention of a barbarous age.' Milton dictated the poem to his daughters due to his blindness, a scene depicted by both Delacroix and Fuseli. The poem was illustrated by William Blake (Thomas and Butts sets) and Gustave Dore (who depicted a fallen angel plummeting to Earth). Stanley Fish's Surprised by Sin argues the poem provokes 'fallen responses'; C.S. Lewis wrote a book-length preface; William Empson investigated its moral stance; and Christopher Ricks wrote about Milton's 'grand style.' T.S. Eliot complained Milton wrote English 'like a dead language,' and Ezra Pound said he was 'chock a block with Latin.' William Blake claimed Milton was 'of the Devil's party without knowing it.' Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar wrote in The Madwoman in the Attic that 'all women writers have been to some extent Milton's daughters.' A.E. Housman wrote 'malt does more than Milton can / To justify God's ways to man,' and Thomas Gray imagined 'some mute inglorious Milton' in his 'Elegy.' Milton coined the word 'pandemonium' in Paradise Lost.

Milton's sonnets are frequently clued, especially 'When I Consider How My Light Is Spent' (on his blindness), which ends 'They also serve who only stand and wait' and includes 'that one Talent which is death to hide' and the question 'Doth God exact day-labour, light denied?' Two sonnets are addressed to Cyriack Skinner, one discussing 'liberty's defense' as 'my noble task.' 'Methought I Saw My Late Espoused Saint' (likely about Katherine Woodcock) ends 'I wak'd, she fled, and day brought back my night.' 'On the Late Massacre in Piedmont' exhorts 'Avenge, O Lord, thy slaughtered saints.' Another sonnet laments 'Time, the subtle thief of youth.'

Lycidas is an elegy mourning the drowning of Milton's Cambridge friend Edward King. It ends 'Tomorrow to fresh woods, and pastures new' and contains 'scrannel pipes of wretched straw' and a 'two-handed engine' that will 'smite once, and smite no more.' Milton coined the phrase 'look homeward Angel' in this poem, which Thomas Wolfe used as a novel title.

Samson Agonistes is a closet drama about the biblical Samson, who is 'eyeless in Gaza' (title used by Aldous Huxley). The giant Harapha challenges or refuses to fight Samson, and Samson's father Manoa tries to negotiate ransom. The play ends with Samson pulling down a temple of Dagon on his captors, and the chorus departing with 'calm of mind all passion spent' (title used by Vita Sackville-West). Milton defended the classical unities in its preface.

Comus (A Masque Presented at Ludlow Castle, 1634) features a Lady trapped by Comus, the son of Bacchus and Circe, who is freed by the water nymph Sabrina. Her brothers receive the magical plant haemony from the Attendant Spirit disguised as the shepherd Thyrsis. The Lady sings to 'Sweet Echo' and the phrase 'silver lining' originates here.

Areopagitica (1644) is Milton's prose tract defending the liberty of unlicensed printing. Its most famous line is 'he who destroys a good book, kills reason itself.' It takes its title from an Isocrates oration named for the Areopagus. It imagines Truth as Isis's scattered limbs and declares 'let Truth and Falsehood grapple.'

L'Allegro and Il Penseroso are companion poems contrasting a happy and serious man, opening 'Hence, loathed Melancholy' and 'Hence, vain deluding Joys.' 'On the Morning of Christ's Nativity' imagines 'the wakeful trump of doom' and pagan oracles going silent. Milton's 'On Shakespeare. 1630' calls Shakespeare a 'son of Memory, great heir of fame.'

Milton wrote the Divorce Tracts (Tetrachordon, The Doctrine and Discipline of Divorce) after his wife Mary Powell left him. He defended regicide in Eikonoklastes and The Tenure of Kings and Magistrates, and was nearly executed after the Restoration. His amanuenses included Andrew Marvell. William Blake wrote a two-book epic titled Milton in which Milton's spirit enters Blake's body through his foot, opening with 'And did those feet in ancient time' (the hymn 'Jerusalem'). Tony Harrison's 'On Not Being Milton' is also frequently referenced. William Wordsworth addressed Milton in 'London, 1802': 'Milton! thou shouldst be living at this hour.'
