Charles Ives (1874–1954) was an American modernist composer who worked simultaneously as a highly successful insurance executive. Known for layering American hymns, folk songs, and patriotic tunes in complex polytonal and polyrhythmic textures, his major works include The Unanswered Question, the Concord Sonata, Three Places in New England, and four symphonies. He was awarded the Pulitzer Prize in 1947 for his Symphony No. 3.
The Unanswered Question

The Unanswered Question (1908, rev. 1930–35) was originally published as one of Ives's 'Two Contemplations' alongside Central Park in the Dark. A solo trumpet plays the same atonal 'question' motif seven times. A quiet string chorale plays in the background throughout, representing 'the Silence of the Druids,' using a ppp triple-piano background. A woodwind quartet (four flutes) responds to each trumpet statement with increasingly frantic, dissonant answers — described as 'Fighting Answerers' who struggle in vain. Bernstein named his Harvard Norton lectures (The Unanswered Question) after this piece. The strings 'quietly prolong a G major triad into eternity' at the end.

Piano Sonata No. 2, 'Concord, Mass., 1840–60' (Concord Sonata)

The Concord Sonata (1919, premiered by John Kirkpatrick in 1939) is Ives's most famous piano work. Its four movements are named for Transcendentalist figures: 'Emerson,' 'Hawthorne,' 'The Alcotts,' and 'Thoreau.' The fate motif from Beethoven's Fifth Symphony is quoted in each movement; a 'human faith' melody is thematically related to these quotations. In the 'Hawthorne' movement, the performer is instructed to depress the keys with a 14¾-inch long piece of wood to create tone clusters. The Thoreau movement includes optional parts for flute (representing Thoreau, who played flute) and viola. A soft, calm B-flat major quotation of Beethoven's fate motif opens 'The Alcotts.' The work mostly lacks time signatures but includes a single bar of 4½/4 time. It was published alongside explanatory 'Essays Before a Sonata.'

Three Places in New England

Three Places in New England (Orchestral Set No. 1, 1903–14) has three movements: 'The Saint-Gaudens in Boston Common' (depicting Augustus Saint-Gaudens' memorial sculpture to Robert Gould Shaw and his Black regiment), 'Putnam's Camp, Redding, Connecticut' (a pastiche of Civil War songs and marching bands, including 'Columbia, Gem of the Ocean'), and 'The Housatonic at Stockbridge' (ending with strings 'quietly prolonging a G major triad into eternity'). The dissonances of the second movement were supposedly inspired by two different marching bands playing simultaneously. Nicholas Slonimsky conducted the premiere, instructing part of the ensemble to play four measures while another part played three to evoke dueling bands.

Symphony No. 3, 'The Camp Meeting'

Ives's Symphony No. 3 in C major (1904) depicts religious camp meetings. Its three movements are 'Old Folks Gatherin',' 'Children's Day,' and 'Communion.' The 'Communion' finale ends with fading church bells marked ppppp (five p's). It won the Pulitzer Prize in 1947, though Ives grumbled about 'badges of mediocrity.' Scored for chamber orchestra, its premiere was much-delayed; Lou Harrison failed to restore the 'shadow lines' of the original. Ives's fourth violin sonata (subtitled 'Children's Day') was inspired by the same religious gatherings.

Symphony No. 2

Ives's Symphony No. 2 (1901, premiered by Leonard Bernstein in 1951) is a five-movement symphony. Its final chord uses all twelve pitches at once, frequently compared to a Bronx cheer or raspberry. The finale includes a boisterous quotation of 'Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean.' Other movements quote 'Camptown Races,' 'Wake Nicodemus,' 'Bringing in the Sheaves,' 'Old Black Joe,' and other folk/patriotic tunes. The premiere was conducted by Bernstein, who also conducted the world premiere 50 years after it was written.

Symphony No. 4

Ives's Symphony No. 4 (unfinished at his death; posthumous premiere 1965 by Leopold Stokowski with assistants José Serebrier and David Katz) is so complex it often requires two conductors and a separate percussion ensemble. Its finale includes a 'distant choir' of harp and violins that play in a separate rhythm from the rest of the orchestra. The second movement 'comedy' juxtaposes a quiet pilgrims' hymn with cacophonous marches and popular songs. The third movement has a fugue based on Ives's First String Quartet. Gunther Schuller served as editorial consultant. The work quotes the hymn 'Bethany.' A part for 'ether organ' appears in the score.

Central Park in the Dark →

Central Park in the Dark (1906, 'A Contemplation of Nothing Serious') was paired with The Unanswered Question as 'Two Contemplations.' It is scored for strings, two ragtime pianos, and a street band. Midway through, strings continue in Molto adagio while the rest accelerates and crescendos to tempo con fuoco. It repeatedly quotes 'Hello! Ma Baby,' 'Washington Post March,' and other popular songs. It depicts a summer evening in Central Park.

General / Biographical

Charles Ives (1874–1954) worked as an insurance executive while composing. He was an American experimental modernist who layered American hymns, folk songs, and patriotic tunes in polytonal, polyrhythmic textures. He was part of the 'American Five.' He wrote three pieces for two pianos tuned a quarter-tone apart. His Universe Symphony was unfinished at death (Johnny Reinhard realized a performing version). He described Impressionism as 'easy music for the sissies.' He often overlapped quotations of American folk songs in his scherzi (with titles like 'In the Barn,' 'This Scherzo is a Joke'). He worked out harmonic ideas after hearing two marching bands play simultaneously as a child. His songs include 'General William Booth Enters into Heaven' (with melody from 'There is a Fountain Filled with Blood' each time the text asks 'Are you washed in the blood?'). J. Peter Burkholder's authoritative study is titled 'All Made of Tunes' (a phrase from an Ives song).

Summary of Facts

Charles Ives (1874–1954) was an American modernist composer who spent his professional life as an insurance executive while producing one of the most distinctive and visionary bodies of music in American history. He layered hymns, folk songs, and popular tunes in polytonal and polyrhythmic textures, often quoting 'Columbia, Gem of the Ocean,' 'Old Black Joe,' and Beethoven's fate motif across multiple works.

The Unanswered Question (1908) is among his most famous pieces. A solo trumpet repeats the same atonal 'question' motif seven times. Throughout, a quiet string chorale plays ppp, representing 'the Silence of the Druids.' A woodwind quartet (four flutes) responds with increasingly frantic and dissonant 'Fighting Answerers.' The strings end the piece 'quietly prolonging a G major triad into eternity.' It was originally paired with Central Park in the Dark as 'Two Contemplations,' and Leonard Bernstein named his Harvard Norton lectures after it.

The Concord Sonata (Piano Sonata No. 2, 1919, premiered by John Kirkpatrick in 1939) is his best-known piano work, with four movements named for Transcendentalist figures: Emerson, Hawthorne, The Alcotts, and Thoreau. The fate motif from Beethoven's Fifth Symphony appears in each movement. In the Hawthorne movement, a 14¾-inch piece of wood is used to depress a tone cluster on the keyboard. The Thoreau movement includes optional parts for flute (which Thoreau played) and viola. 'The Alcotts' opens with a soft B-flat major quotation of Beethoven's fate motif. The work was published with explanatory 'Essays Before a Sonata.'

Three Places in New England (Orchestral Set No. 1) has three movements: 'The Saint-Gaudens in Boston Common' (depicting Augustus Saint-Gaudens' memorial to Robert Gould Shaw and his Black regiment, with Civil War songs in minor thirds), 'Putnam's Camp' (depicting dueling marching bands, with quotations of 'Columbia, Gem of the Ocean'), and 'The Housatonic at Stockbridge.' The dissonances of the second movement were inspired by Ives hearing two bands playing simultaneously.

His Symphony No. 3 ('The Camp Meeting') depicts religious camp meetings in three movements: 'Old Folks Gatherin',' 'Children's Day,' and 'Communion' (which ends with fading church bells marked ppppp). It won the Pulitzer Prize in 1947, though Ives grumbled about 'badges of mediocrity.' His Symphony No. 2 (premiered by Bernstein in 1951) ends with a chord of all twelve pitches simultaneously, compared to a Bronx cheer. His Symphony No. 4 required two conductors and a separate percussion section at its 1965 posthumous premiere (led by Leopold Stokowski with José Serebrier and David Katz); its finale includes a distant choir of harp and violins in a separate rhythm. His Universe Symphony was left unfinished; Johnny Reinhard realized a performing version.

Central Park in the Dark (1906) is scored for strings, two ragtime pianos, and a street band, and repeatedly quotes 'Hello! Ma Baby.' Ives also wrote three pieces for two pianos tuned a quarter-tone apart. J. Peter Burkholder's book All Made of Tunes is the authoritative study of Ives's use of musical borrowing.
