1975 Stanley Kubrick film based on William Makepeace Thackeray's 1844 picaresque novel The Luck of Barry Lyndon. Stars Ryan O'Neal as an 18th-century Irish social climber. Most notable for its revolutionary cinematography — NASA-designed ultra-fast Zeiss f/0.7 lenses were used to shoot scenes entirely by candlelight. Shots were inspired by 18th-century English paintings (William Hogarth), and the camera frequently zooms out to create painting-like tableaux. Handles the Handel Sarabande in D minor plays during the main title and duels.

Summary of Facts

Barry Lyndon (1975) is a film by Stanley Kubrick based on William Makepeace Thackeray's 1844 picaresque novel The Luck of Barry Lyndon. It stars Ryan O'Neal as Redmond Barry, an 18th-century Irish adventurer who fights in the Seven Years' War, becomes a social climber, marries an aristocratic widow and takes her name, viciously beats his stepson, loses his left leg in a duel, and ends up ruined.

The film is most identified by its extraordinary cinematography: Kubrick obtained ultra-fast NASA-designed Zeiss lenses with an f-stop of 0.7 to shoot scenes entirely by candlelight, without arc lights. Actors moved laterally to the camera to stay in focus due to the shallow depth of field. Throughout the film, the camera slowly zooms outward from tableau shots inspired by 18th-century English paintings, especially William Hogarth. The final shot recreates the last piece in Hogarth's Marriage-a-la-Mode series; the scene of Barry slumped drunk in a stupor after his son Bryan's death was influenced by Hogarth's The Tête-à-Tête. A scrapbook of classic English paintings served as the cinematographic reference. Cinematographer John Alcott won the Academy Award for Best Cinematography.

The D minor Sarabande by Handel plays over the main titles and duel sequences. 'The British Grenadiers' (flute and snare drum) plays over battle scenes. Schubert's German Dance No. 1 accompanies Bryan's eighth birthday party, where a magic show features a rabbit appearing in an empty drawer. Bryan later dies when thrown from a horse. The climactic duel takes place in a dilapidated barn with cooing pigeons. The final image is a freeze frame of Barry getting into a carriage with his mother, as the narrator declares him 'utterly baffled and beaten.'

The film grew out of Kubrick's extensive research for an abandoned Napoleon biopic, and sits between A Clockwork Orange (1971) and The Shining (1980) in his filmography.
