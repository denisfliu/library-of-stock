Amiri Baraka

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
African-American poet, playwright, and activist, born LeRoi Jones, who changed his name in 1967 after becoming a black cultural nationalist. Founder of the Black Arts Repertory Theater. Best known for the play Dutchman and the controversial poem 'Somebody Blew Up America.' Also known for the poetry collection Preface to a Twenty Volume Suicide Note and the music criticism book Blues People. Served as Poet Laureate of New Jersey.
Dutchman

Baraka's best-known play, set entirely on a New York City subway car. The white woman Lula enters eating an apple and attempts to seduce the black man Clay, a man in a three-piece suit. She mocks him, dances mock-seductively telling him to 'do the nasty. Rub bellies,' asks him to pretend to be 'free of your own history,' and enrages him by calling him 'Uncle Thomas Woolly-Head.' Clay grabs her arm and launches into a monologue about racism in America, saying that killing white people 'would make us all sane.' After Clay says 'I don't think we could make it,' Lula responds 'Sorry is right' and stabs him through the heart. She orders the other passengers to throw out his body and chillingly contemplates her next victim. Won the Obie Award for Best Play alongside Adrienne Kennedy's Funnyhouse of a Negro.

"Somebody Blew Up America"

A controversial poem about 9/11 that repeatedly asks 'Who? Who? Who?' and inquires 'Who told 4000 Israeli workers at the Twin Towers / To stay home that day,' suggesting Israeli involvement. The poem also refers to those telling the 9/11 tale as people who 'live on Wall Street / The first plantation.' Its publication led to the abolition of the position of Poet Laureate of New Jersey, which Baraka held.

Preface to a Twenty Volume Suicide Note

A poetry collection whose title poem describes the speaker counting the stars every night, 'and when they will not come to be counted,' counting 'the holes they leave.' The speaker sees his daughter praying in her room. The poem begins 'Lately, I've become accustomed to the way the ground opens up and envelopes me each time I go out to walk the dog.' O'Hara's poem 'Personal Poem' revolves around a discussion with Baraka about things they both like and dislike, including Lionel Trilling and Herman Melville, and O'Hara formulated 'Personism' in a conversation with Baraka.

Blues People

A study of 'Negro Music in White America' that maps the shift from blues musicians in the rural south to large jazz ensembles in the urban north. It includes a chapter called 'Swing-From Verb to Noun' tracing white commercial appropriation of black music, and discusses early R&B as a return to more authentically 'Black' music in the chapter 'The Blues Continuum.' Baraka also wrote the music criticism volumes Black Music and briefly headed the record label Jihad.

"Black Art"

A poetic manifesto that begins 'Poems are bullshit unless they are teeth or trees or lemons piled on a step' and declares 'we want poems that kill.' Contains an onomatopoeic representation of an airplane flying and firing a gun. Part of the broader Black Arts Movement aesthetic.

General / Biographical

Born as Everett LeRoi Jones, Baraka changed his name in 1967 after becoming a black cultural nationalist. He was a founder of the Black Arts Repertory Theater, which attracted artists like Sonia Sanchez and Sun Ra and was closely monitored by the FBI. He served as Poet Laureate of New Jersey until the position was abolished after 'Somebody Blew Up America.' He was arrested in the 1967 Newark Riots; his son Ras is Newark's current mayor. He was briefly associated with the Beat Generation alongside figures like Bob Kaufman and Jack Kerouac. He called Langston Hughes a 'torchbearer of black literature.' His Afrocentrism was influenced by Maulana Karenga, founder of Kawaida philosophy, under whom he adopted the name Imamu Amear Baraka. Adrienne Rich addressed him in a ghazal. He also wrote poems asking for 'the spells, to raise up / return, destroy, and create' and declaring that 'all the stores will open if you say the magic words... Up against the wall motherfucker this is a stick up!' — a line that inspired an anarchist affinity group.

Summary of Facts

Amiri Baraka (born Everett LeRoi Jones) is an African-American poet, playwright, and activist who changed his name in 1967 after embracing black cultural nationalism. He founded the Black Arts Repertory Theater, which attracted artists like Sonia Sanchez and Sun Ra and was closely monitored by the FBI. He served as Poet Laureate of New Jersey until the position was abolished following his controversial poem 'Somebody Blew Up America.'

His best-known work is the play Dutchman, set entirely on a New York City subway car. The white woman Lula enters eating an apple and attempts to seduce the black man Clay, who wears a three-piece suit. She dances mock-seductively, telling him to 'do the nasty. Rub bellies,' asks him to pretend to be 'free of your own history,' and enrages him by calling him 'Uncle Thomas Woolly-Head.' After Clay delivers a monologue about racism and says 'I don't think we could make it,' Lula stabs him through the heart, orders passengers to throw out his body, and contemplates her next victim. The play won the Obie alongside Adrienne Kennedy's Funnyhouse of a Negro.

'Somebody Blew Up America' is a controversial poem about 9/11 that repeatedly asks 'Who? Who? Who?' and inquires 'Who told 4000 Israeli workers at the Twin Towers / To stay home that day,' suggesting Israeli involvement. Its publication led to the abolition of the NJ Poet Laureate position.

His poetry collection Preface to a Twenty Volume Suicide Note includes the title poem in which the speaker counts stars and sees his daughter praying, beginning 'Lately, I've become accustomed to the way the ground opens up.' Frank O'Hara's 'Personal Poem' revolves around conversation with Baraka, and O'Hara formulated 'Personism' in a conversation with him.

Blues People is a study of 'Negro Music in White America' that maps the shift from rural blues to urban jazz, with a chapter 'Swing-From Verb to Noun' tracing white appropriation of black music. He also wrote Black Music and headed the record label Jihad.

'Black Art' is a poetic manifesto declaring 'Poems are bullshit unless they are teeth or trees or lemons piled on a step' and 'we want poems that kill.' The Slave ends with Walker Vessels injured by an explosion that kills his daughters. Slave Ship is subtitled 'A Historical Pageant' with unusual props. Baraka was briefly associated with the Beat Generation and called Langston Hughes a 'torchbearer of black literature.' His line 'Up against the wall motherfucker this is a stick up!' inspired an anarchist affinity group. He was arrested in the 1967 Newark Riots, and his son Ras is Newark's current mayor.
