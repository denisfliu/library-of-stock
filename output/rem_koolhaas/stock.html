General / Biographical

Rem Koolhaas (full name Remment Lucas Koolhaas) is a Dutch architect who co-founded the Office of Metropolitan Architecture (OMA) with Elia Zenghelis. He studied at the Architectural Association in London, where Zaha Hadid was among his students. OMA is based in Rotterdam and has incubated notable 'starchitects' including Joshua Prince-Ramus (who went on to found REX) and Bjarke Ingels (who founded BIG). Koolhaas is a Harvard professor and winner of the 2000 Pritzker Prize. He coined the term 'Junkspace.' He collaborated with Ole Scheeren on the CCTV headquarters design. Wikipedia

Seattle Central Library

A glass-and-steel public library in Seattle, Washington, designed by Koolhaas and OMA. The building features an irregular diamond-patterned glass facade and is conceived as five stable platforms ('stability clusters') connected by four 'in between' planes. Interior features include a four-story book spiral (numbers 000–999 painted on the floor), the Mixing Chamber (connected to the Living Room by fluorescent yellow-green escalators), ring-shaped blood-red corridors connecting meeting rooms, and George Legrady's installation 'Making Visible the Invisible' (six plasma screens showing live data), and diagonal half-white/half-black support columns. Has capacity for 1.45 million items. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Rem Koolhaas (full name Remment Lucas Koolhaas) is a Dutch architect and architectural theorist. He co-founded the Office of Metropolitan Architecture (OMA) with Elia Zenghelis; the firm is based in Rotterdam. Zaha Hadid was among his students at the Architectural Association in London. OMA has incubated prominent architects including Joshua Prince-Ramus (later founder of REX) and Bjarke Ingels (later founder of BIG). Koolhaas is a Harvard professor and won the Pritzker Prize in 2000. He coined the term 'Junkspace' in an influential essay.

His most frequently cited building is the Seattle Central Library, a glass-and-steel structure with a distinctive diamond-patterned irregular facade. The interior is organized as five stable platforms connected by four 'in between' planes. Notable features include the Mixing Chamber (connected to the Living Room by fluorescent yellow-green escalators), ring-shaped blood-red corridors, a four-story continuous book spiral (with Dewey numbers 000–999 painted on the floor), and George Legrady's installation 'Making Visible the Invisible' featuring six plasma screens displaying live data. It has capacity for 1.45 million items. Another major architectural work is the CCTV Headquarters in Beijing — designed with Ole Scheeren — which features two slanted towers connected by a 75-meter perpendicular cantilevered section and is often nicknamed for its resemblance to a pair of shorts. He also designed the Casa da Musica (concert hall) in Porto, Portugal; De Rotterdam (the largest building in the Netherlands); the Qatar National Library in Doha (completed 2017); the McCormick Tribune Campus Center at IIT (topped by a tubular metal train tunnel); the Shenzhen Stock Exchange; the RAK Convention Center in the UAE (resembling the Death Star); the Guggenheim Hermitage Museum in Las Vegas; and the Maison a Bordeaux (designed with Cecil Balmond as a moving house for a wheelchair-bound client). OMA also created galleries and a 'Transformer' building for Prada in Milan and Seoul, and worked on the Serpentine Gallery pavilion in 2006.

As a theorist, Koolhaas wrote Delirious New York, which praises the 'chance-like' nature of city living and introduces the term 'Manhattanism' — an ideology embracing urban congestion. He called Manhattan the 'twentieth century's Rosetta Stone.' The book's cover features his wife Madelon Vriesendorp's painting 'Flagrant Delit,' depicting two distorted skyscrapers in bed next to the Statue of Liberty's torch. He co-authored the large-format theoretical book S,M,L,XL with designer Bruce Mau; entries are arranged chronologically within categories Small, Medium, Large, and Extra-Large. The book contains essays including 'Islam after Einstein' and the poem 'Learning Japanese,' describes a Yokohama masterplan as 'programmatic lava,' and develops the concept of the 'Architecture of Bigness.' In 2002, he proposed a never-adopted barcode redesign of the EU flag, praised for its ability to easily incorporate new member states.
