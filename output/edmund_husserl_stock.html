Logical Investigations

Husserl's foundational work, which attacks psychologism in its first volume (countering his own earlier Philosophy of Arithmetic) and examines syntactical and mereological meaning in its second volume. It critiques psychologism by arguing that statements in mathematics and logic are not reducible to psychological states. Volume II contains the rallying cry of phenomenology: 'back to the things themselves' (Zu den Sachen selbst). Adolf Reinach co-founded a journal edited by Husserl and assisted in editing this work. It also introduces Husserl's concept of 'descriptive psychology' and contains his theory of bracketing.

Epoche, Bracketing, and Eidetic Reduction

Husserl's central methodological concept: suspending judgment about the existence of the natural world (epoche, from the Greek skeptics) while analyzing one's experience with objects and phenomena. This 'bracketing' focuses on experiences in the first-person point of view. Related is the 'eidetic reduction,' coined in his book Ideas, which seeks to identify the essential features (eidos) of experience. Husserl sought to found all sciences on a 'Transcendental Subjectivity' developed through this method. He acknowledged the risk of solipsism but argued that 'otherness' is discovered through appresentation.

Intentionality, Noesis, and Noema

Husserl adopted the concept of intentionality (the 'aboutness' or directedness of mental states) from his teacher Franz Brentano. He extended Brentano's concept into the difficult-to-decipher distinction between noesis (conscious intentional acts) and noema (objects-of-intention, or 'intuitive representational content'). He also used the Aristotelian term 'hyle' to refer to sensory material formed from the intentional content of perceptual experience.

Cartesian Meditations

Based on two 1929 Sorbonne lectures, expanded into five 'meditations' modeled on Descartes' Meditations on First Philosophy. It seeks a 'universal constitutive synthesis' of modes of consciousness. Husserl praised Eugen Fink's addition of a sixth meditation. The lengthy fifth section explains how 'transcendental idealism' does not negate external reality. First translated into French by Emmanuel Levinas. It introduces the concept of intersubjectivity, which constructs objective reality.

The Lifeworld (Lebenswelt)

Introduced in The Crisis of European Sciences, the lifeworld is the pregiven framework of beliefs and attitudes that shapes how we conceive of shared human experience. Jurgen Habermas further developed the concept, synthesizing it with 'system' in his Theory of Communicative Action. Alfred Schutz explained the roles of 'contemporaries,' 'predecessors,' and 'successors' in the lifeworld. The concept is often compared to Heidegger's 'being-in-the-world.' Husserl connected intersubjectivity and empathy through the lifeworld.

General / Biographical

Husserl is identified as the founder of phenomenology and a student of Franz Brentano. He studied mathematics under Karl Weierstrass, which influenced his first book Philosophy of Arithmetic. Gottlob Frege criticized this work for its psychologism. He argued 'naturalism refutes itself' in 'Philosophy as Rigorous Science.' He was Heidegger's predecessor at Freiburg; Heidegger dedicated Being and Time to him but rescinded it due to Husserl's Jewish ancestry. Edith Stein was his student, writing her dissertation on empathy. Paul Ricoeur wrote a book-length study of him. Herman Van Breda saved his archives from the Nazis. His 'pre-reflective self-consciousness' was identified by Gallagher and Zahavi. Derrida examined him in Speech and Phenomena and wrote on the 'Origin of Geometry.'

Summary of Facts

Edmund Husserl is an Austrian-German philosopher recognized as the founder of phenomenology, identified as a student of Franz Brentano and the author of Logical Investigations and Cartesian Meditations.

Logical Investigations is his foundational work, which attacks psychologism in its first volume and examines syntactical and mereological meaning in its second. It critiques the position that mathematical and logical statements are reducible to psychological states, countering his own earlier Philosophy of Arithmetic (which Gottlob Frege had criticized for its psychologism). Volume II contains the rallying cry of phenomenology: 'back to the things themselves' (Zu den Sachen selbst). The work also examines mereology (the relationship between wholes and parts) and identifies three increasingly abstract strata of logic: morphology of meanings, logic of consequence, and metalogic. Adolf Reinach assisted in editing the work.

Husserl's central methodological concept is epoche or 'bracketing' — suspending judgment about the existence of the natural world while analyzing one's experience with phenomena. He adopted this from the Greek skeptics. Related is the 'eidetic reduction,' coined in his book Ideas, which focuses on experiences in the first-person point of view to identify essential features. He sought to found all sciences on 'Transcendental Subjectivity.' He acknowledged that bracketing might lead to solipsism but argued that 'otherness' is discovered through appresentation, allowing the ego to enter an intersubjective lifeworld.

Husserl adopted the concept of intentionality (the directedness of mental states toward objects) from his teacher Franz Brentano and extended it into the distinction between noesis (conscious intentional acts) and noema (objects-of-intention). He also used the Aristotelian term 'hyle' to refer to sensory material formed from the intentional content of perceptual experience. His concept of 'pre-reflective self-consciousness' was identified by Shaun Gallagher and Dan Zahavi.

Cartesian Meditations, based on two 1929 Sorbonne lectures, consists of five 'meditations' modeled on Descartes' Meditations on First Philosophy. Husserl praised Eugen Fink's addition of a sixth meditation. The work seeks a 'universal constitutive synthesis' of modes of consciousness and describes how intersubjectivity constructs objective reality, as later elaborated by his student Edith Stein. Emmanuel Levinas first translated the work into French.

The lifeworld (Lebenswelt), introduced in The Crisis of European Sciences, is the pregiven framework of beliefs and attitudes that shapes shared human experience. Jurgen Habermas further developed it, synthesizing lifeworld and 'system' in his Theory of Communicative Action. Alfred Schutz explained the roles of 'contemporaries,' 'predecessors,' and 'successors' in the lifeworld. The concept is often compared to Heidegger's 'being-in-the-world.'

Biographically, Husserl studied mathematics under Karl Weierstrass, which influenced his first book Philosophy of Arithmetic. He was Heidegger's predecessor at Freiburg; Heidegger dedicated Being and Time to him but later rescinded the dedication due to Husserl's Jewish ancestry. His student Edith Stein wrote her dissertation on empathy under him, later became a Carmelite nun, died at Auschwitz, and became a patron saint of Europe. Paul Ricoeur wrote a book-length study of him. Herman Van Breda saved his archives from the Nazis. Jacques Derrida examined his work in Speech and Phenomena and wrote on Husserl's essay on the 'Origin of Geometry.' He argued 'naturalism refutes itself' in his paper 'Philosophy as Rigorous Science,' and argued that perception of time depends on retention and protention.
