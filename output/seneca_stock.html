Roman Stoic philosopher and tragedian who tutored the emperor Nero. Author of violent closet dramas (Thyestes, Hercules Furens, Medea, Phaedra, Oedipus) that influenced English revenge tragedy, as well as philosophical works including the Moral Letters to Lucilius, On Anger, On the Shortness of Life, On Mercy, Consolations, and the Menippean satire The Pumpkinification of Claudius. Thyestes

Seneca's best-known tragedy, in which Atreus tricks his brother Thyestes into feasting on his own sons. The play opens with a Fury convincing the ghost of Tantalus to cause disorder between his grandsons. In the fourth act, a table lifts from the trembling floor and wine falls down a man's jaw. The horrified gods reverse the rising of the sun after the banquet. Atreus ignores his guard's advice and chooses vengeance, and the guard says the best way to get vengeance is with a sword, to which Atreus responds about desiring the punishment itself. An unnatural grove serves as a locus horridus where Atreus kills the children.

Moral Letters to Lucilius (Epistulae Morales)

A series of 124 letters addressed to Lucilius Junior advancing Stoic philosophy. They espouse the idea that 'we are dying every day' and take themes from On Leisure and On the Shortness of Life. Individual letters discuss topics including asthma and death (appreciating his asthma for making him feel on the verge of death), a visit to Scipio Africanus's villa, the praecepta and decreta of philosophy, and dissuading men from vices. One rejects Zeno's syllogism against alcohol use. Seneca quotes Terence's line 'I'm human: nothing human is not my concern' and a line by Naevius. He also rails against crowds and public baths, complaining about 'troops of catamites' and noise of armpit-hair-pluckers. He drew a distinction between things which are valuable and inherently good.

The Pumpkinification of Claudius (Apocolocyntosis)

Seneca's only surviving comic work, a Menippean satire in which the recently deceased Emperor Claudius tries to enter Olympus. Hercules defends the stuttering Claudius in a trial before the gods. Clotho reveals the spindles of Augurinus, Baba, and Claudius to Mercury. Claudius is sentenced to play dice forever with a bottomless cup, until Caligula appears, claims him as a slave, and gives him as a gift to Aeacus. Our Lady of Malaria prevents Claudius from sweet-talking Hercules. The title parodies 'apotheosis' (deification).

Influence on Revenge Tragedy and Dramatic Legacy

Seneca is considered the classical forerunner of English revenge tragedy. His tragedies are the only complete surviving fabulae crepidatae (Latin tragedies based on Greek originals). They feature five acts punctuated by choral interludes, ghosts delivering prologues, and extreme violence, and were likely closet dramas. English revenge tragedies (The Spanish Tragedy, Hamlet) imitated these features. T.S. Eliot discussed how every generation reinvents Shakespeare in a lecture on 'Shakespeare and the Stoicism of Seneca.' Quintilian criticized Seneca's educational influence.

Other Plays (Medea, Phaedra, Oedipus, Octavia)

Seneca's Medea, unlike Euripides' version, depicts the title character blaming herself for the deaths of her and Jason's children. His Phaedra ends with Theseus trying to reassemble the pieces of Hippolytus's corpse after a messenger describes how his chariot was attacked. His Oedipus features a lengthy augury scene where Tiresias performs a ritual sacrifice, and Jocasta rips open her own womb; Creon speaks with the ghost of King Laius. The play Octavia is often misattributed to Seneca and describes his death. One play contains a passage about a 'second Argo' sailing past the ocean's bounds, which Columbus interpreted as a prophecy.

General / Biographical

Seneca the Younger was a Roman Stoic philosopher, tragedian, and tutor to the emperor Nero. He was forced to commit suicide during the Pisonian conspiracy when Nero ordered his death. The death scene is described by Tacitus: Seneca offered 'imago vitae suae' (the image of his life) and a libation to Jupiter the Liberator while being assisted into a bathtub; his wife Pompeia Paulina attempted to kill herself during the event. Cassius Dio mocked the scene by describing a failed attempt to kill his wife. Early Christians admired fake letters between Seneca and Saint Paul. Montaigne claimed never to have 'seriously settled' himself to reading any solid book but Plutarch and Seneca. Harry Sandbach dismissed him as a 'spare-time amateur philosopher'; Macaulay said reading him was 'like dining on nothing but anchovy sauce.' Seneca the Elder (his father) wrote the Controversiae and Suasoriae.

Summary of Facts

Seneca the Younger (Lucius Annaeus Seneca) was a Roman Stoic philosopher, tragedian, and political figure who served as tutor and advisor to the emperor Nero. He was ultimately forced to commit suicide during the Pisonian conspiracy on Nero's orders.

Seneca's dramatic works are among the most frequently clued topics about him. His best-known tragedy, Thyestes, opens with a Fury convincing the ghost of Tantalus to cause disorder between his grandsons, and ends with Atreus revealing that Thyestes has feasted on his own sons. Hercules Furens, based on Euripides' Madness of Heracles, depicts the title character killing Lycus and then going insane and murdering his wife Megara and their children while Amphitryon watches in horror. His tragedies are the only complete surviving fabulae crepidatae (Latin tragedies on Greek subjects), featuring five acts with choral interludes, ghosts delivering prologues, and extreme violence. They were likely closet dramas.

Seneca's other plays include Medea (where, unlike Euripides, the title character blames herself for the children's deaths), Phaedra (ending with Theseus reassembling his son's corpse), and Oedipus (with Tiresias's augury and Jocasta ripping open her womb). The play Octavia is often misattributed to him but actually describes his death. One play's passage about a 'second Argo' was interpreted by Columbus as a prophecy of the New World.

Seneca's dramatic legacy profoundly influenced English revenge tragedy. Thomas Kyd's The Spanish Tragedy and Shakespeare's Hamlet imitated his five-act structure and use of ghosts. T.S. Eliot discussed this influence in his lecture 'Shakespeare and the Stoicism of Seneca.'

On the philosophical side, Seneca wrote over 100 Moral Letters to Lucilius Junior, advancing the philosophy that 'we are dying every day.' Individual letters discuss his asthma, a visit to Scipio Africanus's villa, and reject Zeno's syllogism against alcohol. His treatise On Anger recommends daily self-examination of conscience and illustrates the first, second, and third motions with a story of Plato freezing his arm while about to strike a slave. On Mercy (De Clementia) argues clemency is essential to rule and is considered one of the first 'mirrors for princes.' On the Shortness of Life argues 'it is not that we have a short space of time, but that we waste much of it.'

His sole comic work, The Pumpkinification of Claudius, is a Menippean satire in which the dead emperor is sentenced to play dice with a bottomless cup in the underworld, until Caligula claims him as a slave.

Seneca's death scene, described by Tacitus, involved offering 'imago vitae suae' and a libation to Jupiter the Liberator while being assisted into a bathtub. His wife Pompeia Paulina attempted suicide during the event. According to Cassius Dio, Boudica's revolt in Britain was sparked by forced loans Seneca imposed. Early Christians admired fake letters between Seneca and Saint Paul. Montaigne ranked him alongside Plutarch as essential reading. Quintilian criticized his educational influence, while Harry Sandbach dismissed him as a 'spare-time amateur philosopher.'
