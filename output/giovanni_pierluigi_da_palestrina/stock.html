Italian Renaissance composer (c. 1525–1594) from the town of Palestrina, near Rome. Master of Renaissance polyphony and counterpoint. His style, emphasizing stepwise motion, dissonances on weak beats, and smooth voice leading, became the model for Renaissance sacred music. Apocryphally credited with saving polyphony from being banned at the Council of Trent by composing the Pope Marcellus Mass. His compositional style was codified by Johann Joseph Fux in the influential counterpoint treatise Gradus ad Parnassum.
Pope Marcellus Mass (Missa Papae Marcelli)

Palestrina's most famous work, a missa sine nomine (mass without a name) for six voices. All movements except the Agnus Dei are scored for six voices, and a two-part Agnus Dei adds a seventh voice. Apocryphally credited with convincing the Council of Trent not to ban polyphonic music from worship. A mass by this name was also arranged for orchestra by J. S. Bach. Wikipedia

Gradus ad Parnassum / Counterpoint Style

Palestrina's compositional style became the basis for Johann Joseph Fux's seminal counterpoint treatise Gradus ad Parnassum (1725). In the treatise, the character Aloysius represents Palestrina and teaches the student Josephus (representing Fux). Key principles: leaps countered by stepwise movement in the opposite direction; dissonances on weak beats only; smooth voice leading. Fux introduced the term 'species counterpoint' in the context of imitating Palestrina's style. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina (c. 1525–1594) was an Italian Renaissance composer from the town of Palestrina near Rome, and the central figure of the Roman School of polyphonic sacred music. He is most famous for the Pope Marcellus Mass (Missa Papae Marcelli), a missa sine nomine (mass without a name) scored for six voices — all movements except the Agnus Dei use six voices, while the second Agnus Dei adds a seventh voice and features the head-motif of an ascending fourth followed by a stepwise descent. The Kyrie introduces the recurring motif D–D, D–G–F–E–D. The mass is named for Pope Marcellus II, who reigned for only three weeks. According to apocryphal legend, this mass convinced the Council of Trent not to ban polyphonic music from Catholic worship by demonstrating that multiple voices could still render the text intelligible to parishioners. The story forms the basis of a 1917 opera titled Palestrina by Hans Pfitzner, in which the composer initially refuses a commission from Cardinal Borromeo and is subsequently imprisoned. A version of the mass was arranged for orchestra by J. S. Bach, and it may have influenced Bach's Mass in B Minor. The Tallis Scholars paired it with Allegri's Miserere in their debut recording.

Palestrina's compositional style — characterized by stepwise melodic motion countering leaps, dissonances placed only on weak beats, and smooth voice leading — became the canonical model for Renaissance counterpoint. Johann Joseph Fux codified these principles in his influential 1725 treatise Gradus ad Parnassum, presented as a dialogue between the student Josephus (representing Fux) and the master Aloysius (representing Palestrina). Fux coined the term 'species counterpoint' in reference to exercises designed to imitate Palestrina's style. Knud Jeppesen also wrote a scholarly analysis of Palestrina's dissonance treatment.

Among his many works, Palestrina composed over 250 motets and 140 madrigals — the latter of which he later claimed to 'blush and grieve' over. His Canticum canticorum is a cycle of 29 motets setting texts from the Song of Solomon. The motet O Magnum Mysterium, scored for six voices, features unisons, word painting, and a strong plagal cadence, and was the first polyphonic setting of that text; a related piece emphasizes the word 'dominum' through homorhythmic repetition before a triple-time Alleluja. His motet Sicut Cervus, based on Psalm 42 comparing the soul to a deer thirsting for water, features prominent melismas on the word 'desiderat.' The Stabat Mater is an eight-part motet for unaccompanied double chorus, possibly composed for Pope Gregory XIV. He also wrote two L'homme arme masses (one for four voices, one for five).

Biographically, Palestrina was appointed by Pope Julius III to direct the Cappella Giulia and later served in the papal choir until dismissed for being married. He resigned from the church of St. John Lateran — for which he had written Lamentations — after a dispute over feeding his choirboys, and he spent the last two decades of his career as choirmaster of the Cappella Giulia. He joined with Annibale Zoilo in an unsuccessful attempt to restore the plainsong, and his first publication (1554 book of masses) was modeled on that of Cristobal de Morales. His 19th-century revival in Germany was analyzed by James Garratt, and he was credited with producing the new 'gradual' promoted by the Cecilian Movement. The Sanctus from Anton Bruckner's Mass in E Minor quotes a theme by Palestrina. The Sixteen have released a series of albums dedicated to his music.
