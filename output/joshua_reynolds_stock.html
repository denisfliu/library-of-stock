Summary of Facts

Joshua Reynolds (1723-1792) was an 18th-century English portraitist and the first president of the Royal Academy of Arts. He is most commonly identified in quizbowl by his painting Sarah Siddons as the Tragic Muse (1784), which depicts the actress Sarah Siddons seated on a throne as Melpomene, the muse of tragedy, with allegorical figures of Pity (holding a cup) and Terror (holding a dagger) behind her. Siddons wears pearls, a diadem, and a gold dress. Reynolds used himself to model the facial expression of Pity. Two versions exist (Dulwich and Huntington), and the original dress was blue, not yellow.

His other major works include Colonel Acland and Lord Sydney: The Archers (1769), a life-sized double portrait of two aristocrats hunting with bows and arrows in a forest with dead game behind them; and the Portrait of Augustus Keppel (1752-53), which reverses the pose of the Apollo Belvedere and shows the admiral on a crag against a stormy sea. David Garrick Between Tragedy and Comedy (1761) parodies the classical story of Hercules choosing between virtue and pleasure. The Age of Innocence depicts a young girl in a yellow dress and inspired the title of Edith Wharton's novel.

Reynolds articulated the "Grand Style" or "Grand Manner" in his lecture series Discourses on Art, delivered to Royal Academy students. This emphasized idealizing subjects, erasing faults, generalizing to ideal forms, using visual metaphor and symbolism, and following Renaissance art. He argued that painters should not portray the defects of historical figures like Alexander the Great.

He was a famous rival of Thomas Gainsborough. A myth claims that Reynolds's Eighth Discourse on color theory prompted Gainsborough to paint The Blue Boy (Jonathan Buttall). Reynolds praised Gainsborough's Girl with Pigs but called his late works "fancy pictures." Reynolds was also close friends with Angelica Kauffman; Nathaniel Hone satirized their friendship in The Conjurer.
