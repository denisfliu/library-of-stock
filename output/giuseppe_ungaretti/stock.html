Summary of Facts

Giuseppe Ungaretti was an Italian poet and founder of the Hermetic school of poetry (Ermetismo), alongside Salvatore Quasimodo and Eugenio Montale. The term 'Hermeticism' was coined by critic Francesco Flora in response to the dense, esoteric style of Ungaretti's 1933 collection The Feeling of Time (Sentimento del Tempo).

His best-known collection is L'Allegria (also called The Joy of Shipwrecks or The Buried Port), containing poems written while serving in the trenches of World War I. His most famous poem, 'Morning' (Mattina), consists entirely of two words: 'M'illumino / d'immenso' ('I fill myself / with immensity'), and is often left untranslated in English editions. 'Vigil' describes the sight of a 'slain comrade' with 'clawed fingers / ripping into my silence,' prompting 'Never did I / so / cling to life.' 'Agony' describes 'dying like thirsty larks upon a mirage.' 'Nostalgia' contemplates 'the boundless silence of a slender girl.' 'The Rivers' (I Fiumi) was written in the WWI trenches.

Ungaretti's hermetic turn was influenced by his friend Guillaume Apollinaire, a fellow WWI soldier. He was a one-time member of the Futurist movement; his collection The War contains pro-war poems like 'Brothers.' He lived in Brazil for a period, teaching Italian at São Paulo University.
