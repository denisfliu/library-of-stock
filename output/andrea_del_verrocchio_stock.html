Italian Renaissance sculptor and painter, teacher of Leonardo da Vinci, Perugino, and Lorenzo di Credi. Known for his equestrian statue of Bartolomeo Colleoni, bronze David, Christ and St. Thomas for Orsanmichele, Putto with Dolphin, and the Baptism of Christ. Trained as a goldsmith. Also designed the golden ball and cross atop the Florence Cathedral lantern.

Summary of Facts

Andrea del Verrocchio (c. 1435-1488) was an Italian Renaissance sculptor, painter, and goldsmith based in Florence. He is most frequently clued as the teacher of Leonardo da Vinci (frequency 5, giveaway), and also taught Perugino and Lorenzo di Credi. Before turning to sculpture and painting, he was trained as a goldsmith.

His most frequently clued work is the Equestrian Statue of Bartolomeo Colleoni in Venice, an unfinished equestrian statue completed after his death by Alessandro Leopardi and Lorenzo di Credi (frequency 4). The pedestal features three pillars on each long side, referencing the rumor that Colleoni had three testicles (frequency 3). Verrocchio famously destroyed the statue's head in a fit of rage, and when threatened with beheading, cleverly replied that he could return a head to a statue but not to his own body. The Frick Collection holds a related sculpture attributed to the wrong family due to a mistake over the testicle motif.

The Baptism of Christ, co-executed with Leonardo, is a key biographical clue: Vasari claimed Verrocchio gave up painting after Leonardo drew an angel more beautifully than any of Verrocchio's prior works (frequency 3 across direct and text-mention clues). Leonardo painted the angel at the left of the composition and reportedly drew tendons and muscles while studying in Verrocchio's workshop.

Verrocchio created a bronze Christ and Saint Thomas for a niche in Orsanmichele, depicting Christ raising his right hand above the doubting saint (frequency 3). His bronze David, housed in the Palazzo Vecchio, shows the young hero wearing a leather tunic with flowers on the chest and pseudo-Arabic script, standing on Goliath's head (frequency 2); Leonardo may have modeled for this statue. The Putto with Dolphin, also in the Palazzo Vecchio, depicts a putto raising a leg while holding a fork-tailed dolphin.

Other notable works and facts include: the Lady with Flowers, the first marble bust to include the subject's hands; La Piagnona, the bell used by Savonarola that was subsequently put on trial and flogged; a collaboration with Michelozzo on the Florence Baptistry's silver altar of San Giovanni; life-size wax figures of the targets of the Pazzi Conspiracy (with Orsino Benintendi); a commemorative statue of marble, porphyry, and bronze for Piero and Giovanni de Medici; the golden ball and cross designed for the top of Brunelleschi's Florence Cathedral lantern; a marble relief of Alexander the Great (cited by Andrew Butterfield as possibly the only authentic Verrocchio in the National Gallery); and an altarpiece for Pistoia Cathedral introducing the Madonna, child, and saints framed by a loggia and landscape views.
