Summary of Facts

Andrea del Verrocchio was an Italian Renaissance sculptor and painter, best known as the teacher of Leonardo da Vinci and Perugino. Before turning to sculpture and painting, he was trained as a goldsmith. His most frequently clued work is the Equestrian Statue of Bartolomeo Colleoni, an unfinished equestrian statue that was completed after his death by Alessandro Leopardi and Lorenzo di Credi. The pedestal of this statue features three pillars on each long side, referencing the rumor that Colleoni had three testicles. The Frick Collection holds a related sculpture attributed to the wrong family due to a mistake over the testicle motif. Verrocchio famously destroyed the statue's head in a fit of rage, and when threatened with beheading, cleverly replied that he could return a head to a statue but not to his own body.

Verrocchio also created a bronze Christ and Saint Thomas for a niche in Orsanmichele, depicting Christ raising his right hand above the doubting saint. His bronze David, housed in the Palazzo Vecchio, depicts the young hero wearing a leather tunic and standing on Goliath's head; Leonardo may have modeled for this statue. Other notable works include the Lady with Flowers, the first marble bust to include the subject's hands, and La Piagnona, the bell used by Savonarola that was subsequently put on trial and flogged. He collaborated with Michelozzo on the Florence Baptistry's silver altar of San Giovanni, and with wax-painting specialist Orsino Benintendi to create life-size figures of the targets of the Pazzi Conspiracy. He also created a commemorative statue of marble, porphyry, and bronze for Piero and Giovanni de Medici. According to Vasari, Verrocchio gave up painting after his apprentice Leonardo drew an angel in The Baptism of Christ more beautifully than any of Verrocchio's prior works.
