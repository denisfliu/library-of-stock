Summary of Facts

Utagawa Hiroshige (1797–1858) was a Japanese woodblock print artist of the Utagawa school, considered the last great master of ukiyo-e. His most celebrated series is The Fifty-Three Stations of the Tokaido (1833–1834), 53 prints depicting post stations along the Tokaido road from Edo to Kyoto. The series begins at Nihonbashi Bridge, includes dramatic scenes at stations like Kanagawa, Kambara, and Shono (where a servant removes his jacket to cover his master in a rainstorm), and concludes with carts and travelers crossing a river to reach the imperial capital.

His One Hundred Famous Views of Edo (1856–1858) is equally important. The print Sudden Shower over Shin-Ohashi Bridge and Atake (1857) uses thin diagonal black lines to convey driving rain over a wooden bridge as figures cower with umbrellas; Vincent van Gogh copied it as Bridge in the Rain and painted faux Japanese calligraphy around the border. Plum Park in Kameido (1857) depicts the abstracted, portrait-oriented branches of a plum tree nicknamed the "Sleeping Dragon" against a deep red sky; Van Gogh also copied this print, and its prominent horizontal branch and red hue are believed to have inspired the red field in Gauguin's Vision After the Sermon. The series also includes plum orchard views at Kameido and Kamada, and was completed after Hiroshige's death by his student, who adopted his name.

Beyond these two landmark series, Hiroshige produced a 69-station series on the Kiso Kaido road, a set of Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji (paralleling Hokusai's famous series), and hundreds of bird-and-flower prints including Yellow Rose and Frogs. He was parodied by fellow Utagawa artist Kuniyoshi, who used cats with punny names to reference his works. His influence on Western art was profound: his style shaped Japonism broadly, and the Polish Nobel laureate Wislawa Szymborska drew the title of her poetry collection The People on the Bridge from one of his prints.
