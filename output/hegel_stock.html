German Idealist philosopher who wrote The Phenomenology of Spirit and Elements of the Philosophy of Right. Known for the dialectical method (thesis-antithesis-synthesis/Aufhebung), the master-slave dialectic, the concept of Geist (spirit), absolute idealism, and his influential lectures on the philosophy of history. His Young/Left Hegelian followers included Marx, Feuerbach, and Stirner.
The Phenomenology of Spirit and the Dialectic

The Phenomenology of Spirit (1807) is Hegel's most famous work, in which consciousness moves through progressively higher levels before arriving at 'absolute knowledge.' It introduces the concept of Geist (spirit), which discovers itself through self-alienation. The 'master-slave dialectic' (lord-bondsman) describes two self-conscious beings recognizing each other through a 'struggle to the death,' where the bondsman experiences 'absolute negativity' via fear before rediscovering himself through labor. The book uses the word aufheben for 'sublation,' whereby contradictions of Being and Nothing are resolved in Becoming. Alexandre Kojeve's influential reading argues that the USA and USSR represented two interpretations of Hegel.

Elements of the Philosophy of Right and Ethical Life

In the Philosophy of Right, Hegel develops the concept of Sittlichkeit (ethical life), which encompasses family, civil society, and the state. Abstract right, morality, and ethical life form three spheres. Property is 'embodiment of personality' and an 'abstract right' through 'mutual recognition.' The state is 'the march of God in the world' and 'no ideal work of art.' The preface contains the famous 'owl of Minerva' metaphor: philosophy always arrives too late, like the owl flying at dusk. He rejected democratic electoral systems in favor of representation by economic 'corporations.'

Geist (Spirit) and History

Geist (spirit) is central to Hegel's philosophy. The Absolute Spirit reconciles Subjective and Objective parts through art, religion, and philosophy. History is the process whereby spirit discovers itself and realizes freedom through 'world-historical figures' like Napoleon (the Weltgeist, world-spirit). The Lectures on the Philosophy of History divide history into original, reflective, and philosophical approaches, introduce the 'cunning of reason' and the term 'Volksgeist,' and infamously claim Africa 'has no history.' Freedom progresses: 'one man is free' -> 'some men are free' -> 'all men are free.' Kojeve's reading of Hegel's theory of history influenced Fukuyama's End of History.

Science of Logic

The Science of Logic (1812) has sections on Being, Essence, and Concept. It calls logic a 'realm of shadows,' rejects the law of non-contradiction, and arrives at 'pure thought,' which Hegel compares to God 'in his essence before the creation of nature.' In the Shorter Logic, Hegel describes the unity of the 'absolute idea' using Aristotle's phrase 'thought thinking itself' (noesis noeseos noesis). J.M.E. McTaggart wrote several commentaries.

Aesthetics

Hegel's Lectures on Aesthetics distinguish between classical, romantic, and symbolic art. He argued classical art was the home of 'ideal beauty' and that art 'in its highest vocation remains for us a thing of the past' (the 'end of art' thesis, modernized by Arthur Danto with Warhol's Brillo Boxes). Poetry is the most 'inward' and perfect form of art. His reading of Antigone as illustrating the 'supreme opposition in ethics' (man vs. woman) is critiqued by Kierkegaard in Either/Or.

Young Hegelians and Legacy

After Hegel's death in 1831, his followers split into Right (Old) Hegelians aligned with the Lutheran church and Left (Young) Hegelians who rejected religious orthodoxy. The Young Hegelians met at Hippel's Weinstube in 1840s Berlin and included Feuerbach, Marx, Stirner, Bruno Bauer, David Friedrich Strauss (who coined the terms), and Moses Hess. Kierkegaard attacked Hegelianism as 'the system,' replacing 'the truth is the whole' with truth as subjectivity. British Idealists (F.H. Bradley, T.H. Green, Bosanquet, McTaggart) were influenced by Hegel. The Pittsburgh school (McDowell, Brandom) revives Hegel. Slavoj Zizek identifies as a Hegelian.

General / Biographical

Hegel is identified as a German Idealist philosopher, author of The Phenomenology of Spirit, popularizer of absolute idealism. He used the example of water boiling to illustrate how change in quality doesn't necessarily produce change in quantity ('measure'). He called philosophical work a 'Penelope's web' in the Philosophy of Right. His concept of 'the Absolute' was central. Jakob Bohme was called 'the first German philosopher' by Hegel. Hegel was Schelling's roommate and later rival. The Kyoto School's dialectic differs from Hegel's by returning things to a pre-opposition state.

Summary of Facts

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel is among the most frequently clued philosophers in quizbowl, with 18 tossups and 47 bonus appearances. He is universally identified as the German Idealist author of The Phenomenology of Spirit.

The Phenomenology of Spirit (1807) traces consciousness through progressively higher levels to 'absolute knowledge.' Its central passage is the master-slave dialectic (lord-bondsman), in which two self-conscious beings recognize each other through a 'struggle to the death'; the bondsman experiences 'absolute negativity' via fear before rediscovering himself through labor. Susan Buck-Morss argues this passage was influenced by the Haitian Revolution. The concept of Aufhebung (sublation) resolves contradictions: Being and Nothing synthesize into Becoming. The thesis-antithesis-synthesis model is often attributed to Hegel but actually comes from Heinrich Moritz Chalybaus/Fichte. Alexandre Kojeve's influential reading shaped Fukuyama's End of History.

The Philosophy of Right develops Sittlichkeit (ethical life) through three spheres: abstract right, morality, and ethical life (family, civil society, state). Its preface contains the owl of Minerva metaphor: philosophy arrives too late, like the owl flying at dusk. The state is 'the march of God in the world' and 'no ideal work of art.'

Geist (spirit) is central: the Absolute Spirit reconciles Subjective and Objective through art, religion, and philosophy. The Lectures on the Philosophy of History describe history as spirit discovering freedom through 'world-historical figures' like Napoleon (Weltgeist). They introduce the 'cunning of reason,' the term 'Volksgeist,' and infamously claim Africa 'has no history.'

The Science of Logic has sections on Being, Essence, and Concept, rejects the law of non-contradiction, and in the Shorter Logic uses Aristotle's 'thought thinking itself' for the absolute idea.

Hegel's Lectures on Aesthetics distinguish classical, romantic, and symbolic art; argue art 'remains for us a thing of the past' (Danto modernized this via Warhol); and call poetry the most perfect art form. His reading of Antigone was critiqued by Kierkegaard.

After his death in 1831, followers split into Right/Old Hegelians (Lutheran church) and Left/Young Hegelians (Feuerbach, Marx, Stirner, Bruno Bauer, D.F. Strauss). Kierkegaard attacked 'the system.' British Idealists (Bradley, Green, McTaggart) extended his work until Moore and Russell's revolt. The Pittsburgh school (McDowell, Brandom) revives Hegel today. Zizek identifies as Hegelian.
