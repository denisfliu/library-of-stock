Brasilia (Pilot Plan)

Costa designed the overall urban layout of Brasilia, Brazil's planned capital, in the shape of an airplane or cross, centered on the Monumental Axis. Oscar Niemeyer designed the civic buildings (Cathedral, National Congress, Palace of the Dawn, etc.), while Roberto Burle Marx designed the city's landscapes and gardens. Brasilia was built on the orders of President Juscelino Kubitschek as part of his 'fifty years of progress in five' program. Costa's design was influenced by Le Corbusier's Radiant City / Ville Radieuse concept. He used his apartment designs from the Parque Guinle for many of Brasilia's housing towers. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Lucio Costa (1902–1998) was a Brazilian architect and urban planner best known for designing the layout of Brasilia, Brazil's planned capital. Costa conceived the city's 'pilot plan' in the shape of an airplane or cross, centered on the Monumental Axis. President Juscelino Kubitschek ordered the construction of Brasilia as part of his 'fifty years of progress in five' program, moving the capital from Rio de Janeiro. While Costa designed the urban framework, his collaborator Oscar Niemeyer designed the city's landmark civic buildings, including the National Congress (with two vertical towers flanked by upward and downward facing hemispheres), a hyperboloid-shaped cathedral, and the Palace of the Dawn. Roberto Burle Marx designed Brasilia's landscapes and gardens. Costa's design incorporated concepts from Le Corbusier's Radiant City / Ville Radieuse, and he used his Parque Guinle apartment designs as models for Brasilia's housing towers.

Costa also co-designed the Gustavo Capanema Palace in Rio de Janeiro with Niemeyer and Burle Marx, and designed a museum built using materials salvaged from the ruins of the Church of Sao Miguel Arcanjo.
