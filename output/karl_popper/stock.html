The Logic of Scientific Discovery & Falsifiability

Popper's major work (1934, originally Logik der Forschung), which proposes falsifiability as the solution to the demarcation problem — distinguishing science from non-science. The book argues that scientific theories should be 'risky and prohibitive,' making 'bold' predictions that could be falsified. It rejects verifiability and induction, accepting Hume's critique of induction. The black swan example demonstrates falsifiability: seeing a black swan falsifies 'all swans are white.' The book outlines a four-step process of deduction and introduces 'basic statements' as claims about observable events. Popper also pioneered the propensity interpretation of probability and argued that Marxism and Freudianism are pseudoscientific.

The Open Society and Its Enemies

Popper's political philosophy work attacking totalitarianism's intellectual roots. Volume I, 'The Spell of Plato,' argues Plato's idealized politics are 'fundamentally identical' to totalitarianism. Volume II, 'The High Tide of Prophecy,' attacks Marx and Hegel. The book argues against historicism (the idea that history unfolds according to inevitable laws) and advocates 'piecemeal engineering' — small, gradual reforms rather than utopian revolution. Popper emphasized minimizing suffering on utilitarian grounds. It also traces a process from 'naive monism' to 'critical dualism.'

Conjectures and Refutations & Verisimilitude

Popper's 1963 work introducing the concept of verisimilitude (truthlikeness) — the idea that scientific knowledge grows by getting closer to the truth. He derived a formula: Vs(a) = CtT(a) - CtF(a), where a is a given statement. He adapted Alfred Tarski's work on truth to develop this concept. The idea has 'fallen into disrepute' due to confusion with probability (per Ilkka Niiniluoto).

The Poverty of Historicism & Three Worlds

Popper's attack on historicism — the idea that history follows inevitable patterns or 'trends.' The book distinguishes trends from laws and argues for 'piecemeal engineering' over holistic utopian engineering. Peter Turchin's cliodynamics essay 'Towards Cliodynamics' refutes this book. Popper's 1967 lecture proposed three worlds: World 1 (physical), World 2 (mental, from biological evolution), and World 3 (products of human thought). He also used the 'cloud vs. clock' analogy for indeterminism, comparing a swarm of gnats (cloud) to a steady clock.

General / Biographical

Popper called his philosophy 'critical rationalism.' He was Austrian-born and worked at the University of Canterbury, the London School of Economics, and the University of London. He was knighted in 1965. George Soros was his student. Imre Lakatos extended his work on falsifiability. His 'postscript' consists of three volumes: The Open Universe, Realism and the Aim of Science, and Quantum Theory and the Schism of Physics.

Summary of Facts

Karl Popper is the Austrian-born philosopher of science who proposed falsifiability as the criterion for distinguishing science from non-science, solving the 'demarcation problem.' In The Logic of Scientific Discovery (1934), he argues that scientific theories should make 'bold,' 'risky' predictions that can be falsified — demonstrated by the black swan example. He rejected verifiability and accepted Hume's critique of induction, arguing science is deductive. He accused Marxism and Freudian psychoanalysis of being pseudoscientific because they failed to make falsifiable predictions. Paul Feyerabend criticized Popper's deductive view and later wrote Against Method.

The Open Society and Its Enemies attacks totalitarianism's intellectual roots. Volume I ('The Spell of Plato') argues Plato's politics are 'fundamentally identical' to totalitarianism; Volume II ('The High Tide of Prophecy') attacks Marx and Hegel. The book advocates 'piecemeal engineering' — small, gradual social reforms — over utopian revolution.

Conjectures and Refutations introduces verisimilitude (truthlikeness), the idea that scientific knowledge grows by approximating truth, with the formula Vs(a) = CtT(a) - CtF(a). The Poverty of Historicism attacks the confusion of trends with laws and advocates piecemeal over holistic engineering. Popper proposed three worlds (physical, mental, products of thought) in a 1967 lecture and used the cloud-vs.-clock analogy for indeterminism.

Popper called his philosophy 'critical rationalism,' worked at the LSE and the University of Canterbury, was knighted in 1965, and taught George Soros. Imre Lakatos extended his work on falsifiability. Harry Blackmun quoted Popper on falsifiability in the Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals decision (1993).
