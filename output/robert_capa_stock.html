General / Biographical

Robert Capa (born Endre Friedmann, 1913–1954) was a Hungarian-American war photojournalist. He collaborated closely with Gerda Taro, who shared his pseudonym and may have taken The Falling Soldier. He died stepping on a landmine in French Indochina. He co-founded Magnum Photos with Henri Cartier-Bresson, David Seymour ('Chim'), and George Rodger. His brother Cornell Capa was also a photographer and joined Magnum. Capa was awarded the Medal of Freedom. His famous quote: 'If your pictures aren't good enough, you're not close enough.' He toured the Soviet Union with John Steinbeck in 1947, producing A Russian Journal. He also photographed a Sicilian peasant pointing out troop movements to a soldier, a woman in The Shaved Woman of Chartres, a child helmet-wearing soldier in China (1938), and eight bread rolls in front of Pablo Picasso's arms. His lost negatives (along with Gerda Taro's and David Seymour's) were found in 'The Mexican Suitcase' (2007 film). Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Robert Capa (born Endre Erno Friedmann, 1913–1954) was a Hungarian-American war photographer widely regarded as one of the greatest photojournalists of the 20th century. He covered five wars — the Spanish Civil War, the Second Sino-Japanese War, World War II, the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, and the First Indochina War — and died when he stepped on a landmine in French Indochina in 1954.

His most famous work is The Falling Soldier, purportedly showing Spanish Loyalist militiaman Federico Borrell Garcia collapsing backward with his rifle dropping at the moment of death during the Spanish Civil War. The image has long been disputed as potentially staged, and the debate intensified when lost negatives by Capa, Gerda Taro, and David Seymour were rediscovered in 2007 (documented in the film The Mexican Suitcase). Capa's close collaborator Gerda Taro shared his pseudonym and may have taken this photograph.

Capa's other iconic body of work is The Magnificent Eleven, the surviving photographs from his D-Day landing at Omaha Beach in June 1944. He waded ashore past 'metal hedgehogs' with the 1st Infantry Division and took over 100 photographs, but almost all were destroyed in a darkroom accident by a Life magazine technician — only eleven survived. These blurry, kinetic images are among the most important documents of World War II, and their style served as visual inspiration for the opening sequence of Steven Spielberg's Saving Private Ryan (1998). Capa's autobiography is titled Slightly Out of Focus, after the caption applied to these images. He was awarded the Medal of Freedom for his WWII photography.

Capa co-founded Magnum Photos with Henri Cartier-Bresson, David 'Chim' Seymour, and George Rodger — a cooperative that became the world's most prestigious photographic agency. His brother Cornell Capa also joined Magnum. Other notable works include The Shaved Woman of Chartres (a bald woman holding her baby as a crowd of collaborationist accusers marches), a photograph of a Sicilian peasant pointing out troop movements to a soldier, and a 1938 image of a child helmet-wearing soldier in China. He also photographed eight bread rolls laid out in front of Pablo Picasso's arms.

In 1947, Capa and John Steinbeck toured the Soviet Union together, producing A Russian Journal (1948), a photo-illustrated account that included haunting images of the ruins of Stalingrad. Capa's famous dictum was 'If your pictures aren't good enough, you're not close enough.' A medal named after him is the premier award in war photography.
