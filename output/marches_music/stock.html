The march is a musical genre originally associated with military processions, typically in 2/4 or 6/8 time with a strong rhythmic pulse. In quizbowl, marches appear most often as subcategories of larger composers' output (Elgar's Pomp and Circumstance, Tchaikovsky's march sets), or as the funeral march subgenre (Chopin, Beethoven). Formal structure features strains bridged to trio sections by 'dogfights'; circus-speed marches are called 'screamers.'
Funeral Marches – Overview

Funeral marches are slow, typically minor-key compositions associated with mourning processions. They appear as movements within larger works or as standalone pieces. Key examples: the second movement (Adagio assai) of Beethoven's Eroica Symphony; the third movement of Chopin's Piano Sonata No. 2 in B-flat minor (which gives the sonata its nickname); the third movement of Mahler's Symphony No. 1 (quotes 'Frère Jacques'); the 'Funeral March of a Marionette' by Gounod; the penultimate movement of Shostakovich's 15th String Quartet; the fourth movement of Scriabin's Piano Sonata No. 1; Siegfried's funeral music in Act 3 of Wagner's Götterdämmerung. A heavily chordal C minor prelude by Chopin was given this nickname by Hans von Bülow. Wikipedia

Pomp and Circumstance Marches – Overview

Six military marches for orchestra (Op. 39) by Edward Elgar. The first march's trio contains the tune 'Land of Hope and Glory,' which was fitted with those lyrics for the Coronation Ode of Edward VII and is commonly heard at graduation ceremonies worldwide. The third march is in C minor. The first performance at Yale University, where Elgar received an honorary doctorate (arranged by Samuel Sanford), helped establish these marches as graduation staples. March as Genre – Structure and Subtypes

The march genre features strains bridged to trio sections by 'dogfight' transitions. Very fast marches written for circus use are called 'screamers'; the best-known screamer is Julius Fučík's Entry of the Gladiators. Military marches by William Walton include Crown Imperial and Orb and Sceptre (for the coronation of King George VI). John Philip Sousa's 'The Stars and Stripes Forever' is the best-known American march. Karl King and Frederick Jewell were prolific march composers for circuses.

Summary of Facts

The march is a musical genre characterized by a strong, regular rhythmic pulse suited to procession, typically in 2/4 or 6/8 time. In quizbowl, marches appear most commonly either as components of larger works (Beethoven's Eroica second movement, Chopin's Piano Sonata No. 2 third movement) or as sets of marches by major composers.

The funeral march is the most clued march subgenre. Defining examples: Beethoven's Eroica Symphony (second movement, Adagio assai — later quoted by Richard Strauss in Metamorphosen); Chopin's Piano Sonata No. 2 in B-flat minor (third movement, marked Lento, featuring a repeating F–G-flat motif below a B-flat pedal — this gives the sonata its funeral march nickname); Mahler's Symphony No. 1 third movement (quotes 'Frère Jacques'); Gounod's 'Funeral March of a Marionette'; the penultimate movement of Shostakovich's 15th String Quartet; the fourth movement of Scriabin's Piano Sonata No. 1; and Siegfried's funeral music in Act 3 of Wagner's Götterdämmerung. Hans von Bülow gave the nickname 'funeral march' to a heavily chordal C minor prelude by Chopin.

Edward Elgar's Pomp and Circumstance Marches (six marches, Op. 39) are the most clued march set. The first march contains a trio later set to the words 'Land of Hope and Glory' for Elgar's Coronation Ode for Edward VII. Its association with graduation ceremonies was cemented when it was performed at Yale University when Elgar received an honorary doctorate (arranged by Samuel Sanford). The third march is in C minor. Elgar's set evokes both pomp and circumstance as characterized in its title.

As a genre, marches have a formal structure: strains are bridged to trio sections by 'dogfight' transitions. Very fast marches written for circus performance are called 'screamers'; the best-known screamer is Julius Fučík's Entry of the Gladiators. Prolific circus march composers include Karl King and Frederick Jewell. John Philip Sousa's 'The Stars and Stripes Forever' is the quintessential American march. William Walton wrote military coronation marches Crown Imperial (George VI) and Orb and Sceptre (Elizabeth II). Tchaikovsky composed several marches including the Marche Slave, a Military March in B-flat minor, a 'Jurist's March' in D, and a Festival Coronation March.
