Summary of Facts

Confessional poetry is an American poetic movement of the 1950s and 1960s characterized by its focus on personal experience, emotional trauma, mental illness, and sexuality, using a direct and colloquial style. The term was coined by critic M.L. Rosenthal in a review of Robert Lowell's Life Studies.

The movement was arguably launched by W.D. Snodgrass's Heart's Needle, a ten-poem sequence about his divorce and separation from his daughter Cynthia, with its title and epigraph drawn from The Madness of Suibhne. The collection, which alternates personal imagery with scenes from the Korean War, won the 1960 Pulitzer Prize.

Robert Lowell's Life Studies (1959) is the collection whose review gave the movement its name. It contains "Beyond the Alps" (a train journey from Rome to Paris discussing dwindling Catholic faith), "91 Revere Street" (a prose memoir), and "Skunk Hour" (describing "where the graveyard shelves on the town," a "red fox stain" covering Blue Hill, and the narrator's declaration "I myself am Hell").

Sylvia Plath's Ariel, edited posthumously by Ted Hughes and named for her horse, contains poems like "Lady Lazarus" ("White Godiva, I unpeel" / "the red eye, the cauldron of morning") and "Daddy." Anne Sexton's To Bedlam and Part Way Back includes "Her Kind" ("I have gone out, a possessed witch") and "Wanting to Die" (suicides "want to know which tools. They never ask why build"). Her collection The Awful Rowing Toward God ends with the speaker getting a royal flush in poker upon reaching "the island called God."

John Berryman's Dream Songs, featuring alter ego Henry (sometimes appearing in blackface, addressed as Mr. Bones), are often grouped with confessional poetry. Delmore Schwartz's epic Genesis was an early precursor. Other figures associated with the movement include Sharon Olds.
