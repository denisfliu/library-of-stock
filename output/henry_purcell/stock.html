English Baroque composer (c. 1659–1695), student of John Blow, and organist at Westminster Abbey. Best known for the opera Dido and Aeneas (especially the 'Lament'), and for semi-operas such as King Arthur, The Fairy-Queen, and Dioclesian. His incidental music to Abdelazer provided the theme Benjamin Britten used in The Young Person's Guide to the Orchestra.
General / Biographical

Henry Purcell (c. 1659–1695) was an English Baroque composer and student of John Blow, whom he succeeded as principal organist at Westminster Abbey. He modeled the final chorus of Dido and Aeneas on the final chorus of Blow's Venus and Adonis. His works are catalogued using Z numbers (compiled by musicologist Franklin Zimmerman). Michael Nyman quoted Purcell in works including 'Chasing Sheep is Best Left to Shepherds' and 'Memorial.' He is commonly attributed as the author of the Irish march Lilliburlero. His brother Michael and son Edward were also composers. His posthumous collection of songs was called Orpheus Britannicus.

Dido and Aeneas – Dido's Lament ('When I am laid in earth')

The climactic final aria of Dido and Aeneas, in G minor. Preceded by the secco recitative 'Thy hand, Belinda.' The opening melody descends with the repeating phrase 'Remember me!' in a dotted rhythm, followed by 'but ah! Forget my fate.' The basso ostinato is a chromatic descending fourth (G to D) — a 'lament bass' or chromatic fourth, also called passus duriusculus — with a two-measure cadential return; each repetition is 5 bars (unusual for a ground bass). The handmaid Belinda witnesses Dido's death. Wikipedia

Dido and Aeneas

Purcell's only true opera, with a libretto by Nahum Tate. Depicts the love story of Dido, Queen of Carthage, and the Trojan hero Aeneas (from Virgil's Aeneid). An evil sorceress tricks Aeneas into abandoning Dido. The opera includes a chorus of witches and a chorus of sailors. Purcell modeled the final chorus ('With drooping wings') on the final chorus of Blow's Venus and Adonis. Heavily influenced by Blow's Venus and Adonis and by Cavalli's Didone. Wikipedia

King Arthur (semi-opera)

A semi-opera with libretto by John Dryden. Arthur rescues the blind princess Emmeline from the Saxon King Oswald. Includes the 'Frost Scene' (Cold Genius aria 'What Power art thou,' sung by Cupid) and the aria 'Fairest Isle.' The 'Cold Song' from this opera (with lyrics about a spirit frozen in ice, sung by a countertenor) was used by Klaus Nomi and covered by Olga Neuwirth. A fanfare on 'Fairest Isle' was used as the BBC Radio 3 theme for a 1995 celebration of British music. Wikipedia

Other Works

Purcell's other semi-operas include Dioclesian (subtitled 'The Prophetess,' named for a Roman emperor) and the unfinished The Indian Queen (set in Mexico; its opening sinfonia was reused from the ode 'Come, Ye Sons of Art'). 'Music for a While' is a countertenor song from the incidental music for a play on the Oedipus story, later published in Orpheus Britannicus. He also wrote a chaconne in G minor (arranged by Britten).

Summary of Facts

Henry Purcell (c. 1659–1695) was an English Baroque composer and student of John Blow, whom he succeeded as principal organist at Westminster Abbey. His works are catalogued by Z numbers, compiled by musicologist Franklin Zimmerman. His brother Michael and son Edward were also composers.

Purcell's only true opera is Dido and Aeneas, with a libretto by Nahum Tate. It depicts the love affair between Dido, Queen of Carthage, and the Trojan hero Aeneas, who is tricked by an evil sorceress into abandoning her. The opera was heavily influenced by Blow's Venus and Adonis (Purcell modeled his final chorus 'With drooping wings' on Blow's) and by Cavalli's Didone. Its climax is 'Dido's Lament' ('When I am laid in earth') — the singer pleads 'Remember me, but ah! Forget my fate' — preceded by the recitative 'Thy hand, Belinda.' The lament is built on a chromatic descending ground bass (G down to D, a chromatic fourth / passus duriusculus), with each repetition lasting an unusual 5 bars. Dido's handmaid Belinda witnesses her death.

Purcell's semi-operas include King Arthur (libretto by John Dryden, in which Arthur rescues the blind princess Emmeline from Saxon King Oswald), The Fairy-Queen (adapted from A Midsummer Night's Dream, adding the Drunken Poet character), Dioclesian ('The Prophetess'), and the unfinished The Indian Queen. King Arthur contains the 'Frost Scene' in which Cupid sings 'What Power art thou' over a three-bar ground bass in C minor, with lyrics about snakes from the head of Fury Alecto — the aria is often performed by a countertenor. The opera's 'Fairest Isle' aria was the basis for a BBC Radio 3 1995 celebration of British music. 'Music for a While,' a countertenor song from Purcell's incidental music for an Oedipus-based play, was later published in his posthumous collection Orpheus Britannicus.

Purcell's incidental music for Aphra Behn's Abdelazer provided the rondeau theme Benjamin Britten used in The Young Person's Guide to the Orchestra (Variations and Fugue on a Theme of Purcell). He composed music for Queen Mary II's funeral — a C minor March for four flatt (slide) trumpets and a Canzona — and the setting of 'Thou Knowest Lord.' His birthday ode 'Come, Ye Sons of Art' (honoring Queen Mary II) features the countertenor duet 'Sound the Trumpet'; its opening sinfonia was recycled in The Indian Queen. His Ode to St. Cecilia ('Hail! Bright Cecilia') includes the movement 'Wondrous Machine!' in praise of the organ; his Te Deum was the first English Te Deum with orchestral accompaniment. William Croft used Purcell's setting of 'Thou Knowest Lord' as a homage. Michael Nyman quoted Purcell in 'Chasing Sheep is Best Left to Shepherds' and 'Memorial'; Purcell is also credited with the Irish march Lilliburlero.
