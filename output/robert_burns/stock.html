Scottish national poet known for poems in Scots dialect including "To a Mouse," "A Red, Red Rose," "Tam o'Shanter," "Auld Lang Syne," and "To a Louse." Burns Suppers celebrate his work annually on January 25th.
To a Mouse

A poem addressed to a "Wee, sleeket, cowran, tim'rous beastie" whose home has been destroyed by a plough. The speaker regrets that "Man's dominion / Has broken Nature's social union." Contains the famous line "the best laid schemes o' Mice an' Men gang aft agley," which provided the title for John Steinbeck's Of Mice and Men. The creature is told it is "blessed, compared with me" because "the present only toucheth thee."

Tam o' Shanter

A narrative poem about a farmer who rides home on his grey mare Meg after getting drunk, encountering "warlocks and witches in a dance." He hails "inspiring bold John Barleycorn." The poem warns "Pleasures are like poppies spread / you seize the flower, its bloom is shed." At the end, the witch Nannie rips off Meg's tail as they escape by crossing the River Doon. Tam shouts "Weel done, Cutty Sark!"

Auld Lang Syne

Burns's best-known lyric, often sung at New Year's celebrations. Opens "should auld acquaintance be forgot / and never brought to mind." The speaker invokes a "cup o' kindness" and describes picking "gowans" (daisies) with a friend.

Other Poems and General

Burns wrote "To a Louse" ("Address to a Louse"), about a creature on a lady's bonnet at church, containing "O would some Power the gift give us / To see ourselves as others see us!" "To a Mountain Daisy" describes a flower "crush'd beneath the furrow's weight" with "Stern Ruin's ploughshare drives elate." "Comin Thro' the Rye" features Jenny carrying her petticoat through a field, with "gin a body meet a body" — misquoted by Holden Caulfield in The Catcher in the Rye. "Address to a Haggis" calls haggis the "chieftain o' the puddin' race" and is recited at Burns Suppers on January 25th. "A Man's a Man for A' That" praises the dignity of the honest man. "Scots Wha Hae" was a longtime unofficial anthem of Scotland. "Afton Water" asks the river to "flow gently" so as not to disturb sleeping Mary. Burns commonly names a six-line stanza with AAABAB rhyme ("Burns stanza" or "standard Habbie"). He influenced Ulster's weaver poets and wrote "To the Weavers Gin Ye Go." "Bonnie Lesley" says "To see her is to love her." "Ae Fond Kiss" shares a "fond kiss" with a woman named Nancy.

Summary of Facts

Robert Burns is the national poet of Scotland, celebrated annually on January 25th at Burns Suppers where his "Address to a Haggis" (calling it the "chieftain o' the puddin' race") is recited. His poems, written largely in Scots dialect, are staples of quizbowl literature.

"To a Mouse" is his most frequently clued poem, addressed to a "wee, sleeket, cowran, tim'rous beastie" whose home is destroyed by a plough. The speaker regrets that "Man's dominion / Has broken Nature's social union" and delivers the famous line "the best laid schemes o' Mice an' Men gang aft agley," which provided the title for Steinbeck's Of Mice and Men.

"A Red, Red Rose" compares love to "a red, red rose that's newly sprung in June" and pledges love "while the sands of life shall run" and until "the rocks melt with the sun." "Tam o' Shanter" is a narrative poem about a farmer who rides home drunk on his mare Meg, encounters witches dancing, and barely escapes as Meg loses her tail to the witch Nannie at the River Doon. "Auld Lang Syne," opening "should auld acquaintance be forgot," is traditionally sung at New Year.

Other notable poems include "To a Louse" ("O would some Power the gift give us / To see ourselves as others see us!"), "Comin Thro' the Rye" (whose line "gin a body meet a body" is misquoted by Holden Caulfield, providing the title of The Catcher in the Rye), "To a Mountain Daisy," "Afton Water," "Bonnie Lesley," and "A Man's a Man for A' That." Burns names a six-line AAABAB stanza form (the standard Habbie) and influenced Ulster's weaver poets.
