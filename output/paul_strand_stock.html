American photographer (1890–1976) and cinematographer, known for pioneering 'straight photography' as a shift from Pictorialism. Best known for Wall Street (1915), showing silhouetted figures dwarfed by the dark rectangular windows of the J.P. Morgan building. Also known for White Fence, Blind (a woman wearing a 'Blind' sign, taken with a false/prism lens), and the documentary film Manhatta (co-directed with Charles Sheeler). Student of Lewis Hine; his work was shown at Alfred Stieglitz's 291 Gallery and published in Camera Work. Inspired Ansel Adams and Georgia O'Keeffe.
Wall Street (1915)

Paul Strand's most famous photograph. Shows silhouetted men walking below four (or five) huge, dark, semi-abstract rectangular windows of the J.P. Morgan building. Long shadows stretch out beneath the figures. Published in the 49th and 50th issues of Alfred Stieglitz's Camera Work. Considered a pivotal work in the shift from Pictorialism to Modernist 'straight photography.' Wikipedia

Blind (1916)

Candid portrait of an old woman with one eye shut and the other staring blankly, wearing a cardboard sign reading 'Blind' around her neck. Taken with a false lens (prism lens) that pointed 90 degrees away from the subject so she would not realize she was being photographed. The approach of using a camera that 'shot ninety degrees off from the opposite direction' was used for multiple candid street portraits. Wikipedia

White Fence (1916)

Photograph showing an extremely flat white fence that forms a diagonal line in the foreground, with two Port Kent, New York houses and a barn partially blurred behind it. One of Strand's examples of abstraction and straight photography. Wikipedia

Manhatta (1921)

Short avant-garde city-symphony documentary film co-directed and co-photographed by Strand and Charles Sheeler. Intertitles from Walt Whitman poems are interspersed throughout. The film borrowed from Strand's Wall Street photograph. Considered a pioneer of the 'city-symphony' genre. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

American photographer (1890–1976). Student of Lewis Hine; his interest in photography began when Hine took him to Alfred Stieglitz's 291 Gallery. Work published in Camera Work (the 49th and 50th issues featured his pictures). Worked in Mexico, where he was head of the Education Department's Photo and Film division; work there includes Woman of Alvarado, Veracruz. Photographed an old widow surrounded by her five sons in Italy (trip taken with the screenwriter of Bicycle Thieves). Photographed a Church at the Taos art colony in New Mexico. Inspired Ansel Adams to move away from Pictorialism. His close-cropping technique inspired Georgia O'Keeffe's large-scale flower paintings. Strand's work signals a shift from Pictorialism to 'straight photography.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Paul Strand (1890–1976) was an American photographer and cinematographer who was a pivotal figure in the shift from Pictorialism to modernist 'straight photography.' He was a student of Lewis Hine, and his interest in photography began when Hine took him to Alfred Stieglitz's 291 Gallery. His work was published in issues 49 and 50 of Stieglitz's Camera Work.

His most famous photograph, Wall Street (1915), depicts silhouetted pedestrians dwarfed by the huge dark rectangular windows of the J.P. Morgan building, with long shadows stretching beneath them. Blind (1916) shows an old woman with one eye closed and the other staring blankly, wearing a 'Blind' sign around her neck; it was taken with a false (prism) lens pointing 90 degrees away from the subject so she would not know she was being photographed. White Fence (1916) is an extremely flat composition showing a white fence forming a diagonal line in the foreground before two Port Kent, New York houses.

In 1921, Strand co-directed and co-photographed Manhatta with Charles Sheeler, a short city-symphony documentary with intertitles from Walt Whitman poems, considered a pioneer of the city-symphony genre. He also worked in Mexico, becoming head of the Education Department's Photo and Film division, and created Woman of Alvarado, Veracruz. He photographed a church at the Taos art colony in New Mexico. He traveled to Italy (with the screenwriter of Bicycle Thieves) and photographed an old widow surrounded by her five sons.

Strand's work had notable influence on other artists: he inspired Ansel Adams to move away from Pictorialism, and his close-cropping photographic technique inspired Georgia O'Keeffe's large-scale flower paintings.
