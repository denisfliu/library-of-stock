Early modern English philosopher, often credited as the father of the scientific method. Author of Novum Organum, The Advancement of Learning, and the utopian novel The New Atlantis. Outlined the doctrine of the four idols of the mind and championed inductive reasoning over Aristotelian syllogism. Proposed the Great Instauration as a sixfold reformation of knowledge.
Novum Organum and the Scientific Method

Bacon's most frequently clued work, the Novum Organum (1620), was intended as part of a larger project called the Great Instauration (Instauratio Magna). The title references Aristotle's Organon, the collected works on logic, and Bacon's aim was to replace Aristotelian syllogistic logic with his method of 'true induction' or 'eliminative induction.' The work is best known for its doctrine of the four idols and its systematic method of arranging data into tables of presence, absence, and degrees to investigate natural phenomena like heat.

The Great Instauration (Instauratio Magna)

Bacon's planned magnum opus, a sixfold reformation of all human knowledge, of which only the second part (the Novum Organum) was ever completed. The project aimed to reform knowledge in six parts, likened to God's creation of the world in six days. It included a 'Ladder of the Intellect' and a 'Formula of Interpretation.' The Advancement of Learning became the first volume after revision.

The Advancement of Learning

Published in 1605, this work classifies human knowledge into the categories of history, poesy, and philosophy. It describes three 'distempers' of false learning — the delicate, the contentious, and the fantastical — and distinguishes between 'pure' and 'proud' knowledge. It inspired the 'system of human knowledge' in Diderot's Encyclopedie. A revised version became the first volume of the Great Instauration.

The New Atlantis and Salomon's House

Bacon's utopian novel (1627) set on the fictional island of Bensalem, featuring the ideal research institute called Salomon's House. The institution is 'dedicated to the study of the works and creatures of God' and includes 'Merchants of Light' who travel the world gathering knowledge and 'Inoculaters' who execute experiments. It represents Bacon's vision of organized scientific inquiry.

General / Biographical

Common giveaway identifiers for Francis Bacon include his role as an English philosopher of the early modern period who formulated the scientific method, his association with the Novum Organum, and his being called the 'father of experimental philosophy.' Biographical clues include the famous story of his death from stuffing a chicken with snow to test food preservation, his cosmological ideas involving quaternions of mercury and sulfur, and his classification of magic as applied science.

Summary of Facts

Francis Bacon (1561-1626) is one of the most consistently clued early modern philosophers in quizbowl, appearing primarily as the architect of the scientific method and the author of the Novum Organum. The overwhelming majority of clues center on three topics: the four idols of the mind, the method of induction, and the relationship between Novum Organum and Aristotle's Organon.

The four idols are the single most common clue, appearing in nearly every tossup and many bonuses. They are the Idols of the Tribe (rooted in human nature), the Cave (individual faults and biases), the Marketplace (abuse of language and mutual association), and the Theatre (mindless adherence to received philosophical systems, likened to stage plays). Bacon's championing of 'true induction' over Aristotelian syllogistic logic is the second most common clue and frequently serves as the giveaway. The title Novum Organum itself references Aristotle's Organon.

Bacon's empirical method is described through his use of 'tables of presentation' — tables of presence, absence, and degrees — to systematically investigate phenomena like heat. He described heat as 'motion and nothing else,' making him one of the first to propose a kinetic theory of heat. His 'First Vintage of the Form of Heat' is a specific application of this method. Related power clues include his 27 kinds of 'prerogative instances' (cited by John R. Platt as inspiration for 'Strong Inference') and his concept of 'first vintages' as preliminary approximations to truth.

The Great Instauration (Instauratio Magna) was Bacon's planned sixfold reformation of all knowledge, of which only the Novum Organum was completed. The Advancement of Learning, which became its first volume, classifies knowledge into history, poesy, and philosophy, and identifies three 'distempers' of false learning: the delicate, contentious, and fantastical. It inspired the classification system used in Diderot's Encyclopedie.

The New Atlantis, Bacon's utopian novel set on the island of Bensalem, features Salomon's House, an idealized research institution with specialized roles including 'Merchants of Light' and 'Inoculaters.' This frequently appears in bonus questions about utopian literature alongside Thomas More's Utopia and Campanella's City of the Sun.

Biographical clues include the famous story of Bacon stuffing a chicken with snow to test food preservation (leading to his death), his bee-versus-ant-versus-spider analogy for empiricism, and his cosmological ideas involving quaternions of mercury and sulfur. He is often called the 'father of experimental philosophy,' a title given by Voltaire in his Philosophical Letters. Bacon shares his surname with the medieval philosopher Roger Bacon, which occasionally appears in dual-answer tossups.
