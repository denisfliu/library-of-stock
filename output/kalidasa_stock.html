Classical Sanskrit poet and playwright, dated to the 4th or 5th century CE. Best known for the play The Recognition of Shakuntala, in which King Dushyanta forgets his wife due to a curse by the sage Durvasa until a signet ring is found in a fish. Also wrote Urvashi Won by Valour, Malavika and Agnimitra, the poem The Cloud Messenger, and the epic The Birth of Kumara. Johann Wolfgang von Goethe praised his work and based the 'Prelude' of Faust on a prologue by Kalidasa.
The Recognition of Shakuntala

Kalidasa's most famous play, a seven-act drama in Sanskrit based on a story from the Mahabharata. The play opens with a stage director instructing an actress and a 'Benediction Upon the Audience.' King Dushyanta meets Shakuntala at the hermitage of the sage Kanva after nearly killing a deer. The sage Durvasa curses Dushyanta to forget Shakuntala until he sees a signet ring. Shakuntala loses the ring crossing a river, and it is later found by a fisherman in the belly of a fish, restoring Dushyanta's memory. The play ends with the couple reunited and their son found playing with lion cubs. Goethe based the 'Prelude' of Faust on this play's metatheatrical prologue. Wikipedia

The Cloud Messenger (Meghaduta)

A 111-stanza poem in Sanskrit written in the seventeen-syllable Mandakranta ('slow-stepper') meter. An exiled yaksha (servant of the god Kubera) persuades a cloud to journey to the city of Alaka to send a message of love to his wife. The yaksha grows too thin for his bracelets during his year-long exile. The cloud passes the 'dread shrine' of Shiva in Ujjain on its journey. Inspired later imitations, including one about the wind by Dhoyi. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Kalidasa is a classical Sanskrit poet and playwright dated to the 4th or 5th century CE. He is one of the most important figures in Indian literary history. His works include three plays, two epics, and lyric poetry. He legendarily took up writing after a princess rejected him for being an unintelligent shepherd. His life was depicted in the 1958 Hindi play One Day in the Season of Rain (Ashadh Ka Ek Din) by Mohan Rakesh.

Summary of Facts

Kalidasa is a classical Sanskrit poet and playwright dated to the 4th or 5th century CE, widely regarded as the greatest writer in the Sanskrit language. His most frequently asked-about work is the play The Recognition of Shakuntala, a seven-act drama based on a story from the Mahabharata. In it, King Dushyanta meets the title character at the hermitage of the sage Kanva while hunting, after nearly killing a deer. An ascetic prophesies that the king will father a universal emperor. After falling in love with Shakuntala, Dushyanta gives her a signet ring, telling her to 'Count every day one letter of my name.' The sage Durvasa then curses the king to forget his wife. Shakuntala loses the ring while crossing a river, and a fisherman later discovers it in the belly of a fish (or carp), breaking the curse. The fisherman reports his find to policemen including Suchaka and Januka. A chaplain reports seeing a 'heavenly light' that bears Shakuntala away. The play ends with the couple reunited and their son found playing with lion cubs. The prologue features a Stage Director instructing an actress to sing about summer; Johann Wolfgang von Goethe based the 'Prelude' of his Faust on this metatheatrical opening, and wrote an epigraph praising Kalidasa ('Wouldst thou the earth and heaven itself in one sole name combine?').

Kalidasa's second most-discussed play is Urvashi Won by Valour (Vikramorvasiyam), about the celestial nymph Urvashi and the mortal king Pururavas. Urvashi is rescued from the demon Keshin by her lover. She is later banished from heaven when she accidentally says her real lover's name during a heavenly play-within-a-play (a 'groom-selection'). She enters a forbidden forest and is turned into a vine. She writes of her love on a birch leaf, which is accidentally seen by her lover's wife. The king spots a red gemstone just before discovering his lover in vine form. In a celebrated 'mad scene,' the king wanders a forest talking to a peacock, elephant, river, trees, and flowers while searching for Urvashi.

Kalidasa's first play, Malavika and Agnimitra, tells of King Agnimitra falling in love with a dancing servant named Malavika, who turns out to be a princess. The queen imprisons Malavika out of jealousy. A jester tricks the queen into giving up her ring (snake-seal) by pretending to treat a fake snake bite, then uses the ring to free Malavika from prison. One character is asked to put her feet on a tree to get it to bloom in five days. In the prologue, a stage assistant expresses surprise at performing a new author's work instead of older playwrights. Kalidasa praised the earlier playwright Bhasa in this introduction.

The Cloud Messenger (Meghaduta) is a 111-stanza lyric poem written in the seventeen-syllable Mandakranta ('slow-stepper') meter. An exiled yaksha, a servant of the god of wealth Kubera, persuades a cloud to journey to the city of Alaka to convey a message to his wife. The yaksha grows too thin for his bracelets during his year-long exile. The cloud passes the 'dread shrine' of Shiva in Ujjain on its journey and is compared to a 'glossy braid of hair.' Arthur Ryder's translation describes the cloud as 'a dark nipple on Earth's bosom white.' A passage describes a 'wishing-tree' from which young women get wine, gems, and cosmetics. The poem inspired an imitation about the wind by Dhoyi. Panini is attributed a stanza in which a cloud laments 'What, have I with my showers let fall the moon?'

The Birth of Kumara (Kumarasambhava) is an epic poem beginning with a description of the Himalayas as a 'measuring-rod' of the world, or 'the Snowy Range.' It describes the birth of Parvati, her marriage to Shiva, and the birth of their son Kumara. The fourth canto includes the lament of Rati after Kama (the god of love) is burned to death by Shiva; Kama's widow mistakes his outline in the ashes for him still alive.

Kalidasa's earliest work, the Ritusamhara (Garland of Seasons), is divided into six cantos, each about a different season, and focuses on erotic love. A theory attributing Kalidasa's works to three different authors has been justified using this early poem.

Biographically, Kalidasa legendarily took up writing after a princess rejected him for being an unintelligent shepherd. Mohan Rakesh's 1958 Hindi play One Day in the Season of Rain (Ashadh Ka Ek Din) depicts the love triangle of Kalidasa, his village lover, and his noble wife. The play's title comes from a line in The Cloud Messenger.
