Summary of Facts

Lawrence Ferlinghetti is an American Beat poet who co-founded the City Lights Bookstore in San Francisco. He is best known for his poetry collection A Coney Island of the Mind, whose title comes from Henry Miller's Into the Night Life. The collection contains "Constantly Risking Absurdity" (comparing a poet to an acrobat on "a high wire of his own making"), "I Am Waiting" (the speaker waits for "a new rebirth of wonder" and "retribution for what America did to Tom Sawyer"), and "Junkman's Obbligato" (the speaker looks "like Walt Whitman / a homemade bomb in the pocket").

Ferlinghetti published Allen Ginsberg's "Howl" and was prosecuted on obscenity charges for it, arrested alongside Shigeyoshi Murao. He attended the Six Gallery reading where he heard an incomplete version of "Howl" and subsequently telegrammed Ginsberg offering to publish his works. He also published works by Jack Kerouac.

His poem "Dog" describes a "real live barking democratic dog" with "something to say about ontology" that looks "like a living questionmark / into the great gramophone of puzzling existence." "The World is a Beautiful Place" declares "the world is the best place of all" but warns "right in the middle of it comes the smiling mortician." "Goya's Greatest Scenes" claims the moment of "suffering humanity" is captured in Goya's paintings.

Ferlinghetti witnessed the aftermath of the bombing of Nagasaki while in the Navy. His "Populist Manifesto" asks "where are Whitman's wild children" and references Mayakovsky. His poem "Underwear" riffs on Dylan Thomas with "Do not go naked into that good night." He wrote "Canticle of Jack Kerouac" and "the pennycandy store behind the El" (where the speaker "first fell in love with unreality"). His correspondence with Ginsberg was published as I Greet You At the Beginning of a Great Career, a title borrowed from Emerson's famous letter to Whitman about Leaves of Grass.
