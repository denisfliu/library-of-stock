La Gioconda

Ponchielli's most famous opera, premiered at La Scala in 1876. Set in 17th-century Venice. Libretto by Arrigo Boito (writing as Tobia Gorrio), based on Victor Hugo's play Angelo. The title character (a street ballad singer) helps her rival Laura escape the clutches of the Venetian inquisitor Alvise Badoero and his spy Barnaba. Ends with Gioconda stabbing herself. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Amilcare Ponchielli (1834–1886) was an Italian opera composer best known for La Gioconda (1876), which is his sole work to remain in the standard operatic repertoire. He is also noted as a teacher at the Milan Conservatory, where his students included Giacomo Puccini and Pietro Mascagni.

La Gioconda is set in 17th-century Venice. The libretto was written by Arrigo Boito (under the pseudonym Tobia Gorrio), who also wrote the libretti for Verdi's Otello and Falstaff and composed the opera Mefistofele. The plot centers on Gioconda, a street ballad singer in love with Enzo Grimaldo. Her mother La Cieca, a blind street singer, is saved by the noblewoman Laura, which causes Gioconda to help Laura escape from Laura's husband Alvise Badoero (a Venetian inquisitor) and the villainous spy Barnaba, despite Laura being Gioconda's romantic rival. Gioconda sings the desperate aria 'Suicidio' and ultimately stabs herself rather than yield to Barnaba. An early subplot involves an old woman accused of using witchcraft to influence a regatta. Act II features the tenor aria 'Cielo e mar' (Sky and Sea), sung by Enzo on his ship as he awaits Laura's arrival — in 1982, Carlo Bini was roundly booed at the Met when he sang this aria as a last-minute replacement for Plácido Domingo.

The opera's third act is set at a party at the Ca' d'Oro in Venice, where guests watch the 'Dance of the Hours' (Danza delle ore), a ballet interlude depicting the passage of time through four sections: dawn, day, evening, and night. A chorus sings 'Prodigio! Incanto!' to mark the transition between dawn and day; costume changes and lighting effects differentiate the sections. The Dance of the Hours was animated in Disney's Fantasia (1940), depicting dancing ostriches, hippos, elephants, and alligators, and its tune is the basis for comedian Allan Sherman's 1963 novelty song 'Hello Muddah, Hello Fadduh.' Christopher Wheeldon re-choreographed it for a Met production. In the original music, flutes play 'B4 B5 B4' to punctuate an ascending theme that begins G-sharp, B, B, C-sharp.

Among Ponchielli's other works, I Lituani is based on Adam Mickiewicz's poem Konrad Wallenrod about a Lithuanian pagan captured by the Teutonic Knights; its St. Petersburg premiere was given under the title Aldona. I Promessi Sposi is based on Alessandro Manzoni's eponymous novel.
