A. E. Housman

English poet and classical scholar, best known for his poetry collection A Shropshire Lad and his authoritative edition of Manilius's Astronomica. His poems often meditate on mortality, unrequited love, and the English countryside. He is also the subject of Tom Stoppard's play The Invention of Love.
To an Athlete Dying Young

One of Housman's most frequently clued poems, from A Shropshire Lad. It addresses a young athlete who dies early, telling him 'The time you won your town the race / We chaired you through the market-place.' The poem argues it is better to die young before glory fades, with the lines 'early though the laurel grows / It withers quicker than the rose' and the image of a 'garland briefer than a girl's.' The athlete is told to 'hold to the low lintel up / The still-defended challenge-cup' and 'Eyes the shady night has shut / Cannot see the record cut.' The athlete has an 'early-laurelled head' and becomes a 'townsman of a stiller town.'

Terence, This Is Stupid Stuff

One of the last poems in A Shropshire Lad, in which a character called Terence is told his verse gives people 'the belly-ache.' The poem argues that poetry prepares one for suffering: 'malt does more than Milton can / To justify God's ways to man.' It ends with the story of King Mithridates, who immunized himself against poison by consuming small amounts, concluding 'Mithridates, he died old.' The poem also states 'Luck's a chance, but trouble's sure' and 'There's brisker pipes than poetry.'

Loveliest of Trees, the Cherry Now

The second poem in A Shropshire Lad, about a cherry tree 'hung with snow' that 'stands about the woodland ride / Wearing white for Eastertide.' The speaker resolves to see it because 'of my threescore years and ten / twenty will not come again' and 'fifty springs are little room' to look at 'things in bloom.' Dorothy Parker parodied it: 'how gay 'twould be / to hang me from a flowering tree.'

Other Works and General

Housman was a classical scholar who spent thirty years editing Manilius's Astronomica, calling Manilius 'facile and frivolous.' He wrote the parodic 'Fragment of a Greek Tragedy' ('thy health is poor; But thine arithmetic is quite correct'). His posthumous essay 'De Amicitia' discusses his unrequited love for Moses Jackson. He is the protagonist of Tom Stoppard's play The Invention of Love, in which he crosses the River Styx in Charon's boat at age 77 and encounters Oscar Wilde. His lecture 'The Name and Nature of Poetry' describes how recalling poetry while shaving caused him to cut himself. George Butterworth set eleven of his poems to music. 'Epitaph on an Army of Mercenaries' eulogizes men who 'saved the sum of things for pay.' '1887' sardonically concludes 'God will save the Queen.' His collection Last Poems begins with 'We'll to the woods no more.' 'Oh Who is That Young Sinner' commemorates Oscar Wilde's trial. Max Beerbohm and Ezra Pound also parodied him. He was also parodied by Dorothy Parker. His poem 'Into my heart on air that kills' asks 'What are those blue remembered hills?' An Alice Munro story involves reading 'On Wenlock Edge' while naked.

Summary of Facts

A. E. Housman (Alfred Edward Housman) was an English poet and classical scholar, best known for his poetry collection A Shropshire Lad (1896). He is one of the most distinctively clued poets in quizbowl, with questions centering on his pastoral elegiac poetry, his classical scholarship, and his portrayal in Tom Stoppard's play The Invention of Love.

Housman's most frequently clued poem is 'To an Athlete Dying Young,' which addresses a young man who won a race and was 'chaired through the market-place,' arguing it is better to die before glory fades since 'early though the laurel grows / It withers quicker than the rose.' The athlete is told to 'hold to the low lintel up / The still-defended challenge-cup' and has an 'early-laurelled head.' 'When I Was One-and-Twenty' features a wise man's advice: 'Give crowns and pounds and guineas / But not your heart away.' 'Terence, This Is Stupid Stuff' is a meditation on the value of poetry that famously declares 'malt does more than Milton can / To justify God's ways to man' and ends with the story of Mithridates immunizing himself against poison: 'Mithridates, he died old.' 'Loveliest of Trees, the Cherry Now' describes a cherry tree 'hung with snow' and 'Wearing white for Eastertide,' with the speaker resolving to appreciate it since 'fifty springs are little room.' Dorothy Parker parodied this poem. 'On Wenlock Edge' imagines a Roman now 'ashes under Uricon' who experienced the same wind; Ralph Vaughan Williams set it to music. 'Is My Team Ploughing?' is a dialogue between a dead man and his friend who is now 'cheering' the dead man's sweetheart.

Housman was a classical scholar who spent thirty years editing Manilius's Astronomica, calling Manilius 'facile and frivolous.' He wrote the parody 'Fragment of a Greek Tragedy' ('thy health is poor; But thine arithmetic is quite correct') and 'Epitaph on an Army of Mercenaries' about men who 'saved the sum of things for pay.' His lecture 'The Name and Nature of Poetry' describes how recalling poetry while shaving caused him to cut himself. His unrequited love for Oxford classmate Moses Jackson is discussed in his posthumous essay 'De Amicitia.'

Tom Stoppard's play The Invention of Love dramatizes Housman's life: at age 77, he crosses the River Styx in Charon's boat, encountering Oscar Wilde and reflecting on his love for Moses Jackson. The play features Housman quoting Greek from Aeschylus's lost Myrmidones. George Butterworth set eleven of Housman's poems to music. His poems were also parodied by Ezra Pound, Max Beerbohm, and Dorothy Parker. His later collection Last Poems (1922) opens with 'We'll to the woods no more.' 'Oh Who is That Young Sinner' commemorates Oscar Wilde's trial. An Alice Munro story features a character reading 'On Wenlock Edge' while naked.
