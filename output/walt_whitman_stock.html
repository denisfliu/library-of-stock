American poet associated with the collection Leaves of Grass, which contains poems like 'Song of Myself,' 'Crossing Brooklyn Ferry,' 'I Sing the Body Electric,' 'O Captain! My Captain!,' and 'When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd.' Known for his celebration of democracy, the body, and comradeship, and for his influence on later poets including Allen Ginsberg and Latin American modernists.
Song of Myself

The centerpiece of Leaves of Grass, this long poem is Whitman's most frequently clued work. Questions focus on its celebration of the self, its democratic vision equating all people, and its famously expansive catalogues. The speaker 'loafes' and observes grass, contains 'multitudes,' sounds a 'barbaric yawp,' and interprets grass as a 'uniform hieroglyphic' and 'the handkerchief of the Lord.' The eleventh section describing twenty-eight young men bathing is a particularly notable episode.

General / Biographical

Whitman's biographical details that appear in clues include his residence in Camden, New Jersey; his companion Peter Doyle (coded as '16.4' in notebooks); his use of phrenological terms 'amativeness' and 'adhesiveness'; his experimental memoir Specimen Days; his early temperance novel Franklin Evans; and his wide influence on later poets. He was popularized in Latin America by an 1887 Jose Marti essay, and influenced Hart Crane, Ezra Pound, and the Latin American modernists Ruben Dario and Pablo Neruda.

Musical Settings

Whitman's poetry has been extensively set to music. Ralph Vaughan Williams used his texts for A Sea Symphony (opening with 'Behold!' in B-flat minor); Gustav Holst wrote an overture and The Mystic Trumpeter; Paul Hindemith composed an oratorio When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd; George Walker won the 1996 Pulitzer for Lilacs (for voice and orchestra); and Frederick Delius set 'Out of the Cradle Endlessly Rocking' in 1906.

Summary of Facts

Walt Whitman is one of the most heavily clued American poets in quizbowl, appearing across literature, fine arts, and occasionally geography and history categories. His magnum opus Leaves of Grass, which he revised throughout his life into a 'deathbed' edition completed in Camden, New Jersey, is the central work. Within it, 'Song of Myself' dominates with its celebration of democracy, the body, and the self -- the speaker 'loafes' and observes grass, interprets it as 'a uniform hieroglyphic' and 'the handkerchief of the Lord,' contains 'multitudes,' sounds a 'barbaric yawp,' and asks 'What is the grass?' The eleventh section's twenty-eight young men bathing, watched by a woman, is a frequently clued homoerotic episode.

Whitman's Lincoln elegies form another major cluster: 'When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd' features a hermit thrush singing a 'Song of the bleeding throat' and a 'great star' drooping in the western sky, while 'O Captain! My Captain!' describes the captain 'fallen cold and dead' after the ship is 'anchor'd safe and sound.' 'Crossing Brooklyn Ferry' opens with 'Flood-tide below me!' and describes commuters 'attired in the usual costumes.' 'Out of the Cradle Endlessly Rocking' features a mockingbird, 'the low and delicious word death,' and ends with 'the sea whisper'd me.'

The 'Calamus' poems celebrate 'the manly love of comrades' and are noted for their homoerotic content. Whitman used phrenological terms 'amativeness' and 'adhesiveness' to distinguish types of attraction, and coded his companion Peter Doyle as '16.4' in notebooks. Other frequently clued poems include 'I Sing the Body Electric' ('the armies of those I love engirth me'), 'A Noiseless Patient Spider' (the soul launching 'filament, filament, filament'), and 'I Hear America Singing' (the 'varied carols' of workers).

Whitman's cross-references are extensive. Allen Ginsberg's 'A Supermarket in California' imagines Whitman 'poking among the meats' and 'eyeing the grocery boys,' asking 'Which way does your beard point tonight?' Langston Hughes's 'I, Too, Sing America' responds to 'I Hear America Singing.' Federico Garcia Lorca wrote an ode to Whitman in Poet in New York, calling him 'Adam of blood, Macho.' Ruben Dario coined his Spanish nickname 'el gran viejo,' and Jose Marti's 1887 essay began his 'cult' among Latin American modernists. Hart Crane's The Bridge responds to Whitman especially in 'Cape Hatteras,' and Ezra Pound acknowledged his influence in 'A Pact.'

Whitman's poetry has been extensively set to music: Vaughan Williams's A Sea Symphony (B-flat minor brass fanfare on 'Behold!'), Hindemith's oratorio, George Walker's Pulitzer-winning Lilacs, Frederick Delius's 1906 setting of 'Out of the Cradle,' and Gustav Holst's overture and The Mystic Trumpeter.
