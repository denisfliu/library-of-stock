Russian author born Alexei Maximovich Peshkov, considered a founder of socialist realism. Best known for the play The Lower Depths, set in a boarding house near the Volga where characters like the idealist Luka, the thief Vaska Pepel, and the Actor reside. Also known for the short story 'Twenty-Six Men and a Girl,' the novel Mother, and plays Children of the Sun and Summerfolk. His pen name means 'bitter.' Wrote an essay on the Lumière brothers and the memoir My Childhood. Exiled to Capri, later lured back to Russia by fake fan mail. His birth city (Nizhny Novgorod) was renamed after him. Stalin served as a pallbearer at his funeral.

Summary of Facts

Maxim Gorky (born Alexei Maximovich Peshkov, 1868–1936) is a Russian author considered a founder of socialist realism. His pen name means 'bitter' in Russian.

His most famous and most frequently clued work is The Lower Depths, a play depicting the residents of a run-down lodging house / boarding house basement near the Volga River. The central characters include: Luka, an idealistic old wanderer who comforts the residents and tells the Actor about a sanatorium that could cure his alcoholism; Vaska Pepel, a thief who kills his landlord Kostilyoff (encouraged by Kostilyoff's wife Vassilisa); the cardsharp Satine (Satin), who declares 'Man! It has a proud ring to it!'; and the alcoholic Actor, who claims he cannot sweep the floor because his 'organism is poisoned with alcohol.' Luka tells an anecdote about a man who hanged himself because there was no 'land of righteousness.' The play ends with the Actor hanging himself after Luka disappears, prompting Satine to mutter 'He spoiled/ruined the song!' Natasha's sister Vassilisa burns her legs by throwing boiling oil on them. Gorky was inspired to write the play by residents of the Bugrov Homeless Shelter. The play was adapted by both Jean Renoir and Akira Kurosawa, and indirectly inspired Neecha Nagar, the only Indian film to win a Cannes Grand Prix.

The short story 'Twenty-Six Men and a Girl' follows a group of 26 pretzel/kringle bakers who idolize the young woman Tanya, who visits them daily. After Tanya is seduced by a soldier, the bakers turn on her, abuse and spit at her; she calls them 'scum' and 'miserable prisoners' and leaves. The story uses a collective first-person narrator.

The Mother (1906) is considered the first major work of socialist realism. Pelagea (Nilovna) becomes radicalized through her son Pavel's revolutionary activities. Pavel is arrested at a May Day demonstration and exiled. Pelagea distributes copies of Pavel's speech and is beaten at a railway station. The novel was adapted into a 1926 film by montage theorist Vsevolod Pudovkin, considered his magnum opus.

Children of the Sun is a play set during a cholera epidemic. The chemist Protasov works on his experiments, oblivious to his best friend's attempts to woo his wife. Lisa foresees a mob coming to attack Protasov for allegedly spreading cholera. The play was written while Gorky was imprisoned; he was freed after a campaign involving Rodin, Curie, and Anatole France.

Summerfolk features a debate about whether money earned from selling factories should be enjoyed or reinvested. It ends after Ryumin's failed suicide, with Varvara leaving her husband Bassov.

Gorky's poem 'The Song of the Stormy Petrel' compared seagulls, loons, and penguins to a 'proud, black demon of the tempest' flying unafraid in a storm; the poem got him arrested and earned him the epithet 'Stormy Petrel.' He wrote an essay on the Lumière brothers' cinematograph beginning 'Last night I was in the kingdom of the shadows,' published under the pseudonym 'I. M. Pacatus.' His autobiography My Childhood recalls being beaten unconscious by his grandfather (who owned a dye shop) and frogs falling into a grave. He was exiled to Capri (where he wrote Tales of Italy) and later lured back to Russia by fake fan mail; his birth city Nizhny Novgorod was renamed after him. Lenin wrote him a letter arguing that 'god-building' and 'god-seeking' differ 'no more than a yellow devil differs from a blue devil.' Stalin personally served as a pallbearer at his funeral. Chekhov and Korolenko left the Russian Academy when Gorky's position as Academician was annulled by Tsar Nicholas II. Isaac Babel credited Gorky, saying 'I owe everything to that meeting.' Yevtushenko was kicked out of a literary institute named for Gorky. His other works include the debut play The Philistines (won the Griboyedov prize), the novel The Artamonov Business, Reminiscences of Leo Nikolaevich Tolstoy, 'Chelkash,' 'Old Woman Izergil,' and the plays Enemies and the one-act Children.
