White Noise

DeLillo's breakthrough novel, set at the College-on-the-Hill. Jack Gladney, who invented the academic field of Hitler Studies but doesn't know German, narrates. His wife Babette secretly takes the experimental drug Dylar (obtained through an affair with Willie Mink / Mr. Gray) to combat her fear of death. The novel's second part centers on the 'Airborne Toxic Event' involving the chemical Nyodene D. Jack's friend Murray Jay Siskind gives a simultaneous lecture on Elvis's mother while Jack lectures on Hitler's mother. Murray takes Jack to see the 'most photographed barn in America.' The company SIMUVAC uses real disasters to prepare for simulations. Jack eventually shoots Willie Mink, and the novel ends with Wilder Gladney crossing a highway on a tricycle. Jack's son Heinrich debates whether it's raining.

Libra

DeLillo's historical novel depicting the life of Lee Harvey Oswald leading up to the JFK assassination. The archivist Nicholas Branch reconstructs the central event. DeLillo invents the characters Win Everett and T.J. Mackey. The novel compares the Warren Commission report to Joyce's Ulysses and includes lyrical passages about the U-2 plane and Oswald's time in 'the brig' at Atsugi, Japan.

General / Biographical

Summary of Facts

Don DeLillo (b. 1936) is one of the most prominent postmodern American novelists, though he rejected the label himself. Harold Bloom placed him alongside Pynchon, Roth, and McCarthy as one of the four best living American writers.

White Noise (1985) is DeLillo's most frequently clued work. Jack Gladney, who invented the academic field of Hitler Studies at the College-on-the-Hill but cannot speak German, narrates. (His wife Babette takes the experimental drug Dylar — obtained through an affair with Willie Mink (Mr. Gray) — to combat her fear of death. Dylar side effects: users confuse words with things (saying 'speeding bullet' causes ducking); Mr. Gray curls into fetal position when Jack says 'Falling plane.'.) The novel's second section centers on the 'Airborne Toxic Event' caused by a derailed train releasing Nyodene D. Jack's friend Murray Jay Siskind delivers a simultaneous lecture on Elvis's mother while Jack lectures on Hitler's mother and wants to create a full Elvis course; they visit 'the most photographed barn in America' where 'once you've seen the signs about the barn it becomes impossible to see the barn.' SIMUVAC uses real disasters to rehearse for simulations (Baudrillard influence). Jack eventually shoots Willie Mink and takes him to a hospital run by nuns. The novel ends with Wilder Gladney crossing a highway on a tricycle. Title refers to visual TV static. The supermarket is described as timeless, full of 'Waves and Radiation.' Jack hosts the first Hitler Studies Conference. A character plans to break the world record for longest time in a cage of venomous snakes.

Underworld (1997) is DeLillo's massive Cold War novel. Its prologue, originally published as 'Pafko at the Wall' (named for Dodgers outfielder Andy Pafko backpedaling at the wall), describes the 1951 'Shot Heard Round the World' game. Jackie Gleason eats too many hot dogs and vomits on Frank Sinatra's shoes at the game; J. Edgar Hoover is fascinated by a Life magazine reprint of Bruegel's Triumph of Death. Bobby Thompson's baseball is caught by Black boy Cotter Martin, stolen by his father Manx, traced by Marvin Lundy, and eventually bought by waste management executive Nick Shay ('waste is the secret history'). Klara Sax, the artist, had an affair with teenage Nick Shay; Nick's wife Marian is unfaithful. The Texas Highway Killer shooting is recorded on video by a child; anchorwoman Sue Ann Corcoran receives an on-air call from the killer. Nick visits an art installation in the desert of 230 decommissioned Air Force planes. The graffiti artist Moonman 157 appears. The novel describes the Northeast power outage of 1965.

Libra (1988) fictionalizes Lee Harvey Oswald's life; archivist Nicholas Branch reconstructs the JFK assassination using invented characters Win Everett and T.J. Mackey. DeLillo described the Warren Commission report as 'the megaton novel that James Joyce would have written if he'd moved to Iowa City and lived to be a hundred.'

Mao II follows the reclusive author Bill Gray (assisted by Scott and Scott's lover Karen) who tries to rescue a Swiss poet from Beirut terrorists; it opens with a Unification Church mass wedding at Yankee Stadium and ends 'The future belongs to crowds.' The Names (1982) follows risk analyst John Axton in Athens investigating a cult that kills victims whose initials match town names; archaeologist Owen Brademas is his colleague; Axton's son Tap appears; Library of America republished it with Libra and White Noise in 2023. Falling Man (2007) follows Keith Neudecker surviving 9/11; he has a sexual relationship with Florence Givens while married to estranged wife Lianne; their son fears the imaginary 'Bill Lawton.' Cosmopolis follows billionaire Eric Packer (who worries about his asymmetric prostate) crossing New York in a limo for a haircut; David Cronenberg adapted it in 2012. Point Omega depicts filmmaker Jim Finley documenting Iraq War architect Richard Elster; features Psycho slowed to 24 hours. Running Dog (1978) features reporter Moll Robbins searching for a pornographic film of Adolf Hitler. The Angel Esmeralda collection includes 'Pafko at the Wall' (later Underworld's prologue), stories about nuns Edgar and Grace, orbital spies, and art museum obsession.
