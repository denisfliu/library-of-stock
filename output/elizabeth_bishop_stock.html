Elizabeth Bishop

View source questions · Make cards
20th-century American poet. Most commonly identified by the villanelle "One Art" ("the art of losing isn't hard to master"), "The Armadillo" (dedicated to Robert Lowell, fire balloons), "The Fish" (rainbow, rainbow, rainbow), and the collections Geography III and Questions of Travel. Lived in Brazil with partner Lota de Macedo Soares. Close friendship with Marianne Moore and Robert Lowell.

Summary of Facts

Elizabeth Bishop is a 20th-century American poet, most frequently clued through her villanelle "One Art," which repeats the refrain "the art of losing isn't hard to master" and catalogs progressive losses from door keys to continents before ending with the parenthetical command "(Write it!)." The poem's loss of "a continent" is thought to refer to her relationship with Brazilian architect and landscape designer Lota de Macedo Soares.

"The Armadillo," dedicated to Robert Lowell, is her second most commonly clued poem, describing "frail, illegal fire balloons" climbing a mountain near Rio de Janeiro, an owl's nest that catches fire, and the concluding image of "a weak mailed fist / clenched ignorant against the sky." Lowell's "Skunk Hour" was written in response and dedicated back to Bishop.

"The Fish" is another major poem, in which the speaker catches a fish in a "little rented boat," observes its "brown skin hung in strips / like ancient wallpaper" and "five-haired beard of wisdom," and releases it after "everything was rainbow, rainbow, rainbow!" Both Bishop and her mentor Marianne Moore wrote poems titled "The Fish."

Her collection Geography III contains several frequently clued poems: "In the Waiting Room" (a child reads National Geographic in a dentist's office with Aunt Consuelo and sees horrifying photographs), "Crusoe in England" (Crusoe remembers an island with "fifty-two miserable, small volcanoes" and mourns Friday, who died of measles), and "One Art." The collection opens with 19th-century textbook question-and-answer lessons.

"Sestina" depicts a grandmother and grandchild in a kitchen; the grandmother sings to the "Marvel Stove" while the child draws houses with crayons and an almanac says "Time to plant tears." This reflects Bishop's own upbringing by her grandmother after her mother was institutionalized. "First Death in Nova Scotia" describes a stuffed loon watching over the funeral of cousin Arthur in a "cold, cold parlor."

"The Man-Moth," whose title was inspired by a newspaper misprint of "mammoth," describes a creature who thinks the moon is a small hole in the sky, rides the subway, and drops a tear "pure enough to drink." "Filling Station" opens "Oh, but it is dirty!" and ends "Somebody loves us all," with cans spelling "ESSO-SO-SO-SO."

Biographically, Bishop grew up in Nova Scotia after her mother's institutionalization, was raised by her grandmother, attended Vassar (where she founded the journal Con Spirito and met Marianne Moore), lived in Key West with Louise Crane, and spent nearly two decades in Brazil with Lota de Macedo Soares, inspiring the collection Questions of Travel. She translated works by Carlos Drummond de Andrade, Clarice Lispector, and Octavio Paz. Her correspondence with Robert Lowell was published as Words in Air and adapted into a play by Sarah Ruhl. She rebuked Lowell for using his ex-wife Elizabeth Hardwick's letters in The Dolphin, saying "art just isn't worth that much." She also wrote "Visits to St. Elizabeths" about Ezra Pound's asylum confinement, modeled on "This is the House that Jack Built."
