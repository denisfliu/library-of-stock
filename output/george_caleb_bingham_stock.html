          Summary of Facts

          George Caleb Bingham (1811-1879) was a self-taught American luminist painter known for frontier and river scenes of 19th-century Missouri. He studied at the Dusseldorf Academy alongside Eastman Johnson.

          His most famous painting is Fur Traders Descending the Missouri, originally titled French Trader-Half Breed Son before being renamed by the American Art-Union (an organization founded in 1839 that exhibited and sold many of his works, with members including Emanuel Leutze, Asher Durand, and Thomas Cole). The painting shows a man in a Phrygian cap, a half-Native American boy leaning on a pile of pelts, and a feline-looking bear on a leash, all staring at the viewer.

          Bingham also created a political trilogy about small-town Missouri democracy: Stump Speaking, The County Election (1852), and a third painting featuring a jovial man wearing three hats. The County Election symbolizes chance through boys tossing a knife and a man flipping a coin, while a bandaged man represents underlying violence. Bingham mockingly gave E.D. Sappington, who had beaten him in an election, a shiny top hat.

          Other river scenes include The Jolly Flatboatmen (a man in a red shirt dancing on a ship), Jolly Flatboatmen in Port, Raftsmen Playing Cards, and Shooting for the Beef. His painting Order No. 11 attacks General Thomas Ewing's forced depopulation of part of Missouri, showing a family pleading with 'redleg' troops planning to burn their house. He also painted a portrait of Vinnie Ream playing a harp.
