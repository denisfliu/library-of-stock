Summary of Facts

Frank O'Hara is an American New York School poet, alongside John Ashbery, Kenneth Koch, James Schuyler, and Ted Berrigan. He worked as a curator at the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) and was labeled a "poet among painters" by biographer Marjorie Perloff.

His most famous collection, Lunch Poems, was published by City Lights Bookstore's Pocket Poets Series and written during lunch breaks at MoMA. It contains "The Day Lady Died," a poem mourning Billie Holiday's death, set on "12:20 in New York on a Friday / three days after Bastille day" where the speaker gets a shoeshine and sees Holiday's face on the New York Post, ending "everyone and I stopped breathing" while remembering her performance at the 5 Spot nightclub.

"Having a Coke with You," addressed to his life partner, dancer Vincent Warren, declares the title action "more fun than going to San Sebastian." The speaker says he "would rather look at you than all the portraits in the world / except possibly for the Polish Rider" and never thinks of Duchamp's Nude Descending a Staircase at home.

"Why I Am Not a Painter" depicts Mike Goldberg painting "SARDINES" and notes "how terrible orange is / and life." His tongue-in-cheek manifesto "Personism" proposes putting poems "between two persons rather than two pages," born from realizing he could "use the telephone instead." It asserts "only Whitman and Crane and Williams... are better than the movies" and was born after a lunch with LeRoi Jones.

Other poems include "Lana Turner has collapsed!" (telling the actress "we love you get up"), "To the Film Industry in Crisis" ("Motion Picture Industry, it's you I love!"), "Ave Maria" (asking mothers to let kids go to the movies), and "Mayakovsky" ("I am quietly waiting for the catastrophe of my personality to seem beautiful again").

O'Hara collaborated with Norman Bluhm on "poem-paintings" and with Larry Rivers on the Stones lithographs. He wrote a 1959 monograph on Jackson Pollock despite enduring Pollock's homophobic abuse, and helped bring Abstract Expressionism to Europe in the 1958 exhibition The New American Painting with Dorothy Miller. He praised close friend Willem de Kooning as Action Painting's "Delacroix" in the poem "Radio."
