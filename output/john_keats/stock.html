English Romantic poet known for his Great Odes of 1819, 'On First Looking into Chapman's Homer,' and the concept of negative capability. Died of tuberculosis in Rome at age 25.
"Ode on a Grecian Urn"

Addresses an 'Attic shape' described as a 'foster-child of Silence and slow Time' and 'unravish'd bride of quietness.' Declares 'Beauty is truth, truth beauty — that is all / Ye know on earth, and all ye need to know.' 'Heard melodies are sweet, but those unheard are sweeter.' 'More happy love! more happy, happy love!' 'What pipes and timbrels? What wild ecstasy?' An ekphrastic poem. Referenced in Pale Fire as 'Red Sox Win 5-4 on Chapman's Homer.' Tony Harrison's 'A Kumquat for John Keats' references it.

"Ode to a Nightingale"

Speaker desires to 'die upon the midnight with no pain.' Asks 'Do I wake or sleep?' after declaring 'thou wast not born for death, immortal Bird!' The word 'forlorn' tolls the speaker 'back from thee to my sole self.' 'Fled is that music.' Flying 'on the viewless wings of poesy.' Earl Wasserman critiqued it using Keats's 'Mansion of Many Apartments' metaphor. Melody transforms into a 'plaintive anthem.' 'Where youth grows pale, and spectre-thin, and dies.'

"La Belle Dame Sans Merci"

A ballad beginning 'O what can ail thee, knight-at-arms / Alone and palely loitering?' A haggard knight by a lake 'where the sedge has wither'd and no birds sing' recounts a 'fairy's child' who gives him 'roots of relish sweet, / And honey wild, and manna-dew.' Kings, princes, and warriors appear 'death-pale.' Frank Dicksee and John William Waterhouse painted scenes from it.

"The Eve of St. Agnes"

Madeline falls asleep on the title night to see her future husband in a dream. Porphyro lays out 'candied apple, quince and plum and gourd' as she sleeps. Old nurse Angela and the Beadsman die the same night Porphyro and Madeline elope in a storm. Begins and ends with references to the Beadsman. Porphyro picks up a lute and recites Alain Chartier's La Belle Dame sans Mercy. Millais and Holman Hunt painted scenes.

"To Autumn"

'Season of mists and mellow fruitfulness.' Sun conspires with Autumn to 'bless with fruit the vines.' 'Where are the songs of spring? Ay, where are they?' — ubi sunt formula. Ends with swallows 'twitter[ing] in the skies.' Keats abandoned Hyperion and wrote 'To Autumn' on the same day.

Hyperion and Endymion

Hyperion is an unfinished epic about the fall of the Titans, abandoned because of 'too many Miltonic inversions.' Oceanus cites 'eternal law' that 'first in beauty should be first in might.' Hyperion asks 'Saturn is fallen, am I too to fall?' Endymion opens 'A thing of beauty is a joy forever' and includes a 'Hymn to Pan' that Wordsworth called 'a very pretty piece of paganism.' Cynthia is Endymion's lover.

Letters and Negative Capability

Keats's letters, which T. S. Eliot called 'the most notable ever written by any English poet,' contain the concept of negative capability: 'being in uncertainties, Mysteries, doubts, without any irritable reaching after fact and reason.' Also contain: the 'Mansion of Many Apartments' metaphor (life as rooms), the 'vale of soul-making' (borrowed by John Hick for his theodicy), the 'chameleon Poet' (no self), 'the sense of Beauty overcomes every other consideration.' He annotated Paradise Lost and wrote 'Lines on Seeing a Lock of Milton's Hair.' At Benjamin Haydon's 'immortal dinner,' Keats said Newton 'destroyed all the poetry of the rainbow' (inspiring Dawkins's Unweaving the Rainbow). Walter Jackson Bate wrote the premier biography. Keats names a heuristic for the rhyme-as-reason effect. Countee Cullen dedicated a poem to him 'At Spring Time.'

Summary of Facts

John Keats is one of the most frequently clued British Romantic poets. His 'Ode on a Grecian Urn' is the primary giveaway, declaring 'Beauty is truth, truth beauty' about an 'Attic shape' described as a 'foster-child of Silence and slow Time.' 'Ode to a Nightingale' features the desire to 'die upon the midnight with no pain' and asks 'Do I wake or sleep?' 'On First Looking into Chapman's Homer' opens 'Much have I travell'd in the realms of gold' and compares the experience to 'stout Cortez' staring at the Pacific.

Other major poems include 'When I Have Fears' (anticipating death), 'La Belle Dame Sans Merci' (a knight 'alone and palely loitering'), 'The Eve of St. Agnes' (Porphyro and Madeline), 'To Autumn' ('Season of mists and mellow fruitfulness'), and the unfinished Hyperion (abandoned for 'too many Miltonic inversions'). Endymion opens 'A thing of beauty is a joy forever.'

Keats's letters, called by Eliot 'the most notable ever written by any English poet,' contain the concept of negative capability ('being in uncertainties without irritable reaching after fact and reason') and the 'Mansion of Many Apartments' metaphor. He died of tuberculosis in Rome at 25; his epitaph reads 'Here lies One whose Name was writ in Water.' Shelley's elegy Adonais commemorates his death, with his soul 'beaconing from the abode where the Eternal are.' Byron dismissed his death as being 'snuffed out by an article.'
