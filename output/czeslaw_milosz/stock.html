Summary of Facts

Czeslaw Milosz is a Polish Nobel Prize-winning poet and essayist, best known for his nonfiction work The Captive Mind and his poetry. He won the 1980 Nobel Prize in Literature.

The Captive Mind is Milosz's most frequently clued work. It examines why Stalinism and Marxism appeal to intellectuals, analyzing four Polish writers under the pseudonyms Alpha (the Moralist), Beta (the Disappointed Lover), Gamma, and Delta (the Troubadour). The book outlines seven forms of 'ketman' -- a concept describing how dissidents silently protest by deceitfully accepting communism in public. The first section uses the 'Murti Bing pills' from Stanislaw Witkiewicz's novel Insatiability as a metaphor for dogmatic thought, comparing the novel's plot to the effects of Marxism-Leninism. Beta represents the concentration camp writer Tadeusz Borowski, whose autobiographical stories set in Auschwitz are quoted in the chapter 'Beta, the Disappointed Lover.' Milosz wrote The Captive Mind while in political exile from Poland.

'A Song on the End of the World' is Milosz's most clued individual poem. It describes a fisherman mending 'a glimmering net,' a bee circling a clover, and a 'white-haired old man who would be a prophet / Yet is not a prophet' who is the only one who believes the end of the world 'is happening now.' The poem closes with the repeated line 'There will be no other end of the world.' Those who expect 'lightning and thunder' will be disappointed.

Milosz's poem 'Campo dei Fiori' begins 'In Rome on the Campo dei Fiori / baskets of olives and lemons.' His 'Ars Poetica?' suggests that 'poems should be written rarely and reluctantly, / under unbearable duress' and states 'I have always aspired to a more spacious form.' He references Horace's treatise of the same name. 'Account' opens 'The History of my stupidity would fill many volumes.' 'Late Ripeness' describes 'former lives departing, like ships, together with their sorrow.' 'You Who Wronged' warns 'Do not feel safe. The Poet remembers. / You can kill one, but another is born.' An ekphrastic poem titled 'Realism' describes Bosch's Garden of Earthly Delights, conjecturing that 'mankind exists / To provision and populate Hell.' Another poem describes 'trees outside the window, which probably exist' and opens 'We are not so badly off, if we can / Admire Dutch painting.'

A Treatise on Poetry is a book-length poem that surveys Polish literature and destruction in the first half of the 20th century, culminating in the Warsaw Uprising. Its four books detail the literary life and history of Poland, with the last section set in Pennsylvania. Peter Dale Scott's Ecstatic Pessimist discusses its 'Hegelianism.' Milosz also wrote The Treatise on Morality, which ends with the line 'Before us is The Heart of Darkness.'

Milosz's early mentorship by his cousin Oscar (the Lithuanian poet Oscar Milosz) led to the 'catastrophist' outlook he championed in the Zagary group. His nonfiction includes Emperor of the Earth, which examines 'modes of eccentric vision' and contains an essay on Lev Shestov's 'purity of despair' and one on Vladimir Solovyov's writings. He edited the international poetry anthology A Book of Luminous Things, which includes translations of Anna Swir and Adam Zagajewski's 'Auto Mirror.' His 1995 collection Facing the River and the collection Unattainable Earth were translated with his frequent collaborator Robert Hass, who championed him while they were both at UC Berkeley. Seamus Heaney was heavily influenced by Milosz and read a poem at his funeral. Witold Gombrowicz criticized and later praised The Captive Mind in his Diary.
