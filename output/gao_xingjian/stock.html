Chinese-born Nobel laureate in Literature (2000), the first to write primarily in Chinese. Known for the absurdist play Bus Stop and the novel Soul Mountain, both heavily clued in quizbowl. Also wrote the play The Other Shore, the novel One Man's Bible, and the story collection Buying a Fishing Rod for My Grandfather. Exiled from China to France after his works were banned; self-described 'nothingist' who advocates 'cold literature.' Also a translator of Samuel Beckett and a visual artist known for ink landscapes.
Bus Stop

Gao's most frequently clued play, an absurdist work about characters waiting at a bus stop that turns out to be no longer operational. Characters include Hothead, Glasses, the Old Man, the Carpenter, Girl, Mother, and the Silent Man. The set consists of two iron railings arranged in a cross shape. Glasses checks his watch and discovers that ten years have passed. The play was deemed 'Spiritual Pollution' upon release, was banned in China, and was only performed 13 times in its original run. It shares its English title with a William Inge play set in rural Kansas. The script includes 'polyphonic dialogue' in which characters speak simultaneously. In its original run, every production was preceded by a performance of Lu Xun's The Passerby.

Soul Mountain

Gao's most famous novel and the work most associated with his Nobel Prize. The novel alternates chapters between narrators called 'I' and 'You' (and sometimes 'She' and 'He'), traveling through rural southwestern China — particularly Sichuan province — after 'I' receives a false lung cancer diagnosis. 'You' searches for the mythical title location, Lingshan. Along the way, the narrators hear folk songs from ethnic minorities like the Miao and Yi, encounter legends of sasquatch-like 'Wild Men,' visit a giant panda sanctuary, and meet a frog incarnation of God whose unblinking eye appears in the novel's final chapter. The novel ends with the narrator confronting that tiny frog. It exemplifies Gao's concept of 'cold literature' and was translated into English by Mabel Lee. Gao's most widely viewed painting is on the international cover.

The Other Shore

A play that opens with twelve actors performing a 'rope game' demonstrating the nature of complex relationships, in which the Lead Actor guides the others across a river. A Woman emerges from Oblivion and teaches the Crowd how to use language, but the newly empowered Crowd turns against her and murders her. A thirteenth-generation Dogskin Plaster Seller appears shortly after. A mischievous Card Player rigs a game and sticks slips of paper to the faces of losers. The play's opening stage directions note it occurs on 'the nonexistent other shore,' and it can be performed at 'the hall of a temple, a circus tent, or any empty space.' The title location is mentioned in the Heart Sutra.

Buying a Fishing Rod for My Grandfather

Other Works and Plays

Gao also wrote plays in French, including Between Life and Death and Weekend Quartet. He wrote Absolute Signal (also known as The Alarm Signal), in which a man called Trumpet carries a trumpet while working as an assistant train conductor. He created a 'dance-drama' based on Li Qingzhao's poem 'Shengshengman,' blending music, poetry, and dance. He wrote an essay analyzing 'National Character in Fiction,' comparing 'linked chapter' and 'direct portrayal' forms of prose.

General / Biographical

Gao Xingjian (born 1940) is a Chinese-born author who won the 2000 Nobel Prize in Literature, the first laureate to write primarily in Chinese. He was exiled to France after his works were banned in China, and has since received French citizenship. He is a self-described 'nothingist' who proclaimed he lives 'Without isms' in a 1996 essay. He advocates 'cold literature,' arguing that 'literature has no duty to the masses.' He is a notable translator of Samuel Beckett's works into Chinese. He also creates minimalistic ink landscape paintings that provide the covers of many of his own books. His first encounter with Western art was a 1980 trip where he worked as a French interpreter for the author of The Family (Ba Jin), whose protagonists share Gao's surname.

Summary of Facts

Gao Xingjian is one of the most frequently clued Chinese authors in quizbowl, appearing as an answerline and in mentions across a wide range of tournaments. His two most prominent works are the play Bus Stop and the novel Soul Mountain, both of which appear in virtually every tossup written about him.

Bus Stop is an absurdist play whose characters — Hothead, Glasses, the Old Man, the Carpenter, Girl, Mother, and the Silent Man — wait at a bus stop that is no longer operational. The most reliable giveaway clue is that Glasses checks his watch and discovers ten years have passed. Power-level clues include the set design (two iron railings in a cross), the polyphonic dialogue, the fact that it was deemed 'Spiritual Pollution' and banned after only 13 performances, Hothead wanting to buy yogurt, the Old Man's chess match with Li Mosheng, and the play's connection to Lu Xun's The Passerby. Bus Stop is frequently paired with William Inge's identically titled play.

Soul Mountain is Gao's most famous novel, structured around alternating chapters narrated by 'I' and 'You' who travel through rural Sichuan province after 'I' receives a false lung cancer diagnosis. The search for Lingshan (Soul Mountain) is the novel's central quest. Distinctive clues include the frog incarnation of God, the 'Wild Men' (sasquatch-like creatures), folk songs of the Miao and Yi minorities, the tablet reading 'history is a lie,' an old man's line 'the road is not wrong, it is the traveller who is wrong,' and an 'optional' chapter where a critic accuses the author of nihilism. The novel exemplifies Gao's 'cold literature' philosophy and was translated by Mabel Lee.

The Other Shore is Gao's third most important play, consistently referenced alongside Bus Stop. It opens with a 'rope game' among twelve actors, features a Woman who teaches the Crowd language only to be killed by them, and includes a Card Player who rigs a game and sticks paper to losers' faces. The play can be performed in any empty space, and its title references the Heart Sutra.

One Man's Bible is a semi-autobiographical novel set in Hong Kong, featuring the protagonist Margarethe and juxtaposing sexual encounters with Cultural Revolution scenes. It shares thematic ground with Soul Mountain.

Buying a Fishing Rod for My Grandfather is a short story collection that provides several power clues: the title story's narrator buys a fiberglass fishing rod and searches for vanished stone bridges; 'The Temple' features a honeymooning couple at the Temple of Perfect Benevolence; 'The Cramp' involves a girl on crutches; and 'The Accident' depicts a fatal bicycle crash.

Biographically, Gao is identified as a Chinese Nobel laureate (2000, the first to write primarily in Chinese), an exile to France, a translator of Beckett, a 'nothingist,' an advocate of 'cold literature,' and a painter of minimalistic ink landscapes. His works were banned in China for references to Tiananmen and criticism of the Communist Party.
