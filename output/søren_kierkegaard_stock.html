Danish existentialist philosopher and theologian, considered the 'father of Existentialism.' Known for writing Either/Or and Fear and Trembling, using pseudonyms extensively, arguing that 'subjectivity is truth,' and popularizing the 'leap of faith.' His works explore the aesthetic, ethical, and religious modes of existence.
Either/Or

Kierkegaard's major work (1843), compiled under the pseudonym 'Victor Eremita,' structured around two fictional authors: the anonymous 'A' (representing the aesthetic life) and Judge Wilhelm (representing the ethical life). It opens with the 'Diapsalmata' section, which cites Spinoza. The work contains 'The Seducer's Diary' (in which Johannes pursues a woman named Cordelia), a section comparing aesthetes' constant change to 'crop rotation,' and essays criticizing Hegel's reading of Antigone ('The Tragic in Ancient Drama') and analyzing Don Giovanni ('The Immediate Erotic Stages'). Don Juan is cited as the aesthetic mode taken to an unhealthy extreme. The ethical sphere involves repetition contrasted with recollection.

General / Biographical

Kierkegaard is identified as a Danish philosopher, the 'father of Existentialism,' and a Christian existentialist. He wrote extensively using pseudonyms. His journals reference the 'Great Earthquake' of discovering his father once cursed God, which he blamed for the deaths of five siblings. He processed his breakup with Regine Olsen through writing (a book titled from Psalm 55). He attacked established Christianity ('Christendom') in pamphlets comparing churches to disease-ridden hospitals. Karl Barth quoted his 'infinite qualitative distinction' between God and Man. He admired Socrates as a 'noble, simple wise man.' Gilles Deleuze references his 1843 book Repetition in Difference and Repetition. He also wrote 'From the Papers of One Still Living,' a review accusing Hans Christian Andersen of lacking 'any philosophy of life.' He authored The Concept of Anxiety and Three Upbuilding Discourses.

Summary of Facts

Soren Kierkegaard is a Danish philosopher widely considered the 'father of Existentialism' and a forerunner of the existentialist movement. He is a Christian existentialist who wrote extensively using pseudonyms and is most commonly identified by his works Either/Or and Fear and Trembling.

Either/Or (1843) is Kierkegaard's major work, compiled under the pseudonym 'Victor Eremita.' It is structured around two fictional authors: the anonymous 'A' (representing the aesthetic life) and Judge Wilhelm (representing the ethical life). The work opens with the 'Diapsalmata' section, which cites Spinoza ('I am continually aeterno modo'). Key sections include 'The Seducer's Diary,' in which a character named Johannes becomes obsessed with and pursues a woman named Cordelia, befriending a boy named Edward as part of his scheme. The famous 'crop rotation' section compares aesthetes' constant change of activity to avoid boredom with the pastoral practice. The essay 'The Tragic in Ancient Drama Reflected in the Tragic in Modern Drama' criticizes Hegel's reading of Antigone, while 'The Immediate Erotic Stages' analyzes Don Giovanni and criticizes Hegelian aesthetics. Don Juan is cited as the aesthetic mode taken to an unhealthy extreme. In the ethical sphere, repetition is contrasted with recollection.

Fear and Trembling (1843) explores the sacrifice of Isaac by Abraham and popularized the metaphor of the 'leap of faith.' It introduces the concept of the 'teleological suspension of the ethical,' comparing the 'Knight of Infinite Resignation' with the 'Knight of Faith.' The work also compares the ethical dilemmas facing Agamemnon and Abraham.

The Concluding Unscientific Postscript to Philosophical Fragments, written under the pseudonym 'Johannes Climacus,' argues that 'subjectivity is truth' and distinguishes between Religiousness A and the paradoxical Religiousness B. It pays homage to the Orthodox saint John of the Ladder (Climacus). The author famously 'revokes' the entire work after 500-plus pages, and it describes the failure to produce advice 'essentially relating to existence' as 'the scandal of modern philosophy.' A memorable passage illustrates subjectivity with the story of an escaped mental patient who tries to avoid detection by saying the objectively true fact 'Bang, the world is round!'

Stages on Life's Way adds the religious mode to the aesthetic and ethical modes already explored in Either/Or. Victor Eremita gives a speech about how women awaken the consciousness of immortality in men at a banquet similar to Plato's Symposium. Kierkegaard wrote 'purity of heart is to will one thing' in this work.

Kierkegaard's master's thesis, The Concept of Irony, analyzes irony 'with Continual Reference to Socrates,' building on Hegel's discussion and defining Socratic irony as 'infinite absolute negativity.'

Biographically, Kierkegaard's journals reference the 'Great Earthquake' of discovering his father had once cursed God, which he blamed for the deaths of five siblings. His breakup with Regine Olsen influenced his writing. He attacked established Christianity, which he called 'Christendom,' in pamphlets comparing churches to disease-ridden hospitals. Karl Barth quoted his formulation of the 'infinite qualitative distinction' between God and Man. He admired Socrates as a 'noble, simple wise man.' His early work 'From the Papers of One Still Living' reviewed Hans Christian Andersen, accusing him of 'completely' lacking 'any philosophy of life.' Gilles Deleuze references Kierkegaard's 1843 Repetition in Difference and Repetition.
