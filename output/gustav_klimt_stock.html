Gustav Klimt

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Austrian Symbolist painter and founding member of the Vienna Secession. Known for The Kiss (gold leaf, inspired by Ravenna mosaics), Beethoven Frieze (Typhoeus, Gorgons, Ode to Joy), Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I (Woman in Gold, stolen by Nazis), and Danae. Characteristic 'golden phase' inspired by Byzantine mosaics in Ravenna. Egon Schiele was his protege; both died in 1918. Other works include Stoclet Frieze, Pallas Athene, Judith, Water Serpents, and Goldfish (To My Critics).

Summary of Facts

Gustav Klimt (1862-1918) was an Austrian Symbolist painter and the founding member of the Vienna Secession, most commonly identified by The Kiss and the Beethoven Frieze. His 'golden phase,' inspired by Byzantine mosaics in Ravenna and Venice, featured extensive use of gold leaf. He died in 1918, the same year as his protege Egon Schiele.

The Kiss depicts a couple in golden robes embracing on a flowered cliff, with the man's garment decorated with rectangles and the woman's with circles. It was inspired by Byzantine mosaics at the Basilica San Vitale in Ravenna and shares the same pose as a painting by Francesco Hayez. It is the highlight of the Belvedere Palace and was initially sold for 25,000 crowns. It is the last piece of his 'Golden Period.'

The Beethoven Frieze is a massive work (7 feet tall, 112 feet wide) covering three walls of the Secession Building, depicting Beethoven's Ninth Symphony. The 'Yearning for Happiness' section shows Ambition (woman holding a wreath) and Compassion flanking a knight in golden armor. The 'Hostile Forces' section features the ape-like giant Typhoeus and his three Gorgon daughters. The final section shows an embracing couple before a chorus singing Ode to Joy. It was created for the 14th Vienna Secessionist exhibition alongside Max Klinger's bare-chested Beethoven sculpture. The Secession Building was financed by Karl Wittgenstein and is featured on the Austrian 50-cent coin.

Klimt painted two portraits of Adele Bloch-Bauer. The first (1907), also called 'Woman in Gold,' exemplifies his gold phase with a dress decorated with eyes, inspired by a mosaic of Empress Theodora at San Vitale in Ravenna. Critics punned it had 'more brass than Bloch-Bauer.' It was stolen by the Nazis and reclaimed by Maria Altmann. Ronald Lauder paid $130 million for it for the Neue Galerie. The second portrait is at MoMA. Adele was also the model for Klimt's eroticized 1901 Judith holding the head of Holofernes.

Danae shows a nude red-haired woman curled in the fetal position, draped in a purple veil, impregnated by Zeus's golden rain, and is his only mythological subject besides Pallas Athene (gold breastplate with Medusa head sticking out its tongue).

The Stoclet Frieze for the Palais Stoclet in Brussels (Josef Hoffmann's most famous building) features the Tree of Life with spiral motifs and panels The Expectation (eyes in Egyptian style) and The Embrace. The building has a hexagonal-patterned marble facade with a tower topped by a bronze rose bush and four nudes by Franz Metzner.

His University of Vienna ceiling paintings (Philosophy, Medicine, Jurisprudence) caused scandal and were destroyed by fire at Schloss Immendorf. He painted Goldfish (To My Critics), showing a woman baring her bottom, as a response to the university rejection. Schubert at the Piano, commissioned by Nikolaus Dumba, was also destroyed. Lady with a Fan was his 'last masterpiece,' completed the year he died.

Klimt mentored Egon Schiele and Oskar Kokoschka. Other works include Water Serpents (gold threads around two naked women), Hope I and II (pregnant model Herma), Three Ages of Woman, Birch Forest, The Park (canopy of colored dots), square-shaped landscapes of Lake Attersee, and a portrait of partner Emilie Floge. The Secession's motto was 'To every age its art, to every art its freedom.' Ver Sacrum was the Secession's magazine. The Secession Building was designed by Joseph Maria Olbrich, nicknamed the 'Golden Cabbage,' with owl decorations by Koloman Moser, who co-founded the movement. Roses Under the Trees was restituted to the Stiasny family in 2022.
