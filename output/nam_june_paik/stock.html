Korean-American founder of video art and member of the Fluxus movement. Known for installations using CRT televisions and video cameras. Collaborated frequently with cellist Charlotte Moorman. Major works include Electronic Superhighway, TV Buddha, TV Cello, and Good Morning, Mr. Orwell. Coined the term 'electronic superhighway' in a 1974 proposal.
Electronic Superhighway: Continental U.S., Alaska, Hawaii (1995)

A large-scale video installation depicting a map of the United States made from approximately 300 CRT television screens and neon tubing. Scenes from The Wizard of Oz play on the Kansas section. Alaska and Hawaii are depicted with neon lights. Currently at the Smithsonian American Art Museum. Wikipedia

Good Morning, Mr. Orwell (1984)

A live satellite broadcast / video installation aired on New Year's Day, January 1, 1984 — in ironic response to Orwell's dystopian year. Hosted by journalist George Plimpton. Featured Merce Cunningham dancing with time-delayed images of himself, John Cage improvising, Thompson Twins performing 'Hold Me Now', and Laurie Anderson and Peter Gabriel performing the title song. Broadcast from Centre Pompidou in Paris and WNET in New York; a satellite link disruption marred a planned 'space yodel.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Nam June Paik is identified in quizbowl as the Korean-American founder and father of video art, and a prominent member of the Fluxus avant-garde movement. His work centered on the idea that televisions would link the world together, and he was among the first artists to use CRT television sets and video cameras as artistic media.

His most-clued work is Electronic Superhighway: Continental U.S., Alaska, Hawaii, a sprawling map of the United States constructed from approximately 300 television screens and neon tubing. The Kansas segment notably plays scenes from The Wizard of Oz. The installation's title derives from a 1974 report Paik wrote called 'Media Planning for the Postindustrial Society,' in which he coined the phrase 'electronic superhighway' and envisioned a future broadband communication network incorporating 'audio cassettes, telex, data pooling' and other technologies — a vision he set '26 years from now.'

TV Buddha (1974) is another signature piece: a Buddha statue placed before a closed-circuit television that records and displays its own live image, so that the statue appears to watch itself. Paik also created installations called Portable God and TV Buddha that explored Buddhist themes and religious iconography.

Paik's most famous collaborative works involve cellist Charlotte Moorman, whom he described as his assistant. He constructed TV Cello — a playable instrument made from stacked television sets — for Moorman to perform on. He also designed the TV Bra for Living Sculpture, a brassiere whose cups are small TV screens, worn by Moorman during performances.

Good Morning, Mr. Orwell was a live satellite broadcast aired on New Year's Day, January 1, 1984 — a deliberate ironic response to Orwell's dystopian year. The event was hosted by journalist George Plimpton and featured Merce Cunningham dancing with time-delayed images of himself, John Cage and Merce Cunningham improvising over a Salvador Dalí poem recording, Thompson Twins performing 'Hold Me Now,' and Laurie Anderson and Peter Gabriel performing the title song. Problems with a satellite link to France disrupted a 'space yodel' Paik had planned for the event.

As a Fluxus member, Paik created a series of 'Danger Music' pieces and 'Playable Pieces' with extreme instructions. His Danger Music for Dick Higgins instructs the performer to 'crawl into the womb of a living whale.' Another Playable Piece instructs the performer to 'cut your left forearm a distance of ten centimeters' — this was performed by composer Joseph Byrd. He also proposed that an 'acoustic space' could be created by placing 'all the politicians from the world inside a circle' and activating '23 machine guns at the same time.'

Biographically, Paik is noted for having once leapt off a stage to cut the necktie of composer John Cage (a significant Fluxus connection). He placed magnets on top of television sets to generate abstract distorted forms. His breakthrough came when he used a newly purchased Sony Portapak to film Pope Paul VI's arrival in New York City. His wife was artist Shigeko Kubota, who created documentary and sculptural homages to Marcel Duchamp. A Guggenheim retrospective featured his work with Charles Falco as technical advisor. He also created the installation Media Shuttle.
