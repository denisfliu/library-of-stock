Italian Baroque painter from Bologna, leader of the Bolognese school alongside cousin Ludovico and brother Agostino. Known for the Farnese Gallery ceiling frescoes (The Loves of the Gods), The Butcher's Shop, The Beaneater, and Landscape with the Flight into Egypt. Founded the Accademia dei Desiderosi. Often contrasted with Caravaggio. Teacher of Guido Reni.
The Loves of the Gods (Farnese Gallery ceiling)

Fresco cycle in the Farnese Gallery (Palazzo Farnese) depicting scenes from Ovid. An early example of illusionistic quadri riportati technique. Center panel is The Triumph of Bacchus and Ariadne. Also painted The Choice of Hercules for the camerino of the same building. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Annibale Carracci (1560-1609) was an Italian Baroque painter from Bologna who led the Bolognese school alongside his cousin Ludovico and brother Agostino. The Carracci family founded the Accademia dei Desiderosi (Academy of the Desirous) in Bologna. Annibale is often contrasted with Caravaggio: the Baroque critic Giovanni Bellori praised Carracci while being less enthusiastic about Caravaggio's realism. John Ruskin notoriously called the Carracci 'the scum of Titian.' Annibale was also the teacher of Guido Reni.

His most frequently clued work is the Loves of the Gods (frequency 5, giveaway), a fresco cycle on the ceiling of the Farnese Gallery in the Palazzo Farnese depicting scenes from Ovid's Metamorphoses. This cycle is notable as an early example of the illusionistic quadri riportati technique (frequency 2), and its center panel is The Triumph of Bacchus and Ariadne. He also painted The Choice of Hercules for the camerino of the same building, a subject also treated by Paolo Veronese. According to biographer Filippo Baldinucci, Bernini was inspired as a young boy to create the St. Peter's Baldachin to fulfill a prophecy by Annibale Carracci.

Carracci's early genre paintings are also heavily clued. The Butcher's Shop (frequency 4) depicts men posed in front of slabs of meat and is grouped with The Beaneater as early examples of genre painting in Italy. Two versions exist: one at Oxford's Christ Church Picture Gallery and another at the Kimbell Art Museum. One version was originally attributed to brother Agostino. The painting may have been made for a Beccai guild sign. Specific details include a man in a red shirt crouching next to a lamb in a pose borrowed from Raphael's Sacrifice of Noah, and a man in a feathered hat with a halberd and codpiece that may be a parody of Mannerism. C. D. Dickerson examined the work in his book Raw Painting. His 1580 painting A Boy Drinking was stolen from Christ Church Picture Gallery in 2020.

His Landscape with the Flight into Egypt (frequency 3, giveaway) is a semicircular lunette painting in the Doria Pamphilj gallery, commissioned by Pietro Aldobrandini. It features an ideal classical landscape with the central group walking on the banks of a river, a ferryman crossing, and a red cloak draped over the back of a donkey.

Carracci is credited with inventing the caricature, saying the task was exactly the same as for the classical artist. A member of the family famously called Vasari a 'shitface' in the margins of his copy of the Lives. His brother Agostino created the only surviving copy of I Modi, erotic engravings by Marcantonio Raimondi. William Hogarth praised Carracci's Christ and the Samaritan Woman as an example of properly illustrating attitudes. Gentileschi's Susanna borrowed a pose from a Roman sarcophagus while eschewing the lush garden in Carracci's version (per Mary Garrard). Other works include Venus, Adonis, and Cupid; Two Children Teasing a Cat; Domine, Quo Vadis?; and St. John the Baptist Bearing Witness.
