Ancient Greek philosopher, founder of the Lyceum and Peripatetic School, student of Plato, teacher of Alexander the Great. Known as 'the First Teacher' in Islamic philosophy. Author of the Metaphysics, Nicomachean Ethics, Physics, Politics, Poetics, De Anima, and the Organon. Associated with hylomorphism, four causes, the unmoved mover, eudaimonia, virtue ethics, and the doctrine of the mean.
Metaphysics and 'Being qua Being'

The Metaphysics is Aristotle's most frequently clued single work. It concerns 'being qua being' and 'first philosophy.' Key clues include the unmoved mover (a 'thought thinking about thinking'), the four causes (formal, efficient, material, final), the concepts of potentiality and actuality, and the opening line 'All men by nature desire to know.' Book Zeta asks 'what is substance?' Book Twelve discusses 47 or 55 gods needed for heavenly motions. The Metaphysics was enormously influential in Islamic philosophy: Avicenna read it 40 times, al-Farabi wrote a commentary on its 'Purposes,' al-Kindi used its infinite regress argument, and Averroes wrote the famous 'Long Commentary.'

Nicomachean Ethics and Virtue Ethics

The Nicomachean Ethics, named for Aristotle's son, is the founding text of virtue ethics. Eudaimonia (happiness/flourishing) is identified as the highest good. The Doctrine of the Mean holds each virtue lies between two extremes (e.g., courage between cowardice and rashness). Phronesis (practical wisdom) helps cultivate hexis (dispositions). The 'function argument' identifies the 'rational principle' as uniquely human. Book IX divides friendships into utility, pleasure, and goodness. Megalopsuchia ('great-souledness') includes a slow gait and deep voice. The work endorses Socrates's claim that no one knowingly chooses evil (akrasia). Alasdair MacIntyre's After Virtue and Anscombe's 'Modern Moral Philosophy' revived virtue ethics based on this work.

Physics and the Four Causes

Aristotle's Physics discusses change, motion, and nature. It introduces the four causes using the example of an architect designing a house (or a bronze statue). Time and space are 'continuous' because indefinitely divisible. The work refutes Zeno's paradoxes and argues that the impossibility of actual infinities means change must have begun with a Prime Mover. Aristotle rejected actual infinites while allowing potential infinites by division. Whitehead's process philosophy updates the four causes.

Pseudo-Aristotelian Works and Islamic Reception

A major clue cluster concerns works falsely attributed to Aristotle. The Theology of Aristotle is actually a paraphrase of Plotinus's Enneads. The Secretum Secretorum (framed as a letter to Alexander the Great) influenced Roger Bacon. The Book of Causes was shown by Aquinas to derive from Proclus's Elements of Theology. 'Aristotle's Masterpiece' was a popular sex manual. The 'recovery' of Aristotle's works in medieval Europe resulted in many false attributions. Aristotle was called 'the First Teacher'; al-Farabi was 'the Second Teacher'; Averroes was 'the Commentator.' Boethius's translation of Porphyry's Isagoge was an introduction to Aristotle's Categories.

Politics

The Politics argues that 'man is by nature a political animal.' It uses the analogy of a hand detached from the body to show humans are naturally part of a polis. The work controversially claims slaves lack the deliberative element while women possess it but it 'lacks authority.' It includes a sixfold classification of constitutions. Joan Tronto and Hannah Arendt engage with its distinction between public (polis) and private (oikos). Aristotle argues citizenship should be extended to those who hold public office.

Poetics and De Anima

The Poetics concerns tragedy and introduces mimesis (imitation) and catharsis (purification/purgation). Poets are generally better at desis than lusis. A suffering animal illustrates 'tragic pleasure.' De Anima divides mental capacities into nutritive, perceptual/sensitive, and intellectual/rational souls. Book III introduces active vs. passive intellect (nous poietikos vs. nous pathetikos), which became a major debate in medieval Arabic philosophy. The concept of tabula rasa originates here.

General / Biographical

General identification: student of Plato, founder of the Lyceum, teacher of Alexander the Great. Works cited with Bekker numbers; standard English edition edited by Jonathan Barnes. The Organon collects his logical works. Introduced the syllogism. His 'future contingents' problem uses the sea battle example (Elizabeth Anscombe wrote 'Aristotle and the Sea Battle'). James of Venice's translation demarcates logica nova vs. logica vetus. Aquinas reconciled Aristotle with Christianity. Duns Scotus and Aquinas influenced by hylomorphism and four causes. William of Ockham reduced Aristotle's ten categories to substance and quality.

Summary of Facts

Aristotle is one of the most broadly clued philosophers in quizbowl, with clues spanning his own works, pseudo-Aristotelian texts, and his massive influence on Islamic and medieval European philosophy. The Metaphysics is the most frequently clued individual work, concerning 'being qua being' with key concepts including the unmoved mover (a 'thought thinking about thinking'), the four causes, and potentiality and actuality. Its reception in the Islamic world is a major clue cluster: Avicenna famously read it 40 times, al-Farabi was called 'the Second Teacher' for his commentary, Averroes's Long Commentary introduced 'monopsychicism,' and al-Kindi used its infinite regress argument.

The Nicomachean Ethics, named for Aristotle's son, is the founding text of virtue ethics. It identifies eudaimonia as the highest good and proposes the Doctrine of the Mean. Phronesis and the 'function argument' are frequently clued. The modern revival of virtue ethics by MacIntyre (After Virtue) and Anscombe ('Modern Moral Philosophy') draws heavily on this work.

The Physics introduces the four causes (often illustrated with an architect/house or bronze statue), refutes Zeno's paradoxes, and argues for the Prime Mover. Hylomorphism (form and matter) is central. The Politics is known for 'man is by nature a political animal,' its controversial views on slaves and women, and its sixfold classification of constitutions.

Pseudo-Aristotelian works form a distinct clue cluster. The Theology of Aristotle is actually Plotinus. The Secretum Secretorum influenced Roger Bacon. The Book of Causes derives from Proclus. 'Aristotle's Masterpiece' was a sex manual. Porphyry's Isagoge, an introduction to the Categories, was translated by Boethius and became a medieval logic standard.

The Poetics introduces mimesis and catharsis. De Anima divides the soul into nutritive, sensitive, and rational components, with the active/passive intellect distinction becoming central to medieval Arabic debates. Aristotle introduced the syllogism, the problem of future contingents (sea battle example), and his works are cited using Bekker numbers.
