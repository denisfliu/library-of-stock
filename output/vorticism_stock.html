Summary of Facts

Vorticism is a British pre-WWI modernist art and literary movement spearheaded by Wyndham Lewis. The movement was announced through the magazine BLAST, which ran for only two issues. Its manifesto, beginning "Beyond Action and Reaction we would establish ourselves," lists things to be "blasted" (such as England's climate "for its sins and infections") and "blessed" (such as hairdressers and mariners, since they fight nature). The magazine also called English humour "the great barbarous weapon of the genius among races."

Ezra Pound named the movement and defined the vortex as "the point of maximum energy." In his poem "Vortex" (published in BLAST), he used all caps to declare Impressionism and Futurism "CORPSES" and lauded the imagery of H.D.'s "Oread" ("whirl up sea - whirl your pointed pines"). T.S. Eliot and Pound were published in BLAST, along with paintings by Pound's wife Dorothy Shakespear. The first edition of BLAST also published a short story that grew into Ford Madox Ford's The Good Soldier.

The movement railed against Futurism, calling it "superficial," while adopting its concrete poetry techniques. Key figures and signatories included Jessica Dismorr, Henri Gaudier-Brzeska, Frederick Etchells, and Lewis himself. Jacob Epstein's Rock Drill (1913), a plaster torso on industrial equipment, is often seen as a peak of Vorticist sculpture. David Bomberg's The Mud Bath is a defining image of the movement. Lewis's own works include Slow Attack and Workshop. Paul Nash's angular war paintings were also influenced by Vorticist aesthetics.
