The Mirror Stage and Ego Formation

Lacan's foundational concept: the 'mirror stage' occurs when infants (6-18 months) first recognize their reflection, developing apperception and forming the ego. The infant simultaneously recognizes the reflection as both itself and not-itself. The ego is a meconnaissance (misrecognition). This creates a pre-language concept of self and is key to entering the Imaginary order. The concept is presented in his Ecrits.

The Three Orders: Imaginary, Symbolic, and Real

Lacan divided reality into three registers: the Imaginary (related to the mirror stage and ego), the Symbolic (language, law, the phallus as signifier), and the Real (that which resists symbolization). He portrayed these as a Borromean Knot. The Real roughly corresponds to Freud's id. Julia Kristeva's Powers of Horror argues that the 'abject' is experienced when the Real erupts into our lives. Zizek defines ideology in terms of Lacan's 'fantasies' explaining loss of the 'sublime object.'

Objet Petit a, Jouissance, and Mathemes

The objet petit a is Lacan's term for the object-cause of desire, related to his concept of 'surplus jouissance' (paralleling Marx's surplus value). Jouissance describes the intense pleasure and pain felt as the Subject futilely tries to recapture the Object. His 'mathemes' are pseudo-mathematical symbols (e.g., $ <> a for 'fantasy') used to formalize psychoanalytic concepts. He identified four discourses: the master, the university, the analyst, and the hysteric. His seminars at Sainte-Anne Hospital ran from 1954-1976, edited by Jacques-Alain Miller.

General / Biographical and Influence

Summary of Facts

Jacques Lacan is a French psychoanalyst best known for theorizing the 'mirror stage,' dividing reality into the Imaginary, Symbolic, and Real orders, and calling for a 'return to Freud.' His work is published primarily in his Ecrits.

The mirror stage is Lacan's foundational concept: it occurs when infants (aged 6-18 months) first recognize their reflection, forming the ego through apperception. The infant simultaneously recognizes the reflection as itself and not-itself, making the ego a meconnaissance (misrecognition). This creates a pre-language concept of self and initiates the Imaginary order.

Lacan's three orders — Imaginary, Symbolic, and Real — form a Borromean Knot. The Real roughly corresponds to Freud's id and resists symbolization. Julia Kristeva's Powers of Horror argues the 'abject' erupts when the Real intrudes. The Symbolic involves language, law, and the phallus as a signifier. His concept of sexuation upon entering the symbolic order was critiqued by feminists like Irigaray and Kristeva.

The objet petit a is the object-cause of desire, connected to 'surplus jouissance' (paralleling Marx's surplus value). Jouissance describes intense pleasure and pain in the futile attempt to recapture the Object. Lacan formalized these concepts with 'mathemes' — pseudo-mathematical symbols. He identified four discourses (master, university, analyst, hysteric). His seminars at Sainte-Anne Hospital ran from 1954-1976 and were edited by Jacques-Alain Miller.

His seminar on Poe's 'The Purloined Letter' argues the title document is a signifier of the unknown whose presence-absence drives events. He influenced Laura Mulvey's concept of the 'male gaze' and used Holbein's The Ambassadors to analyze the 'gaze.' Slavoj Zizek frequently applies Lacan to political and cultural questions. He was stripped of IPA recognition for variable-length sessions. He took his Hegel reading from Kojeve and called Hegel 'the most sublime hysteric.' Felix Guattari developed schizoanalysis to improve on his methods. Lee Edelman coined 'sinthomosexual' from Lacan's sinthome concept.
