José Saramago

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Portuguese Nobel laureate (1998) known for novels featuring allegorical scenarios and unnamed characters. His most famous work, Blindness, depicts an epidemic of 'white sickness' in which only the doctor's wife is immune. Other major works include The Gospel According to Jesus Christ, Death with Interruptions, The Year of the Death of Ricardo Reis, The Stone Raft, All the Names, Cain, and The Double. His prose style eschews conventional punctuation, and his works often engage with questions of mortality, religion, and political systems.

Summary of Facts

José Saramago is a Portuguese author and Nobel Prize laureate (1998) whose novels are defined by allegorical premises, unnamed characters, and explorations of mortality, religion, and political systems.

His most famous and most frequently clued work is Blindness (1995), in which an unnamed city is struck by an epidemic of 'white sickness' that renders everyone blind except the doctor's wife. Characters are known only by descriptions: the girl with the dark glasses, the old man with the black eye patch, the boy with the squint. Early victims are quarantined in a disused mental hospital where a group of hoodlums (led by the 'King of Ward Three') demands that women sleep with them in exchange for food. The doctor's wife kills the leader with scissors, and a mattress fire burns down the ward. A car thief develops a leg infection after being kicked by the girl with dark glasses with a stiletto heel. A dog called the 'dog of tears' comforts the doctor's wife by licking her face and joins her group. The novel opens with a man unable to drive at a green stoplight. Saramago used his own dog Camões as inspiration for the dog of tears.

The sequel, Seeing, continues the story: 83% of voters turn in blank ballots, the government brands them as terrorists, and the dog of tears (now named 'Constant') is the only named character. The doctor's wife is suspected of being behind the blank ballot movement.

Death with Interruptions depicts a country where people stop dying. The 'maphia' (spelled with 'ph') smuggles people across the border so they can die, and offers funeral directors protection from 'eternal life activists.' Death is personified as a woman who signs her name with a lowercase 'd,' sends violet-colored letters one week before people die, and declares a 'unilateral truce' on New Year's Eve. She falls in love with a cellist who keeps returning her letters. A character is nicknamed 'fifty-eight seconds' for his love of Chopin's Butterfly étude.

The Gospel According to Jesus Christ is a controversial retelling of the life of Jesus. Jesus is apprenticed to a shepherd who turns out to be the Devil in disguise and is forced to sacrifice his favorite sheep. Jesus meets God and Satan—an old man and a shepherd—in the middle of a lake (the Sea of Galilee). Jesus's father is executed after being mistaken for a Zealot or trying to help Ananias, and Jesus claims his father's sandals. Jesus has recurring nightmares of being a Roman soldier who kills his own son. The title character is born in a cave rather than a manger, lives with Mary Magdalene, and has second thoughts about the crucifixion.

The Year of the Death of Ricardo Reis centers on one of Fernando Pessoa's heteronyms, who returns to Lisbon from Brazil after Pessoa's death and frequently converses with Pessoa's ghost. Reis has affairs with the chambermaid Lydia (whom he impregnates) and the socialite Marcenda, whose paralyzed arm is treated uselessly by her father Dr. Sampaio to cover visits to his mistress. Reis reads Herbert Quain's The God of the Labyrinth and repeats the maxim 'wise is the man who contents himself with the spectacle of the world.' The novel ends with Reis putting on his jacket and walking to a cemetery.

The Stone Raft depicts the Iberian Peninsula breaking off from Europe and floating across the Atlantic. Five characters travel across Iberia; Joana Carda's actions cause the separation. One character is followed everywhere by a flock of birds.

Cain, Saramago's final novel, reimagines the Biblical Cain as a time traveler who becomes Lilith's lover, stops Abraham from sacrificing Isaac, witnesses God's cruelty (the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah), and is ultimately doomed to live alone on Earth after killing everyone on Noah's Ark.

All the Names follows Senhor José, a bureaucrat at the 'Central Registry of Births, Marriages, and Deaths,' who accidentally grabs an unknown woman's index card while collecting clippings about famous people, sparking an obsessive search.

The Double features The History teacher Tertuliano, who rents a film and discovers an actor who looks exactly like him. The History of the Siege of Lisbon follows a proofreader, Raimundo Silva, who inserts the word 'not' into a medieval manuscript and falls in love with his supervisor Dr. Maria Sara. Baltasar and Blimunda features Bartholomew Lorenzo's flying machine, the Passarola. The Elephant's Journey follows an elephant named Solomon given by King João III to Archduke Maximilian.

Saramago's first novel was retitled Land of Sin. He also wrote Manual of Painting and Calligraphy. He was an avowed atheist, and his widow Pilar del Río translated the sequel Seeing. Margaret Jull Costa is his principal English translator.
