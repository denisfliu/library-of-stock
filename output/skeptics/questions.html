T1 2024 Chicago Open (2024) Diff 10 · History
The unlikely estimate that there were 50,000 of these people living in Paris in 1625 comes from a book by Marin Mersenne. A book by Lucien Febvre (“FEV-ruh”) argues that Bonaventure des Périers (“day pair-YAY”) and Étienne Dolet (“ate-YEN doh-LAY”) were only called these people as a rhetorical gesture, since these people didn’t exist in the 16th century. David Hume claimed he did not believe in these people, only to be told he was sitting with fifteen of them, while at dinner with his host’s “Coterie.” The “testament” of one of these people, the (*) priest Jean Meslier (“mel-YAY”), was expurgated by Voltaire, who attacked these people for celebrating John Needham’s creation of “eels” from flour and quipped against them in reply to the Treatise of the Three Imposters. The 18th century’s most famous one of these people wrote The System of Nature. For 10 points, the Baron d’Holbach was what sort of person once thought unable to swear oaths?
ANSWER: atheists [accept unbelievers, nonbelievers, materialists, or naturalists; or word forms like atheism or “problem of unbelief”; prompt on freethinkers, skeptics, libertines, philosophes, blasphemers, heretics, or anti-clericalists; reject “agnostics” or “deists”] (Hume was eating with D’Holbach’s Coterie.)

B1 2023 ACF Winter (2023) Diff 7 · Philosophy
In his Treatise of Human Nature, David Hume notes that the coherence of an object leads “vulgar” people to reject this view. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this broad philosophical position that doubts the certainty of knowledge.
ANSWER: skepticism [or word forms like skeptics; accept Pyrrhonian skepticism or Pyrrhonism]
[10] In An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, Hume extends his skepticism to encompass this phenomenon by claiming that when we observe “constant conjunction,” we unjustly posit it out of habit.
ANSWER: causation [or causality; or descriptions of cause-and-effect relationships]
[10] In Hume’s skeptical discussion of sympathy, these things are unusually assumed to exist. A 1967 book by Alvin Plantinga analogizes an epistemological problem named for these things to believing in God.
ANSWER: other minds [accept the problem of other minds; accept God and Other Minds; accept descriptions of minds that are not one’s own; prompt on minds]
B2 2021 ACF Regionals (2021) Diff 8 · Philosophy
A super-fan of this school of thought had quotes from it inscribed on the long rafters of his tower study in Dordogne (“dor-DOHN”). For 10 points each:
[10] Name this tradition that influenced Michel de Montaigne’s essay “Apology for Raymond Sebond.” Montaigne’s version of this philosophy was particularly inspired by Sextus Empiricus’s Outlines of Pyrrhonism.
ANSWER: skepticism [accept Pyrrhonian skepticism or Academic skepticism; accept Pyrrhonism until “Pyrrhonism” is read]
[10] Roderick Chisholm traced the “Problem of the Criterion” of knowledge through Montaigne’s version of a skeptical argument named for this shape. This shape names a type of reasoning that assumes its own premises.
ANSWER: circle [accept diallelus, the wheel, or vicious circle; accept circular reasoning or circular argument or circular logic]
[10] Montaigne’s personal medal had the motto “what do I know?” on one side and this Greek word central to ancient skepticism on the other. Edmund Husserl used this Greek word to refer to phenomenological “bracketing.”
ANSWER: epoché [or epocho] (The skeptics used “epoché” to refer to suspension of judgement.)
