Summary of Facts

Alfred Schnittke (1934–1998) was a Soviet composer of Jewish Volga German ancestry, widely considered the foremost Soviet composer after Shostakovich. Known for his long 'dissident-length' hair and his provocative mixing of musical styles, his music was suppressed by the Soviet regime. He formed part of the 'Big Troika' of modernist Soviet composers alongside Sofia Gubaidulina and Edison Denisov.

Schnittke's most famous theoretical contribution is 'polystylism' — the juxtaposing and intersecting of contrasting musical idioms within a single work, ranging from Baroque pastiche to tango to film music. He theorized this approach in a 1971 lecture presented in Moscow. His gravestone famously features a fermata over a rest marked fff. He suffered a severe stroke in 1985 and composed the Cello Concerto No. 1 after this, using an even more jarring and fragmented polystylist style. He wrote 8 symphonies, 6 concerti grossi, and film music including a score for a Soviet animated film about a glass harmonica that was banned by authorities.

His Concerto Grosso No. 1 (1977), commissioned by violinist Gidon Kremer, is his most frequently clued work. It calls for two solo violins, a harpsichord, and a piano prepared with kopeks (coins) in the opening Preludio movement. The Toccata features two violins in a frantic cascading stretto canon. The fifth movement Rondo contains a 'banal' E-flat minor tango on harpsichord, offering brief respite from the cacophonous parody of Tchaikovsky's Violin Concerto and Schnittke's own film music. The composer described it as mixing 'U' and 'E' music — entertaining and serious.

Schnittke also wrote controversial polystylist cadenzas for Beethoven's Violin Concerto, championed and recorded by Gidon Kremer. The first cadenza quotes from Bartók's two violin concerti and Shostakovich; another quotes from Alban Berg. Most soloists prefer Fritz Kreisler's cadenzas. His Third String Quartet opens with three explicitly labeled quotations of Orlando di Lasso, Beethoven, and the DSCH motif. His Quasi una sonata (Violin Sonata No. 2) uses the BACH motif in a passacaglia movement and quotes 'La Cucaracha' in the finale. His Symphony No. 3 uses an eight-tone row spelling out 'das Böse' ('the evil'). His polystylist choral Faust Cantata ends with a wild tango movement 'Es geschah' accompanying Faust's death. He also composed the ballet Peer Gynt and the work Moz-Art à la Haydn for two violins and orchestra.
