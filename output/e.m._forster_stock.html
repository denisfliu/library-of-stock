Summary of Facts

E.M. Forster was an Edwardian British novelist known for exploring social class, English manners, and connection across divides.

Howards End (1910), with its epigraph 'Only connect,' centers on three families: the intellectual Schlegels (Margaret and Helen), the materialist Wilcoxes, and the lower-class Basts. Ruth Wilcox pens a handwritten note leaving the title house to Margaret, but her husband Henry refuses to honor it. Leonard Bast, who seeks to improve himself by reading John Ruskin, is discovered to have married Jacky, a former prostitute who once had an affair with Henry in Cyprus. The first chapter is epistolary, with a love affair devolving into 'telegrams and anger.' Helen hears 'goblins walking quietly over the universe' while listening to Beethoven's Fifth Symphony ('the most sublime noise that has ever penetrated into the ear of man') and accidentally takes Leonard's umbrella. Charles beats Leonard with the flat of a German sword, causing a bookcase to fall and kill him; Charles is sentenced to three years for manslaughter. Helen returns pregnant with Leonard's child. The house was based on Forster's childhood home of Rooks Nest. Miss Avery opens boxes uninvited; residents stick pigs' teeth into a wych-elm to cure toothache. The Wilcoxes own the Imperial and West Africa Rubber Company (cited by Said in Culture and Imperialism). Zadie Smith's On Beauty is modeled on it; Matthew Lopez's The Inheritance is a stage adaptation featuring gay New Yorkers; a Merchant Ivory film featured a screenplay by Ruth Prawer Jhabvala.

A Passage to India features Dr. Aziz, accused of assaulting Adela Quested in the Marabar Caves. Broken field glasses become key evidence at The Trial, where Cyril Fielding defends Aziz. Mrs. Moore is tormented by the echo 'boum' in the caves and dies of heatstroke sailing to England; spectators chant 'Esmiss Esmoor' hoping she will testify. Ronny Heaslop breaks off his engagement with Adela after she recants. Aziz shows a photo of his dead wife, pretending the visitor is a 'brother.' Adela distinguishes 'mysteries' from 'muddles' after Mr. Turton's bridge party, where she meets Professor Godbole. Major Callendar is the Chief Civil Surgeon. The novel ends with Fielding asking 'Why can't we be friends?' as the earth, horses, and birds say 'No, not yet' and rocks prevent them from riding side-by-side. A religious festival features signs misprinted 'God si love' and a rowboat collision with a model of Gokul village.

A Room with a View follows Lucy Honeychurch, who arrives at the Pensione Bertolini in Florence with cousin Charlotte Bartlett, complaining about not seeing the Arno. George Emerson kisses her in a field of violets; she witnesses a knifing that bloodies her photographs. Eleanor Lavish steals her Baedeker guidebook and uses the pseudonym 'Joseph Emery Prank' for a romance novel that Cecil later reads aloud during a tennis match. Lucy breaks off her engagement to the snobbish Cecil Vyse (described as an 'ideal bachelor' by Reverend Beebe) and elopes with George. Three men swim in a pool and have a splash fight. A carriage driver called 'Phaethon' picks up a girl called 'Persephone.' Mr. Beebe connects Lucy's boldness to her Beethoven piano playing.

Where Angels Fear to Tread follows the Herriton family: Lilia marries Italian Gino Carella, and siblings Philip and Harriet are sent to retrieve her child. The baby dies in a carriage accident. Maurice was written in 1913 but published posthumously in 1971. The title character learns about sex from Mr. Ducie's beach diagrams, begins a relationship with Clive Durham at Cambridge, and ultimately chooses gamekeeper Alec Scudder, who climbs through a window.

Forster's Aspects of the Novel first distinguished 'flat' and 'round' characters, noting that Dickens's 'immense vitality' makes his flat characters 'vibrate a little.' He stated 'history develops, art stands still.' His essay 'What I Believe' asserts one should sooner betray one's country than one's friend. 'The Machine Stops' is a prescient sci-fi story. 'The Story of a Panic' features an encounter with Pan in Ravello. He corresponded with the Alexandrian poet Cavafy, describing him as 'standing absolutely motionless at a slight angle to the universe' in Pharos and Pharillon.
