French painter, co-founder of Orphism with his wife Sonia Delaunay and František Kupka. Known for cubist paintings of the Eiffel Tower rendered in red, colorful disc paintings, and the theory of "simultaneism" derived from Chevreul's color theory. Member of Section d'Or. Named by Guillaume Apollinaire.

Summary of Facts

Robert Delaunay (1885-1941) was a French painter who, along with his wife Sonia Delaunay and František Kupka, co-founded the art movement Orphism, a colorful offshoot of Cubism. The name was coined by the poet Guillaume Apollinaire, who noted the musical quality of Delaunay's multicolored discs. Delaunay was a devotee of Eugène Chevreul's color theory (Chevreul being the chemist whose work also led to the invention of margarine) and used the term "simultaneism" to describe the use of contrasting colors in his works.

His most iconic subject was the Eiffel Tower, which he painted in a series of cubist works rendered in red, including Champs de Mars: The Red Tower. His abstract paintings include Simultaneous Contrasts: Sun and Moon, a circular-framed piece, and Homage to Blériot, which features propellers, wing fuselages, colorful discs, and airplanes in tribute to the English Channel-crossing aviation pioneer Louis Blériot. He also created a series of seven paintings inspired by light streaming through stained-glass windows at the Gothic church of Saint-Séverin, one of which was included in the Blue Rider Almanac.

Delaunay was a member of Section d'Or alongside the Duchamp brothers. Other works include La Ville de Paris (one of the largest Cubist paintings), Simultaneous Windows on the City, The Cardiff Team (part of a series of Runners), Man with a Tulip (portrait of Jean Metzinger), and Sun, Tower, Airplane. He supposedly punched Georges Rouault at the Salon d'Automne after Rouault stepped over his paintings.
