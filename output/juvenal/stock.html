Early 2nd-century Roman satirist (Decimus Junius Juvenalis), author of the sixteen Satires. Coined famous phrases including "bread and circuses" (panem et circenses), "who watches the watchmen?" (quis custodiet ipsos custodes?), and "a sound mind in a sound body" (mens sana in corpore sano). His 10th Satire was adapted by Samuel Johnson as "The Vanity of Human Wishes" and his 3rd Satire as Johnson's "London."
Satires (general)

Juvenal's collected 16 Satires, his only surviving work, divided into five books. Written in dactylic hexameter, they savage Roman vice, corruption, and social decline. The collection is the source of many of Juvenal's most famous phrases and Samuel Johnson adapted it into English verse. Wikipedia

Satire 6 (Against Women/Marriage)

Juvenal's longest poem, a diatribe against marriage and women addressed to Postumus, whom the speaker urges to hang himself from the Aemilian bridge or take a boy lover rather than marry. It contains the famous line "Sed quis custodiet ipsos custodes?" — in context mocking the decline of womanly virtue rather than government. Ben Jonson's Epicoene adapts an anti-feminist diatribe from it. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Juvenal (Decimus Junius Juvenalis) is an early 2nd-century Roman satirist, the author of sixteen Satires that are his only surviving work. Clues overwhelmingly identify him via Samuel Johnson's English adaptations: Satire 10 was transformed into "The Vanity of Human Wishes" (beginning "Let observation with extensive view / Survey mankind, from China to Peru") and Satire 3 became "London" (beginning "Tho' Grief and Fondness in my Breast Rebel," with Thales substituting for Juvenal's Umbricius, the friend through whom Juvenal declares Rome a place where "no decent Roman" belongs). Dryden also translated Juvenal, inserting the line "When the King's Trump, the Mob are for the King" and adapting a "penniless exile" into a self-portrait. The Satires are the source of Juvenal's most famous phrases: "bread and circuses" (panem et circenses, from Satire 10), "a sound mind in a sound body" (mens sana in corpore sano, also from Satire 10, immediately preceding "a stout heart that has no fear of death"), and "quis custodiet ipsos custodes?" ("who watches the watchmen?", from Satire 6, where in context it mocks the decline of womanly virtue rather than government corruption; the phrase titled Alan Moore's Watchmen). Satire 1 is the programmatic opener, declaring that "it is difficult not to write" satire, and complains about women participating in boar hunts and the exiled Marius Priscus drinking all afternoon. Satire 3 attacks foreigners packing Rome. Satire 4 is a mock-epic in which the courtier Crispinus buys a huge turbot for 6,000 sesterces and a Council of Domitian is summoned to decide how to cook a fish too large for any pan, with Montanus proposing potters make a giant vessel. Satire 5 depicts the worst possible dinner party, where the patron Virro sips old wine "bottled when Consuls wore their hair long," orders water "cooler than frost in Thrace," and eats a Messina lamprey, while the poor client addressee gets "the stringy snake's relative" from a Subura drain; Juvenal concludes "you won't be afraid to endure the whip: that's the dinner and friend you're worthy of." Satire 6 is a long diatribe against marriage addressed to Postumus (who is urged to hang himself from the Aemilian bridge or find a boy lover instead), featuring a cringing husband whose drunk wife vomits "like a snake" on a marble floor and noting that "luxury" is "more damaging than any foe"; Ben Jonson adapted Truewit's anti-marriage diatribe in Epicoene from it. Satire 10 mocks Hannibal among other deluded ambitions. Satire 12 concerns friendship (amicitia). Satire 14, "On education in avarice," bemoans those who bring safety deposit boxes to dinner and a barber richer than the wealthy, references the Old Testament as "the sacred volume of Moses," and blames a father's observance of the Sabbath as the "cause and culprit" of his child's vices, while calling Hercules and the Minotaur stories frivolous. Satire 15 argues men are crueler than animals, citing a riot in Egypt in which a man was torn to pieces and eaten. The fragmentary final Satire 16 discusses the advantages soldiers enjoy over ordinary citizens.
