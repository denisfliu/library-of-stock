Gottlob Frege

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
German logician and philosopher, founder of analytic philosophy and the logicist program. Author of Begriffsschrift, The Foundations of Arithmetic, and the essay 'On Sense and Reference.' Known for the sense/reference distinction illustrated by 'Hesperus is Phosphorus,' Basic Law V (shown inconsistent by Russell's paradox), the context principle, and Frege's theorem (Hume's principle derives Peano axioms in second-order logic).
On Sense and Reference (Uber Sinn und Bedeutung)

Frege's most famous essay, which introduces the distinction between sense (Sinn, the 'mode of presentation') and reference (Bedeutung, the object represented). The key puzzle: 'Hesperus is Hesperus' is trivially true while 'Hesperus is Phosphorus' conveys new information, because both names refer to Venus but differ in sense (morning star vs. evening star). This resolves why identity statements of the form 'a = b' have different 'cognitive value' than 'a = a.' The essay also discusses propositional attitude puzzles (substitutivity fails in belief contexts). Bertrand Russell's 'On Denoting' rejects Frege's sense/reference distinction. David Kaplan replaced Frege's two categories with 'content' and 'character.'

The Foundations of Arithmetic (Grundlagen der Arithmetik)

Frege's 1884 work that defines numbers as extensions of concepts using 'equinumerous' sets and introduces the context principle (words only have meaning in the context of a proposition). It criticizes Kant's claim that arithmetic is synthetic a priori and attacks psychologistic theories of mathematics. Michael Dummett attributes the beginning of the linguistic turn to Frege's use of the context principle (paragraph 62). The work includes three 'fundamental principles': separating the psychological from the logical, never asking for the meaning of a word in isolation, and distinguishing concept from object. It also contains the Julius Caesar problem (can't determine if Caesar is a number). J.L. Austin translated the Grundlagen.

Basic Law V and Russell's Paradox

Frege's Basic Law V states that the value-ranges of two functions are identical if and only if they map every object to the same value. This axiom of unrestricted comprehension was shown to be inconsistent by Bertrand Russell through the paradox of the set of all sets that don't contain themselves. Russell wrote a letter informing Frege of the flaw just as the second volume of Basic Laws of Arithmetic was at the printing press, causing Frege to add a last-minute appendix that turned out to imply the universe of discourse contains exactly one object. The rehabilitation of Frege's program by Crispin Wright and George Boolos replaces Basic Law V with Hume's principle.

Frege's Theorem and Logicism

Frege's theorem (rediscovered by Crispin Wright) states that Hume's principle is sufficient to derive all the Peano axioms in second-order logic. This is the central result of Frege's logicist program, the attempt to ground arithmetic in pure logic. George Boolos named the key concept (Hume's principle) that replaces Basic Law V. Frege formalized equinumerosity, which Boolos identified as Hume's principle. Frege defended mathematical Platonism (the view that abstract mathematical objects exist). Both Brouwer's intuitionism and Hilbert's formalism disagreed with Frege's logicism.

Begriffsschrift (Concept Script)

Frege's 1879 work that introduced a formal system of logic (a 'formula language, modeled on that of arithmetic, of pure thought'). It introduced the turnstile symbol, the universal quantifier, the conditional, and a unique two-dimensional notation. It established second-order logic. The preface notes that Leibniz underestimated the difficulties of creating a calculus philosophicus. Frege used the ancestral relation to define natural numbers. Wittgenstein used the term 'Begriffsschrift' as a synonym for logical formalism in the Tractatus. The work also introduced devices for expressing multiple generality in first-order logic.

General / Biographical and Legacy

Frege is considered a founder of analytic philosophy. Michael Dummett was the foremost Frege scholar, writing books on his philosophy of language and mathematics, and claiming Wittgenstein's reference to Frege in the Tractatus explains why he is read today. Dummett was shocked that Frege apparently espoused anti-Semitic views, though Frege maintained a good relationship with the Kabbalah scholar Gershom Scholem. Susanne Bobzien identified 120 passages where Frege appeared to plagiarize a Stoic philosophy anthology (paralleling the Stoic concept of lekta). Frege wrote against David Hilbert's geometry lectures. A late fragment calls non-Euclidean geometry a 'pseudoscientific museum piece.' Davidson followed Frege's reasoning on 'the true' being the referent for all true statements. Nathan Salmon's 'modified naive theory' concerns Frege's puzzle. Zalta wrote the oldest SEP article about Frege.

Summary of Facts

Gottlob Frege (1848-1925) was a German logician and philosopher considered a founder of analytic philosophy and the logicist program. His three most important works are Begriffsschrift (1879), The Foundations of Arithmetic (1884), and the essay 'On Sense and Reference' (1892).

Frege's most well-known contribution is the distinction between sense (Sinn) and reference (Bedeutung), introduced in 'On Sense and Reference.' The key puzzle: 'Hesperus is Hesperus' is trivially true, while 'Hesperus is Phosphorus' conveys new information, because both names refer to Venus but differ in their 'mode of presentation.' This resolves why identity statements 'a = b' have different 'cognitive value' than 'a = a.' The essay also addresses propositional attitude puzzles (substitutivity fails in belief contexts). Russell's 'On Denoting' rejected the sense/reference distinction, while David Kaplan replaced Frege's categories with 'content' and 'character.' The Frege-Geach problem, named for Frege and Peter Geach, challenges expressivism by showing that moral statements seem to mean different things when embedded in conditionals.

The Foundations of Arithmetic defines numbers as extensions of concepts using 'equinumerous' sets and introduces the context principle: words only have meaning in the context of a proposition. Michael Dummett attributes the beginning of the linguistic turn to Frege's context principle (specifically paragraph 62). The work includes three fundamental principles, the Julius Caesar problem, and criticizes both Kant's views on arithmetic and psychologistic theories of mathematics.

Frege's Basic Law V, which defines when two functions share a value-range, was shown inconsistent by Russell's paradox. Russell wrote to Frege just as the second volume of Basic Laws of Arithmetic was at the press, leading Frege to add a last-minute appendix. The rehabilitation of Frege's program by Crispin Wright and George Boolos replaces Basic Law V with Hume's principle, yielding Frege's theorem: Hume's principle suffices to derive all Peano axioms in second-order logic.

Begriffsschrift introduced the turnstile symbol, the universal quantifier, the conditional, a unique two-dimensional notation, and second-order logic. Wittgenstein paid tribute by using the term as a synonym for logical formalism in the Tractatus.

Michael Dummett was the foremost Frege scholar. Dummett was shocked to discover Frege's anti-Semitic views, though Frege maintained a relationship with Gershom Scholem. Susanne Bobzien identified 120 passages where Frege appeared to plagiarize a Stoic anthology, finding similarities to the concept of lekta. Frege also wrote against Hilbert's geometry lectures and called non-Euclidean geometry a 'pseudoscientific museum piece.' He defended mathematical Platonism and argued it was the only defensible view of mathematics.
