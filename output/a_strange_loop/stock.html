A 2019 musical written by Michael R. Jackson. Won the 2020 Pulitzer Prize for Drama and the 2022 Tony Award for Best Musical. The show follows Usher, a self-described 'fat, Black, queer' man who works as an usher at a production of The Lion King on Broadway, while trying to write a 'big, black, queer-ass, American, Broadway show' — which turns out to be the musical itself (the titular 'strange loop,' a term drawn from Douglas Hofstadter). The protagonist battles six personified 'intrusive thoughts' and grapples with his family's religious conservatism, his identity, and self-loathing.
Plot and Characters

The protagonist Usher is a Black queer man who works as an usher at a production of The Lion King while trying to write an autobiographical musical. He battles six personified 'Thoughts' including his 'daily self-loathing' and a white 'Inwood Daddy.' His mother calls him on his birthday to 'remind him periodically' that she loves him before telling him 'hell is real.' On days when 'his blackness feels like another hurdle,' he reflects that 'white girls can do anything, can't they / black boys must always obey their mothers.' Tyler Perry seeks a ghostwriter for a gospel play, after which the protagonist envisions figures accusing him of being a race traitor. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

A Strange Loop is a recent (2019) musical by Michael R. Jackson that has become a notable quizbowl answer, particularly at higher difficulties. It won both the 2020 Pulitzer Prize for Drama and the 2022 Tony Award for Best Musical. The show is meta-theatrical: its protagonist Usher is a Black queer man working as an usher at The Lion King while trying to write the very musical the audience is watching — creating the titular 'strange loop,' a term borrowed from Douglas Hofstadter.

In quizbowl, the musical appears most often as a direct tossup at high difficulty (diff 10), with power-mark clues focused on specific scenes: 'Show Me How to Pray' (a parody about Michelle finding a husband), the preacher's AIDS sermon about cousin Darnell, and the mother's birthday call ending with 'hell is real.' The 'Intermission Song' with its 'Usher Usher' ostinato is also clued.

In mentions, A Strange Loop primarily appears in questions about musicals as a genre, about Michael R. Jackson (often in contrast with Michael Jackson the singer), and about the concept of self-reference/recursion. The show is frequently identified by its Tony and Pulitzer wins, its Hofstadter-derived title, and the description of Usher as a 'fat, Black, queer' protagonist trying to write a 'big, black, queer-ass, American, Broadway show.'
