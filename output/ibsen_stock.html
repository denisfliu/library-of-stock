Summary of Facts

Henrik Ibsen is a Norwegian playwright, widely considered The Father of modern drama. His plays are central to quizbowl literature, with A Doll's House being by far his most frequently clued work.

A Doll's House shocked audiences by ending with protagonist Nora Helmer slamming The Door on her marriage to the banker Torvald. Nora waits eight years for a 'wonderful thing' to happen before realizing she has never had a serious conversation with her husband. She tells Torvald 'I no longer believe in miracles.' Krogstad blackmails Nora over a forged signature. Doctor Rank, who suffers from 'tuberculosis of the spine' (widely held to be a euphemism for syphilis), confesses his love for Nora and helps her rehearse the Tarantella. Dr. Rank leaves visiting cards marked with a black cross to indicate his impending death. Nora sneaks in forbidden macaroons. A symbolic Christmas tree loses its decorations as Nora's marriage deteriorates. George Bernard Shaw wrote 'The Quintessence of Ibsenism' about Ibsen, analyzing his plays and critiquing Victorian ideals of the 'Womanly Woman.' Shaw's essay imagines a community of a thousand with '700 Philistines, 299 Idealists, and only one lone realist.' August Strindberg wrote The Father as a response, arguing Nora is the true villain. Adrienne Rich's essay 'When We Dead Awaken: Writing as Re-Vision' takes its title from Ibsen. Lu Xun gave a 1923 speech arguing Nora will likely 'fall into degradation.' Elfriede Jelinek's first play imagines the fate of one of Ibsen's characters. Ibsen called an alternate ending where Nora shouts 'Motherless!' 'an act of barbaric violence.'

Hedda Gabler: the title character Burns Eilert Lovborg's manuscript and is blackmailed by Judge Brack. She imagines her lover dying with 'vine-leaves in his hair,' but he actually dies in a brothel run by Mademoiselle Diana. The play ends with the exclamation 'Good God! -- people don't do such things.' Hedda plays a 'wild dance' on the piano and shoots herself with one of her father's pistols.

The Wild Duck: Gregers Werle convinces the short-sighted Hedvig to sacrifice the title animal, but she shoots herself instead. Dr. Relling calls illusion 'the stimulating principle' and introduces the concept of the 'life-lie.' Hjalmar Ekdal's 'Great Invention' in photography is revealed to be a life-lie. Hjalmar's father Old Ekdal lives in an attic. Hjalmar's wife Gina admits an affair with Hakon Werle. Gregers mocks his father's efforts to reconnect as a 'tableau of filial affection' for his mistress Mrs. Sorby. Alfred Adler's work on the 'life-lie' was inspired by this play.

The Master Builder: Hilda Wangel convinces the acrophobic architect Halvard Solness to climb a Church steeple, from which he falls to his death. Hilda travels from Lysanger to remind him of his promise to build her a kingdom. Solness fears the 'Younger generation' knocking at his door and wishes a chimney crack would burn down his wife's house.

An Enemy of the People: Dr. Thomas Stockmann discovers that the water at a newly opened spa is contaminated and gives a speech denouncing the 'compact majority.' He compares a political party to a 'sausage machine.' Mrs. Busk reluctantly fires Stockmann's daughter. The play ends with a declaration about 'the spirit of truth and the spirit of freedom.'

Ghosts: Mrs. Alving dedicates an orphanage in memory of her late husband. The orphanage is left uninsured and Burns down. Her son Oswald has syphilis and mutters 'the sun, the sun' in the final scene as his mother decides whether to euthanize him. Pinero reconsidered his own work after seeing Ghosts.

When We Dead Awaken is Ibsen's final play. Irene sings 'I am free as a bird!' and calls posing for the sculpture Resurrection 'a self-murder.' She and sculptor Arnold Rubek are buried by an avalanche. A Sister of Mercy speaks the final line 'Pax vobiscum!' The play includes a speech about how after the title action, 'we find that we have never lived.'

Other works: Pillars of Society features Consul Bernick and the dangerously unseaworthy Indian Girl. John Gabriel Borkman features a banker living in his attic since imprisonment. The Lady from the Sea features Ellida, married to a doctor, haunted by a former American sailor husband. Little Eyolf features the Rat-Wife and a boy's death, leaving a floating crutch. Peer Gynt features a funeral and auction. Rosmersholm is discussed by Strindberg as 'psychic murder.' Brian Johnston argued Ibsen's final twelve plays form a single Hegelian framework. William Archer and Edmund Gosse popularized Ibsen in Britain. Ibsen characterized Norwegian literature after the sagas as 'four hundred years of darkness.' He coined the Scandinavian ideal of 'Friluftsliv' (free air living). Arthur Miller compared his plays to a 'spiritual CAT scan.'
