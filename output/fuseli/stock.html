Swiss-born Romantic painter (born Johann Heinrich Fussli), best known for The Nightmare. Prolific contributor to Boydell's Shakespeare Gallery and creator of the failed Milton Gallery. Friend of Johann Caspar Lavater and William Blake. Royal Academy professor whose diploma work was Thor Battering the Midgard Serpent.
The Nightmare (1781)

Fuseli's most famous work, depicting an incubus crouching on the chest of a sleeping woman while a ghostly horse with pale, white eyes pokes its head through a red curtain. He created at least four versions. The original was widely popularized by a Thomas Burke engraving. A small table in the scene holds a book, mirror, and vial; a later version added an erotic sculpture. Fuseli's unrequited fantasies about Anna Landolt (Johann Caspar Lavater's niece) may have inspired the painting. Doja Cat recreated it for her Scarlet tour posters in 2024. The horse's head has been interpreted as a symbol of phallic penetration.

Thor Battering the Midgard Serpent (1790)

Fuseli's Royal Academy diploma work, depicting a nude Thor standing on the prow of a boat next to Hymir, pulling a chained sea serpent from the water. A spray of blood is visible from the creature. Based on Thomas Percy's translation of the Northern Antiquities. One of Fuseli's most frequently referenced mythological scenes.

Milton Gallery / The Shepherd's Dream

Fuseli's ambitious but commercially failed venture: a gallery of 47 paintings (some sources say 40) based on scenes from Paradise Lost, produced over an eight-year period. The Shepherd's Dream, depicting fairies bewitching a peasant (based on Paradise Lost's comparison of fallen angels to fairies), is one of the most frequently cited individual paintings from this series. The financial failure of the Milton Gallery required rescue by his patron Thomas Coutts.

Shakespeare-Inspired Works (Titania and Bottom, Lady Macbeth Seizing the Daggers, Lady Macbeth Sleepwalking)

Fuseli was a major contributor to John Boydell's Shakespeare Gallery and created numerous Shakespeare-inspired works. Titania and Bottom depicts a tiny man with outstretched arms in the palm of a character with a donkey's head, surrounded by an eerie fairy retinue from A Midsummer Night's Dream. Lady Macbeth Seizing the Daggers shows the ghostly Lady Macbeth raising her finger to hush Macbeth as he yields his weapons. He also painted Lady Macbeth Sleepwalking, Macbeth Consulting the Vision of the Armed Head, and The Vision of Catherine of Aragon (from Henry VIII).

General / Biographical

Henry Fuseli (born Johann Heinrich Fussli) was a Swiss-born painter who settled in England after 1779. A Romantic and proto-Surrealist figure, he was a friend of William Blake (who eulogized him as 'both Turk and Jew') and Johann Caspar Lavater. He translated Lavater's Essays on Physiognomy and Winckelmann's Reflections on the Painting and Sculpture of the Greeks. He became a professor at the Royal Academy in 1790, beating Joseph Bonomi — which caused Sir Joshua Reynolds to resign in protest. Reynolds had earlier encouraged Fuseli to take up painting. He was rumored to fuel his imagination with raw pork and opium. His wife was Sophia Rawlings, and his former residence was at Somerset House.

Summary of Facts

Henry Fuseli (born Johann Heinrich Fussli, 1741-1825) is one of the most frequently asked about Romantic painters in quiz bowl, almost always identifiable through his signature work The Nightmare. The painting's iconic imagery — an incubus crouching on the chest of a sleeping woman while a pale-eyed horse peers through red curtains — appears in nearly every tossup and bonus about him and serves as the standard giveaway. Questions often note that multiple versions exist (at least four), that a Thomas Burke engraving made it widely known, and that the work may have been inspired by Fuseli's unrequited feelings for Anna Landolt, Lavater's niece. In 2024, Doja Cat recreated the painting's imagery for her Scarlet tour posters.

Fuseli's second most frequently referenced work is Thor Battering the Midgard Serpent, his Royal Academy diploma piece, which shows a nude Thor standing on the prow of a boat pulling a chained sea serpent while a companion cowers. Questions regularly note it was based on Thomas Percy's Northern Antiquities. His ambitious Milton Gallery — 47 paintings based on Paradise Lost produced over eight years — is referenced as a famous commercial failure, often alongside The Shepherd's Dream (depicting fairies bewitching a peasant). His patron Thomas Coutts rescued him from the resulting financial ruin.

Fuseli's Shakespeare-related works form another major cluster. He contributed to John Boydell's Shakespeare Gallery and produced numerous Shakespeare-inspired paintings, most notably Titania and Bottom (a tiny man in the palm of a donkey-headed figure surrounded by an eerie fairy retinue) and Lady Macbeth Seizing the Daggers (the ghostly Lady Macbeth raising a finger to hush Macbeth). Other Shakespeare works include Lady Macbeth Sleepwalking, Macbeth Consulting the Vision of the Armed Head, and The Vision of Catherine of Aragon.

Biographical clues center on his Swiss origins and English career. He was a friend of Johann Caspar Lavater (translating his Essays on Physiognomy) and William Blake (who eulogized him as 'both Turk and Jew'). He studied with Jakob Bodmer, was encouraged to paint by Joshua Reynolds, and became a Royal Academy professor in 1790 — beating Joseph Bonomi and prompting Reynolds's protest resignation. Less common works that appear include Silence, The Three Witches, Mad Kate, Oath of the Rutli (inspired by David's Oath of the Horatii), Kreimhild and Gunther, Odysseus in Front of Scylla and Charybdis, The Apotheosis of Penelope Boothby, and his unusual Trompe-L'oeil painting of bulls on paper.
