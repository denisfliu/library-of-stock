Last major French Symbolist poet. Most commonly identified by 'The Graveyard by the Sea' (Le Cimetière marin), 'The Young Fate' (La Jeune Parque), and his intellectual persona Monsieur Teste. Known for his 'Great Silence' — a roughly 20-year hiatus from poetry (c. 1892–1917) provoked by the death of Stéphane Mallarmé. Advocated for 'pure poetry' influenced by Poe. Refused to write novels. Walter Benjamin quoted him in the epigraph of 'The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction.'

Summary of Facts

Paul Valéry was the last major French Symbolist poet, associated with Stéphane Mallarmé and the broader Symbolist movement. He is most commonly identified by two major poems and his intellectual persona Monsieur Teste.

'The Graveyard by the Sea' (Le Cimetière marin) is his best-known poem, a 24-stanza meditation set in a cemetery overlooking the Mediterranean. It opens and closes with images of a 'quiet roof, where dove-sails saunter by' and famously ends with the command 'The wind is rising... we must try to live!' The poem addresses 'Zeno, Zeno, cruel philosopher Zeno' and asks 'Have you then pierced me with your feathered arrow,' describing an arrow as 'flying, but yet not flying' (referencing Zeno's paradox). The speaker climbs to see a 'temple of time,' describes death as 'that empty skull, that everlasting grin,' and discusses a 'worm unanswerable' for which 'Love' and 'hatred of self' are equally valid names — 'Life is his meat, and I am still his host.' The speaker takes a break from thought to luxuriate in 'long vistas of celestial calm,' and observes that fruit 'changes into delight through dissolution.' In Stephen Spender's translation, the speaker evokes a 'bitter, dark, and echoing reservoir' hiding 'intimations of my secret power.' In the final stanza, waves 'dare to explode out of the rocks in reeking spray.' It appears in the collection Charms (Charmes).

'The Young Fate' (La Jeune Parque) is a long poem of 512 alexandrine couplets that celebrates the French language. Published in 1917, it broke Valéry's 'Great Silence.' In G.S. Fraser's translation, it opens with 'Who or what is crying, if not only the wind, at this hour / Only, of uttermost diamonds?' and features 'distant diamonds dying.' It states 'I must, O Sun, worship this heart where you seek to know yourself.' The speaker is awoken by a serpent.

The 'Great Silence' was Valéry's roughly 20-year hiatus from poetry (c. 1892–1917), provoked by the death of Stéphane Mallarmé. It began after a 'stormy night' in Genoa where Valéry reminisced about a love affair and resolved to abolish the falsehoods of 'literature and sentiment.' During this period he studied mathematics and law, recorded his thoughts in notebooks called Cahiers, and created the character Monsieur Teste — an extremely dedicated intellectual whose motto is 'Que peut un homme?' ('Of what is man capable?') and who claims 'stupidity is not my strong suit.' Valéry wrote a 'series of essays' about Teste.

Other works include 'Sketch of a Serpent,' whose title creature declares 'Tremble, mortals — I'm strong when my insatiate yawn stretches to snap a spring' and calls the universe 'a defect in the purity of non-being' — Yvor Winters praised this poem as the greatest ever written. 'The Bee' is a sonnet claiming 'A vivid and a clear-cut pain / Is better than a drowsy torment,' appearing in the collection Charms. His adaptation of the Faust legend (Mon Faust / My Faust) features a secretary called Demoiselle Luste who seduces Faust in his garden. He wrote a pseudo-Platonic dialogue in which Eupalinos describes the qualities of an architect.

His aphorisms include 'a poem is never finished, only abandoned' and 'One should be light like a bird, and not like a feather' (quoted by Italo Calvino in his Norton Lectures on 'lightness'). He advocated for 'pure poetry,' arguing that poetry with purity builds a 'system of reciprocal relations,' influenced by Poe's 'The Poetic Principle.' He claimed 'all known literature is written in the language of common sense' except for Rimbaud's. He found the essential connection between Baudelaire and Poe in the first line of Mallarmé's 'The Tomb of Edgar Poe.'

His poem 'Palme' inspired James Merrill's 'Lost in Translation,' in which it is called a 'sunlit paradigm' evoking a 'wellspring of authority.' Rilke translated 'Palme' into German. He collaborated with composer Germaine Tailleferre on Cantate de Narcisse. Paul Muldoon included translations of Valéry in his Pulitzer-winning Moy Sand and Gravel. Carolyn Kizer borrows his line 'The whole green sky is dying' in a villanelle about the Gulf War. Critics link Giorgios Seferis's metaphysical poetry to Valéry's work.

He filled Anatole France's seat on the Académie Française. André Breton was a friend; the Surrealist Manifesto cites Valéry's famous refusal to write novels because he could never bring himself to write 'The Marquise went out at five o'clock.' Edmund Wilson relayed an anecdote in Axel's Castle about Valéry angering blowhards by being too well-dressed. Walter Benjamin opens 'The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction' with Valéry's quote: 'For the last twenty years neither matter nor space nor time has been what it was.' Sartre used 'Paul Valéry is no longer here' as an example of negation in Being and Nothingness. Valéry wrote an essay defending Berthe Morisot for a 1941 exhibition at the Musée de l'Orangerie, and signed a petition (with Camus) to commute Robert Brasillach's death sentence. Miyazaki's film The Wind Rises takes its title from a Valéry poem.
