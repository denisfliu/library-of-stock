Summary of Facts

Paul Valéry was the last major French Symbolist poet, associated with Stéphane Mallarmé and the broader Symbolist movement. He is most commonly identified by two major poems and his intellectual persona Monsieur Teste.

'The Graveyard by the Sea' (Le Cimetière marin) is his best-known poem. It opens and closes with images of a 'quiet roof, where dove-sails saunter by' and famously ends with the command 'The wind is rising... we must try to live!' The poem addresses 'Zeno, Zeno, cruel philosopher Zeno' and asks 'Have you then pierced me with your feathered arrow,' describing an arrow as 'flying, but yet not flying' (referencing Zeno's paradox). The speaker climbs to see a 'temple of time,' and describes death as 'that empty skull, that everlasting grin.' It appears in the collection Charms (Charmes).

'The Young Fate' (La Jeune Parque) is a long poem of 512 alexandrine couplets that celebrates the French language. Published in 1917, it broke Valéry's 'Great Silence.' It opens with 'Who is that weeping, if not simply the wind?' and 'distant diamonds dying,' and states 'I must, O Sun, worship this heart where you seek to know yourself.'

The 'Great Silence' was Valéry's roughly 20-year hiatus from poetry (c. 1892–1917), provoked by the death of Stéphane Mallarmé. It began after a 'stormy night' in Genoa where Valéry reminisced about a love affair and resolved to abolish the falsehoods of 'literature and sentiment.' During this period he studied mathematics and law, recorded his thoughts in notebooks called Cahiers, and created the character Monsieur Teste — an extremely dedicated intellectual whose motto is 'Que peut un homme?' ('Of what is man capable?') and who claims 'stupidity is not my strong suit.'

Other works include 'Sketch of a Serpent,' whose title creature declares 'Tremble, mortals — I'm strong when my insatiate yawn stretches to snap a spring'; 'The Bee,' a sonnet claiming 'A vivid and a clear-cut pain / Is better than a drowsy torment'; an adaptation of the Faust legend featuring a woman named Lust; and a pseudo-Platonic dialogue in which Eupalinos describes the qualities of an architect. His aphorisms include 'a poem is never finished, only abandoned' and 'One should be light like a bird, and not like a feather' (quoted by Italo Calvino in his Norton Lectures on 'lightness'). His poem 'Palme' inspired James Merrill's 'Lost in Translation.' He filled Anatole France's seat on the Académie Française. Carolyn Kizer borrows his line 'The whole green sky is dying' in a villanelle about the Gulf War. Critics link Giorgios Seferis's metaphysical poetry to Valéry's work.
