Beloved

Morrison's most celebrated novel, dedicated to 'Sixty Million and more,' inspired by the true story of Margaret Garner. Set at 124 Bluestone Road, the escaped slave Sethe is haunted by the ghost of the daughter she killed to save from slavery. Paul D arrives from the Sweet Home plantation, where they were both enslaved under Schoolteacher. Sethe's back bears scars she describes as a 'chokecherry tree.' The ghost Beloved materializes as a young woman. Denver, Sethe's surviving daughter, eventually seeks help from the community. Key moments include Sethe attacking Mr. Bodwin with an ice pick (mistaking him for Schoolteacher), Sixo being burned alive, and the famous opening line '124 was spiteful.'

Song of Solomon

The novel follows Milkman Dead III, named after the janitor Freddie catches Ruth breastfeeding him well into childhood. His sisters First Corinthians and Magdalene Called Lena were named by randomly putting a pin in the Bible. The novel opens with Robert Smith jumping from the roof of Mercy Hospital. Milkman discovers the Seven Days, a vengeful society, and his friend Guitar Bains is a member. He travels to Shalimar, Virginia, to learn his family history and discovers his grandfather Solomon could fly. The theme of flight permeates the novel, from the opening song 'O Sugarman' to Milkman leaping toward Guitar at the end. His aunt Pilate has no navel.

The Bluest Eye

Morrison's first novel, set in Lorain, Ohio. Pecola Breedlove, an African-American girl, desires blue eyes as a symbol of white beauty. She is raped by her alcoholic father Cholly, and the resulting premature baby dies — foreshadowed by the failure of marigolds to bloom. The novel's chapter titles parody Dick and Jane readers. Pecola is manipulated by the charlatan Soaphead Church. Claudia and Frieda MacTeer are her neighbors who plant the marigolds.

Paradise

The third novel in Morrison's trilogy. Opens with the line 'They shoot the white girl first,' but never reveals which Convent resident is white. Nine men from the all-Black town of Ruby, Oklahoma attack the women at the Convent. The town was founded by darker-skinned Black residents fleeing colorism in their former home of Haven. The Oven, a communal structure, has a disputed inscription about 'the Furrow of His Brow.' Midwife Lone DuPres is shunned for allegedly causing birth defects.

Tar Baby

Morrison said the title 'came to mean the black woman who can hold things together.'

Playing in the Dark

Morrison's book of literary criticism adapted from her 1990 Massey Lectures. She explores the 'Africanist presence' in American literature and asserts the importance of 'American Africanism' to the American literary tradition. The chapter 'Romancing the Shadow' analyzes Poe's Arthur Gordon Pym and the ending's 'figurations of impenetrable whiteness.' She also discusses Willa Cather's Sapphira and the Slave Girl.

General / Biographical

Morrison was the first Black woman to win the Nobel Prize in Literature (1993). She was an editor at Random House, where she compiled The Black Book and diversified publishing. She was born Chloe Ardelia Wofford. She won the 1988 Pulitzer Prize after dozens of writers petitioned for it. Her works are often set in Ohio (Lorain, specifically). She collaborated on operas including Desdemona (with Peter Sellars) and Margaret Garner (with Richard Danielpour). Her acceptance speech included the parable of the blind old woman and the bird that represents language.

Summary of Facts

Toni Morrison is among the most heavily clued American authors in quizbowl, recognized as the first Black woman to win the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1993. Born Chloe Ardelia Wofford, she served as an editor at Random House where she compiled The Black Book and championed Black authors. Her works are frequently set in Ohio and the American South, and she is known for her rich exploration of African-American history, memory, and identity.

Beloved is Morrison's most prominent work in clues, set at 124 Bluestone Road where the escaped slave Sethe is haunted by the ghost of the daughter she killed to prevent her return to slavery. The novel is dedicated to 'Sixty Million and more' and was inspired by the real story of Margaret Garner. Key details include Sethe's back scars described as a 'chokecherry tree,' Paul D's arrival from the Sweet Home plantation, Schoolteacher's cruelty (including burning Sixo alive), and the famous opening '124 was spiteful.'

Song of Solomon follows Milkman Dead III, named because Freddie caught Ruth breastfeeding him well into childhood. The theme of flight pervades the novel from the opening (Robert Smith jumping from Mercy Hospital's roof and the song 'O Sugarman') to Milkman's discovery that his ancestor Solomon could fly. The Seven Days vigilante society and Guitar Bains are frequently clued, as is Milkman's journey to Shalimar, Virginia.

The Bluest Eye, Morrison's debut, depicts Pecola Breedlove's tragic desire for blue eyes as a symbol of white beauty. Pecola is raped by her father Cholly, her premature baby dies, and the failure of marigolds to bloom foreshadows this. Jazz and Paradise complete the Beloved trilogy: Jazz features Joe Trace's murder of Dorcas and Violet stabbing the corpse; Paradise opens 'They shoot the white girl first' and depicts nine men from Ruby, Oklahoma attacking women at the Convent.

Other works include Tar Baby (Son and Jadine on Isle des Chevaliers with Valerian Street), Sula (National Suicide Day, the Bottom, Shadrack), A Mercy (17th-century Virginia, Florens and Jacob Vaark), and her only short story 'Recitatif' (Roberta and Twyla's racially ambiguous friendship). Playing in the Dark, her literary criticism adapted from the Massey Lectures, examines the 'Africanist presence' in American literature. She also collaborated on operas and was known for her Nobel acceptance speech parable about a blind woman and a bird representing language.
