French photographer and co-founder of Magnum Photos, considered the father of modern photojournalism and street photography. Best known for his concept of the 'decisive moment' — capturing a specific instant when visual elements are perfectly aligned. His iconic image Behind the Gare Saint-Lazare shows a man leaping over a puddle. Published The Decisive Moment (also known as Images on the Sly, with cover designed by Henri Matisse). Documented major 20th-century events including Gandhi's assassination and the fall of Nationalist China.

General / Biographical

French photographer (1908–2004), considered the father of modern photojournalism and a pioneer of street/candid photography. Co-founded the Magnum Photos agency with Robert Capa and others. Used a 35mm Leica camera. Developed the concept of 'the decisive moment.' Photographed Gandhi an hour before his assassination. Documented the last days of the Kuomintang in China. Also photographed the last imperial eunuchs in China. Assisted filmmaker Jean Renoir on A Day in the Country and The Rules of the Game. Told William Eggleston that 'color is bullshit.' Said all it took to do his job was 'one finger, one eye, and two legs.' Had an epiphany viewing a Martin Munkacsi photograph of three naked children running into the surf of Lake Tanganyika. Summary of Facts

Henri Cartier-Bresson is a French photographer widely considered the father of modern photojournalism and a pioneer of street/candid photography. He is most closely associated with the concept of 'the decisive moment' — the idea that a photographer must have the instinct to recognize and capture a specific instant in time when visual elements are perfectly balanced. This concept forms the title and central argument of his seminal photobook, known in English as The Decisive Moment and in the original French as Images à la sauvette ('Images on the Sly'). Henri Matisse designed the cover of this book, which also documents Gandhi's funeral and the founding of Communist China.

His single most iconic photograph is Behind the Gare Saint-Lazare, which shows a man mid-leap over a pool of standing water behind the Gare Saint-Lazare train station in Paris. The word 'RAILOWSKY' appears twice in the background, and the man is seen leaping from a horizontal ladder. This image is universally cited as the quintessential illustration of the decisive moment. Nearly as famous is Hyeres (also identified simply as his bicycle/staircase photograph), which looks down from the top of a winding spiral staircase at a cyclist in motion on the street below.

Cartier-Bresson co-founded Magnum Photos with Robert Capa and others, a photographic agency dedicated to spreading documentary photography. He used a 35mm Leica camera throughout much of his career. He documented a series called 'The Last Days of the Kuomintang,' photographing the last imperial eunuchs in China and capturing the frenzied rush to buy gold in Shanghai during the Communist takeover. He photographed Gandhi just an hour before his assassination, and the images from India in this period appeared in The Decisive Moment.

Among his other notable works is a portrait of sculptor Alberto Giacometti, in which Giacometti strides behind one of his own Walking Man sculptures, unconsciously imitating its pose — a perfect example of the decisive moment in portraiture. A 2005 exhibition organized by Tobia Bezzola paired Walking Man I with Cartier-Bresson's man-jumping image. His photograph Children Playing in the Ruins, associated with the Spanish Civil War, features a hole blasted into a wall forming most of the frame. A lesser-known photograph shows a man in a raincoat walking with a child, both dwarfed by a giant poster of Lenin in a similar pose. He also photographed a Barong Dance from Bali, which appeared in Antoine Artaud's book The Dances of Bali, and a scene at an Indian funeral pyre.

Before his photography career, Cartier-Bresson worked as an assistant to filmmaker Jean Renoir on A Day in the Country and The Rules of the Game. He reportedly had a creative epiphany after viewing a Martin Munkacsi photograph of three naked children running into the surf of Lake Tanganyika. He famously told William Eggleston that 'color is bullshit.' His aphorism about his craft was that it required only 'one finger, one eye, and two legs.' A 2015 essay by Teju Cole, titled 'Perfect and Unrehearsed,' discusses his image of three men riding a canoe in Suzhou Creek.
