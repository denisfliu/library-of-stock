Bach composed three major collections of 'partitas': Six Partitas for keyboard (Clavier-Übung I, BWV 825–830); three Partitas for solo violin (paired with three Sonatas, BWV 1001–1006); and one Partita for solo flute in A minor (BWV 1013). The term 'partita' was essentially synonymous with 'suite' in Bach's usage. The violin Partita No. 2 in D minor ends with the celebrated Chaconne; the keyboard partitas open the Clavier-Übung series.
Sonatas and Partitas for Solo Violin (BWV 1001–1006)

Six works for solo violin by J. S. Bach: three Sonatas (Nos. 1, 3, 5) and three Partitas (Nos. 2, 4, 6), alternating. The probable inspiration was Johann Paul von Westhoff's six solo violin partitas, which make extensive use of double stops. Joseph Joachim revived the playing of these works. Partita No. 2 in D Minor – Chaconne (BWV 1004)

The fifth and final movement of the Partita No. 2 in D minor. Based on a four-bar ostinato (romanesca chord progression) with 64 variations. Called 'one of the greatest achievements of any man' by violinist Joshua Bell. Alex Ross observes hints of Monteverdi's lament figure in this movement. Jascha Heifetz famously performed it. The last of the six works (No. 6 in E major) was also transcribed for lute. Wikipedia

Six Keyboard Partitas (Clavier-Übung I, BWV 825–830)

Six partitas for keyboard, forming the first volume of Bach's Clavier-Übung (Keyboard Practice). Published 1726–1731. Their tonics follow a pattern of expanding intervals in alternating directions: B-flat, C, A, D, G, E. A hypothetical seventh would follow the same pattern. The last keyboard partita includes two movements derived from an early version of the sixth violin sonata (Corrente and Tempo di Gavotta). Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

The term 'partita' was essentially synonymous with 'suite' in Baroque music and was used by Bach for several major collections. J. S. Bach's three most important sets of partitas are: (1) the Six Sonatas and Partitas for Solo Violin (BWV 1001–1006), alternating three sonatas with three partitas; (2) Six Keyboard Partitas (Clavier-Übung I, BWV 825–830); and (3) a Partita for Solo Flute in A minor (BWV 1013), his only unaccompanied flute work.

The Sonatas and Partitas for Solo Violin are the most frequently clued set. Their probable inspiration was Johann Paul von Westhoff's six solo violin partitas, which used extensive double stops. Joseph Joachim revived their performance in the 19th century. Nathan Milstein and Henryk Szeryng each recorded them twice in a 'Romantic' style. The Partita No. 2 in D minor culminates in the celebrated Chaconne (Ciaccona) — the fifth and final movement, presenting 64 variations on a four-bar romanesca ostinato. Violinist Joshua Bell called it 'one of the greatest achievements of any man.' Alex Ross detected hints of Monteverdi's lament figure in it. Jascha Heifetz famously performed it. The Partita No. 1 in B minor is unusual in that each of its four movements is paired with a 'Double' (variation), and it ends with a 'Tempo di Borea' instead of the expected Gigue. The Partita No. 3 in E major opens with a 'Preludio' that Bach later arranged as the 'Sinfonia' for Cantata 29, and includes a 'Gavotte en Rondeau'; it was later transcribed for lute. The third Sonata's second-movement fugue subject derives from the chorale 'Komm, Heiliger Geist.'

The Six Keyboard Partitas form the first volume of Clavier-Übung (Keyboard Practice). Their tonics follow a pattern of expanding intervals in alternating directions: B-flat, C, A, D, G, E — suggesting what key a hypothetical seventh might use. The last keyboard partita incorporates two movements (a Corrente and a Tempo di Gavotta) derived from an early version of the sixth violin sonata. These keyboard partitas belong to the same lineage as the French and English Suites for keyboard.

In modern usage, the title 'partita' has been adopted by composers like Caroline Shaw (Partita for 8 Voices, 2013 — begins 'To the side,' includes 'the detail of the pattern is movement,' uses Sacred Harp style), Luigi Dallapiccola (1932 Partita, his first major success, ending with 'Naenia B.M.V.'), and by Elliott Carter for the first movement of his symphony 'sum fluxae pretium spei.' Mozart's Serenade No. 10 for 12 winds and string bass is nicknamed the 'Gran Partita.'
