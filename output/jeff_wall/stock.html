Canadian conceptual photographer (born 1946) known for large-format, staged 'near documentary' photographs, often displayed as backlit transparencies. Known for recreating famous artworks as photographs: Picture for Women (after Manet's A Bar at the Folies-Bergère), The Destroyed Room (after Delacroix's The Death of Sardanapalus), and A Sudden Gust of Wind (after Hokusai). Also known for Mimic (depicting a racist white man mocking an Asian man) and Dead Troops Talk (sold for $3.7 million in 2012). Part of the Vancouver photoconceptualist school.
Picture for Women (1979)

Large-format photograph recreating Manet's A Bar at the Folies-Bergère. Shows a camera on a tripod in the center of the frame between a male photographer (Wall himself) in profile and a female model, all reflected in a mirror. Two metal stands split the frame into three sections, and the woman on the left rests her hands on a table. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Jeff Wall (born 1946) is a Canadian conceptual photographer from Vancouver, known for large-format staged photographs displayed as backlit transparencies. He is part of the Vancouver photoconceptualist school alongside Kevin Lum and Rodney Graham. Wall frequently recreates well-known artworks as photographs: Picture for Women (1979) recreates Manet's A Bar at the Folies-Bergère, placing Wall's camera on a tripod between himself and a female model, both reflected in a mirror; The Destroyed Room (1978) recreates Delacroix's The Death of Sardanapalus as a wrecked woman's room with torn red walls exposing insulation, broken furniture, a slashed mattress, and clothes strewn on the floor; and A Sudden Gust of Wind (1993) recreates a Hokusai print from Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji with men in business suits and paper thrown into an airstream.

Wall's Mimic (1982) shows a White man pulling at the corner of his eye to mock an Asian man walking past him on the street. Dead Troops Talk (1992) is a staged image of wounded soldiers in a trench set during the Soviet-Afghan War; it sold for $3.7 million in 2012 (then the third most expensive photograph ever sold) and is extensively analyzed in the final essay of Susan Sontag's Regarding the Pain of Others. Wall also made Restoration (a 180-degree panoramic photo of workers mending a large painting) and spent several days photographing a man cleaning the Barcelona Pavilion. Two of his photographs are identical except that one shows an octopus and the other a pile of beans on a table.
