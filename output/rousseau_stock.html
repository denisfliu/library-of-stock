Swiss-French Enlightenment philosopher known for The Social Contract ('man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains'), Discourse on Inequality (amour-propre vs. amour de soi, the first man to fence off land), and the educational treatise Emile. His ideas on the general will, the noble savage, and natural pity deeply influenced political philosophy.
The Social Contract and the General Will

Rousseau's The Social Contract is his central work of political philosophy. Its famous opening line is 'man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains.' The work distinguishes between the 'general will' and the 'will of all,' proposes a Lawgiver who inspires political conditions but does not legislate, and argues that those who refuse to join the 'whole body' will be 'forced to be free.' Mary Astell anticipated its opening with her question 'If all men are born free, how is it that all women are born slaves?' Charles Mills's The Racial Contract plays on its title.

Discourse on Inequality (Second Discourse)

The Discourse on Inequality (Rousseau's Second Discourse), written for a contest by the Academy of Dijon, traces civil society to the first man who 'enclosed a piece of ground' and declared 'This is mine.' It contrasts amour de soi (natural self-love shared with animals) with amour-propre (self-love dependent on others' perception), argues man in the state of nature is happy rather than brutal, introduces the concept of human 'perfectibility,' and contains the stag hunt scenario that inspired Brian Skyrms's game theory. It is dedicated to the Republic of Geneva.

Emile, or On Education

Emile is Rousseau's treatise on education written in the style of a Bildungsroman. It contains the 'Profession of Faith of a Savoyard Vicar,' an anti-religious polemic that Voltaire admired and that caused the book to be burned. It proposes educating a 'natural man' and argues women should be educated for attractiveness and domesticity (prompting Mary Wollstonecraft's rebuttal in Vindication of the Rights of Woman). Sophie is Emile's female counterpart, and in a posthumous sequel she is drugged and raped.

General / Biographical

Rousseau is identified as a Swiss-French or Genevan Enlightenment philosopher. He saw 'another universe' when reading the Academy of Dijon's essay prompt about whether culture purifies morals. He accused David Hume of plotting against him. Derrida used masturbation to illustrate his reworking of Rousseau's concept of the supplement. Paul de Man analyzed Derrida's reading and also analyzed Rousseau's Confessions (the Marion ribbon episode). Reveries of a Solitary Walker describes his happiest moments on St. Peter's Island. Heinrich Mann asserted Rousseau had 'the greatest, most palpable success' of anyone who ever wrote. Germaine de Stael and Plutarch's Parallel Lives also connect to him. He also wrote the Essay on the Origin of Languages and a letter to D'Alembert arguing against a theater in Geneva.

Summary of Facts

Jean-Jacques Rousseau appears in quizbowl as a Swiss-French Enlightenment philosopher whose most common giveaway is the opening of The Social Contract: 'man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains.' The Social Contract distinguishes between the 'general will' and the 'will of all,' proposes a Lawgiver, and argues those who refuse to join the community will be 'forced to be free.' Mary Astell anticipated its opening by asking 'if all men are born free, how is it that all women are born slaves?' Charles Mills's The Racial Contract plays on its title.

The Discourse on Inequality (Second Discourse), written for a contest by the Academy of Dijon, traces civil society to the first man who fenced off land and said 'This is mine.' It contrasts amour de soi (natural self-preservation) with amour-propre (self-love dependent on others), argues man in the state of nature is happy rather than brutal, and introduces human 'perfectibility.' Its stag hunt scenario inspired Brian Skyrms's game theory. Rousseau argues against Mandeville's Fable of the Bees that mankind has natural pity.

Emile, or On Education is structured as a Bildungsroman containing the 'Profession of Faith of a Savoyard Vicar,' which caused the book to be burned. It controversially argues women should be educated for domesticity, prompting Mary Wollstonecraft's Vindication of the Rights of Woman. Sophie is Emile's female counterpart.

Julie, or the New Heloise is an epistolary novel with Saint-Preux as teacher; Judith Shklar noted its 'happy family' contradicts Rousseau's republican ideals. Wolmar is based on d'Holbach.

Biographically, Rousseau accused David Hume of plotting against him, wrote Reveries of a Solitary Walker, and wrote the Essay on the Origin of Languages (critiqued in Derrida's Of Grammatology). Paul de Man analyzed the Marion ribbon theft in his Confessions. Nietzsche used 'Rousseauist' thinking as a foil, and Germaine de Stael described adopting some of his views.
