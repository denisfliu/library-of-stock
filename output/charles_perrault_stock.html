17th-century French author who popularized fairy tales with his collection Tales of Mother Goose (Contes de ma mere l'Oye, also known as Stories or Tales from Past Times with Morals). The collection includes definitive versions of Cinderella (glass slipper, fairy godmother), Sleeping Beauty (Princess Aurora, spindle curse), Puss in Boots (Marquis of Carabas), Bluebeard (forbidden room, blood-stained key), Little Red Riding Hood (whose version ends abruptly with the wolf eating her), and Hop-o'-My-Thumb (seven-league boots). Also sparked the Quarrel of the Ancients and the Moderns by reading his poem The Age of Louis the Great at the Academie Francaise, which mocked Homer's shield of Achilles passage. Angela Carter translated his fairy tales and adapted them in The Bloody Chamber. Ravel's Mother Goose suite is based on his tales. His contemporary Madame d'Aulnoy coined the term 'fairy tale.'

Summary of Facts

Charles Perrault was a 17th-century French author best known for his collection of fairy tales, Tales from Past Times (also called Tales of Mother Goose or Contes de ma mere l'Oye). He adopted the persona of Mother Goose for this collection, which includes popular versions of Cinderella, Sleeping Beauty, Puss in Boots, Bluebeard, Little Red Riding Hood, and Hop-o'-My-Thumb. The name 'Prince Charming' was inspired by a passage from his version of Sleeping Beauty. Angela Carter was commissioned to translate his fairy tales into English during the 1970s, which led to her writing The Bloody Chamber. Carlo Collodi translated Perrault's tales into Italian in 1875. Many 'anti-tales' (fairy tales with tragic endings) were adapted from his stories. His contemporary Madame d'Aulnoy coined the term 'fairy tale.' Ravel's Mother Goose suite (Ma mere l'oye) is based on his tales.

Perrault's Cinderella introduces the glass slipper, pumpkin coach, and fairy godmother. Six mice become dapple gray horses. The tale has thousands of worldwide versions including the Chinese Ye Xian and Greek Rhodopis. It inspired Prokofiev's ballet (featuring Skinny and Dumpy as stepsisters, four season fairies, and a Rose Adagio), Rossini's opera La Cenerentola (where the title character is named Angelina and Don Magnifico replaces the evil stepmother), and an Anne Sexton poem.

Perrault's Bluebeard (Aarne-Thompson type 312) tells of a murderous nobleman whose new wife opens a forbidden room containing the bodies of his previous wives, staining a key with blood. She is saved by her dragoon and musketeer brothers. An English variant is 'Mr. Fox,' which repeats 'be bold, be bold.' The tale inspired Bartok's opera Duke Bluebeard's Castle (Judith opens seven doors; 'blood motif' dissonant minor second; 'Are you afraid?'), Angela Carter's 'The Bloody Chamber' (ruby choker, twelve mirrors), Vonnegut's novel Bluebeard (Rabo Karabekian's 'Now It's the Women's Turn' in a potato barn), and Paul Dukas's opera Ariane et Barbe-bleue.

Perrault's Sleeping Beauty tells of Princess Aurora cursed by the evil fairy Carabosse to prick her finger on a spindle on her 16th birthday. The Lilac Fairy softens the curse to 100 years of sleep, broken by a prince's kiss. Tchaikovsky's ballet (choreographed by Petipa) features the Rose Adagio with four suitors, the Bluebird pas de deux in the third-act wedding, and roles for Puss-in-Boots. Enrico Cecchetti originated the Bluebird role. Frederick Ashton's 1948 production was his first full-length ballet.

Perrault's Puss in Boots features a clever cat who helps a poor miller's son marry a princess by creating the persona of the Marquis of Carabas and tricking a shapeshifting ogre into becoming a mouse. Earlier versions by Basile and Straparola feature 'Lucky Constantino.' Ludwig Tieck wrote a metatheatrical play about an audience reacting to a performance of this tale.

Perrault's Little Red Riding Hood notably ends abruptly with the wolf eating the girl — there is no rescue by a huntsman (that was added by the Brothers Grimm). French oral tradition includes a choice between the 'path of pins' and the 'path of needles.' The earliest precursor appears in Egbert of Liege's Fecunda ratis.

Perrault also wrote Hop-o'-My-Thumb (a tiny character who steals seven-league boots from a sleeping ogre and tricks the ogre into killing his own daughters by swapping their crowns with nightcaps) and Les Souhaits ridicules (a black pudding craving causes a couple to use wishes ridiculously).

Perrault also played a key role in the Quarrel of the Ancients and the Moderns. He outraged members of the Academie Francaise by reading his poem The Age of Louis the Great, which mocked Homer's shield of Achilles passage and contained a couplet praising the modern Age of Louis over the ancient age of Augustus.
