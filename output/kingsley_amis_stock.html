Lucky Jim

His paper on shipbuilding techniques is plagiarized in an Italian translation. Margaret Peel is a manipulative character who claims to have attempted suicide after Catchpole dumps her. Jim grows close to Christine Callaghan after she helps him hide a bedsheet he burned by smoking in bed. Philip Larkin served as the basis for the protagonist.

Martin Amis (Father-Son Relationship)

Kingsley's son Martin Amis was also a major novelist. Kingsley called Martin a 'little shit' in a letter for spending a year abroad as a tax exile. Martin's memoir Experience reprints letters to Kingsley and his second wife Elizabeth Jane Howard, including a description of asking for 'one little more hug' while Kingsley was dying. Kingsley accused Martin of 'breaking the rules' and 'buggering about with the reader' for including a character named after himself in a novel. Kingsley admitted on TV he hadn't read Martin's London Fields, though it was dedicated to him. Kingsley often expressed distaste for Vladimir Nabokov and Saul Bellow, the literary heroes of Martin. Terry Eagleton called Kingsley 'a drink-sodden, self-hating reviler of women, gays and liberals.'

Other Works and Associations

Amis wrote The Old Devils, in which life at the Bible and Crown pub is changed by the return of Alun Weaver from England. He was part of the Movement literary group. He reread G.K. Chesterton's The Man Who Was Thursday every year. He wrote The King's English, a usage book that criticizes the frequent use of the word 'fuck' in James Kelman's How late it was, how late. He wrote the poem 'The Bookshop Idyll' containing 'Women are really much nicer than men: / No wonder we like them.' He requested du Maurier write 'The Breakthrough' for a sci-fi anthology that never got published. Summary of Facts

Kingsley Amis was an English novelist and poet associated with the Movement literary group. His most famous work is Lucky Jim, a comic campus novel about Jim Dixon, a history professor who gives a disastrous drunken lecture on 'Merrie England' and is subsequently fired. Jim's paper on shipbuilding techniques is plagiarized in Italian, and the manipulative Margaret Peel claims attempted suicide. Philip Larkin reportedly served as the basis for the protagonist. Jim grows close to Christine Callaghan after she helps him hide a bedsheet he burned by smoking in bed.

Amis also wrote The Old Devils, in which life at a Welsh pub called the Bible and Crown is changed by the return of Alun Weaver from England. He wrote The King's English, a usage book that criticizes frequent profanity in James Kelman's How Late It Was, How Late. He was a sci-fi enthusiast who requested Daphne du Maurier write 'The Breakthrough' for an anthology. He reread G.K. Chesterton's The Man Who Was Thursday annually.

Kingsley's relationship with his son Martin Amis is frequently clued. He called Martin a 'little shit' in a letter for spending a year as a tax exile. Martin's memoir Experience reprints letters to Kingsley and his second wife Elizabeth Jane Howard. Kingsley accused Martin of 'breaking the rules' for self-inserting in a novel and admitted on TV he hadn't read London Fields, which was dedicated to him. Kingsley disliked Nabokov and Bellow, Martin's literary heroes. Terry Eagleton called Kingsley 'a drink-sodden, self-hating reviler of women, gays and liberals.'

Wendy Cope titled a collection Making Cocoa for Kingsley Amis, which includes the fictional poet Jason Strugnell and parodies of T.S. Eliot's style.
