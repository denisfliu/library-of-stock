General / Biographical

Spanish Baroque painter from Seville. Contemporary of Velazquez. Cultivated 'cool,' 'warm,' and 'vaporous' (estilo vaporoso) styles depending on degree of tenebrism. Early career influenced by Zurbaran (the 'Spanish Caravaggio'). Died after falling from scaffolding at a church in Cadiz while painting Mystic Marriage of St. Catherine. Painted St. Isidore of Seville (patron saint of the Internet) for Seville Cathedral.

Summary of Facts

Bartolome Esteban Murillo (1617-1682) was a Spanish Baroque painter from Seville, where he spent most of his career. He was a contemporary of Velazquez. His paintings are categorized into three styles based on degree of tenebrism: 'cool,' 'warm,' and 'vaporous' (estilo vaporoso), with the vaporous style representing his mature work with soft, diffuse tones.

He is best known for his nearly twenty versions of the Immaculate Conception, which feature Mary in a blue cloak surrounded by cherubs, with putti blending into the background like haze. In many versions the central figure stands on a crescent moon surrounded by cherubs. The Immaculate Conception of Los Venerables was possibly the most expensive painting in the world when the Louvre purchased it from Marshal Soult's estate. 'The Colossal' version unusually features a full moon at the bottom with cherubs struggling to hold up a cloud. Unlike other painters' versions, his omit references to the Marian litanies. In one version, Mary stands atop a pile of angels.

His genre scenes of the street children and beggars of Seville are equally famous. The Young Beggar and Boys Eating Grapes and Melon depict poor children in vivid detail. Early tenebrist paintings include The Young Beggar and Girl of Galicia. Two Women at a Window (A Girl and Her Duena) shows a flirtatious woman lifting her veil (or hiding her snicker beneath her shawl) next to a somber bespectacled duena holding a boy's head, depicting Galician-born prostitutes in Madrid.

Murillo's early career was influenced by Zurbaran (the 'Spanish Caravaggio'), and like Velazquez, he lightened his use of tenebrism later in his career. He was also later influenced by Velazquez, the leading light of Philip IV's court. He created the most widely circulated portrait of St. Isidore of Seville (patron saint of the Internet) for Seville Cathedral. Other religious works include Vision of St. Anthony (man prostrate on knee, hands out at throng of angels), Saints Justa and Rufina, Young Man with a Basket of Fruit, the unfinished Heavenly and Earthly Trinities, and a Penitent Magdalene featuring a skull and cross on a ledge with the saint clad in red looking upward and to the left. A portrait attributed to him features concentric circles representing the heliocentric model, linked to the Galileo legend of 'Eppur si muove.' He died after falling from scaffolding while painting the Mystic Marriage of St. Catherine for a Capuchin church in Cadiz.
