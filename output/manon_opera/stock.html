Manon Lescaut (Puccini) – The Work as a Whole

Italian opera (1893) by Giacomo Puccini, also based on the Abbé Prévost novel. Puccini dismissed Massenet's opera as full of 'powder and minuets.' The title character dies on the outskirts of New Orleans in a desert. The opera includes an intermezzo between Acts 2 and 3 representing the protagonist's journey to Le Havre; the finale draws on a string quartet Puccini composed for the funeral of the Duke of Aosta. Summary of Facts

The character Manon Lescaut, from the Abbé Prévost novel, titles two major operas: Jules Massenet's Manon (1884) and Giacomo Puccini's Manon Lescaut (1893). Both center on the same character: a young French woman torn between love for the Chevalier Des Grieux and a life of luxury and comfort, who ultimately dies in Des Grieux's arms.

In Massenet's opera, Manon sings the celebrated aria 'Adieu, notre petite table' when she chooses to leave Des Grieux for the wealthier Monsieur de Bretigny. Later she also sings the seduction aria 'N'est-ce plus ma main' before being arrested for cheating at gambling. The opera ends with Manon dying in Des Grieux's arms while being marched to Le Havre for deportation. Carmen is considered a precursor to the character type that Manon represents. In 1974, choreographer Kenneth MacMillan created the ballet L'histoire de Manon using Massenet's music but not drawing from the opera itself; Massenet's 'Elegie' underscores the pas de deux between Manon and Des Grieux in that ballet.

Puccini's Manon Lescaut has become the more frequently clued work. Puccini famously dismissed Massenet's opera on the same subject as full of 'powder and minuets,' while boasting of his own Italian passion — though he included a minuet of his own in Act 2. In Act 1, Des Grieux sings the aria 'Donna non vidi mai' ('I have never seen a woman such as this') upon meeting Manon, who is heading to a convent. In Act 2, Manon sings 'In quelle trine morbide,' expressing frustration over how boring her current lover Geronte is. The opera includes an intermezzo between Acts 2 and 3 representing the protagonist's journey to Le Havre, with descriptions of a character's thoughts written into the score. The title character dies in the desert on the outskirts of New Orleans, singing the aria 'Sola, perduta, abbandonata' ('lonely, lost, and abandoned') as she nears death. The opera's finale draws on a string quartet Puccini composed for the funeral of the Duke of Aosta.
