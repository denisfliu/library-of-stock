[B1] 2024 ARCADIA (2024) | Diff: 8 | Literature
     Leadin: A ballate by this poet inspired the refrain "Because I do not hope to turn again" in T. S. Eliot's poem "Ash Wednesday." For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: Guido Cavalcanti):
    Name this poet who wrote several canzoni in the "sweet new style," including one whose first line translates as "a lady asked me." This poet was referred to as "first of my friends" in the work La Vita Nuova.
  Part 2 (ans: love [accept courtly love or divine love; accept Amore or Ars Amatoria]):
    Cavalcanti's canzone "Donna me prega" addresses a personification of this concept. An influential 12th-century work by Andreas Capellanus titled for this concept was inspired by a poetic "textbook" by Ovid.
  Part 3 (ans: Beatrice):
    Though he addressed his love poetry to others, Cavalcanti entered a strategic marriage with a woman of this name. Another woman with this name was the lifelong love of the author of La Vita Nuova, Dante Alighieri.

[B2] 2017 Early Fall Tournament (EFT) (2017) | Diff: 7 | Literature
     Leadin: This work's author states "I abhor intercourse that does not relieve both," explaining why he did not partake in his culture's common practice of enjoying young boys. For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: Ars Amatoria [or The Art of Love; or The Art of Loving]):
    Name this instructional set of elegies by Ovid, which humorously likens its central subject to military service.
  Part 2 (ans: Latin [or lingua Latina]):
    Ovid, as well as the much more explicit Catullus, wrote his poetry in this language used by Vergil.
  Part 3 (ans: Martial [or Marcus Valerius Martialis]):
    Catullus's poetry served as the model for this later Roman author's Epigrams, which are every bit as vulgar in discussing topics of everyday life, such as advising Sextillus to give people the finger when they call him a queen.

[B3] 2014 DEES (2014) | Diff: 8 | Literature
     Leadin: This poet attributed his exile to a "carmen et error" in his Tristia. For 10 points:
  Part 1 (ans: Publius Ovidius Naso):
    Name this Roman poet best known for his Metamorphoses.
  Part 2 (ans: Ars Amatoria [or The Art of Loving]):
    This poem by Ovid was written in 2 CE and consists of three sections of romantic advice. It was followed shortly by his Remedia Amoris.
  Part 3 (ans: Callimachus):
    Ovid also wrote the poem Ibis, which was inspired by a poem by this Greek and Libyan writer of Aitia, a poem about mythological causes. He wrote the Pinakes while working at the Library of Alexandria.
