Tom Stoppard's Travesties involves a lawsuit between Joyce and Henry Carr over a staging of this play.

The Picture of Dorian Gray

Wilde's only novel, in which the artist Basil Hallward creates a portrait of the beautiful Dorian Gray that ages and decays instead of Dorian himself. The preface declares 'All art is quite useless' and compares dislike of Realism and Romanticism to 'different rages of Caliban.' Dorian reads a 'poisonous French novel' (likely Huysmans's A rebours) given by Lord Henry Wotton. Dorian poses for Hallward, murders him, and eventually stabs the portrait, dying himself. A 'yellow book' is read by Dorian. Albert Lewin's film adaptation uses Technicolor for Ivan Albright's grotesque painting. A long-lost uncensored version included more explicit romance between Dorian and Basil. Andre Gide's autobiography discusses Wilde and Dorian Gray.

"The Ballad of Reading Gaol" and Prison Writings

Wilde was imprisoned at Reading Gaol for homosexuality. 'The Ballad of Reading Gaol' declares 'each man kills the thing he loves' — 'the coward does it with a kiss / The brave man with a sword!' The prisoners look 'with a wistful eye' at the 'little tent of blue' called the sky. De Profundis was written in prison as a letter to his lover 'Bosie' (Lord Alfred Douglas), asserting 'Christ's place indeed is with the poets.' Patti Smith read from De Profundis at Reading in 2016. After release, Wilde adopted the pseudonym Sebastian Melmoth (after Saint Sebastian and Melmoth the Wanderer). His tomb at Pere Lachaise Cemetery, designed by Jacob Epstein with a sphinxlike creature, had to have a glass barrier to prevent lipstick damage.

Essays and Criticism

Wilde's essays include 'The Decay of Lying' (Vivian criticizes 'our monstrous worship of facts'; Cyril prompts him to argue 'Life imitates art far more than Art imitates life'), 'Pen, Pencil, and Poison' (discusses Thomas Griffiths Wainewright and claims 'a mask tells us more than a face'), and 'The Soul of Man under Socialism' (endorses socialism for strengthening artistic individualism; remarks 'To live is the rarest thing in the world'). Wilde was associated with the Decadent movement and The Yellow Book. He became a US celebrity when Richard 'Oily' Carte sent him on an 1882 lecture tour so Americans could compare Reginald Bunthorne from Gilbert and Sullivan's Patience to a real-life aesthete. Napoleon Sarony photographed him on a Persian carpet.

Summary of Facts

Oscar Wilde is an Irish playwright, novelist, poet, and essayist who is among the most frequently clued literary figures in quizbowl. His most recognizable giveaway is The Importance of Being Earnest, featuring Lady Bracknell's exclamation 'a handbag?!' and Jack Worthing's discovery that he was abandoned at Victoria station. The Picture of Dorian Gray is his only novel, whose preface declares 'All art is quite useless' and compares reactions to Realism and Romanticism to 'different rages of Caliban.' The portrait painted by Basil Hallward ages instead of Dorian, who reads a 'yellow book' from Lord Henry Wotton. Albert Lewin's film adaptation uses Technicolor for Ivan Albright's grotesque painting.

Wilde's other major plays include Lady Windermere's Fan (Mrs. Erlynne's sacrifice, 'we are all in the gutter'), An Ideal Husband (blackmail over insider trading), A Woman of No Importance (Hester Worsley and Lord Illingworth), and Salome (written in French for Sarah Bernhardt, illustrated by Aubrey Beardsley). His short stories include 'The Happy Prince' (swallow distributing jewels), 'The Canterville Ghost' (Pinkerton's Stain Remover), and 'Lord Arthur Savile's Crime' (exploding clock).

Wilde was imprisoned at Reading Gaol for homosexuality. 'The Ballad of Reading Gaol' declares 'each man kills the thing he loves.' De Profundis, his prison letter to lover 'Bosie,' asserts 'Christ's place indeed is with the poets.' His tomb at Pere Lachaise, designed by Jacob Epstein, required a glass barrier due to lipstick damage. His essays include 'The Decay of Lying' ('Life imitates art') and 'The Soul of Man under Socialism.' He was associated with the Decadent movement and became famous in America through an 1882 lecture tour tied to Gilbert and Sullivan's Patience.
