General / Biographical

Gordon Parks (1912–2006) was an African-American photographer, filmmaker, author, and composer. He was the first Black staff photographer for Life magazine. Before Life, he worked for the Farm Security Administration, having bought a camera from a pawnshop and taught himself photography. He documented Jim Crow Alabama in the Life series 'The Restraints: Open and Hidden' (documenting the Causey family, who were forced to leave town for their safety after its publication). He wrote a coming-of-age novel that he adapted as the film The Learning Tree. He photographed a malnourished Brazilian boy named Flavio and wrote a book of poetry titled after that photograph. He photographed Ingrid Bergman surrounded by three black-clad women with unfocused faces. He composed a Symphonic Set for Piano and Orchestra, premiered in Venice with conductor Dean Dixon. He photographed a 'Doll Test' showing a child pointing at a white doll. His photographs of nuns and rural Alabama children were recreated in the music video for Kendrick Lamar's 'ELEMENT.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Gordon Parks (1912–2006) was an African-American photographer, filmmaker, author, and composer, widely regarded as a groundbreaking figure in American photography and civil rights documentation. He was the first Black staff photographer for Life magazine.

His most famous photograph is American Gothic, Washington D.C. (1942), taken on his first day with the Farm Security Administration. It depicts Ella Watson, a Black janitor at the FSA offices, standing in front of an American flag while holding a broom and mop — deliberately echoing the pose of Grant Wood's painting American Gothic to critique racial inequality. Parks had learned that Watson was passed over for a desk job given to white employees; he took her portrait in front of that employee's office. He then went on to document Watson and her family in a 60-photo series. Parks taught himself photography after buying a camera from a pawnshop, and joined the FSA under Roy Stryker.

For Life magazine, Parks earned his position by photographing the daily life of Red Jackson, a Harlem gang leader, including an image of Red Jackson looking outside through a broken window. He later documented Jim Crow Alabama in the Life photo essay 'The Restraints: Open and Hidden,' which portrayed the Causey family; the family was forced to leave town for their safety after its publication. Parks collaborated with Ralph Ellison on two largely unpublished essays, producing a photograph titled The Invisible Man showing a man emerging from a manhole while holding a manhole cover over his head — a direct reference to Ellison's novel. He also photographed a 'Doll Test' using a low angle to show a child pointing at a white doll.

As a filmmaker, Parks directed the 1971 blaxploitation film Shaft, for which Isaac Hayes wrote an Oscar-winning funk theme. Parks also directed The Learning Tree, adapted from his own coming-of-age novel. His son Gordon Parks Jr. directed Super Fly before dying in a 1979 plane crash.

Parks was also a composer, premiering his Symphonic Set for Piano and Orchestra in Venice with conductor Dean Dixon. He photographed the malnourished Brazilian boy Flavio and wrote a book of poetry named after that photograph. He took a portrait of actress Ingrid Bergman surrounded by three black-clad women with unfocused faces in the background. His photographs of nuns and rural Alabama children were recreated in the music video for Kendrick Lamar's 'ELEMENT.'
