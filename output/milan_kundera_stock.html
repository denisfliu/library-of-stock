Summary of Facts

Milan Kundera is a Czech-French author whose work combines philosophical meditation with fiction set against the backdrop of Communist Czechoslovakia and the Prague Spring. He is an exophonic writer, having switched from Czech to French.

His most famous novel, The Unbearable Lightness of Being, begins by discussing Nietzsche's concept of eternal return and the idea that 'the absolute absence of a burden' produces lightness. The surgeon Tomas considers Beethoven's phrase 'Es muss sein!' ('It must be') when deciding whether to return to Prague to be with his wife Tereza, who photographs Russian tanks invading Prague and dreams of her husband making her march naked around a pool. Tomas loses his job and becomes a window cleaner after writing an editorial comparing the Communist Party to Oedipus. The painter Sabina wears her grandfather's bowler hat during sex with Tomas; though Tomas finds it erotic, her lover Franz is confused by it. The bowler hat is a 'motif in the musical composition that is Sabina's life.' A 'Short Dictionary of Misunderstood Words' contrasts Sabina and Franz. Sabina defines 'kitsch' as 'the absolute denial of shit.' The protagonists' female dog Karenin (named after the husband in Anna Karenina) befriends the pig Mefisto and dies of cancer in the novel's final section, smiling at its owners; Tomas imagines the tombstone reading 'he gave birth to two rolls and a bee.' Tomas and Tereza eventually move to the countryside. Ivan Klima wrote Love and Garbage in response to what he perceived as chauvinism in the novel.

The Joke is Kundera's first novel. Its protagonist Ludvik Jahn writes on a postcard to his classmate Marketa: 'Optimism is the opium of the people! A healthy spirit stinks of stupidity! Long live Trotsky!' This gets him expelled from the Communist Party and sentenced to hard labor. Helena, seduced by Ludvik in a revenge plot, mistakenly attempts suicide with laxatives rather than aspirin. During a Moravian folk festival, an effort to revive a traditional 'Ride of the Kings' ceremony is interrupted by a motorcycle gang. Ludvik realizes the woman shaving his face is an old acquaintance named Lucie Sebetka. Kundera's own expulsion from the Communist Party inspired the novel. He advocated structuring novels in seven parts in The Art of the Novel and followed this in The Joke.

The Book of Laughter and Forgetting is 'a novel in the form of variations' divided into seven parts. It opens with the Communists erasing Clementis from a photograph but forgetting to erase the hat he loaned to Gottwald. The first and fourth sections are both titled 'Lost Letters'; the third and sixth are 'The Angels.' The novel includes Mirek's arrest while destroying old love letters, prefiguring Tamina's later attempt to recover her love letters. The narrator notes that Karel's mother (Mama) is right to care more about ripe pears than the tank invasion, since 'tanks are perishable, pears are eternal.' In one section, a student who possesses 'Litost' (an untranslatable Czech word for a kind of shame) tries to get with Kristyna, while the professor Voltaire hosts a gathering of Poets. Ostriches at a zoo cause Tamina to phone her father. The author compares his father's death to Tamina's grief.

The Art of the Novel is an essay collection in which Kundera examines 'The Depreciated Legacy of Cervantes,' discusses modernity as 'terminal paradoxes,' champions 'polyphony' (borrowing from Bakhtin) in Hermann Broch's The Sleepwalkers, advocates seven-part novel structure, outlines '63 words' central to his Aesthetics, and includes a Paris Review interview in which he asserts his novels are 'outside the aesthetic of the novel normally termed psychological.' Testaments Betrayed discusses Max Brod's Betrayal of Kafka and includes his Diderot-inspired play Jacques and his Master. The Curtain is a seven-part essay on The History and value of the Western novel, claiming Cervantes first tore through the 'veil of pre-interpretation.' Ignorance opens with an Odyssean etymology of 'nostalgia' from Greek nostos and algos.

Immortality features the serial tire-slasher Professor Avenarius and a celestial trial in which Paul Eluard, Rilke, and Romain Rolland rule against the ghost of Goethe. The narrator sees a woman named Agnes waving at a swimming teacher, and Goethe converses with Hemingway. Rubens has an affair with Agnes. Life is Elsewhere features The Poet Jaromil, who claims 'Lyric Poets generally come from homes run by women' and turns his girlfriend's brother into the police for attempting to flee the country. The Farewell Waltz is set in a spa town with eight characters, including a doctor who secretly injects women with his own sperm. Laughable Loves includes 'Eduard and God' (an atheist teacher pretends to be a believer to win Alice) and a story about a couple roleplaying as hitchhiker and driver. Kundera only received his Czech citizenship back in 2019 after being blacklisted. Philip Roth featured his work in the Writers from the Other Europe series.
