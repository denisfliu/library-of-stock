American photographer (1903–1975), famous for documenting the Great Depression for the Farm Security Administration. Best known for his collaboration with writer James Agee on Let Us Now Praise Famous Men (1941), which documented three sharecropper families in Alabama (the Burroughs, Fields, and Tingle families — pseudonymously called Gudger, Woods, and Ricketts). Also known for his secret subway portraits (collected as Many Are Called) and his portrait of Allie Mae Burroughs. Sherrie Levine appropriated his photographs in After Walker Evans. Famously declared 'Color photography is vulgar.'
Let Us Now Praise Famous Men (1941)

Book combining photographs by Evans and prose by James Agee, documenting three Alabama sharecropper families. The families are pseudonymously called Gudger, Woods, and Ricketts (real names: Burroughs, Fields, and Tingle). The book opens with the section 'On the Porch,' preceded by the lines 'The house had now descended / All over Alabama the lamps are out.' Evans was on leave from the FSA and commissioned by Fortune magazine. W.J.T. Mitchell discussed Evans's photo of 'Annie Mae Gudger' (i.e., Allie Mae Burroughs) in Picture Theory. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Walker Evans (1903–1975) was an American photographer who worked for the Farm Security Administration and documented the Great Depression. His most important work is Let Us Now Praise Famous Men (1941), a collaboration with writer James Agee that documented three Alabama sharecropper families (pseudonymously Gudger, Woods, and Ricketts — real names Burroughs, Fields, and Tingle). The book opens with the section 'On the Porch,' preceded by lines from a poem. Evans's portrait of Allie Mae Burroughs (a thin-lipped woman against a cabin wall) became one of the most famous photographs of the era. In 1981, appropriation artist Sherrie Levine rephotographed Evans's images from a catalogue and exhibited them as After Walker Evans, challenging authorship and ownership in art.

Evans also created Many Are Called, a collection of over 600 candid portraits secretly taken on the New York City subway between 1938 and 1941. He hid a 35mm Contax camera under his coat and triggered the shutter via a cable in his sleeve. The collection was published in 1966 with an introduction by James Agee. Evans was commissioned by Fortune magazine (while on leave from the FSA) to photograph tenant farmers in Alabama. He befriended Ernest Hemingway while photographing for Carleton Beals's The Crime of Cuba. He famously declared 'Color photography is vulgar.' Photographer Robert Frank (The Americans) was his student.
