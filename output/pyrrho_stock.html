Pyrrhonian Skepticism and Epoche

Pyrrhonian skepticism holds that certainty about truth or knowledge is unattainable. Its core practice is epoche (suspension of judgment), which leads to ataraxia (tranquility). The ten 'modes' for achieving epoche were given by Aenesidemus and later Agrippa formulated five modes (the Agrippan trilemma: circularity, infinite regress, or dogmatic axiom). The school rejected katalepsis (Stoic 'cognitive impression') in favor of acatalepsia ('incomprehensibility'). Sextus Empiricus's Outlines of Pyrrhonism is the main surviving text, criticizing both 'Academics' and 'Dogmatics.' The tradition also discussed the story of a frustrated artist flinging a sponge at a horse painting, discovering tranquility by accident.

Pyrrho's Journey to India and Buddhism

Pyrrho traveled with Alexander the Great's campaigns to India, where he may have encountered gymnosophists ('naked wise men'). Christopher Beckwith drew parallels between Pyrrhonian ideas and the Buddhist 'three marks of existence.' His concepts of adiaphora, astathmeta, and anepikrita may have been inspired by Buddhist thought. The Indian Ajnana school of radical skepticism may have been the source. Adrian Kuzminski's book examines 'How the Ancient Greeks Reinvented Buddhism.' Pyrrho's ideas are also compared to the Carvaka school.

Montaigne and Renaissance Reception

Michel de Montaigne was deeply influenced by Pyrrhonism, reading Sextus Empiricus and inscribing Pyrrhonist quotations on the rafters of his tower study in Dordogne. His personal medal had 'epecho' (a verb form of epoche) inscribed on one side and 'que sais-je?' ('what do I know?') on the other. His essay 'Apology for Raymond Sebond' defends Pyrrhonism using the ten modes and a fideist interpretation of Natural Theology. Richard Popkin argued that the Renaissance rediscovery of Pyrrhonism provoked a 'crisis' that spurred both the Reformation and Counter-Reformation. Pierre Charron's On Wisdom reconciled Pyrrhonism with Socratic and Ciceronian views. Francisco Sanches also re-popularized skepticism.

Academic Skepticism vs. Pyrrhonism

Pyrrhonian skepticism is contrasted with Academic skepticism (from Plato's Academy under Arcesilaus and Carneades). Academic skeptics argued for the eulogon (reasonable) and pithanon (probable) as criteria, while Pyrrhonists insisted on complete suspension of judgment. Carneades advanced probabilism and gave contradictory lectures on justice in Rome. The 'Problem of the Criterion,' traced through Montaigne by Roderick Chisholm, involves a circular argument. Pyrrho's ideas were compared to the honey-sweetness example distinguishing 'what is apparent' from 'the apparent itself.'

Summary of Facts

Pyrrho of Elis (c. 360-270 BCE) founded Pyrrhonian skepticism, the more radical of the two main branches of ancient skepticism. The core practice is epoche (suspension of judgment), which leads to ataraxia (tranquility). The school's ideas were primarily preserved through Sextus Empiricus's Outlines of Pyrrhonism, which catalogs the ten modes of Aenesidemus and Agrippa's five modes (the Agrippan trilemma of circularity, infinite regress, and dogmatic axiom). Pyrrhonists rejected the Stoic concept of katalepsis in favor of acatalepsia.

Pyrrho traveled with Alexander the Great to India, where he may have met gymnosophists. Christopher Beckwith drew parallels with Buddhist 'three marks of existence,' and Adrian Kuzminski examined 'How the Ancient Greeks Reinvented Buddhism.' His concepts of adiaphora, astathmeta, and anepikrita may derive from Indian philosophy, possibly the Ajnana school of radical skepticism.

Pyrrhonism profoundly influenced the Renaissance through Michel de Montaigne, who inscribed Pyrrhonist quotations on his tower study rafters and used the motto 'que sais-je?' His 'Apology for Raymond Sebond' defends Pyrrhonism using the ten modes. Richard Popkin argued the Renaissance rediscovery provoked a 'crisis' spurring the Reformation. Edmund Husserl borrowed 'epoche' for phenomenological 'bracketing.'

Pyrrhonian skepticism is contrasted with Academic skepticism (from Plato's Academy under Arcesilaus and Carneades). Academic skeptics promoted probabilism (pithanon), while Pyrrhonists insisted on complete suspension. An anecdote describes a frustrated artist accidentally discovering tranquility by flinging a sponge at a horse painting. The tradition discussed the 'Problem of the Criterion,' a circularity challenge to all knowledge claims.
