Tristram Shandy

A nine-volume, extremely digressive novel (1759-1767) narrated by Tristram, who takes three volumes to reach his own birth. The novel is famous for its experimental techniques: a completely black page marks the death of Parson Yorick, blank and marbled pages appear throughout, and digressions are described as the 'soul of reading.' Dr. Slop's forceps flatten Tristram's nose at birth because Walter Shandy believes nose shape determines success. Tristram is accidentally circumcised by a falling window sash while urinating out a window. Uncle Toby is obsessed with military earthworks (his 'hobby-horse'), reenacting the Siege of Namur with his servant Corporal Trim using jackboots as mortars. Widow Wadman pursues Uncle Toby for eleven years. Walter ritually winds the family clock and has sex with his wife on the first Sunday of each month; his wife asks 'have you forgotten to wind up the clock?' at the moment of conception. Walter names the child 'Trismegistus' but a clerical error changes it to 'Tristram.' The monograph On Noses by Hafen Slawkenbergius is discussed. Samuel Johnson said the novel 'did not last' because 'Nothing odd will do long.' Shklovsky credited it as the 'most typical novel in world literature.' Diderot lifted passages for Jacques the Fatalist. Friedrich Schlegel compared Jean Paul's novels to it. Its structure inspired Carlos Fuentes's Christopher Unborn.

A Sentimental Journey Through France and Italy

A satirical travel novel narrated by Parson Yorick, who travels through France. It opens 'They order, said I, this matter better in France' at Calais and breaks off mid-sentence at Lyon with the narrator catching hold of a chambermaid: 'So that when I stretch'd out my hand I caught hold of the fille de chambre's—' Sterne called Tobias Smollett 'Smelfungus' in this novel. Yorick travels with the young Le Fleur and narrowly escapes the Bastille after being mistaken for the king's jester.

Summary of Facts

Laurence Sterne was an Irish-born English novelist and Anglican clergyman, best known for The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gentleman (1759-1767) and A Sentimental Journey Through France and Italy (1768).

Tristram Shandy is a nine-volume, extremely digressive novel narrated by Tristram, who famously takes three volumes to reach his own birth. It features many experimental techniques: a completely black page marks the death of Parson Yorick (Sterne's alter ego), and digressions are described as the 'soul of reading.' Dr. Slop's forceps flatten Tristram's nose at birth, a disaster for his father Walter, who believes nose shape determines success — the monograph On Noses by Hafen Slawkenbergius is discussed at length. Tristram is accidentally circumcised by a falling window sash while urinating out a window. Uncle Toby, whose 'hobby-horse' is military earthworks, reenacts the Siege of Namur in his garden with servant Corporal Trim, using jackboots as mortars. Widow Wadman pursues Uncle Toby for eleven years. Walter ritually winds the family clock and has sex with his wife on the first Sunday of each month; his wife asks 'have you forgotten to wind up the clock?' at the moment of Tristram's conception. Walter names the child 'Trismegistus' but a clerical error changes it to 'Tristram.' Samuel Johnson said the novel 'did not last' because 'Nothing odd will do long.' Viktor Shklovsky credited it as 'the most typical novel in world literature,' and Diderot lifted passages for Jacques the Fatalist.

A Sentimental Journey opens 'They order, said I, this matter better in France' and breaks off mid-sentence with the narrator catching hold of a chambermaid. Sterne coined the term 'Smelfungus' for Tobias Smollett in this novel. Yorick travels with Le Fleur and narrowly escapes the Bastille.

Sterne's skull was reinterred at a Coxwold estate in 1969 after being recognized by a Cambridge anatomy student when his body was stolen. His correspondence with Ignatius Sancho in support of abolitionism helped bring Sancho to prominence. He published 45 sermons before A Political Romance was burned by the church.
