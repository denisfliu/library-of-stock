Florentine Renaissance painter. Teacher of Michelangelo. Known for Portrait of an Old Man and His Grandson (man with rhinophyma/bulbous nose), frescoes in the Tornabuoni Chapel (life of the Virgin and John the Baptist) and Sassetti Chapel (life of St. Francis), and the Adoration of the Shepherds. Painted for the Sistine Chapel.

Summary of Facts

Domenico Ghirlandaio was a Florentine Renaissance painter of the late 15th century, best known as the teacher of Michelangelo -- a fact well-documented despite Ascanio Condivi's propaganda claiming Michelangelo was self-taught. His brother Davide worked in his workshop and his son Ridolfo later completed Raphael's La Belle Jardiniere.

His most frequently clued work is the Portrait of an Old Man and His Grandson, which depicts a man with rhinophyma (a bulbous nose deformity) gazing at a young boy. The painting uses three-quarters perspective and the old man is possibly modeled on Francesco Sassetti. Its attribution was challenged by Adolfo Venturi, who credited it to brother Davide for lacking the 'sweet' character.

Ghirlandaio painted extensive fresco cycles in the Tornabuoni Chapel in Santa Maria Novella, commissioned by Giovanni Tornabuoni, depicting 8 scenes from the life of Mary facing 8 scenes from the life of John the Baptist. These include The Nativity of Mary (a woman pouring from a jug as a reclining woman stares at a baby, sitting next to Joachim's Expulsion from the Temple), the Birth of the Virgin (set in a 15th-century Florentine bedroom with a frieze of jocular putti), the Adoration of the Magi (with a giraffe in the background, possibly inspired by one gifted to Lorenzo de' Medici), and the Massacre of the Innocents. A portrait of patron Giovanna Tornabuoni was also part of this commission. Some work on these frescoes may have been done by the then-13-year-old Michelangelo.

For the Sassetti Chapel at Santa Trinita, he painted frescoes about the life of St. Francis, including The Confirmation of Franciscan Rule (showing Lorenzo de' Medici emerging from a staircase) and inserted himself as a shepherd observing the Adoration. The chapel's altarpiece, the Adoration of the Shepherds, shows Jesus in front of a grey sarcophagus with visitors walking along a narrow cliffside trail, passing through an arch dedicated to Pompey the Great. This composition was inspired by the central panel of Hugo van der Goes's Portinari Altarpiece, differing mainly in the addition of the cliffside procession. Shepherds kneel near the sarcophagus. His separate Adoration of the Magi version contains a sarcophagus inscribed with a prophecy by the augur Fulvius: 'the urn that covers me will bring forth a god.' Another Adoration of the Magi was painted for the Ospedale degli Innocenti (Brunelleschi's foundling hospital), also inspired by the Portinari Altarpiece.

His St. Jerome in His Study was painted for the Church of Ognissanti, commissioned by the Vespucci family, and shares similarities with van Eyck's version; it was commissioned alongside Botticelli's Saint Augustine in his Study. He also painted a Last Supper in the Ognissanti (with John shown sleeping), likely influenced by Andrea del Castagno's depiction, and a second version for the Badia di Passignano primarily executed by brother Davide.

For the Sistine Chapel, he painted The Vocation of the Apostles and helped paint the Stories of Moses and Jesus on the walls alongside Botticelli. His Resurrection of Christ in the Sistine Chapel was later damaged and replaced by Hendrick van den Broeck and Matteo da Lecce. He also painted a St. Barbara holding a model building while crushing her infidel father underfoot. The landscape of one of his paintings was interpolated over Botticelli's face by Iranian artist Aydin Aghdashloo.
