Ancient Greek and Roman philosophical school founded by Zeno of Citium at the 'painted porch' (stoa) in Athens. Core doctrines: virtue is the only good, control of emotions, prohairesis (volition), pneuma, ekpyrosis (great conflagration), and propositional logic. Major figures include Chrysippus, Cleanthes, Epictetus, Seneca, and Marcus Aurelius. Divided philosophy into logic, physics, and ethics.
Prohairesis, Volition, and Stoic Ethics

The central ethical concept of Stoicism is prohairesis (volition) -- the only thing fully within our control. Epictetus argued in the Discourses (compiled by Arrian) and the Enchiridion (Handbook/Manual) that one should only be concerned with one's own actions. The Stoics held that virtue is the only good, with health and wealth classified as 'indifferents' having only 'selective value.' The school distinguished between katorthomata (perfect action) and kathekonta (appropriate action). Kathekon (proper action in accordance with nature) was taught from the 'painted porch.'

Stoic Physics: Pneuma, Ekpyrosis, and Cosmology

Stoic physics held that the universe is infused with pneuma, a mixture of air and fire serving as a vital organizing substance. The cosmos undergoes cyclical destruction through ekpyrosis (great conflagration) at the end of each 'Great Year.' This doctrine of eternal recurrence was later adopted by Nietzsche. Heraclitus's 'fire everliving' influenced the Stoic belief in conflagration. The Stoics held that only bodies exist and that moral qualities and the soul are corporeal. The logos spermatikos is the supreme creative force.

Stoic Logic

The Stoics developed the first form of propositional logic, based on propositions rather than Aristotelian terms. Truth-bearers were lekta ('sayables'). Chrysippus created a system with five proposition connectives and was the most prolific Stoic logician. He died laughing (supposedly after seeing a donkey eat figs). His 'growing argument' (Dion thought experiment) addressed identity, inspiring modern work like Wiggins's Tibbles the Cat. The Stoics used a hand analogy for levels of understanding (open palm = perception, covered fist = knowledge). The 'cognitive impression' was the criterion of truth.

General / School Identity and Reception

Founded by Zeno of Citium at the 'painted porch' (stoa) in Athens. Divided philosophy into logic, physics, and ethics (compared to an egg with logic on the outside). Pierre Hadot connected the three disciplines to desire, action, and assent. The school's ideal 'sage' was a recurring concept. Justus Lipsius attempted to synthesize Stoicism with Christianity (neostoicism) in On Constancy, but neostoics abandoned the belief in fate. The cognitivist theory of emotions traces back to Stoic understanding of emotions as judgments. Cicero critiqued Stoicism in Pro Murena. The dog-tied-to-cart analogy illustrated determinism and free will.

Summary of Facts

Stoicism is one of the most heavily clued Hellenistic philosophical schools, founded by Zeno of Citium at the 'painted porch' in Athens and including major figures such as Chrysippus, Cleanthes, Seneca, Epictetus, and Marcus Aurelius. The school divided philosophy into logic, physics, and ethics, which Pierre Hadot connected to the disciplines of desire, action, and assent.

The central ethical concept is prohairesis (volition) -- the only thing fully within our control, as taught by Epictetus in his Discourses (compiled by Arrian) and the Enchiridion. Virtue is the only good, with health and wealth classified as 'indifferents.' Marcus Aurelius's Meditations, written during military campaigns, discusses the ideal sage and proposes 'either Providence or blind atoms.' Seneca wrote moral letters to Lucilius, On the Shortness of Life, and On Mercy (dedicated to Nero).

Stoic physics held that the universe is infused with pneuma (a mixture of air and fire) and undergoes cyclical destruction through ekpyrosis (great conflagration). Heraclitus's fire influenced this doctrine. The logos spermatikos is the supreme creative force. Only bodies truly exist in Stoic ontology.

The Stoics developed the first form of propositional logic, using lekta ('sayables') as truth-bearers rather than Aristotelian terms. Chrysippus created five proposition connectives and died laughing at a donkey eating figs. His 'growing argument' about Dion addressed personal identity. He described a dog using disjunctive syllogism and used the cone/cylinder analogy for determinism. The 'cognitive impression' served as the criterion of truth.

Justus Lipsius synthesized Stoicism with Christianity in On Constancy, founding neostoicism. The cognitivist theory of emotions traces to Stoic judgments. Cicero critiqued Stoicism in Pro Murena. The dog-tied-to-cart analogy (Cleanthes) illustrated the harmony of autonomous will with determinism. Albert Ellis's REBT framework was inspired by Stoicism, and Nietzsche's amor fati was inspired by Epictetus and Marcus Aurelius.
