American Postmodern architect (1925–2018), known for the quip 'Less is a bore.' Authored Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture and co-authored Learning from Las Vegas with his wife Denise Scott Brown and Steven Izenour. Key buildings include the Vanna House, the Sainsbury Wing of London's National Gallery, and the Seattle Art Museum. Won the 1991 Pritzker Prize.
Vanna House (Vanna Venturi House)

House built for Venturi's mother Vanna in Philadelphia. Features an exposed chimney, a wrap-around staircase, a gently sloping roof, and a large monumental facade with a split gable. Described by critic Frederick Schwartz as 'the first Postmodern anything.' Located near the Esherick House by Louis Kahn. Wikipedia

Learning from Las Vegas

1972 book co-authored by Venturi, his wife Denise Scott Brown, and Steven Izenour. Celebrated roadside decor and the Las Vegas strip. Divided buildings into two types: 'ducks' (where the shape represents the building's function) and 'decorated sheds.' Aron Vinegar's I am a Monument is a critical examination of this book. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Robert Venturi (1925–2018) was the foremost American Postmodern architect, best known for the quip 'Less is a bore,' a pointed rebuke of Mies van der Rohe's Modernist dictum 'Less is more.' He won the Pritzker Prize in 1991, though his long-time partner and wife Denise Scott Brown refused to attend the ceremony because the prize was awarded to him alone for projects on which they had collaborated, including the Sainsbury Wing of London's National Gallery.

His most celebrated building is the Vanna House (Vanna Venturi House) in Philadelphia, designed for his mother Vanna. It features an exposed chimney, a wrap-around staircase, a gently sloping roof, and a monumental split-gable facade. Critic Frederick Schwartz called it 'the first Postmodern anything.'

Venturi co-authored two foundational texts of Postmodern architecture. Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture (1966) called for an eclectic 'messy vitality' against Modernist purity. Learning from Las Vegas (1972), written with Denise Scott Brown and Steven Izenour, celebrated Las Vegas roadside commercial architecture and introduced the distinction between 'ducks' (buildings whose form represents their function) and 'decorated sheds.' Aron Vinegar's I am a Monument is a critical examination of that book.

Other notable projects include Fire Station No. 4 in Columbus, Indiana (with a giant gold '4' making the structure look like an overturned number four); the Lieb House (a 'sailboat' window, the number nine beside the door; floated up the East River to escape New Jersey); Franklin Court in Philadelphia (two steel-tube 'ghost structures' in the shape of homes); the Seattle Art Museum; additions to the Allen Art Museum at Oberlin and to Cass Gilbert's building there; the Brant-Johnson Ski House in Vail; and the Haute-Garonne Capitol Building in Toulouse.
