Sic et Non and Dialectical Method

Abelard's most frequently identified work, Sic et Non ('Yes and No'), juxtaposes 158 contradictory theological statements from Church fathers as an exercise in applying logic to theology. It heavily influenced Peter Lombard's Four Books of Sentences, the standard medieval theology textbook. Abelard's dialectical method of alternating between opposing theses to find common truth was widely copied.

Heloise and Historia Calamitatum

Abelard's autobiographical Historia Calamitatum ('History of My Calamities') recounts his love affair with Heloise, the birth of their son Astrolabe, and his castration by Heloise's uncle Fulbert. Their letters were the first full vernacular translation by Jean de Meun, who recounted their story in the Roman de la Rose. The relationship inspired Rousseau's Julie, or the New Heloise.

Nominalism and the Problem of Universals

Abelard is often called the first nominalist philosopher, arguing that universals cannot exist in the world (as they would need to be simultaneously rational and irrational) and that 'being a man' is a linguistic concept, not a physical or metaphysical universal. He argued that Boethius's criteria for universals cannot be satisfied by any real object. Despite using the term nomina, he is often credited as the originator of conceptualism.

Ethics and Intentionalism

In his Ethics, or Know Thyself, Abelard developed an intentionalist theory according to which actions themselves are morally neutral; only intentions determine moral worth. He rejected the Slave's Wager (an early form of Pascal's Wager) and illustrated his ethics with the thought experiment of two house-builders. Against Anselm's satisfaction theory, he claimed Christ's death was to exemplify perfect love.

Bernard of Clairvaux and Condemnation

Abelard's contention that reason has a role in interpreting faith angered 'anti-dialecticians' such as Bernard of Clairvaux, who had nineteen of Abelard's propositions condemned at the Council of Sens (1140/1141). Despite this, Abelard is noted for stating that one should not rashly pass judgment on contradictory church fathers.

Summary of Facts

Peter Abelard is a 12th-century French Scholastic philosopher, most commonly identified as the author of Sic et Non and the lover of Heloise. Sic et Non ('Yes and No') juxtaposes 158 contradictory theological statements from Church fathers as an exercise in applying dialectical logic to theology. The work heavily influenced Peter Lombard's Four Books of Sentences, which became the standard medieval theology textbook. Abelard's dialectical method of alternating between opposing theses was widely copied.

Abelard is equally famous for his tragic love affair with Heloise, recounted in his autobiographical Historia Calamitatum. The affair produced a son named Astrolabe and ended when Heloise's uncle Fulbert had Abelard castrated. Their letters were traditionally divided into four 'personal' and three 'of direction,' with Jean de Meun producing the first vernacular translation and recounting their context in the Roman de la Rose. Heloise argued that marriage was 'fatal to a philosopher' and that it was better to be a mistress than a wife. She later addressed 42 questions about scripture to Abelard while living at the Paraclete, the oratory he founded. Rousseau used their story as the basis for his novel Julie, or the New Heloise.

Philosophically, Abelard is considered the first nominalist, arguing that universals cannot exist in the world because they would simultaneously be rational and irrational, and that 'being a man' is merely a linguistic concept. He argued that Boethius's criteria for universals cannot be satisfied by any real object. Despite using the term nomina, scholars often credit him as the originator of conceptualism, which holds that universals exist only in the mind. His teacher Roscelin promoted an extreme nominalism condemned for tritheism.

In ethics, Abelard developed an intentionalist theory in Ethics, or Know Thyself, arguing that actions themselves are morally neutral and only intentions determine moral worth. He rejected an early version of Pascal's Wager (the 'Slave's Wager') and against Anselm's satisfaction theory, claimed that Christ's death was to exemplify perfect love.

Abelard's contention that reason plays a role in interpreting faith angered Bernard of Clairvaux, who had nineteen of Abelard's propositions condemned at the Council of Sens (1140/1141). His biographer John Marenbon cited Abelard and his student John of Salisbury as advocates for the virtuosity of ancient pagans.
