Lamentation over the Dead Christ

Extremely foreshortened body of Christ on a marble slab with prominent nail wounds on feet. Mary, St. John, and Mary Magdalene weep at the left. In Milan's Brera Gallery. Exemplifies foreshortening technique with large feet and small head to illustrate Christ lying away from the viewer. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Court artist to the Gonzaga family in Mantua. Born near Padua. Brother-in-law of Giovanni Bellini. Other works: Parnassus (Mars and Venus on arch; for Isabella d'Este's studiolo), Triumph of the Virtues / Minerva Driving Away Vice, Judith and Holofernes (androgynous figure in blue toga), St. James Led to His Execution (destroyed 1944, Ovetari Chapel), Agony in the Garden (Judas leads procession along winding path), Descent into Limbo (attributed 2018), Adoration of the Magi, Battle of the Sea Gods. Lorenzo Costa finished his Reign of Comus after his death.

Summary of Facts

Andrea Mantegna (1431-1506) was an Italian Renaissance painter born near Padua who became the court artist to the Gonzaga family in Mantua. He was the brother-in-law of Giovanni Bellini. He is most commonly identified by The Dead Christ (extreme foreshortening) and the Camera degli Sposi.

The Lamentation over the Dead Christ is his most frequently clued work. It shows an extremely foreshortened body on a marble slab with prominent nail wounds on the hands and feet, with large feet and a small head illustrating the figure lying away from the viewer. Mary, St. John, and Mary Magdalene weep at the left. It is housed in Milan's Brera Gallery. Man Lying on a Slab is a preparatory study drawing.

The Camera degli Sposi (c. 1465-1474) is a frescoed room in the Ducal Palace of Mantua, among the first Renaissance illusionistic ceilings. Its ceiling uses the di sotto in su technique (meaning 'from below, upward') depicting an oculus opening to a blue sky surrounded by putti, a peacock, and a turbaned man. The Court Scene shows patron Ludovico Gonzaga turning in his chair to speak to his secretary, with his family including a dwarf. The ceiling includes grisaille roundels of the first eight Roman emperors and the artist's self-portrait. Originally called Camera Picta, it is not actually a bridal chamber despite its name.

The Triumphs of Caesar (c. 1484-1492) is a nine-panel series depicting Caesar's victory procession with SPQR banners, slaves, vases, and spoils, now at Hampton Court Palace. Caesar rides in an ornate chariot celebrating his military victories in Gaul. A man carries a cage of exotic birds while soldiers lean on a marble statue. A preparatory sketch discovered in 2020 shows two bugle players with SPQR banners, and Andrea Andreani produced a 12-foot-wide chiaroscuro print of the series.

His three versions of St. Sebastian (Vienna, Venice, Louvre) show the saint tied to a column pierced by arrows. One version has a scythe-carrying rider (Death) in the clouds with anachronistic Roman ruins behind. The paintings were made to ward off the plague in Mantua. Mantegna replaced the traditional central tree with a marble column and depicted the Celestial Jerusalem on a mountain in the background.

The San Zeno Altarpiece (1456-1459) is a triptych for the Basilica of San Zeno in Verona with four Corinthian columns dividing three scenes connected by hanging garlands, the Crucifixion as the central predella, and pseudo-Kufic script on Mary's halo. Its predellas were stolen by the French and replaced with copies.

Other important works include Parnassus (Mars and Venus on a natural arch, for Isabella d'Este's studiolo, with Anteros shooting a blow dart at Vulcan's genitals), Triumph of the Virtues / Minerva Driving Away Vice, St. James Led to His Execution (destroyed in 1944 bombing of Ovetari Chapel in Padua, featuring an extremely low worm's-eye viewpoint where St. James blesses a man), Agony in the Garden (three sleeping men, vulture on bare tree, Judas leading a procession), Judith and Holofernes (Judith depicted androgynously in blue toga), Descent into Limbo (attributed to Mantegna in 2018), Adoration of the Magi (Balthazar in leopard-skin turban), and engravings like Battle of the Sea Gods. Lorenzo Costa finished The Reign of Comus after Mantegna's death for Isabella d'Este's studiolo.
