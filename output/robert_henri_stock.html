Summary of Facts

Robert Henri (born Robert Henry Cozad) was an American Realist painter and the leading figure behind 'The Eight,' which later developed into the Ashcan School — a movement dedicated to gritty, realistic depictions of lower-class New York City street life. His father, John Cozad, killed a man in a land dispute on the Platte River in Nebraska and fled, changing the family name. The name 'Ashcan School' originated from a caption in a Philadelphia Record drawing disparaged by cartoonist Art Young.

Henri collected his artistic teachings in the influential book The Art Spirit, in which he encouraged interest in color and light as 'means of expression' and promoted 'art for life's sake.' He praised Winslow Homer's realism, saying 'The big strong thing can only be the result of big strong seeing.' He organized the Exhibition of Independent Artists with John Sloan and other members of The Eight including George Luks and Everett Shinn.

His notable portraits include The Masquerade Dress (his second wife, newspaper cartoonist Marjorie Organ, against a plain black background), The Little Dancer (Isadora Duncan in a red dress), several portraits of Edna Smith, and works like Salome, La Neige, Spanish Gypsy, and Snow in New York (a carriage among brownstone apartments). His nudes Mata Moana and Figure in Motion were exhibited at the 1913 Armory Show. He taught George Bellows, who became famous for boxing paintings like Dempsey and Firpo, Stag at Sharkey's, and Both Members of This Club.
