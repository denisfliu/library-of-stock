Chilean novelist and poet (1953-2003), one of the most important Latin American authors of the late 20th century. Best known for his massive posthumous novel 2666 and The Savage Detectives. Co-founded the infrarealist poetry movement. Frequently writes about literary obsessives, violence in Latin America, and the relationship between literature and evil. His alter ego Arturo Belano appears in several works. Also wrote By Night in Chile, Amulet, Distant Star, Nazi Literature in the Americas, and the poetry collection The Romantic Dogs.

Summary of Facts

Roberto Bolano (1953-2003) was a Chilean novelist and poet who is among the most important Latin American authors of the late 20th century. He co-founded the infrarealist poetry movement and wrote both poetry (The Romantic Dogs, which includes 'Godzilla in Mexico' and 'The Ghost of Edna Lieberman') and prose fiction.

His most frequently clued work is 2666, a massive posthumous five-part novel published in 2004, the year after his death. He had requested that it be divided into five separate books. The novel centers on the fictional city of Santa Teresa (based on Ciudad Juarez, Mexico) and the serial murders of hundreds of women there. Its five parts are: 'The Part about the Critics' (four literary scholars -- Jean-Claude Pelletier, the wheelchair-using Piero Morini, Manuel Espinoza, and Liz Norton -- searching for the reclusive German author Benno von Archimboldi), 'The Part about Amalfitano' (a professor who goes mad, makes geometric drawings with philosopher names at vertices, and hangs Euclid's Elements from a clothesline), 'The Part about Fate' (American journalist Oscar Fate, also known as Quincy Williams, who works for the Harlem magazine Black Dawn and is sent to cover a boxing match in Santa Teresa), 'The Part about the Crimes' (the notorious section graphically detailing hundreds of femicides), and 'The Part about Archimboldi' (revealing the reclusive author to be Hans Reiter, who as a child had a one-legged father and a one-eyed mother, stole a copy of Animals and Plants of the European Coastal Region, and became obsessed with seaweed). Archimboldi takes his pseudonym from a Mannerist painter; his fictional novels include The Garden, The Leather Mask, D'Arsonval, Bifurcaria Bifurcata, and The Blind Woman. His nephew Klaus Haas is suspected of the murders. The novel opens with a Baudelaire epigraph about 'an oasis of horror in a desert of boredom.' Other details include: Oscar Fate sees a mural of the Virgin of Guadalupe with one eye closed and is shown a snuff film attributed to Robert Rodriguez; a girl who won the Laura Damian Prize has a sister obsessed with the Marquis de Sade; Barry Seaman is an ex-Black Panther motivational speaker who wrote the cookbook 'Eating Ribs with Barry Seaman'; Azucena Esquivel Plata tells the story of her friend Kelly Parker to reporter Sergio Gonzalez; Oscar Fate takes Rosa Amalfitano to the US to escape; and a bizarre passage makes prophecies about future literary popularity ('Paul Eluard shall appeal to the masses in the year 2101'). A character from the novel is reimagined as a widower in the companion book Woes of the True Policeman.

The Savage Detectives is his other major novel, about a group of Mexican poets called the Visceral Realists. The protagonists Arturo Belano (Bolano's alter ego, based on himself) and Ulises Lima (based on Mario Santiago Papasquiaro) search for Cesarea Tinajero, the mysterious founding mother of Visceral Realism, eventually taking a Ford Impala into the Sonora Desert with the prostitute Lupe. The novel ends with the narrator being disappointed by Tinajero's notebooks. One character narrates choking a prostitute; another ends up in jail in Israel.

By Night in Chile is a novella in which Father Urrutia recounts educating Pinochet and his generals about Marxism. The final section depicts the literary salons of Mariana Callejas (a real Pinochet secret agent), where leading Chilean writers attended events while dissidents were tortured in the basement. Priests become falconers to protect churches from pigeon droppings.

Amulet features narrator Auxilio Lacouture hiding in a university bathroom stall for thirteen days during a fictionalization of the 1968 Tlatelolco Massacre. It is based on the real Uruguayan poet Alcira Soust Scaffo, who hid for 15 days during the occupation of UNAM.

Nazi Literature in the Americas is a fictional encyclopedia of Nazi-affiliated authors in the Americas, many of whom escaped to South America after WWII. It has been compared to Borges's A Universal History of Infamy. One Cuban author's novel spells 'LONG LIVE HITLER' acrostically. An architect designs the cover of the literary magazine Lee Harvey Oswald, which contains poems from his 'poetess' daughters Angelica and Maria.

Other works include: Distant Star (alter ego Arturo appears); Monsieur Pain (a Mesmerist tries to reach a dying hiccuping poet, a fictionalization of Cesar Vallejo in Paris); The Third Reich (strategy board game champion Udo Berger); The Insufferable Gaucho (story/essay collection); Between Parentheses (assembled essays and columns); a novel consisting of a single sentence about heroin addict Beach; a story about a relative of Kafka's Josephine who is a rat policeman rather than a mouse singer; and essays including 'Exiles' (lamenting Chilean writers' diaspora) and 'Eight Seconds with Nicanor Parra.' His major novels were translated into English by Natasha Wimmer. His literary agents were Carmen Balcells and later Andrew Wylie. He famously condemned Isabel Allende's work as ranging 'from kitsch to the pathetic.'
