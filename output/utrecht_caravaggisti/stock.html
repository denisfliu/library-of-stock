General / Movement

The Utrecht Caravaggisti (also called the Utrecht School) were Dutch painters based in Utrecht who brought Caravaggio's style to the Netherlands. Key members: Gerrit van Honthorst (nicknamed 'Gherardo delle Notti' for his candlelit scenes), Hendrik ter Brugghen, and Dirck van Baburen. Named for the Dutch city of Utrecht and for the painter of The Calling of St. Matthew (Caravaggio). The movement also influenced later Dutch artists like Aelbert Cuyp's golden lighting in seascapes. Summary of Facts

The Utrecht Caravaggisti (also known as the Utrecht School) were Dutch painters from the city of Utrecht who traveled to Rome and absorbed the dramatic chiaroscuro lighting technique of Caravaggio, particularly his painting The Calling of St. Matthew. The movement's name combines the Dutch city of Utrecht with a reference to Caravaggio as its stylistic inspiration.

The three principal members are Gerrit van Honthorst, Hendrik ter Brugghen, and Dirck van Baburen. Van Honthorst earned the Italian nickname 'Gherardo delle Notti' (Gerard of the Nights) for his distinctive use of small, visible light sources such as candles. His key works include The Denial of St. Peter and Christ Before the High Priest, in which a woman's candle is obscured by a guard's arm as she holds the robe of the title figure. Ter Brugghen painted The Deposition (housed at San Pietro in Montorio in Rome), a scene where three distraught women witness the entombment as a pale hand points to a tool basket and crown of thorns in the lower corner; he also painted musician subjects such as the Singing Lute Player and Flute Player. Van Baburen contributed The Procuress to the school's notable corpus of paintings depicting prostitutes, another example of which is the Scene of Venal Love — a smiling, bare-chested woman grabbed by an old man.

The Utrecht School's influence extended to later Dutch artists: Aelbert Cuyp's golden lighting in seascape paintings such as The Maas at Dordrecht is attributed to the movement's influence.
