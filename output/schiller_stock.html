Summary of Facts

Friedrich Schiller is a German playwright, poet, and philosopher, best known for the plays The Robbers, William Tell, and the Wallenstein trilogy, as well as the poem 'Ode to Joy.'

The Robbers is a quintessential Sturm und Drang play about the conflict between brothers Karl and Franz Moor. Franz banishes Karl and tries to seduce Karl's betrothed Amalia. Karl joins the title outlaws; men throw firebrands into a city's powder-magazine to rescue Roller from the gallows. Karl upholds an oath made upon Roller's death by stabbing Amalia. Franz complains 'Must my projects bend to the iron yoke of a mechanical system?' A character picks up a guitar and sings a dialogue between Brutus and Caesar. One character includes a soliloquy about the 'Fearful key, which locks behind me the prisonhouse of life.' Karl's father's 'love' is called 'pure vanity on his part, the besetting sin of all Artists.' Fyodor compares Dmitri to Franz Moor in The Brothers Karamazov.

William Tell follows the Swiss hero who foils the tyrant Gessler by shooting an apple off his son's head. Tell is imprisoned by Gessler for refusing to salute a hat/cap on a pole. Melchtal and a council of three territories agree to postpone a revolt until Christmas. John Parricida dresses as a monk to visit Tell, but Tell refuses to harbor the murderer. Tell personally ferries a fugitive across a stormy lake. Tell coined the aphorism 'the axe in the house spares the carpenter.' Tell kills Gessler while the latter threatens the peasant woman Armgart.

The Wallenstein trilogy consists of Wallenstein's Camp, The Piccolomini, and The Death of Wallenstein. It depicts the Thirty Years' War general Albrecht von Wallenstein. Max Piccolomini intercedes on behalf of soldiers while Wallenstein negotiates secretly with the Swedes, aided by Illo and Terzky. His father Ottavio betrays Wallenstein. Macdonald and Devereux (or Gordon and Butler) assassinate Wallenstein. Wallenstein's speech 'So slender is / The boundary that divideth life's two paths' introduced the German phrase 'eternal yesterday.' An uncut performance takes 10 hours. Robert Pinsky made a condensed translation.

'Ode to Joy' ('An die Freude') describes entering 'fire-drunk' into the 'sanctuary' of the 'Daughter of Elysium.' It declares 'Every man becomes a brother / Where thy gentle wings abide.' Beethoven set it in the finale of his Ninth Symphony.

Don Carlos features the title character rejecting The Princess de Eboli because he loves his father's wife Elizabeth of Valois. The Marquis of Posa dies for Dutch liberty. King Philip II shares a dream of his palace on fire with Count Lerma. Schiller denied being a member of the Illuminati in letters about this play.

Mary Stuart begins with the jailer Paulet breaking into the protagonist's desk. Mary confronts her cousin Elizabeth in a park outside her prison. Mortimer kills himself after his rescue plan fails.

Intrigue and Love (Kabale und Liebe) features the doomed romance of Ferdinand von Walter and the middle-class Luise Miller. Lady Milford loves Ferdinand. President von Walter and his secretary Wurm devise a scheme with a fake love letter. Ferdinand and Luise both die by drinking poison.

Schiller's philosophical essays are also frequently clued. 'On the Aesthetic Education of Man' (Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Mankind) responds to Kant, describes how the 'form' and 'sense' drives combine to create the 'play' drive, and argues greater appreciation of art curbs 'lawless instincts.' 'On Naive and Sentimental Poetry' contrasts how modern Poets 'touch us through ideas' while ancient poets 'touch us through nature,' explaining Homer's 'dry truthfulness.' Thomas Mann's story 'Difficult Hour' fictionalizes Schiller's writer's block based on this essay. 'Theatre Considered as a Moral Institution' and 'On Grace and Dignity' are other notable essays. An essay argues tragedy derives from overcoming our own natures.

Other poems: 'Nanie' recounts stories of Orpheus and Aphrodite, beginning 'Even the beautiful must die.' 'The Gods of Greece' was censored for being anti-Christian. 'The Song of the Bell' introduced the phrase 'eye of the law' into German; it opens with 'Vivos voco. Mortuos plango. Fulgura frango.' 'Shakespeare's Ghost -- A Parody' laments no 'Caesar, no Achilles' on stage. The Bride of Messina features brothers Manuel and Cesar falling in love with their sister Beatrice.

Biographically, Schiller collaborated with Goethe on the Xenien (distichs mocking critics). He was Novalis's teacher at the University of Jena. Goethe claimed Schiller's skull from a mass grave (deciding the largest must be his). Erich Auerbach's Mimesis discusses Intrigue and Love.
