Summary of Facts

Masaccio (Tommaso di Ser Giovanni di Simone, 1401-1428) was an early Italian Renaissance painter who pioneered linear perspective. His nickname means 'clumsy' or 'messy,' contrasting with his teacher Masolino's diminutive nickname.

His most famous works are in the Brancacci Chapel at Santa Maria del Carmine in Florence. The Tribute Money shows Jesus in a blue robe directing Peter to retrieve a coin from a fish's mouth to pay a tax collector, who appears twice in the composition. The Expulsion from the Garden of Eden depicts a sword-wielding angel expelling weeping Adam and Eve; Cosimo III ordered fig leaves added to cover their genitalia.

The Holy Trinity at Santa Maria Novella uses linear perspective (learned from Brunelleschi) for a barrel vault, with God holding the crucified Christ above a skeleton bearing the inscription 'I was once what you are, and what I am you will become.' It was hidden by Vasari and reconstructed in 1952.
