Meditations (Ta eis heauton)

Marcus Aurelius's twelve-book work of personal Stoic philosophical writings, originally titled Ta eis heauton ('To Himself'). The first book ('Debts and Lessons') thanks various teachers, including Rusticus for introducing him to Epictetus's Discourses, which inspired the view of man as a 'soul attached to a corpse.' The work frequently uses the slogan 'providence or atoms' as two possible ways of interpreting the world. Key maxims include 'erase impressions,' 'remember that all is opinion,' 'very little is needed to make a happy life,' 'do nothing at random,' and comparing a grieved man to a pig that 'kicks and screams.' The self is divided into body, soul, and intellect (soma, pneuma, nous). Pierre Hadot studied it in The Inner Citadel, identifying three 'disciplines' of perception, action, and will.

Marcomannic Wars and Military Campaigns

Marcus Aurelius fought the Marcomannic Wars against Germanic tribes including the Marcomanni, Quadi, and the Sarmatian Iazyges. During the campaigns, the 'miracle of the rain' supposedly saved the Legio XII Fulminata from defeat (speculatively attributed to the Egyptian magician Harnuphis). The legion acquired the name 'Certa Constans' (surely constant) for loyalty during Avidius Cassius's revolt. Under Marcus Aurelius, Statius Priscus recovered Armenia, and Avidius Cassius sacked Ctesiphon. His victories over the Sarmatians are depicted on his victory column. He wrote the Meditations during these campaigns at Carnuntum.

Equestrian Statue

The bronze equestrian statue of Marcus Aurelius depicts him raising his right hand in the adlocutio gesture, riding without stirrups. It survived the medieval smelter because it was mistaken for another emperor (Constantine). There is evidence an enemy cowered beneath the horse's lifted right forehoof. Michelangelo moved it to the Capitoline Hill as the centerpiece of his reorganization. It inspired Verrocchio's Bartolommeo Colleoni and Donatello's Gattamelata. Renaissance artists gave it an oval-shaped base despite usually avoiding that figure.

General / Biographical

Marcus Aurelius was the last of the Five Good Emperors and the first co-emperor (with Lucius Verus, starting 161 CE). He was the husband of Faustina the Younger, who received the title 'Mater Castrorum' (Mother of the Camps) after the Rain Miracle. His mother Domitia Lucilla was in the brickmaking industry. He was tutored by Fronto (many letters survive), the Homeric scholar Alexander of Cotiaeum, and the Athenian millionaire Herodes Atticus. He succeeded his adoptive father Antoninus Pius. He was the father of Commodus, who signed a treaty with the Marcomanni and was assassinated by a wrestler after 735 gladiatorial victories. The Antonine Plague killed his co-ruler Lucius Verus. Edward Gibbon described the rule of the Five Good Emperors as 'under the guidance of wisdom and virtue.' Pierre Hadot equates amor fati with the 'discipline of desire' in The Inner Citadel.

Summary of Facts

Marcus Aurelius (121-180 CE) was a Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher, the last of the Five Good Emperors. He is best known for his Meditations (Ta eis heauton, 'To Himself'), twelve books of personal philosophical writings composed during military campaigns at Carnuntum and along the River Gran.

The Meditations opens with 'Debts and Lessons,' thanking teachers including Rusticus, who introduced Marcus Aurelius to Epictetus's Discourses and inspired the view of man as a 'soul attached to a corpse.' The work repeatedly uses the slogan 'providence or atoms' to describe two possible interpretations of the world, and commands readers to 'erase impressions' and 'do nothing at random.' Other memorable passages include the advice to throw away a bitter cucumber without asking 'why were such things made?', the comparison of a grieved man to a pig that 'kicks and screams,' the observation that cracks on bread have a certain beauty, and the dismissal of sexual preoccupation as 'the rubbing together of abdomens.' The self is divided into soma, pneuma, and nous. Pierre Hadot studied the Meditations extensively in The Inner Citadel, identifying three disciplines of perception, action, and will.

Marcus Aurelius fought the Marcomannic Wars against the Marcomanni, Quadi, and Iazyges. The famous 'miracle of the rain' supposedly saved the Legio XII Fulminata from defeat, an event attributed by Cassius Dio to the Egyptian magician Harnuphis. Under his rule, Statius Priscus recovered Armenia and Avidius Cassius sacked Ctesiphon. His victories over the Sarmatians are depicted on his victory column.

His bronze equestrian statue, depicting him raising his right hand in the adlocutio gesture while riding without stirrups, is one of the most important ancient sculptures. It survived medieval destruction because it was mistaken for Constantine. Michelangelo placed it as the centerpiece of his reorganization of the Capitoline Hill. It inspired later equestrian statues including Verrocchio's Colleoni and Donatello's Gattamelata.

Marcus Aurelius was the first co-emperor (with Lucius Verus from 161 CE). He married Faustina the Younger, who received the title Mater Castrorum. He was tutored by Fronto, Alexander of Cotiaeum, and Herodes Atticus. His son Commodus succeeded him and was eventually strangled by a wrestler. Edward Gibbon described the Five Good Emperors' rule as 'under the guidance of wisdom and virtue.'
