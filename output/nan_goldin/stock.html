P.A.I.N. and anti-Sackler activism

Goldin founded the advocacy organization P.A.I.N. (Prescription Addiction Intervention Now) to protest the Sackler family's role in the opioid crisis through their company Purdue Pharma. P.A.I.N. staged 'die-ins' at museums (including the Met, the Louvre, and the Guggenheim's ascending spiral ramp) funded by the Sackler family. General / Biographical

Nan Goldin (born Nancy Goldin, 1953) is an American photographer known for diaristic, intimate photography documenting queer, drag, and no-wave subcultures in New York (particularly the Bowery district) during the 1980s. Her first solo exhibition depicted the Boston transgender community (e.g., Ivy wearing a fall, Boston). She photographed drag queens, including the famous Misty and Jimmy Paulette in a Taxi, NYC. She depicted her own bruised face one month after being battered by her boyfriend. She photographed Vivienne Dick (a no-wave filmmaker) in Vivienne in the green dress, NYC. In 1989 she curated the exhibition Witnesses: Against Our Vanishing about the AIDS crisis. In 2023 she was named the most influential person in the art world by ArtReview Power. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Nan Goldin (born Nancy Goldin, 1953) is an American photographer known for her raw, diaristic, and intimate documentary photography of queer, drag, and no-wave subcultures in New York City, particularly the Bowery district, in the 1970s and 1980s.

Her most famous work is The Ballad of Sexual Dependency — originally a slideshow exhibition, later a photobook — documenting the lives of her 'recreated family' of hard-partying bohemians, drag queens, and LGBTQ+ people in New York. The title comes from a song in Kurt Weill and Bertolt Brecht's Threepenny Opera. The slideshow was partly set to music by The Velvet Underground. The cover shows Goldin in bed staring up at a smoking shirtless man. Goldin dedicated the book to her sister, who committed suicide, and described the work as 'frozen in amber,' focused on 'intimacy and autonomy.' Vivienne Dick helped pick the music for the slideshow. Goldin's image of her friend Greer Lankton curled in bed appeared on the title page.

Goldin began her photographic career documenting the Boston transgender community, with works like Ivy wearing a fall, Boston, shown in her first solo exhibition. She photographed many drag queens, most famously in Misty and Jimmy Paulette in a Taxi, NYC. She also depicted her own bruised face in a photograph taken one month after being beaten by a boyfriend. In 1989, she curated the exhibition Witnesses: Against Our Vanishing, one of the first major artistic responses to the AIDS crisis.

Goldin is also known as an activist. She founded P.A.I.N. (Prescription Addiction Intervention Now) to protest the Sackler family's role in the opioid epidemic through Purdue Pharma. P.A.I.N. staged die-ins at museums funded by the Sacklers — including occupying the Guggenheim's ascending spiral ramp and unfurling a banner over the Louvre's glass pyramid. Goldin called for the Met's Sackler Wing to be renamed. Laura Poitras documented this activism in the Oscar-nominated 2022 documentary All the Beauty and the Bloodshed. Goldin published 'I Survived the Opiate Crisis' in Artforum. In 2023 she was named the most influential person in the art world by ArtReview Power.
