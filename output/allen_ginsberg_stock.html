Allen Ginsberg

American Beat Generation poet from Paterson, New Jersey, best known for 'Howl' and 'Kaddish.' Associated with City Lights Books, the Six Gallery reading, and figures like Jack Kerouac, Lawrence Ferlinghetti, and Walt Whitman.
Howl

Ginsberg's most famous poem, first read at the Six Gallery Reading in October 1955. The poem opens with the iconic line 'I saw the best minds of my generation destroyed by madness.' Part II addresses the Canaanite deity Moloch, described as a 'sphinx of cement and aluminum' whose attributes include 'whose fate is a cloud of sexless hydrogen' and 'whose name is the Mind!' Moloch is 'the heavy judger of men' and is envisioned as the god of capitalist America. Part III repeatedly tells Carl Solomon 'I'm with you in Rockland.' A lengthy footnote proclaims 'the world is holy! the soul is holy!' The poem caused a notorious obscenity trial due to the line about being 'fucked in the ass by saintly motorcyclists.' City Lights Books published it; Lawrence Ferlinghetti was arrested alongside Shigeyoshi Murao for publishing it. William Carlos Williams wrote the introduction, ending with 'Hold back the edges of your gowns, Ladies, we are going through hell.' The collection Howl and Other Poems also includes 'A Supermarket in California,' 'Sunflower Sutra,' and 'Transcription of Organ Music.' Howl was originally dedicated to Lucien Carr, who formulated the 'New Vision' of the Beat poets and went to jail for the murder of David Kammerer. The poem describes someone dreaming of a 'pure vegetable kingdom.' Ginsberg usually read it in a dull monotone but would emphasize the word 'were' in the line about 'visionary Indian angels.' The poem also describes 'weeping and undressing while the sirens of Los Alamos wailed.'

A Supermarket in California

A poem in which the speaker imagines encountering Walt Whitman in a supermarket. Whitman is described as a 'childless, lonely old grubber, poking among the meats in the refrigerator' and asks questions like 'Who killed the pork chops? What price bananas? Are you my angel?' while eyeing the grocery boys. The speaker also asks Federico Garcia Lorca 'what were you doing down by the watermelons?' The speaker exclaims 'What peaches and what penumbras!' The poem ends with Whitman as a 'lonely old courage-teacher' watching 'the boat disappear on the black waters of Lethe.' The speaker parenthetically states 'I touch your book and dream of our odyssey... and feel absurd' and asks 'Will we walk all night through solitary streets?'

America

A poem in which the speaker addresses America directly, opening with 'I've given you all and now I'm nothing.' The poem's most quoted line is 'Go fuck yourself with your atom bomb.' The speaker is 'obsessed by Time Magazine,' describes attending a Communist meeting at age seven where 'everybody must have been a spy' and being sold garbanzos. The speaker asks 'America when will you send your eggs to India?' and 'when will we end the human war?' The speaker compares his strophes to Henry Ford's automobiles: 'my strophes are as individual as his automobiles.' Another poem, 'Death to Van Gogh's Ear,' states that 'India starved and screamed' while 'mountains of eggs were reduced to white powder in the halls of Congress.'

Kaddish

A long poem written for Ginsberg's mother Naomi, who died in a mental institution. The poem opens 'Strange now to think of you, gone without corsets & eyes, while I walk on the sunny pavement of Greenwich Village.' Naomi is called blessed 'in tears,' 'in fears,' 'in sickness,' 'in Hospitals,' and 'in Death.' The poem describes Naomi 'eating the first poisonous tomatoes of America' and having a 'Communist Party and a broken stocking' and a 'fear of Hitler.' In a letter, Naomi writes 'I have the key' and 'the key is in the bars, in the sunlight in the window.' The poem recounts Ginsberg's upbringing in Paterson, New Jersey, with his mother reading from a 'Communist fairy book.' The image of 'your mother' watching 'Chaplin dance in youth' appears. The speaker describes spending 'long nites... watching over your nervousness.' Naomi is said to be 'buried in the same Ground where I stand in Angel.' Ginsberg's letter to William Carlos Williams appears in the fourth book of Williams's Paterson.

Sunflower Sutra

A poem about an outing with Jack Kerouac in which Ginsberg picks up a 'skeleton thick' sunflower in a railyard and has a Blakean revelation that 'we're not our skin of grime, we're not dread bleak dusty imageless locomotives.' The poem was inspired by a hallucination Ginsberg had while reading William Blake, and he is reminded of Blake by the title plant, which is asked 'when did you look at your skin and decide you were an impotent dirty old locomotive?' The poem incorporates Buddhist themes.

Other Works and General

Ginsberg wrote many other poems and had a rich biographical life that appears in clues. He was born in Paterson, New Jersey, and was a native of that city (his letter appears in Book IV of Williams's Paterson). He wrote his own poem 'Paterson' in which the speaker would 'rather go mad, gone down the dark road to Mexico.' He had an auditory hallucination of William Blake's voice in 1948. He was fond of chanting with Hare Krishnas and helped Chogyam Trungpa establish the Naropa Institute. He invented the 'American Sentence,' a single line of 17 syllables, and translated Basho's frog haiku ending with 'Kerplunk!' He appeared as a guest vocalist on The Clash's 'Ghetto Defendant,' reciting a Buddhist sutra. He collaborated with William S. Burroughs on Korean Tyger, and with Phillip Glass and Paul McCartney on 'The Ballad of the Skeletons.' He photographed the Orlovsky brothers and produced a 'heroic portrait' of Jack Kerouac. Other poems include 'Wild Orphan,' 'Transcription of Organ Music,' 'Improvisation in Beijing,' 'Death to Van Gogh's Ear,' 'Wales Visitation,' 'Wichita Vortex Sutra,' 'Love Poem on a Theme by Whitman,' and 'At Apollinaire's Grave.' Collections include Planet News and Reality Sandwiches. The essay 'Taking a Walk Through Leaves of Grass' highlights Whitman's eroticism. Rita Dove's Penguin Anthology excluded Ginsberg due to licensing fees.

Summary of Facts

Allen Ginsberg is an American Beat Generation poet, born in Paterson, New Jersey, and one of the most influential poets of the 20th century. He is most commonly identified through his poem 'Howl' and his association with the Beat movement.

'Howl' is Ginsberg's most famous work, first read at the Six Gallery Reading in October 1955. Its opening line, 'I saw the best minds of my generation destroyed by madness,' is one of the most recognizable in American poetry. The poem's three sections address the experiences of Ginsberg's generation, the Canaanite deity Moloch (a 'sphinx of cement and aluminum' representing capitalist America), and Carl Solomon ('I'm with you in Rockland'). A lengthy footnote proclaims 'the world is holy! the soul is holy!' The poem triggered a notorious obscenity trial when City Lights Books published it; Lawrence Ferlinghetti was arrested alongside Shigeyoshi Murao. William Carlos Williams wrote the introduction. The poem was originally dedicated to Lucien Carr. Whitman's long lines directly inspired Ginsberg's style in Howl.

'A Supermarket in California' imagines encountering Walt Whitman in a grocery store. Whitman is a 'childless, lonely old grubber' who asks 'Who killed the pork chops? What price bananas? Are you my angel?' while eyeing the grocery boys. The speaker also asks Garcia Lorca 'what were you doing down by the watermelons?' Whitman ends the poem as a 'lonely old courage-teacher' by the 'black waters of Lethe.'

'America' directly addresses the nation, opening with 'I've given you all and now I'm nothing.' Its most quoted line is 'Go fuck yourself with your atom bomb.' The speaker is obsessed with Time Magazine, attended a Communist meeting at age seven where he was sold garbanzos, and asks 'when will you send your eggs to India?'

'Kaddish' is a long elegy for Ginsberg's mother Naomi, who suffered from mental illness and paranoia. It opens 'Strange now to think of you, gone without corsets & eyes.' Naomi is blessed 'in tears, in fears, in sickness, in Hospitals, in Death' and is described with a 'Communist Party and a broken stocking' and a 'fear of Hitler.' In a letter, she wrote 'I have the key' and 'the key is in the bars, in the sunlight in the window.' The poem recounts Ginsberg's childhood in Paterson.

'Sunflower Sutra' describes an outing with Jack Kerouac in which Ginsberg finds a 'skeleton thick' sunflower in a railyard, experiencing a Blakean revelation that 'we're not our skin of grime.' The poem was inspired by a hallucination while reading William Blake.

Biographically, Ginsberg had an auditory hallucination of William Blake's voice in 1948. He practiced Buddhism, chanted with Hare Krishnas, and helped establish the Naropa Institute with Chogyam Trungpa. He invented the 'American Sentence' (17 syllables), translated Basho, and appeared on The Clash's 'Ghetto Defendant.' He collaborated with Burroughs, Philip Glass, and Paul McCartney. His letter appears in Book IV of Williams's Paterson.
