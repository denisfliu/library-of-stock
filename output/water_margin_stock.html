Water Margin

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
One of the Four Great Classical Novels of Chinese literature, attributed to Shi Nai'an. Follows 108 bandits known as the Stars of Destiny, led by Song Jiang, who oppose the corrupt official Gao Qiu from their base at Mount Liang (Liangshan) marsh during the Song dynasty. The novel opens with the release of 108 spirits from a stone tortoise. Famous characters include Wu Song (the tiger-slayer), Lin Chong, and the "Black Whirlwind." The Plum in the Golden Vase is a spinoff featuring minor characters from this novel. Translated by Sidney Shapiro and Pearl Buck (as All Men Are Brothers).
Opening / Origin Story

The novel begins with Marshal Hong accidentally releasing 108 spirits imprisoned under an ancient stone tortoise, which shoot out as golden rays from a black cloud. These spirits are reborn as heroes — the Stars of Destiny — divided into 36 Heavenly Spirits and 72 Earthly Fiends. They are ranked by a tablet in the Loyalty Hall. This origin sequence is one of the most frequently clued aspects of the novel. Wikipedia

Editions and Translations

The novel exists in 'simple' and 'complex' editions (70-chapter and 100-chapter versions) that diverge at the point where the main characters are granted amnesty. Sidney Shapiro produced the most acclaimed translation, amalgamating the editions. Pearl Buck made a translation titled All Men Are Brothers, often described as controversial or bad. Arthur Waley is another translator associated with Chinese classics.

The novel also ends with the characters fighting the forces of Fang La and beating back an invasion by the Liao Dynasty.

Summary of Facts

Water Margin (Shui Hu Zhuan) is one of the Four Great Classical Novels of Chinese literature, attributed to the 14th-century author Shi Nai'an. The novel follows 108 bandits known as the Stars of Destiny who gather at Mount Liang (Liangshan) marsh to oppose the corrupt Song dynasty official Gao Qiu. The story opens with Marshal Hong accidentally releasing the 108 spirits from imprisonment under a stone tortoise, from which they emerge as golden rays shooting out of a black cloud. These spirits are reborn as heroes divided into 36 Heavenly Spirits and 72 Earthly Fiends, ranked by a tablet in the Loyalty Hall.

The group's initial leader is Chao Gai, who dies before the Grand Assembly, after which Song Jiang takes over as leader. Song Jiang himself kills his concubine Yan Poxi when she attempts to blackmail him. Gao Qiu, the principal antagonist, rose from being a street urchin to Grand Marshal after impressing a prince with his football (cuju) skills. He frames the character Lin Chong for attempted assassination and conducts multiple sieges against Mount Liang, ultimately being captured during the fifth siege by characters with nicknames like 'Black Whirlwind' and 'Pilgrim.'

One of the most famous characters is Wu Song, who drinks 18 bowls of wine despite a warning sign and then beats a tiger to death with his bare fists. Wu Song later kills his brother's wife Pan Jinlian and her lover Ximen Qing. These episodes became the basis for the erotic spinoff novel The Plum in the Golden Vase (Jin Ping Mei), in which minor characters from Water Margin take center stage, and Ximen Qing dies from an aphrodisiac overdose.

Other memorable episodes include a couple nicknamed 'the Ogress' and 'the gardener' who run an inn where guests are killed and used as meat filling for buns; a character cursed to walk at superhuman speed after being caught eating beef without permission; a character who leaves his blind mother to fetch water only to find she has been eaten upon his return; and a character who accepts one gold piece out of a hundred offered, then murders his mistress when she demands all hundred. Only three of the outlaws are women, one of whom owns the Black Inn. Other notable characters include the physician An Daoquan, a messenger who uses Taoist talismans to travel vast distances, and Xiao Rang, the 'Sacred Handed Scholar.'

The novel exists in 'simple' and 'complex' editions (70- and 100-chapter versions) that diverge at the point where the main characters are granted amnesty. In later chapters, 68 of the 108 Stars of Destiny die in a campaign against the rebel Fang La, whose hierarchy includes Four Great Marshals and Eight Valiant Riders. The novel also ends with the characters beating back an invasion by the Liao Dynasty.

Sidney Shapiro produced the most acclaimed English translation, amalgamating the editions. Pearl Buck made a controversial translation titled All Men Are Brothers (drawing on a Confucius quote). The Japanese artist Utagawa Kuniyoshi created famous woodblock illustrations of the characters that inspired a craze for full-body tattooing. Wu Hsing-kuo adapted the novel into a Peking Rock Opera. The novel's opening was parodied in Wu Jingzi's The Scholars, and Yi Sik claimed the anonymous Korean novel about Hong Gildong was written to imitate Water Margin.
