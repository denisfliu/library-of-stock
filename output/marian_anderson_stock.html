Summary of Facts

Marian Anderson (1897–1993) was an American contralto widely considered one of the greatest singers of the twentieth century. Her career intersected repeatedly with American civil rights history. In 1939, the Daughters of the American Revolution (DAR) invoked their 'white performers only' policy to deny her use of Constitution Hall in Washington, D.C. Eleanor Roosevelt publicly resigned from the DAR in protest, writing about it in her syndicated column 'My Day,' and worked with Interior Secretary Harold Ickes to arrange an alternative venue. Ickes used his jurisdiction over the National Mall to allow Anderson to perform on the steps of the Lincoln Memorial on Easter Sunday, April 9, 1939, before an audience estimated at 75,000. Ickes opened the event by declaring 'all of us are free.' During the phrase 'of thee I sing' in 'My Country, 'Tis of Thee,' Anderson reportedly substituted 'to thee we sing.' The concert became a landmark civil rights event. Horace Pippin later painted a portrait of Anderson in front of Daniel Chester French's statue of Lincoln. Beauford Delaney painted her portrait with a characteristic yellow background.

In 1955, Anderson became the first African American to sing at the Metropolitan Opera, performing the role of Ulrica (the fortune-teller) in Verdi's Un ballo in maschera. She also sang at the 1963 March on Washington, sharing the stage with Mahalia Jackson, Joan Baez, and Mary Travers. She made a celebrated chamber recording of Brahms's Two Songs for Voice, Viola, and Piano alongside violist William Primrose and pianist Franz Rupp.
