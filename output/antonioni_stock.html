Italian film director (1912–2007) known for the 'Trilogy of Alienation' (L'Avventura, La Notte, L'Eclisse), all starring Monica Vitti, and for the English-language Blow-Up (1966). His films are characterized by themes of alienation, unresolved mysteries, and modernist landscapes. Blow-Up, based on a Julio Cortazar story, follows a London photographer who realizes he has photographed a murder. Died on the same day as Ingmar Bergman.

Summary of Facts

Michelangelo Antonioni (1912–2007) was an Italian filmmaker celebrated for his exploration of alienation, modern ennui, and unresolved ambiguity. He is most closely associated with his 'Trilogy of Alienation' — L'Avventura (1960), La Notte (1961), and L'Eclisse (1962) — all starring Monica Vitti. Seymour Chatman extended this to a tetralogy including Red Desert (1964). Antonioni died on the same day as Ingmar Bergman in 2007, sparking critical debate about their respective styles.

L'Avventura (1960) is Antonioni's most critically praised work. Anna, Claudia (Monica Vitti), and Sandro (Gabriele Ferzetti) go on a yacht trip to the volcanic Aeolian Islands; Anna disappears and is never found. The rest of the film follows Claudia and Sandro as they fall into an affair while ostensibly searching for Anna. The film ends with Claudia stroking Sandro's weeping head on a bench, with Mount Etna looming in the background. It was booed at its Cannes premiere but won the Special Jury Prize 'for seeking to create a new film language.' It placed second behind Citizen Kane in the 1962 Sight & Sound poll. Pauline Kael wrote about her struggle to get people to see it in her essay 'The Come-Dressed-As-the-Sick-Soul-of-Europe Parties.' Sofia Coppola used the technique of rain masking dialogue in Lost in Translation as a homage to the trilogy's style.

La Notte (1961), the middle part of the trilogy, follows the dissolving marriage of Giovanni and Lidia over one day. It stars Marcello Mastroianni and Jeanne Moreau, and ends with Mastroianni trying to make love to Moreau in a golf course bunker. Manny Farber attacked it in his essay 'White Elephant Art vs. Termite Art' as 'an example of the evils of continuity.'

L'Eclisse (1962), the third part, follows Vittoria (Monica Vitti), a translator, who begins an affair with Piero (Alain Delon) after meeting him at the stock exchange. The film's famous ending consists solely of shots of a streetlight where the couple agreed to meet — Terence McNally called it 'the empty requiem for two dead souls.'

Blow-Up (1966) is Antonioni's most frequently clued film and his most widely seen work. A London fashion photographer (David Hemmings) inadvertently photographs a murder in a park when he captures a couple embracing. He discovers the murder only after enlarging his photos. After a Yardbirds concert (where Jeff Beck smashes his guitar and the protagonist grabs the guitar neck before discarding it), the film ends with the photographer watching mimes play an imaginary tennis match in the park and eventually joining in — suggesting the reality of the murder has dissolved. Along the way, two nude women (including 19-year-old Jane Birkin and Gillian Hills) wrestle on an overturned clothes rack and then on a large purple sheet of paper. The protagonist also buys a large propeller for eight pounds from an antique shop. Scored by Herbie Hancock (his first English-language score), the film was based on Julio Cortazar's short story 'Las babas del diablo.' The femme fatale was played by Vanessa Redgrave. Francis Ford Coppola cited Blow-Up rather than Watergate as the inspiration for The Conversation.

Zabriskie Point (1970), Antonioni's only American film, is critically derided — included in The Fifty Worst Films of All Time. It ends with protagonist Daria imagining a modernist house in the desert exploding, with household items flying through the air in slow motion. Pink Floyd's 'Us and Them' was based on their work for this film's score. The Passenger (1975) stars Jack Nicholson as reporter David Locke. It is famous for its seven-minute tracking shot that begins in a Spanish hotel room, moves through the window bars into the courtyard, circles the plaza, and returns to reveal hitmen killing Locke — all in one uncut movement. Nicholson's character also picks up a young architecture student first shown reading in a Gaudí building.
