Venetian Renaissance architect (1508–1580), born Andrea di Pietro della Gondola, active in and around Vicenza. Best known for the Villa La Rotonda, the Teatro Olimpico, San Giorgio Maggiore, and the treatise The Four Books of Architecture. His Palladian style profoundly influenced Thomas Jefferson, Inigo Jones, and English Palladianism.
Villa La Rotonda (Villa Almerico-Capra)

Country villa built for Paolo Almerico outside Vicenza, with four identical temple porticos facing each of the cardinal directions and a large circular domed central hall. The design was inspired by the Pantheon in Rome. Thomas Jefferson used it as the primary model for Monticello; it also inspired Chiswick House and many other Palladian villas. The rooms all receive sunlight because the building is oriented 45 degrees from the cardinal directions. A service corridor by Vincenzo Scamozzi is adjacent. Small rooms flanking two of the building's entrances break its strict symmetry. The dome changed from a hemisphere to a flatter shape with an oculus, which itself was later covered by a cupola. Palladio himself classified it as a palace because of its proximity to the city, and praised its 'most exquisite views' giving 'the appearance of a grand theater.' Wikipedia

The Four Books of Architecture (I Quattro Libri dell'Architettura)

Palladio's 1570 architectural treatise divided into four books covering the five classical orders, city planning, and descriptions of Roman temples. Thomas Jefferson called it his 'Bible.' It popularized Palladian style in England after Inigo Jones's visit to Italy with the Earl of Arundel. The treatise ends with detailed descriptions of 26 Roman temples. It also claims that dimensions should follow mathematical ratios found in musical intervals, satisfying a universal mathematical order. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Born Andrea di Pietro della Gondola in 1508. Nickname 'Palladio' given by patron Gian Giorgio Trissino, after Pallas Athena. Active primarily in and around Vicenza, Italy. He adapted Roman temple designs for Renaissance villas, adapting Vitruvian principles for his buildings. He also designed a triumphal arch for Henry III's visit to Venice in 1574, and the church Il Redentore in Venice (with two minaret-like bell towers). Palladian style was introduced to England by Inigo Jones and later championed by Colen Campbell and Lord Burlington. His urban palazzi were inspired by Bramante's Palazzo Caprini. Rudolf Wittkower wrote about him extensively in Architectural Principles in the Age of Humanism. Karl Jenkins composed a string suite named 'Palladio' for De Beers. He championed the Tuscan/Etruscan column style as representing core Italian tradition.

Summary of Facts

Andrea Palladio (1508–1580), born Andrea di Pietro della Gondola, was a Venetian Renaissance architect active primarily in and around Vicenza in northern Italy. His nickname 'Palladio' was given to him by his patron, the scholar Gian Giorgio Trissino, after Pallas Athena. He adapted Roman temple designs for his many Renaissance villas and churches, synthesizing Vitruvian principles with practical innovation. Vicenza was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site to commemorate his many buildings there.

Palladio's most famous work is the Villa La Rotonda (also called Villa Almerico-Capra or Villa Capra), a country villa built for Paolo Almerico outside Vicenza. The villa features four identical temple porticos, one on each of the four cardinal directions, and a large central circular hall covered by a dome. The design was inspired by the Pantheon in Rome. Thomas Jefferson modeled Monticello directly on it, and Lord Burlington used it as the template for Chiswick House in England (William Kent's successive garden plans for Chiswick transitioned from French formal to early English landscape style). Le Corbusier's Villa Savoye was also inspired by the Villa Rotonda. The building's rooms all receive sunlight because the structure is oriented 45 degrees from the cardinal directions. Small rooms flanking two of its entrances break its strict symmetry, and the dome changed from a hemisphere to a flatter shape with an oculus later covered by a cupola. Palladio classified it as a palace because of its proximity to the city, praising its views giving 'the appearance of a grand theater.' The Portico of Octavia in Rome inspired its striking outer features.

His 1570 treatise The Four Books of Architecture (I Quattro Libri dell'Architettura) became one of the most influential architectural texts ever written. Jefferson called it his 'Bible.' The work covers the five classical orders, city planning, and ends with detailed descriptions of 26 Roman temples. The treatise also asserts that dimensions should follow mathematical ratios found in musical intervals, satisfying a universal mathematical order. Palladio also illustrated Daniele Barbaro's commentary on Vitruvius, and is called 'the architect of Vicenza' in Barbaro's introduction. Colen Campbell's 'Vitruvius Britannicus' praised Palladio as 'the best of the moderns.' The treatise became popular in England after the Earl of Arundel and Inigo Jones traveled to Italy. Leon Battista Alberti's De Re Aedificatoria also forms a key lineage.

His final commission was the Teatro Olimpico in Vicenza. After Palladio died, his student Vincenzo Scamozzi completed the theater and designed its famous trompe l'oeil stage set depicting streets receding from the stage. Palladio also designed the loggia of the Basilica Palladiana in Vicenza, whose tripartite arched openings are called 'Palladian windows' or Serlian windows (also named for Sebastiano Serlio).

Scamozzi also completed the facade of San Giorgio Maggiore in Venice, another major Palladio project. That church features a striking white marble facade — a four-column elevated classical temple facade superimposed over a lower, wider facade — designed to be seen from across the water. Palladio built on the geometric principles of Alberti for this monastery church. Inside hangs Tintoretto's diagonal Last Supper. Palladio's original plan for an oculus over the portal was never realized. Palladio also built Il Redentore church in Venice, incorporating two minaret-like bell towers.

Other major works include the Villa Foscari (La Malcontenta), a white stone mansion on the Brenta Riviera, built for brothers Nicolo and Alvise Foscari, with a raised front portico accessible by twin side stairways. It is nicknamed for the legend of an owner's wife locked inside. The interior has frescoes by Giambattista Zelotti. The Villa Barbaro, in Maser (Treviso), was built for Daniele Barbaro and decorated with elaborate frescoes by Veronese — including Abundance, Fortitude, and Fury, and an octagonal ceiling fresco of seven Olympians around a woman riding a snake. His urban palazzi were inspired by Bramante's Palazzo Caprini.

Palladian style was brought to England by Inigo Jones, whose Banqueting House at Whitehall was the first consciously classical building in Britain. Jones deviated from Palladio's mathematical constraints in the Queen's House, designed for Anne of Denmark. Rudolf Wittkower wrote about Palladio extensively in Architectural Principles in the Age of Humanism. The Welsh composer Karl Jenkins wrote a string suite titled 'Palladio' (used in De Beers advertisements).
