Venetian Renaissance architect (1508–1580), born Andrea di Pietro della Gondola, active in and around Vicenza. Best known for the Villa La Rotonda, the Teatro Olimpico, San Giorgio Maggiore, and the treatise The Four Books of Architecture. His Palladian style profoundly influenced Thomas Jefferson, Inigo Jones, and English Palladianism.
Villa La Rotonda (Villa Almerico-Capra)

Country villa built for Paolo Almerico outside Vicenza, with four identical temple porticos facing each of the cardinal directions and a large circular domed central hall. The design was inspired by the Pantheon in Rome. Thomas Jefferson used it as the primary model for Monticello; it also inspired Chiswick House and many other Palladian villas. The rooms all receive sunlight because the building is oriented 45 degrees from the cardinal directions. A service corridor by Vincenzo Scamozzi is adjacent. Wikipedia

The Four Books of Architecture (I Quattro Libri dell'Architettura)

Palladio's 1570 architectural treatise divided into four books covering the five classical orders, city planning, and descriptions of Roman temples. Thomas Jefferson called it his 'Bible.' It popularized Palladian style in England after Inigo Jones's visit to Italy with the Earl of Arundel. The treatise ends with detailed descriptions of 26 Roman temples. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Born Andrea di Pietro della Gondola in 1508. Nickname 'Palladio' given by patron Gian Giorgio Trissino, after Pallas Athena. Active primarily in and around Vicenza, Italy. He adapted Roman temple designs for Renaissance villas, adapting Vitruvian principles for his buildings. He also designed a triumphal arch for Henry III's visit to Venice in 1574. Palladian style was introduced to England by Inigo Jones and later championed by Colen Campbell and Lord Burlington.

Summary of Facts

Andrea Palladio (1508–1580), born Andrea di Pietro della Gondola, was a Venetian Renaissance architect active primarily in and around Vicenza in northern Italy. His nickname 'Palladio' was given to him by his patron, the scholar Gian Giorgio Trissino, after Pallas Athena. He adapted Roman temple designs for his many Renaissance villas and churches, synthesizing Vitruvian principles with practical innovation.

Palladio's most famous work is the Villa La Rotonda (also called Villa Almerico-Capra or Villa Capra), a country villa built for Paolo Almerico outside Vicenza. The villa features four identical temple porticos, one on each of the four cardinal directions, and a large central circular hall covered by a dome. The design was inspired by the Pantheon in Rome. Thomas Jefferson modeled Monticello directly on it, and Lord Burlington used it as the template for Chiswick House in England, among many other Palladian derivatives. The building's rooms all receive sunlight because the structure is oriented 45 degrees from the cardinal directions.

His 1570 treatise The Four Books of Architecture (I Quattro Libri dell'Architettura) became one of the most influential architectural texts ever written. Jefferson called it his 'Bible.' The work covers the five classical orders, city planning, and ends with detailed descriptions of 26 Roman temples. Palladio also illustrated Daniele Barbaro's commentary on Vitruvius, and is called 'the architect of Vicenza' in Barbaro's introduction. Colen Campbell's 'Vitruvius Britannicus' praised Palladio as 'the best of the moderns.'

His final commission was the Teatro Olimpico in Vicenza. After Palladio died, his student Vincenzo Scamozzi completed the theater and designed its famous trompe l'oeil stage set depicting streets receding from the stage. Scamozzi also completed the facade of San Giorgio Maggiore in Venice, another major Palladio project. San Giorgio Maggiore features a striking white marble facade—a four-column elevated classical temple facade superimposed over a lower, wider facade—designed to be seen from across the water. Inside hangs Tintoretto's diagonal Last Supper. Palladio's original plan for an oculus over the portal was never realized.

Other major works include the Villa Foscari (La Malcontenta), a white stone mansion on the Brenta Riviera with a raised front portico accessible by twin side stairways, nicknamed for the legend of an owner's wife locked inside; and the Villa Barbaro, built for Daniele Barbaro and decorated with frescoes by Veronese. Palladio's Palladian style was brought to England by Inigo Jones, whose Banqueting House at Whitehall was the first consciously classical building in Britain.
