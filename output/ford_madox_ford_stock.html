English modernist novelist, born Ford Hermann Hueffer. Best known for The Good Soldier (opens 'This is the saddest story I have ever heard'; narrated by unreliable narrator John Dowell about the intertwined lives of two couples at the spa town of Nauheim; Florence commits suicide by drinking prussic acid; Nancy Rufford becomes catatonic repeating 'shuttlecocks') and the Parade's End tetralogy about 'the last Tory' Christopher Tietjens and his manipulative wife Sylvia. Collaborated with Joseph Conrad on The Inheritors, Romance, and The Nature of a Crime. Founded The English Review and The Transatlantic Review. Championed literary impressionism. Also wrote The Fifth Queen trilogy about Catherine Howard and the war poem 'Antwerp.' Tom Stoppard adapted Parade's End for BBC TV in 2012. Hemingway described Ford as a 'well-clothed, up-ended hogshead' in A Moveable Feast.

Summary of Facts

Ford Madox Ford (born Ford Hermann Hueffer, 1873-1939) is an English modernist novelist, editor, and critic. His most famous work is The Good Soldier (1915), subtitled 'A Tale of Passion,' which opens with the iconic line 'This is the saddest story I have ever heard.' Ford had originally intended 'The Saddest Story' as the title, but his publisher objected due to the association with the war. An excerpt was published in the first issue of BLAST, the Vorticist magazine edited by Wyndham Lewis. The novel is narrated by the unreliable John Dowell about the entangled affairs of two couples at the German spa town of Nauheim. Florence Dowell fakes a heart ailment to conceal her affair with the title character, Captain Edward Ashburnham, and eventually commits suicide by drinking prussic acid after a character named Jimmy cuckolded Dowell. Nancy Rufford becomes catatonic after learning of Edward's suicide, able only to say 'shuttlecocks' and 'Credo in unum Deum omnipotentem,' and is sent to Ceylon. Edward's suicide comes by slitting his throat with a penknife after a telegram reading 'Safe Brindisi. Having a rattling good time.' Other characters include La Dolciquita, a Spanish dancer after Edward's money, and Maisie Maidan. The Kilsyte case involves Edward trying to comfort a young girl on a train. Dowell buys Branshaw Manor from Leonora and cares for Nancy. John Rodker called it 'the finest French novel in the English language.' Arthur Mizener's biography The Saddest Story reads Florence as inspired by Ford's affair with Violet Hunt.

Parade's End is a tetralogy following Christopher Tietjens, called 'the last Tory' and 'the most brilliant man in England,' who works in the Imperial Department of Statistics before serving in WWI. His manipulative wife Sylvia torments him throughout, while he develops a relationship with the suffragette Valentine Wannop, whom he meets after two suffragettes harass a minister on a golf course. Sylvia plots to cut down the Great Tree of Groby, a symbol of Tietjens's family. The third volume, A Man Could Stand Up--, is set on Armistice Day, featuring Valentine receiving a call from Mrs. Duchemin and a skylark nearly flying into Tietjens's mouth during battle. Tietjens is haunted by the death of Welsh soldier O Nine Morgan, killed by a mortar shell, and saves the life of Second Lieutenant Aranjuez. Two characters play a game composing sonnets and translating them into Latin. Ford's real-life relationship with Jean Rhys partly inspired the Tietjens-Valentine dynamic; Rhys fictionalized her side in Quartet (also published as Postures). Ford told Rhys to translate sentences into French to check their quality. Tom Stoppard wrote the screenplay for a 2012 BBC television adaptation starring Benedict Cumberbatch.

Ford collaborated extensively with Joseph Conrad on The Inheritors (a science fiction novel about Fourth Dimensionists taking over Earth), Romance, and The Nature of a Crime. He wrote a 'Personal Remembrance' of Conrad and The English Novel: From the Earliest Days to the Death of Joseph Conrad, describing how Conrad would 'scrutinize your face as a watchmaker looks into the works of a watch.' He founded The English Review and The Transatlantic Review (the latter publishing Gertrude Stein's The Making of Americans), helping to launch careers of writers including D.H. Lawrence and Hemingway. Hemingway memorably described Ford as a 'well-clothed, up-ended hogshead' in A Moveable Feast. Ford championed literary impressionism, more explicitly than Conrad, and admired the impressionism of Stephen Crane's The Red Badge of Courage. He wrote The Fifth Queen trilogy about Catherine Howard (with Privy Seal as the second volume) and the war poem 'Antwerp.' A Robert Lowell poem opens with Ford playing golf with Lloyd George. Ford worked for the War Propaganda Bureau during WWI, writing When Blood is Their Argument. His close friend May Sinclair coined 'stream of consciousness' in a literary context. J.M. Coetzee wrote his thesis on Ford Madox Ford, as depicted in his 'autrebiographical' trilogy. Ford also wrote Lady Whose Bright Eyes, which reverses the premise of Mark Twain's A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court.
