Italian director of Spaghetti Westerns. Best known for the Dollars Trilogy (A Fistful of Dollars, For a Few Dollars More, The Good, the Bad and the Ugly) starring Clint Eastwood, and the later Once Upon a Time in the West. Longtime collaborator with composer Ennio Morricone. A Fistful of Dollars was plagiarized from Kurosawa's Yojimbo, for which Leone was successfully sued. The term 'Ramen Western' (e.g. Tampopo) was coined in homage to his Spaghetti Westerns.

Set during the American Civil War. Score by Ennio Morricone, including 'The Ecstasy of Gold.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Sergio Leone is an Italian film director and the defining figure of the Spaghetti Western genre. He is most identified with The Good, the Bad and the Ugly (1966) and its climactic three-way standoff (Mexican standoff) at Sad Hill Cemetery between Tuco, Angel Eyes, and Blondie set to Ennio Morricone's 'The Ecstasy of Gold.' The film stars Clint Eastwood (Blondie), Eli Wallach (Tuco), and Lee Van Cleef (Angel Eyes) against an American Civil War backdrop.

Leone's Dollars Trilogy began with A Fistful of Dollars (1964), which was plagiarized from Akira Kurosawa's Yojimbo — Leone was successfully sued and settled out of court. The trilogy continued with For a Few Dollars More (1965) and concluded with The Good, the Bad and the Ugly. All three starred Clint Eastwood as 'The Man with No Name.' Eastwood later dedicated his own film Unforgiven to Leone and his other mentor Don Siegel.

Once Upon a Time in the West (1968) followed the trilogy. Its casting of Henry Fonda as villain Frank was deliberately jarring. Charles Bronson plays a nameless harmonica-playing protagonist seeking revenge. The film opens with a 10-minute mostly silent sequence of three killers waiting at a train station. Claudia Cardinale plays Jill McBain, whose husband Brett had dreamed of building the town of Sweetwater. Jason Robards plays bandit Cheyenne. Martin Scorsese later restored Leone's final major film, Once Upon a Time in America, and the 'Once Upon a Time' title structure directly inspired Quentin Tarantino's Once Upon a Time in Hollywood.

Leone's Zapata Western Duck, You Sucker! (1971) opens with a close-up of bandit Juan urinating on ants and features an eerie montage of upper-class Mexicans showing disgust at the poor.

Leone's most important collaborator was composer Ennio Morricone, who scored all his major Westerns. The Japanese genre 'Ramen Westerns' (e.g., Itami Juzo's Tampopo) was named as a direct homage to Leone's Spaghetti Westerns. Other notable 'Sergio' directors in the Spaghetti Western genre include Sergio Corbucci (Django, The Great Silence) and Sergio Sollima (The Big Gundown).
