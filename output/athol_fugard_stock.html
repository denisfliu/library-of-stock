Athol Fugard

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
White South African anti-apartheid playwright. Best known for "Master Harold"...and the Boys, about the white teenager Hally and his black servants Sam and Willie. Also wrote Blood Knot, The Island (co-written with John Kani and Winston Ntshona), Sizwe Bansi Is Dead, The Road to Mecca, and Boesman and Lena. Founded the Serpent Players in Port Elizabeth. Many plays set in Port Elizabeth. Also wrote the novel Tsotsi.
"Master Harold"...and the Boys

Fugard's best-known play, set in a tearoom in Port Elizabeth. The white teenager Hally (Master Harold) becomes distraught after learning that his alcoholic, racist, crippled father is returning from the hospital. He discusses an essay about 'a world without collisions,' using ballroom dancing as a metaphor. Sam reminisces about helping Hally make a homemade kite but not being able to fly it with him because he was sitting on a Whites Only bench. The play ends with Hally spitting in Sam's face. Sam and Willie practice ballroom dancing throughout; Willie beats his girlfriend Hilda Samuels when she makes mistakes. The kite symbolizes the irreparable rift. Hally's scalars homework leads to a discussion about Tolstoy, Darwin, and Fleming as 'men of magnitude.' Sam spends his bus fare to play Sarah Vaughan's 'Little Man' on the jukebox. Based on a fictionalized incident from Fugard's childhood. Wikipedia

Blood Knot

A play about two brothers, Morris and Zachariah, who live in a shack in the 'colored' section of Port Elizabeth. Morris can pass for white; Zachariah cannot. They exchange letters with a white pen-pal named Ethel Lange, and Morris buys gentleman's clothes with farm money for a date with Ethel. They play role-playing games to prepare for Ethel's visit. Morris beats Zachariah with an umbrella after being called a 'swartgat.' They relive a childhood game of 'To Hell and Gone' in a rusted Chevy, imagining driving through a flock of butterflies. Zachariah's job as a gatekeeper at a zoo gives him sore feet; Morris prepares hot water baths (with bath salts). Opens with Zach discovering Morris bought cheaper bath salts than Dr. Schultz's. The Island

Co-written with John Kani and Winston Ntshona (Serpent Players). Two prisoners, John and Winston (named after the actors), rehearse and perform an abridged version of Sophocles's Antigone in prison. Originally titled after the guard Hodoshe. Opens with a mute scene of the two shoveling dirt into piles at each other's feet. During the Antigone performance, a character shouts 'Gods of Our Fathers! My Land! My Home! Time waits no longer.' One character urinates on his hand to wash his friend's bloodied eye. Props include a necklace of rusted nails and plastic breasts (which one man refuses to wear). Nominated for 1975 Tony Award for Best Play. Wikipedia

Sizwe Bansi Is Dead

Co-written with John Kani and Winston Ntshona. The title character Sizwe Bansi adopts the identity of the dead Robert Zwelinzima to obtain a work-seeker's permit. He gets his photograph taken in Styles's photography studio and sends a letter and photo to his wife. Styles left his Ford factory job to become a photographer. Also nominated for 1975 Tony (Kani and Ntshona won Best Leading Actor). Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Athol Fugard is a white South African anti-apartheid playwright, overwhelmingly identified by his play "Master Harold"...and the Boys. He co-founded the Serpent Players in Port Elizabeth with actors John Kani and Winston Ntshona.

"Master Harold"...and the Boys is Fugard's most frequently clued work, appearing as the giveaway in nearly every tossup. Set in a tearoom in Port Elizabeth, it features the white teenager Hally (Master Harold) and his black servants Sam and Willie. The central conflict arises when Hally learns his alcoholic, racist, crippled father is returning from the hospital. Hally discusses an essay about 'a world without collisions,' using ballroom dancing as a metaphor for politics. The kite motif is crucial: Sam reminisces about helping Hally build a homemade kite, but couldn't fly it with him because he was sitting on a Whites Only bench. The play climaxes with Hally spitting in Sam's face. Sam and Willie practice ballroom dancing throughout; Willie beats his girlfriend Hilda Samuels during practice. Hally's scalars homework leads to a discussion of Tolstoy, Darwin, and Fleming as 'men of magnitude.' Sam spends his bus fare to play Sarah Vaughan's 'Little Man' on the jukebox.

Blood Knot features brothers Morris (who can pass for white) and Zachariah living in the 'colored' section of Port Elizabeth. They exchange letters with a white pen-pal, Ethel Lange, and Morris buys gentleman's clothes for a date with her. They play role-playing games and relive a childhood game of 'To Hell and Gone' in a rusted Chevy, imagining driving through butterflies. Morris beats Zachariah with an umbrella after being called a 'swartgat.' Zachariah's job as a gatekeeper gives him sore feet that Morris soothes with bath salts.

The Island was co-written with John Kani and Winston Ntshona. Two prisoners (John and Winston, named after the actors) rehearse and perform an abridged Antigone in prison. Originally titled for the guard Hodoshe. Opens with a mute shoveling scene. Props include a necklace of rusted nails and plastic breasts. One character shouts 'Gods of Our Fathers! My Land! My Home! Time waits no longer.' during the Antigone performance. Nominated for the 1975 Tony.

Sizwe Bansi Is Dead (also co-written with Kani and Ntshona) features Sizwe adopting the identity of the dead Robert Zwelinzima for a work permit, getting photographed in Styles's studio. Styles left his Ford factory job. Also nominated for the 1975 Tony.

The Road to Mecca is set in New Bethesda and centers on the elderly widow Miss Helen, who sculpts a whimsical statue garden (including an Easter Island head), based on the real 'Owl House.' Elsa Barlow tries to prevent her from moving to a retirement home. Helen realizes the big word is 'trust.'

Other works include Boesman and Lena (home bulldozed, wandering, old man Outa dies at campfire), My Children! My Africa! (teacher Mr. M killed by revolutionaries), The Train Driver (white man's guilt after running over black woman and baby), the novel Tsotsi (gang leader finds a baby), Valley Song, The Painted Rocks at Revolver Creek, Hello and Goodbye, A Place with the Pigs, The Coat, and No-Good Friday.
