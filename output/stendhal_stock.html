French author born Marie-Henri Beyle. Best known for the novels The Red and the Black and The Charterhouse of Parma. His treatise On Love describes the process of 'crystallization' as a metaphor for falling in love, inspired by a visit to the Salzburg salt mines. His near-fainting experience in Florence's Basilica of Santa Croce inspired the coining of 'Stendhal syndrome,' which occurs when people are overwhelmed by art of great beauty. His autobiography The Life of Henry Brulard is disguised as a 'novel imitating The Vicar of Wakefield.' W.G. Sebald's Vertigo opens with a biography of him.
The Red and the Black

Stendhal's most frequently asked-about novel, published in 1830. It follows the ambitious Julien Sorel, a carpenter's son who becomes a tutor to the mayor's children in Besancon, has an affair with the mayor's wife Madame de Renal, attends a seminary under director Monsieur Pirard, and later becomes secretary to the Marquis de la Mole in Paris. He seduces the marquis's daughter Mathilde de la Mole and courts Madame de Fervaques to make Mathilde jealous. He is ultimately executed after shooting Madame de Renal. The title colors symbolize the French army (red) and the Catholic church (black). The novel was placed on the Catholic Church's Index of banned books. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Stendhal (pen name of Marie-Henri Beyle) is a major figure in 19th-century French literature, asked about primarily through his two great novels, The Red and the Black and The Charterhouse of Parma, as well as his treatise On Love.

The Red and the Black (1830) is by far the most clued work. It follows Julien Sorel, an ambitious carpenter's son who becomes tutor to the mayor's children and begins an affair with the mayor's wife, Madame de Renal, which is discovered by the mayor's adversary M. Valenod. After studying at a seminary under director Monsieur Pirard, Julien becomes secretary to the Marquis de la Mole in Paris and seduces the marquis's daughter Mathilde. He courts Madame de Fervaques to make Mathilde jealous. The novel's most commonly clued endpoint is Julien's execution after he shoots Madame de Renal, and Mathilde's act of kissing his severed head and burying it in a marble cave — imitating the legend of her ancestor and Queen Margot (Marguerite de Valois). The title colors symbolize the French army (red) and the Catholic church (black). The novel was placed on the Catholic Church's Index of banned books over a century after publication, and the epigraph to the American Gigolo screenplay is a quote about Julien Sorel.

The Charterhouse of Parma centers on Fabrizio (Fabrice) del Dongo, who dresses as a dead hussar and stumbles into the Battle of Waterloo during Napoleon's Hundred Days campaign. Fabrice kills Giletti, the protector of his actress lover Marietta Valsera, and is imprisoned in a tower where he falls in love with the bird-tending Clelia, communicating with her by holding up large lettered signs. He is aided by his aunt, the Countess Gina, and her lover Count Mosca, and ultimately becomes a monk. Naples is a primary setting.

Stendhal's treatise On Love describes the process of 'crystallization' — his metaphor for falling in love, inspired by tree branches left in a Salzburg salt mine that become encrusted with crystals. He sketched the concept as a diagram on a playing card, depicting a journey from Bologna to Rome with stages including Admiration, Acknowledgement, Hope, and Delight. The treatise also contains his idea that beauty is 'the promise of happiness,' illustrated by an anecdote about Alberic admiring his mistress's smallpox scar.

Biographically, Stendhal is associated with the 'Stendhal syndrome' — the phenomenon of being overwhelmed by great art — inspired by his near-fainting in Florence's Basilica of Santa Croce. W.G. Sebald's novel Vertigo opens with a biography of Stendhal, including a photograph of the plaster cast of Metilde Dembowski's hand from his desk. Stendhal discussed his feelings for Dembowski in the essay 'Maxims of Love.' His autobiography, The Life of Henry Brulard, was disguised as 'a novel imitating The Vicar of Wakefield,' and he described seducing lovers by reading Rousseau's The New Heloise. His first novel was Armance, about Octave de Malivert fighting for Greek independence, and his unfinished novel Lamiel follows an orphan leaving Normandy. He also wrote a short story inspired by The Cenci murder (1837) and the novella Vanina Vanini.
