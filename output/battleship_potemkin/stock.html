Mutiny / 'Men and Maggots' (Acts I–III)

The mutiny is sparked by sailors' refusal to eat borscht/soup made with maggot-infested meat. Sailor leader Vakulinchuk dies in the conflict and is laid in a tent on a pier. The doctor Smirnov is killed. A wild-haired priest thumps his cross into his palm. Soldiers cover sailors under a tarpaulin before being convinced not to fire. A plate inscribed 'Give us this day our daily bread' is smashed by a character who discovers the maggots. The only color in the film is on a red flag.

Director / Production / Legacy

Directed by Sergei Eisenstein; cinematography by Eduard Tisse. Commissioned by the Soviet government on the success of Eisenstein's earlier film Strike (1924). Made in 1925. The Berlin cut introduced Edmund Meisel's score. Considered a founding example of intellectual Montage Theory. Francis Bacon extensively referenced the screaming nurse image in his work. Chris Marker's A Grin Without a Cat opens with footage from the film.

Summary of Facts

Battleship Potemkin is a 1925 silent Soviet film directed by Sergei Eisenstein with cinematography by Eduard Tisse. It was commissioned by the Soviet government following the success of Eisenstein's earlier film Strike (1924). The film dramatizes the 1905 mutiny aboard the Imperial Russian battleship Potemkin and the subsequent Cossack massacre of civilians in Odessa.

The film is structured in five acts. The first act, 'Men and Maggots,' shows the mutiny being triggered when sailors discover their meat/borscht is infested with maggots. A plate inscribed 'Give us this day our daily bread' is found or smashed after the discovery. A wild-haired priest bounces his cross into his palm while the doctor Smirnov is killed. Soldiers are about to fire on fellow sailors covered by a tarpaulin who refused the borscht, until they are convinced not to shoot. Sailor leader Vakulinchuk dies in the conflict and is laid in a tent on a pier. A man in the crowd shouts 'kill the Jews' and is beaten by those gathered around the tent. The only color in the entire film appears on a red flag.

The Odessa Steps sequence in Act IV is the film's most famous section and a foundational example of intellectual montage. Tsarist Cossack soldiers descend the steps massacring a crowd of civilians. The sequence features a baby carriage rolling uncontrolled down the steps; a nurse wearing pince-nez whose glasses are broken and who bleeds from her eye; a legless man crawling alongside the retreating mob; a woman carrying her trampled child back toward the soldiers; and three separate shots of stone lion statues edited together to give the illusion of the lions rising up. The sequence begins after the caption 'suddenly...' interrupts a shot of the waving crowd. The final shot of the whole film shows the battleship charging directly at the viewer, appearing to split the screen.

The Berlin cut of the film introduced composer Edmund Meisel's score. Eisenstein's Montage Theory categorized five types of montage: intellectual, overtonal, tonal, rhythmic, and metric; he developed these concepts alongside peers including Vsevolod Pudovkin. The film was a major influence on Francis Bacon, who painted 'Study for the Nurse in the Battleship Potemkin, 1957' and incorporated the screaming, bloody-eyed nurse image across his screaming popes series. Chris Marker's A Grin Without a Cat opens with footage from the film spliced with protest footage. Brian De Palma paid direct homage to the Odessa Steps sequence in The Untouchables, moving a shootout to Chicago's Union Station with a pram rolling down stairs. Terry Gilliam parodied the sequence in Brazil by substituting a vacuum cleaner for the baby carriage. Romanian director Radu Jude engaged with the film in two works: a short (The Potemkinists) about a sculptor making a canal-side monument to the film's protagonists, and a 2022 feature that freeze-frames the film's final scene of men waving their hats.
