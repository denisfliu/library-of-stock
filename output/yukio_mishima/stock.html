Japanese novelist and playwright (born Kimitake Hiraoka) who committed seppuku after a failed coup attempt in 1970 (the Mishima Incident). Best known for the Sea of Fertility tetralogy, The Temple of the Golden Pavilion, Confessions of a Mask, and The Sailor Who Fell from Grace with the Sea. Founder of the Shield Society. Obsessed with bodybuilding, martial arts, and right-wing nationalism. Wrote the autobiographical essay Sun and Steel. Also wrote plays including Madame de Sade and My Friend Hitler.
The Sea of Fertility Tetralogy

A four-novel tetralogy about the lawyer/judge Shigekuni Honda, who discovers that his friend Kiyoaki Matsugae is successively reincarnated — as the reactionary nationalist Isao Iinuma (Runaway Horses), the Thai princess Ying Chan (The Temple of Dawn), and the teenager Toru Yasunaga (The Decay of an Angel). Honda recognizes each reincarnation by a distinctive pattern of three moles. Spring Snow features the Ayakura and Matsugae families and Kiyoaki's affair with Satoko. The tetralogy ends at Gesshu Temple, where an abbess does not recall if another character existed, leading Honda to ponder 'perhaps there has been no I' in a garden with 'no memories, nothing.' "Patriotism" and the Mishima Incident

"Patriotism" is a short story thought to foreshadow Mishima's own death: Lieutenant Shinji and his wife Reiko have sex under a scroll displaying 'Sincerity' before killing themselves rather than suppressing a mutiny. Mishima's failed coup (the Mishima Incident) in 1970: he led the Shield Society, delivered a barely audible speech from a balcony to the JSDF, then committed seppuku. His kaishakunin botched three attempts to behead him. Oe Kenzaburo's The Day He Himself Shall Wipe Our Tears Away parodies the event. Mishima also wrote a death poem: 'falling is the essence of a flower.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Yukio Mishima (born Kimitake Hiraoka) is a Japanese novelist, playwright, and nationalist who committed seppuku after a failed coup attempt in 1970, making his death and the circumstances surrounding it one of the most frequently clued aspects of any literary figure.

The Sea of Fertility tetralogy is his most extensively clued work. It consists of four novels — Spring Snow, Runaway Horses, The Temple of Dawn, and The Decay of an Angel — following the lawyer/judge Shigekuni Honda as he discovers successive reincarnations of his friend Kiyoaki Matsugae. Honda recognizes each reincarnation by a distinctive pattern of three moles. Spring Snow features the Ayakura and Matsugae families, Kiyoaki's affair with Satoko, the humiliation of two Siamese princes searching for a lost emerald ring, and Kiyoaki's deathbed promise to Honda ('I'll see you again'). Runaway Horses features the reactionary nationalist Isao Iinuma. The Temple of Dawn is set partly in Thailand, where Honda visits and holds a pool party at his villa to check Ying Chan's moles. The Decay of an Angel features the teenager Toru Yasunaga, whose smoking in bed inadvertently burns Honda's villa. The tetralogy ends at Gesshu Temple, where an abbess does not recall whether another character existed, leading Honda to ponder 'perhaps there has been no I' in a garden with 'no memories, nothing.' The phrase 'When you meet the Buddha, kill the Buddha' appears in the tetralogy.

The Temple of the Golden Pavilion (Kinkaku-ji) is based on a real-life arson. The protagonist Mizoguchi burns down the Golden Temple, which he calls 'a symbol of the real world's evanescence.' He is struck by impotence after visions of the temple and remarks 'the first real problem I faced in my life was that of beauty.' He resolves to destroy it after seeing the sea. Kashiwagi slaps his girlfriend after asking Mizoguchi to steal flowers for ikebana.

Confessions of a Mask is a semi-autobiographical 'I-novel' in which Kochan explores his repressed homosexuality. He has a sexual awakening while masturbating to Guido Reni's painting of Saint Sebastian, daydreams about cannibalizing a fellow student, pretends to be attracted to Sonoko, is fascinated by the armpits of Omi, and is 'choked by desire' after seeing a night-soil man. The novel was inspired by Mori Ogai's Vita Sexualis.

The Sailor Who Fell from Grace with the Sea features Noboru spying through a peephole on his mother Fusako having sex with the sailor Ryuji (who works on the Rakuyo). Noboru and his gang of boys drug Ryuji's tea and dissect him. Noboru murders a cat during a meeting with 'the chief.'

'Patriotism' foreshadows Mishima's own death: Lieutenant Shinji and wife Reiko have sex under a scroll displaying 'Sincerity' before killing themselves. The Mishima Incident of 1970 saw Mishima, leader of the Shield Society, deliver a barely audible speech from a balcony to the JSDF before committing seppuku. His kaishakunin botched three attempts to behead him. His death poem reads 'falling is the essence of a flower.' Oe Kenzaburo parodied the incident in The Day He Himself Shall Wipe Our Tears Away.

Sun and Steel is an autobiographical essay about Mishima's bodybuilding obsession, comparing words to 'white ants' for their 'corrosive function.' Its epilogue describes flying in an F-104 and comparing a cloud to an Ouroboros that 'mocks all opposites.'

Other works include The Sound of Waves (pearl diver Shinji and Hatsue), Forbidden Colors (Shunsuke crafts Yuichi into a weapon of revenge against women), Madame de Sade (all-female play staged by Ingmar Bergman), My Friend Hitler (all-male play), Five Modern Noh Plays, and Life for Sale. Mishima modeled for the erotic photography collection Ordeal by Roses and posed as Saint Sebastian. Paul Schrader directed the biopic Mishima: A Life in Four Chapters. Philip Glass's Third String Quartet is nicknamed after Mishima. Henry Miller wrote Reflections on the Death of Mishima, and Marguerite Yourcenar wrote the essay 'A Vision of the Void' about his suicide. South Korean author Shin Kyung-sook plagiarized from 'Patriotism.'
