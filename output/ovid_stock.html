Metamorphoses

The Metamorphoses is Ovid's fifteen-book epic narrative poem recounting mythological Transformations, from the creation of the universe through the apotheosis of Julius Caesar. It is the most frequently clued work by Ovid, appearing in multiple tossups and bonus parts. The poem opens with the creation of the world and the ages of man, and its final book discusses Pythagoras, metempsychosis, and Julius Caesar's soul becoming a comet. Key stories include Pyramus and Thisbe, Pomona and Vertumnus, Arachne's tapestry, and Iphis. The poem has been translated by Arthur Golding (in fourteeners), retold by Ted Hughes in Tales from Ovid, and adapted in the medieval Ovide Moralise.

Amores

The Amores is Ovid's collection of elegiac love poetry. Its key clue is the conceit in which Cupid steals a metrical foot from The Poet, forcing him to write in elegiac couplets rather than epic hexameters. The collection is also associated with Christopher Marlowe's translation and Donna Zuckerberg's analysis of 'The Ovid Method' in her book Not All Dead White Men. Geoffrey Hill's 'Ovid in the Third Reich' juxtaposes the Amores' immorality with Nazi atrocities.

Ars Amatoria

The Ars Amatoria (Art of Love) is Ovid's didactic poem written unconventionally in elegiac couplets. It consists of three sections, with the first two giving male readers advice on picking up women. The poem humorously likens love to military service and imagines the Rape of the Sabine Women as a theatrical production. It is identified as the 'carmen' in Ovid's 'carmen et error' explanation for his exile, and it inspired Andreas Capellanus's medieval work on courtly love.

Exile and Later Works

Ovid's exile is a major biographical topic in quizbowl. He was exiled (likely by Augustus) and spent his banishment in Tomis near the Black Sea. He attributed his exile to 'carmen et error' ('a poem and a mistake') in the Tristia, with the 'carmen' referring to the Ars Amatoria and the 'error' remaining the subject of speculation (theories include a scandal involving Augustus's daughters). His exile works include the Tristia, Letters from the Black Sea (Epistulae ex Ponto), and the curse poem Ibis. David Malouf fictionalized the exile in An Imaginary Life.

Heroides

The Heroides is a collection of verse epistles written by Ovid from the perspectives of mythological heroines. The most notable clue involves Dido's letter to Aeneas, in which she asks 'What crime have I committed except to have loved?' before her suicide.

General / Biographical

Ovid is identified as a Roman poet of the Amores and Ars Amatoria, and is the author of the Metamorphoses. He wrote in elegiac couplets and is associated with the Augustan age of Latin poetry. A Quevedo poem about Gongora's nose also compares it to Ovid's nose, 'but nosier,' suggesting Ovid was known for having a large nose.

Summary of Facts

Ovid (Publius Ovidius Naso) is a Roman poet most frequently clued through his epic poem the Metamorphoses, his elegiac love poetry collection Amores, and his didactic poem Ars Amatoria. The Metamorphoses, a fifteen-book narrative poem recounting mythological Transformations, is by far his most clued work. It opens with the creation of the universe and the four (or five) ages of man, paralleling Hesiod's account. Key stories within the Metamorphoses include the tragedy of Pyramus and Thisbe (a man stabs himself after falsely believing a lion ate his beloved, causing mulberries to turn purple; the lovers communicated through a crack in their wall), the tale of Pomona and Vertumnus (including the disguised old lady telling the story of Iphis hanging himself), the ekphrastic tapestry of Arachne, and the myth of Iphis transformed into a man. The poem's fifteenth and final book centers on apotheosis, discussing Pythagoras and his doctrine of metempsychosis, the soul of Julius Caesar becoming a comet, and the famous closing line 'vivam' ('I will live'). The poem ends with a claim that The Poet's fame will live eternally beyond the stars. The Metamorphoses was influenced by an earlier work by Nicander of Colophon. Arthur Golding produced the first complete English translation using 'fourteeners' rather than the original hexameters, a translation Ezra Pound called the most beautiful book in the English language. Ted Hughes wrote Tales from Ovid, a retelling of twenty-four stories from the poem. The medieval Ovide Moralise is a vast verse adaptation with ethical commentary in Old French. Jeffrey Eugenides references the Metamorphoses in Middlesex, where Cal translates 'ex ovo omnia' and markets himself as Hermaphroditus. Ali Smith's Girl Meets Boy reinterprets the myth of Iphis. Patricia Westerford in Richard Powers' The Overstory receives a copy and muses on its opening line.

Ovid's Amores is an elegiac poetry collection in which Cupid steals a metrical foot to force The Poet to write elegiac couplets. A poem from the Amores has the speaker succumb to being a 'fresh prize' for a god who 'stole a foot away' and shot him through the heart with an arrow. Donna Zuckerberg's book Not All Dead White Men includes a chapter titled 'The Ovid Method' demanding an ironic reading of his poem calling a mistress' birthday 'one of great terror.' Christopher Marlowe translated an elegy by Ovid, changing the speaker's lament of impotence into insults toward his partner. Geoffrey Hill's poem 'Ovid in the Third Reich' examines Nazi atrocities by juxtaposition with the immorality Ovid advocated in the Amores.

Ovid's Ars Amatoria is a didactic work written in elegiac couplets (unconventionally for didactic poetry) that gives advice on love and seduction. Its first two of three sections give male readers advice on how to pick up women. It humorously likens love to military service and imagines the Rape of the Sabine Women as a theatrical production. The Ars Amatoria inspired Andreas Capellanus's influential 12th-century work on courtly love. It was followed by the Remedia Amoris.

Ovid's exile is a major biographical topic. He was exiled (likely by Augustus) and spent his years of banishment in Tomis near the Black Sea. He attributed his exile to 'carmen et error' ('a poem and a mistake') in his Tristia, with the 'carmen' referring to the Ars Amatoria and the 'error' still fueling speculation, including theories about a scandal involving Augustus's daughters. The Tristia also comments that the exile resulted in the Metamorphoses being 'cut short by its author's sad flight.' His Letters from the Black Sea (Epistulae ex Ponto) were also written in Tomis. David Malouf fictionalized the exile in his novella An Imaginary Life. Ovid also wrote the curse poem Ibis, which may target Augustus, inspired by a poem by Callimachus. Ovid is also noted for having had a large nose, referenced humorously in a Quevedo poem about Gongora.
