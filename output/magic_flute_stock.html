Singspiel opera by Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (Die Zauberflöte), premiered in 1791. Features the hero Tamino and the comic birdcatcher Papageno on a quest to rescue Pamina from the sorcerer Sarastro, aided by the title enchanted flute and magic bells. Includes the Queen of the Night — one of opera's most demanding coloratura soprano roles — and Masonic symbolism throughout.

Papageno – The Birdcatcher

The comic character of the birdcatcher who accompanies Tamino on his quest. Given magic bells (glockenspiel) by the Queen of the Night. Typically costumed with colorful bird-like attire. The role was debuted by librettist Emanuel Schikaneder himself; Mozart often doubled his vocal lines because Schikaneder was not the best singer. Happily finds a similarly named girlfriend (Papagena) at the end. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

The Magic Flute (Die Zauberflöte) is a comic singspiel opera by Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, premiered on September 30, 1791 at the theater of its librettist, Emanuel Schikaneder, in Vienna. It is based loosely on Jean Terrason's pro-Masonic fantasy novel The Life of Sethos, and Masonic symbolism pervades the libretto and stagecraft.

The plot follows the hero Tamino, who is saved from a serpent by three servants of the Queen of the Night in the opening scene. The Queen shows him a portrait of her daughter Pamina and sends him on a quest to rescue her from the sorcerer Sarastro — who turns out not to be evil at all. Tamino is given the enchanted title instrument (which can change sorrow into joy and cause wild animals to dance) along with the comic birdcatcher Papageno, who receives a set of magic bells. Tamino falls in love with Pamina's portrait. Three mysterious spirits (children) guide the pair on their quest. In Act I, a quintet occurs in which several characters can only sing 'Hm! Hm! Hm! Hm!' after their mouths are padlocked. Tamino and Pamina pass through chambers of fire and water guarded by the Men in Armour. Act II opens in a palm grove where Sarastro invokes the gods Isis and Osiris. Papageno uses the magic bells to make the pursuing villain Monostatos dance away, and later to summon Papagena after being talked out of suicide.

The Queen of the Night is one of opera's most demanding coloratura soprano roles. In her Act II aria 'Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen' (Hell's Vengeance Boils in My Heart), sung when Pamina refuses her order to murder Sarastro, the soprano must reach F6 and cover two full octaves (F4 to F6). The coloratura passage begins with a turn on A followed by eight eighth notes on high C descending a fifth to F, then repeated down a third. The role was created specifically by Mozart for the soprano Josepha Hofer (the sister of his sister-in-law Aloysia Weber).

Sarastro's bass aria 'In diesen heil'gen Hallen' (In These Sacred Halls) features him forgiving the woman ordered to stab him. The role of Papageno was debuted by Schikaneder himself; because he was not a skilled singer, Mozart often doubled his vocal lines in the orchestra.

Productions have ranged from an 1815 staging in which the Queen descended through a starry sky on a moon, to Julie Taymor's celebrated 2004 Met production inspired by Indonesian shadow puppet theater and Japanese bunraku. Fernando Sor composed an 'Introduction and Variations' on the theme 'Das klinget so herrlich' from the opera. A dubious theory holds that the composer Joseph Boulogne (Chevalier de Saint-Georges) was the inspiration for the villain Monostatos; another holds that Empress Maria Theresa inspired the Queen of the Night.
