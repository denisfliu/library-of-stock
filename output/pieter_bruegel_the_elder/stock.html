Summary of Facts

Pieter Bruegel the Elder (c. 1525–1569) was a Flemish Northern Renaissance painter renowned for peasant genre scenes, proverb paintings, and landscapes. He was the father of Jan 'Velvet' Brueghel and Pieter 'Hell' Brueghel the Younger, and worked with Antwerp publisher Hieronymus Cock on the Large Landscapes print series.

Landscape with the Fall of Icarus is his most commonly tested painting. A ploughman ignores Icarus's legs sticking out of the sea while a ship sails toward the setting sun; a shepherd with a brown dog leans on his staff looking skyward and a fisherman also ignores Icarus. Both extant versions (including the smaller van Buren Museum copy) are now considered early copies. The partridge, shepherd, and fisherman derive from Ovid's Metamorphoses. Andre Breton attached a cutout of the painting to 'Dusseldorf was bombed yesterday for the fiftieth time' in his First Papers of Surrealism. The painting inspired W.H. Auden's 'Musee des Beaux Arts,' William Carlos Williams's ekphrasis ('the whole pageantry of the year was awake tingling'), and Robert Baldwin argued the nearest figure represented hope per First Corinthians. It belongs to the world landscape (Weltlandschaft) tradition pioneered by Joachim Patinir.

Hunters in the Snow (1565) is the first panel of his Labors of the Months series (five of six survive: The Gloomy Day, Haymaking, The Harvesters, The Return of the Herd). Three hunters and dogs trudge through a winter landscape; one carries a dead fox; villagers skate, play ice-stock, and hockey on a frozen lake. A tavern sign on the left reads 'this is in the stag' featuring St. Hubertus and a deer. Black birds perch in trees arranged in a receding diagonal. A mill with a frozen water wheel and a woman pulled on a board toward a double-arched bridge appear in the lower right. Abbas Kiarostami used this painting for the first shot of his posthumous film 24 Frames.

Netherlandish Proverbs (1559) — also called The Blue Cloak, The Topsy Turvy World, or The Folly of the World — illustrates 112 Dutch idioms from a high vantage point. A woman puts a blue cloak on her cuckolded husband; a fox and stork dine together; a man defecates out a window; a woman ties the devil to a pillow; two bears dance with backs to each other; a man struggles to reach two loaves of bread. It does not include written words, departing from Frans Hogenberg's similar print. Mark Meadow argues it was informed by Erasmus's treatise Copia.

The Tower of Babel (three versions; most famous in Vienna's Kunsthistorisches Museum) was possibly based on Colosseum engravings. King Nimrod stands in the foreground; two men walk in the wheel of a giant pulley; men haul supplies near red rock slabs. Zygmunt Wazbinski argued the Vienna version contains a portrait of Alexander the Great.

The Fight Between Carnival and Lent (1559) shows a gaunt man with two fish facing a fat man with a meat pie on his head who straddles a beer barrel and wields a spit. The Peasant Wedding shows a feast in a barn with soup carried on a door, a boy in a red cap, and a paper-crowned bride not feasting. The Peasant Dance companion shows a devotional image of Mary on a tree ignored by the revelers.

Other major works: Mad Meg (sword-wielding woman leads army to Hell); The Triumph of Death (skeletons playing trumpets); The Fall of the Rebel Angels (Bosch-influenced transformation into fish, butterflies, gnomes); The Blind Leading the Blind (six men stumbling diagonally, which influenced Sargent's Gassed); The Harvesters (wheat field, peasants under tree); Big Fish Eat Little Fish (engraving of helmeted man attacking giant fish); Two Monkeys (chained under archway overlooking Antwerp).
