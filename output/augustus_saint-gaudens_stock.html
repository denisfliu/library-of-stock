American Renaissance sculptor known for the Robert Gould Shaw Memorial in Boston, the Standing Lincoln, the Sherman Memorial, the Adams Memorial, the Double Eagle gold coin, and Diana. Founded the Cornish Art Colony in New Hampshire. Born in Dublin. Involved in the City Beautiful movement and the 1893 Chicago World's Fair.

Summary of Facts

Augustus Saint-Gaudens (1848-1907) was an Irish-born American Renaissance/Beaux-Arts sculptor, one of the most prominent American sculptors of the 19th century. Born in Dublin, he later created a monument for his birthplace honoring Charles Stuart Parnell.

His most frequently clued work is the Robert Gould Shaw Memorial (frequency 6, giveaway), a bronze relief on Boston Common depicting Robert Shaw on horseback leading the soldiers of the all-black 54th Massachusetts Infantry Regiment, with an angel of Victory (holding olive branch and poppies) flying overhead. The work commemorates the assault on Fort Wagner. It was inspired by Meissonier's Campaign of France 1814, and Saint-Gaudens made 40 paper and clay sketches of heads to prepare. The Latin motto of the Society of Cincinnati is inscribed on it. African-American model Hettie Anderson posed for the winged Victory figure. The work was originally meant to be a single portrait but was expanded. 34 stars line the top. Robert Lowell's poem 'For the Union Dead' calls it 'a fishbone in the city's throat,' and it is depicted in the first of Charles Ives' Three Pieces in New England.

The Standing Lincoln (frequency 3) in Lincoln Park, Chicago, shows Lincoln standing before a chair with an eagle motif, subtitled 'The Man.' The Adams Memorial depicts a shrouded woman resting her cheek on her right hand, honoring Marian Adams who committed suicide by ingesting potassium cyanide, inspired by Japanese Buddhist art introduced by John La Farge.

The Sherman Memorial in Manhattan's Grand Army Plaza features an equestrian statue of General Sherman with a gilded Victory figure (modeled on the Winged Victory of Samothrace) holding a palm frond; it was Saint-Gaudens's last monument. The Amor Caritas motif, originally three figures for the tomb of Edwin Morgan, depicts a winged angel wearing a belt and crown of passion flowers holding a tablet inscribed 'Amor Caritas.'

His Diana for Stanford White's Madison Square Garden was a nude weathervane in strong contrapposto, shooting an arrow. It was clothed to satisfy Anthony Comstock, but the clothes blew off in the wind. His design of a torch-bearing nude was overturned, starting a feud with Charles Barber.

Saint-Gaudens designed the $20 Double Eagle gold coin for the US Mint (produced 1907-1933), with Liberty striding forward with an olive branch; the relief had to be lowered for practical minting. He founded the Cornish Art Colony in New Hampshire and was involved in the City Beautiful movement, proposing the McMillan plan for the National Mall with Charles McKim. He worked on the 1893 Chicago World's Fair. He created numerous bronze relief medallion portraits (of Sargent, Washington, Roosevelt, Robert Louis Stevenson in bed writing) and designed four caryatids for the Albright-Knox Art Gallery in Buffalo.
