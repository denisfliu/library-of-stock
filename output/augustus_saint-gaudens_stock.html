Summary of Facts

Augustus Saint-Gaudens was an American Renaissance/Beaux-Arts sculptor, best known for the Robert Gould Shaw Memorial on Boston Common, a bronze relief depicting Shaw on horseback leading the soldiers of the all-black 54th Massachusetts Infantry Regiment, with an angel of Victory flying overhead. The memorial is inscribed 'he relinquished everything to serve the Republic' and was created at the suggestion of Henry Hobson Richardson. African-American model Hettie Anderson (also referred to as Eugenia Anderson) posed for the Victory figure.

Saint-Gaudens created the Standing Lincoln in Lincoln Park, Chicago, showing Lincoln rising from or standing before a chair with an eagle motif. He also sculpted a series of bronze Lincoln statues subtitled 'The Man.' The Adams Memorial depicts a shrouded woman resting her cheek on her right hand, honoring Marian Adams who committed suicide by ingesting potassium cyanide. It was inspired by Japanese Buddhist art introduced to him by John La Farge.

The Sherman Memorial in Manhattan's Grand Army Plaza features an equestrian statue with a gilded Victory figure holding a palm frond. His recurring Amor Caritas motif, originally created as three figures for the tomb of Edwin Morgan, depicts a winged angel with raised wings wearing a belt and crown of passion flowers, holding a tablet inscribed 'Amor Caritas.' He designed the Diana statue for Stanford White's Madison Square Garden, a torch-bearing nude in strong contrapposto.

Saint-Gaudens designed the $20 Double Eagle gold coin for the US Mint, with Liberty striding forward with an olive branch on the obverse. He founded the Cornish Art Colony in New Hampshire, created numerous bronze relief medallion portraits (of John Singer Sargent, George Washington, and Theodore Roosevelt), and was born in Dublin, where he later created a monument honoring Charles Stuart Parnell. He sculpted two lions for a Civil War memorial on the main staircase of the Boston Public Library.
