French Rococo painter best known for The Swing. Painted The Progress of Love series for Madame du Barry. Also known for Blind Man's Bluff, The Bolt, and his Fantasy Figures portraits. Wife Marie-Anne was also a painter.
The Swing (1767)

Fragonard's most famous work. A woman on a swing kicks off her shoe as a reclining man below looks up her dress, while a stone statue of Cupid holds a finger to its lips. Originally commissioned for a bishop, but Fragonard painted a layman instead. Contains Falconet's Menacing Love sculpture on the left side.

Summary of Facts

Jean-Honoré Fragonard was a major French Rococo painter of the 18th century, best known for The Swing (1767), in which a woman's slipper flies off as she swings between two lovers, with a reclining man looking up her pink dress and a stone statue of Cupid holding a finger to its lips. He originally painted a layman instead of the commissioned bishop, and the left side of the painting features Falconet's sculpture Menacing Love. His other major work is The Progress of Love (1771-1773), a cycle of four paintings on 'four ages' of courtship, commissioned by Madame du Barry for her villa at Louveciennes and now housed in the Frick Collection. It includes The Meeting (a man in red climbs a garden balcony), The Lover Crowned (a woman places a wreath on her lover's head amid a lute, tambourine, and sheet music), and concludes with a couple courting next to letters. Falconet's sculptures including Mischievous Cupid provided the basis for watchful statues in the series.

Other notable works include The Bolt (a man reaches for the title bolt while his lover restrains him, featuring chiaroscuro and a red curtain symbolizing profane love), Blind Man's Bluff (a woman peeks from under a cloth to cheat at the game while a baby grasps at a toy), Young Girl Reading (a woman in a yellow dress with a white collar and open book), and his Fantasy Figures (portraits de fantaisie) -- quickly executed genre portraits in vaguely Spanish clothing, with 14 sketches unveiled at a 2012 Paris auction. His drawing Rinaldo in the Enchanted Forest depicts a knight chopping down a tree to break Armida's spell. His wife Marie-Anne was also an artist, argued by Pierre Rosenberg to have painted miniatures including a boy in pale clothing with a black-feathered hat. His anatomist cousin Honoré Fragonard created a flayed horse and rider for a veterinary school in Maisons-Alfort.
