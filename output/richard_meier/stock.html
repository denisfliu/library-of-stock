Summary of Facts

Richard Meier is an American architect best known for his trademark use of white on building exteriors — a quality so consistent it is clued as his defining characteristic across nearly every question. He won the Pritzker Prize and is a member of the New York Five, a group of architects associated with pure modernism that also includes Michael Graves and Peter Eisenman. In the Whites vs. Grays architectural debate that followed the 1970s postmodern turn, Meier and the New York Five were identified as the 'Whites,' championing modernism against the postmodern ornamentation of the 'Grays' led by Robert Venturi. When Meier first arrived in New York, he worked for Marcel Breuer, who had been on the faculty of the Bauhaus (founded by Walter Gropius) and was known for the Wassily Chair.

Meier's most frequently clued building is the Getty Center in Los Angeles, a multi-building campus designed for the J. Paul Getty Trust. The complex is accessed via tram and features white travertine-clad exteriors; one question specifies that it used 16,000 tons of Italian limestone and has an elevated cactus garden overlooking Los Angeles. It houses notable works including Van Gogh's Irises and Alma-Tadema's Spring.

His second most prominent work is the Museum of the Ara Pacis in Rome, built to house the ancient altar commissioned by Augustus. The museum features a travertine-plated white exterior with a low-slope staircase and glass walls filled with argon to filter light. It replaced a 1937 structure by Vittorio Ballio Morpurgo, whose wall inscribed with the Res Gestae was the only surviving element.

In Barcelona, Meier designed the MACBA (Museu d'Art Contemporani de Barcelona), featuring a white porcelain-enameled steel exterior and glass-paved hallways. It is nicknamed 'the pearl' for its contrast with the nearby Gothic Quarter (Barri Gòtic). In Rome, Meier also designed the Jubilee Church (Dio Padre Misericordioso), located next to the Tor Tre Teste housing complex, featuring three circular sectors with white concrete curved surfaces and glass.

Other buildings frequently cited in clues include: the Atheneum in New Harmony, Indiana; the High Museum of Art in Atlanta; Frankfurt's Museum of Decorative Arts; the Sandra Day O'Connor Courthouse in Phoenix; the Rothschild Tower in Tel Aviv; a namesake Model Museum on Long Island; and the Westbeth Artist Community in Manhattan's West Village (converted from a former Bell Telephone labs building). One question also describes a house on a steep slope in Michigan with tubed white supports and large windows bursting from a dense treeline. His first all-black building — a departure from his white signature — is a glass residential tower under construction at 685 First Avenue in New York.
