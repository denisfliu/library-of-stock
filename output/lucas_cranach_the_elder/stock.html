Wittenberg Altarpiece

General / Biographical

Court painter to Electors of Saxony (Frederick the Wise, etc.). Signed with bat-winged snake/serpent carrying a ruby ring (coat of arms from Frederick III; later changed to single crow's wing after son Hans's death). Close friend of Luther. Sometimes Mayor of Wittenberg. Shares feast day with Durer (April 6) in Lutheran/Episcopal churches. Pupil of his: Albrecht Altdorfer. Also painted Cardinal Albert von Brandenburg as Saint Jerome (despite Lutheran allegiance; composition derived from Durer's woodcut). Painted Phyllis Riding Aristotle and many other 'Power of Women' scenes. Saint Eustace: kneeling before stag with cross on antlers. Other works: The Judgment of Paris, Venus and Cupid with Honeycomb, Law and Gospel, The Fountain of Youth, Nymph of the Spring, Melancholia, Weimar Altarpiece, Adam and Eve against black background, Crucifixion (1503).

Summary of Facts

Lucas Cranach the Elder (1472–1553) was a German Renaissance painter and court painter to the Electors of Saxony, most commonly identified by his many portraits of Martin Luther and his distinctive bat-winged serpent signature emblem.

He signed his works with a bat-winged snake carrying a ruby ring — a coat of arms bestowed by Frederick the Wise (Frederick III). After his son Hans died, the emblem was changed to a single crow's wing. He was attached to Frederick the Wise's court from 1504, was sometimes Mayor of Wittenberg, and was a close personal friend of Luther. He painted Luther many times in a characteristic floppy black hat and black robe, also portraying Luther's wife Katharina von Bora and his parents. Cranach shared his printing presses with Luther and provided 22 woodcuts for Luther's German Bible translation. This figure appears with a swan in many depictions (due to a prophecy at the Council of Constance). Despite his Lutheran allegiance, Cranach accepted a commission to paint Cardinal Albert von Brandenburg as Saint Jerome, deriving his composition from Durer's woodcut of the saint. He also painted Durer's rival.

His Judith with the Head of Holofernes series depicts Judith 'dressed to kill' in contemporary dress with jeweled necklaces, ornate hats, a smug sly smile — most recognizably wearing a large red hat with feathers and white gloves with gold rings. Another version shows steel gloves and court clothes. These are part of his 'Power of Women' (Weibermacht) motif, which also includes Phyllis Riding Aristotle and Virgil in his basket. Art historians have connected Cranach's Judith interest to Luther's ideological struggle against Charles V.

The Stag Hunt of Elector Frederick the Wise shows archers in bushes, deer chased across a river, a large party in a boat, the artist's winged snake emblem visible on a tree in the foreground, set near Hartenfels Castle; a woman probably identified as Sibylle (wife of John Frederick the Magnanimous) steadies a crossbow.

The Wittenberg Altarpiece's central Last Supper shows Jesus dining with Germans, includes Cranach's self-portrait as a waiter and a portrait of a priest as one of the apostles; its bottom panel depicts the Crucifixion between a preacher (Luther) and his audience. Joseph Koerner's The Reformation of the Image analyzes it, arguing it 'negates sacred geography, revealing church as activity, not place.'

Other major works: The Judgment of Paris (Mercury holds a glass sphere, Paris in red cape and armor); Venus and Cupid with Honeycomb (Cupid holds honeycomb while attacked by bees, 'complaining' to Venus; a sign on a tree moralizes on pleasure and pain; Cranach's nude Venuses wear nothing but jeweled chokers and large elaborate hats with pompoms; his Eves are indistinguishable from his Venuses); Law and Gospel (made in consultation with Philip Melanchthon; Master IW's rendition of the brazen serpent borrowed the tent-filled background from Cranach's version); The Fountain of Youth (carts of old women to a central rectangular pool); Melancholia (woman in red dress whittles a stick while 15 babies/nude infants dance beside her); Weimar Altarpiece (blood of Christ lands on the artist's own head; completed by his son Lucas Cranach the Younger); Nymph of the Spring (Latin: 'I am a nymph of the sacred fountain, do not disturb my sleep'); Adam and Eve against a black background; and the Crucifixion (1503) with a turbulent landscape, Jesus in profile off to the right (focus on Mary looking up), a knight in a feathered hat, and a soldier in pink full body armor — now in the Alte Pinakothek, Munich. Albrecht Altdorfer was a pupil of Cranach. He shares a feast day with Durer on April 6 in Lutheran and Episcopal churches. Ernst Kirchner was inspired by Cranach's Venus with transparent shawl.
