German Renaissance painter and court painter to the Electors of Saxony. Close friend of Martin Luther and prolific portraitist of the reformer. Signed works with a winged serpent emblem. Wittenberg Altarpiece

Central Last Supper panel shows Jesus dining with Germans; Cranach inserted a self-portrait as a waiter. Bottom panel depicts the crucifixion between a preacher and his audience. Joseph Koerner's book The Reformation of the Image analyzes it, arguing it 'negates sacred geography, revealing church as activity, not place.' Companion to the Weimar Altarpiece (completed by his son Lucas Cranach the Younger).

General / Biographical

Court painter to Electors of Saxony (Frederick the Wise, etc.). Signed with winged snake/serpent carrying a ruby ring (coat of arms from Frederick III; later changed to single crow's wing after son Hans's death). Close friend of Luther. Shares feast day with Durer (April 6) in Lutheran/Episcopal churches. Sometimes Mayor of Wittenberg. Pupil of his: Albrecht Altdorfer. Other works: The Judgment of Paris (Mercury with glass sphere, Paris in red cape and armor), Venus and Cupid (skinny tall nudes; Cupid holds honeycomb attacked by bees), Law and Gospel (consulted Philip Melanchthon), The Fountain of Youth (carts of old women to rectangular pool), Nymph of the Spring (Latin inscription), Melancholia (woman in red dress whittles stick, nude infants do precocious things), Weimar Altarpiece (blood of Christ splashes onto his head; completed by son), Adam and Eve against black background, Venus with Cupid and Honeycomb, The Ill-Matched Couple, Crucifixion (1503, knight with feathered hat).

Summary of Facts

Lucas Cranach the Elder was a German Renaissance painter and court painter to the Electors of Saxony, most commonly identified by his many portraits of Martin Luther and his distinctive winged serpent signature emblem.

He signed his works with a winged snake carrying a ruby ring, a coat of arms bestowed by Frederick the Wise (Frederick III); after his son Hans died, the emblem was changed to a single crow's wing. He was attached to Frederick the Wise's court from 1504, and was a close personal friend of Luther. He painted Luther many times in a characteristic floppy black hat and black robe, also portraying Luther's wife Katharina von Bora and his parents. Cranach shared his printing presses with Luther and provided 22 woodcuts for Luther's German Bible translation.

His Judith with the Head of Holofernes series depicts Judith in contemporary dress with a smug, sly smile — most recognizably wearing a large red hat with feathers and white gloves with gold rings (part of his 'Power of Women' theme). The Stag Hunt of Elector Frederick the Wise shows archers in bushes, deer chased across a river, a large party in a boat, the artist's winged snake emblem visible on a tree in the foreground, and is set near Hartenfels Castle; a woman probably identified as Sibylle (wife of John Frederick the Magnanimous) steadies a crossbow. The Wittenberg Altarpiece's central Last Supper shows Jesus dining with Germans and includes Cranach's self-portrait as a waiter; its bottom panel depicts the Crucifixion between a preacher and his audience; Joseph Koerner's The Reformation of the Image is the key scholarly study of this work.

Other major works: The Judgment of Paris (Mercury holds a glass sphere, Paris in red cape and armor); Venus and Cupid with Honeycomb (Cupid holds honeycomb while attacked by bees, 'complaining' to Venus; Cranach's Eves are famously indistinguishable from his skinny tall Venuses); Law and Gospel (made in consultation with Philip Melanchthon); The Fountain of Youth (carts of old women to a central rectangular pool); Melancholia (woman in red dress whittles a stick while nude infants perform precocious feats like passing a ball through a hoop); Weimar Altarpiece (blood of Christ lands on the artist's own head; completed by his son Lucas Cranach the Younger); Nymph of the Spring (Latin inscription: 'I am a nymph of the sacred fountain, do not disturb my sleep'); Adam and Eve against a black background; and a 1503 Crucifixion partly titled for a centurion in which a knight with a feathered hat looks up. Albrecht Altdorfer was a pupil of Cranach. He shares a feast day with Durer on April 6 in Lutheran and Episcopal churches.
