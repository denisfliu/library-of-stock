Spanish Golden Age playwright who succeeded Lope de Vega as the leading dramatist of Spain. Best known for Life is a Dream and The Mayor of Zalamea. Also wrote around seventy autos sacramentales (one-act allegorical plays) and a series of 'honor plays' (wife-murder tragedies). His rediscovery began with August Schlegel's translations. Prolific author of plays including Devotion to the Cross, The Surgeon of His Honor, The Phantom Lady, and The Great Theater of the World.
Life is a Dream (La vida es sueño)

Calderón's most famous play, set in Poland. Prince Segismundo is imprisoned in a tower by his father King Basilio because of a prophecy predicting his violent nature. Rosaura arrives from Russia/Muscovy and finds him chained. Themes are often compared to Cartesian skepticism — Segismundo is unsure whether he is awake or dreaming. The title comes from a conversation between Clotaldo and Segismundo about the nature of reality. The play ends with Segismundo defying the prophecy by sparing his father. Wikipedia

The Mayor of Zalamea (El alcalde de Zalamea)

A play about the farmer Pedro Crespo, whose daughter Isabel is raped by the soldier Don Alvaro de Ataide when a regiment passes through the village of Zalamea. Crespo hangs Don Alvaro and is appointed to the title position of mayor by King Philip II. Features the soldier Rebolledo and his mistress 'La Chispa,' who sings a marching song beginning with 'Titiri tiri.' Ends less violently than the honor plays, as the dishonored Isabella is allowed to become a nun. Wikipedia

Autos Sacramentales (including The Great Theater of the World)

Calderón wrote around seventy plays in the genre of the auto sacramental — one-act allegorical plays, also translated as 'sacramental plays.' The most notable is The Great Theater of the World, in which God is represented as the 'Author.' Calderón was the most prolific author in this genre. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Pedro Calderón de la Barca is one of the most frequently asked-about Spanish Golden Age playwrights in quizbowl, consistently paired with his two most famous works: Life is a Dream and The Mayor of Zalamea, which appear as dual giveaways in nearly every tossup.

Life is a Dream (La vida es sueño) is by far the most heavily clued work. Set in Poland, it centers on Prince Segismundo, who is imprisoned in a tower by his father King Basilio due to a prophecy that he will become a tyrant. The arrival of Rosaura — variously described as coming from Russia or Muscovy — is a key plot point; she finds Segismundo chained and wearing animal skins, and Clotaldo recognizes a sword she carries as one he gave to her mother, revealing that Rosaura is his daughter. The sword recognition is one of the most frequently clued details. Astolfo, who has a competing claim to the Polish throne, is impelled to marry Rosaura at the play's conclusion after Basilio abdicates in favor of Segismundo. The jester Clarin provides comic relief and is ultimately shot while hiding behind a rock, proving that fate cannot be avoided. The play's themes are compared to Cartesian skepticism, and its title derives from a conversation between Clotaldo and Segismundo about the nature of reality. Segismundo's soliloquy declaring that the greatest sin is 'having been born' is also noted. The play opens with Rosaura arriving on a 'wild hippogriff.' Giannina Braschi references this play in her novel United States of Banana.

The Mayor of Zalamea (El alcalde de Zalamea) is the second-most-clued work. The farmer Pedro Crespo's daughter Isabel is raped by the soldier Don Alvaro de Ataide, who is billeted in Crespo's house when a regiment passes through the village. Crespo hangs (garrottes) Don Alvaro and is appointed to the title position of mayor by King Philip II. Supporting characters include Rebolledo, who stages a mock fight so Don Alvaro can meet Isabel, and La Chispa, a soldier's mistress who sings a marching song beginning 'Titiri tiri.' Unlike the honor plays, Isabel is allowed to become a nun rather than being killed.

The Surgeon of His Honor (El médico de su honra) is the most prominent of Calderón's 'honor plays' or wife-murder tragedies. Don Gutierre suspects his wife Mencia of having an affair with Prince Enrique (sometimes after finding a prince's dagger in her bedroom) and orders a bloodletter to bleed her to death. Coquin bets King Pedro he can make the king laugh, risking having his teeth pulled. The play ends with Leonor becoming Gutierre's new wife.

Devotion to the Cross features Lisardo killed in a duel by Eusebio, who turns out to be the twin brother of Lisardo's sister Julia; both Julia and Eusebio bear matching scars. Curcio hunts Eusebio and recognizes him by the cross he wears.

Calderón wrote approximately seventy autos sacramentales (one-act allegorical/sacramental plays), the most notable being The Great Theater of the World, in which God is represented as the 'Author.' Other works mentioned include Secret Vengeance for Secret Insult (another wife-murder tragedy), The Hair of Absalom (about Amnon's rape of Tamar, improving on Tirso de Molina), The Phantom Lady (Doña Angela woos Don Manuel), Love Honor and Power (King Edward and the Countess of Salveric), Three Judgments in One, and a retelling of the Faustian story of Saints Cyprian and Justina.

Biographically, Calderón succeeded Lope de Vega as the leading Spanish dramatist. His rediscovery was initiated by August Wilhelm Schlegel's translations. The Irish poet Denis Florence MacCarthy was also a prominent translator. Calderón is credited with moderating the excesses of culteranismo in Spanish drama.
