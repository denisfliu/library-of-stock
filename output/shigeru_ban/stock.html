The Centre Pompidou-Metz in northeastern France (in the city of Metz, the most populous city in Lorraine, located at the confluence of the Moselle and Grand Seille rivers). Ban designed its distinctive roof: an undulating hexagonal shell of glue-laminated timber (also described as curving fiberglass), inspired by a Chinese bamboo hat that Ban found in Paris. Humanitarian / Paper Tube Work

Shigeru Ban is renowned for using paper tubes as cheap, recyclable building materials after natural disasters. Following the 1995 Kobe earthquake (Great Hanshin earthquake), he built refugee shelters and the Takatori Catholic Church ('Paper Dome') in Kobe, which was later moved to Taomi Village in Taiwan in 1999. He also substituted aluminum rods with cardboard tubes for refugee camps in Byumba, Rwanda. He designed an Expo 2000 pavilion (with Frei Otto) whose roof was made entirely of paper/cardboard tubes in a honeycomb shape. He also developed the Paper Partition System for temporary housing for earthquake victims. A 2007 Artek pavilion in Finland was also built from paper waste products. The New York Times critic Michael Kimmelman called him an 'accidental environmentalist.' He stresses 'invisible structure' in his work.

Summary of Facts

Shigeru Ban (born 1957) is a Japanese architect known for using paper tubes and other recycled or lightweight materials for humanitarian disaster relief housing and temporary structures. He won the 2014 Pritzker Prize (an annual architecture prize established in 1979 by a co-founder of the Hyatt hotel chain; the preceding year's winner was Toyo Ito of the Sendai Mediatheque). Ban is often described as a 'disaster architect' who stresses 'invisible structure,' and the New York Times critic Michael Kimmelman called him an 'accidental environmentalist.'

His most internationally recognized project is the Cardboard Cathedral in Christchurch, New Zealand, built after the 2011 earthquake destroyed the original Anglican cathedral. The transitional building uses eight shipping containers as its walls, with cardboard tubes stacked on top, and a triangular polycarbonate roof.

For the Centre Pompidou-Metz in the city of Metz (the most populous city in the Lorraine region of northeastern France, at the confluence of the Moselle and Grand Seille rivers), Ban designed a distinctive undulating roof of glue-laminated timber — also described as a curving fiberglass structure — with a hexagonal pattern inspired by a Chinese bamboo hat that Ban found in Paris. A paper tube studio he used during the design process was incorporated into the final building. The museum also features a glass elevator inspired by a gondola, from which visitors 'ski' down to the galleries.

Ban's humanitarian work is central to his reputation. After the 1995 Kobe earthquake (Great Hanshin earthquake), he designed refugee houses using paper tubes and built the Takatori Catholic Church ('Paper Dome') in Kobe; that circular church was later shipped to Taomi Village in Taiwan in 1999. He substituted aluminum rods with cardboard tubes for refugee camps in Byumba, Rwanda. He developed the Paper Partition System for temporary housing for earthquake victims. He collaborated with German engineer Frei Otto on the Japanese Pavilion at Expo 2000 in Hanover, which had a roof made entirely of cardboard tubes shaped like a honeycomb. He also built a 2007 pavilion in Finland for the furniture company Artek out of paper waste products.

Other notable works include transparent toilets for the 2020 (Covid-delayed) Tokyo Olympics, whose glass walls turn opaque when occupied using 'smart glass'; the Aspen Museum of Art (his first US museum, replacing a facility formerly housed in a converted hydroelectric plant); and the Nomadic Museum for photographer Gregory Colbert's Ashes and Snow exhibition. Some of his structures are anchored by sand-filled Kirin beer crates. His own summer house on Lake Yamanaka was among the first buildings to use paper tubes as a structural material.
