Sor Juana Ines de la Cruz

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
17th-century Mexican nun, poet, and polymath, known as the 'Tenth Muse' and 'American Phoenix.' Best known for the long philosophical poem First Dream and the Reply to Sister Filotea, which defends women's right to education. Also wrote the satire 'You Foolish Men,' the auto sacramental The Divine Narcissus, and the sonnet 'To Her Portrait.' Child prodigy at the viceregal court of New Spain. Subject of Octavio Paz's intellectual biography The Traps of Faith. Eventually forced to sell her books and scientific instruments and cease writing by Archbishop Aguiar y Seijas. Signed 'I, the worst of all women' in her own blood.
First Dream (Primero sueno)

A long philosophical poem in silva form. The soul is liberated from the body by Night and embarks on a quest to understand all of Creation, travelling through levels representing different forms of knowledge (often linked to Aristotle's ten categories). Opens with the image of a 'pyramidal, funereal' or 'death-born shadow of earth' aimed at heaven. Ends with 'the world illuminated, and I awake' as the golden sun adorns the hemisphere 'with skeins of judicious light.' The soul tries and fails to comprehend a vision of all creation. Described by Octavio Paz as about 'the vertigo of being on the edge of the infinite.' Paz analyzes it as an allegory for Sor Juana's desire for knowledge in The Traps of Faith. The soul is 'roused by her own precipitous fall' and 'crowns herself with the globe's abandoned half.' Wikipedia

Reply to Sister Filotea

A letter defending women's right to education, written in response to the Bishop of Puebla (Manuel Fernandez de Santa Cruz), who had published Sor Juana's Letter Worthy of Athena under the pseudonym 'Sister Filotea.' Contains the famous quip that if Aristotle had spent more time cooking, he would have written much more. Argues that the intellect has no gender since all intellects originate from the Divine. Quotes extensively from St. Jerome's letter To Leta, on the Education of her Daughter. Thanks learned forebears like Aspasia and St. Gertrude. Claims she learned 'secrets of nature' from cooking. States 'by studying I may become less ignorant.' Led to Sor Juana being forced to stop writing in 1693. Paz praised her 'double solitude' as a woman and intellectual. Wikipedia

"You Foolish Men" / "Arraignment of the Men"

A satirical redondilla that mocks men for their hypocrisy toward women. Asks the title group 'Why be outraged at the guilt / that is of your own doing?' Describes men as 'perverse, schooled to condemn... by witless laws' and says they 'mist a mirror' and then complain 'that it's not clear.' The final stanza states that men's 'arrogance' fights 'with many weapons' because in 'promise and insistence' they combine 'world, flesh, and devil.' Also translated as 'Foolish Men.' Wikipedia

Loa to Divine Narcissus / The Divine Narcissus

An auto sacramental (sacramental play) written at the request of Maria Luisa, the Viceroy's wife. The loa (prologue) is an allegory in which Religion spares two Aztecs named America and Occident during the Spanish conquest. Zeal complains that the characters disrespect his wife Religion because they exalt 'The God of Seeds.' Links Christ's crucifixion to Aztec human sacrifices. Written about the spread of Catholicism in the Americas. Wikipedia

"To Her Portrait"

A sonnet in which the speaker describes a portrait of herself as 'bright-colored deceit' and a 'vain artifice.' Concludes with the line ''Tis dead, 'tis dust, 'tis shadow, yea, 'tis nought.' Alan Trueblood's translation titles it 'She Disavows the Flattery Visible in a Portrait of Herself, Which She Calls Bias.' Wikipedia

Other Works

General / Biographical

Born Juana Ines de Asbaje y Ramirez de Santillana. 17th-century Hieronymite nun from New Spain (colonial Mexico). Known as the 'Tenth Muse' and 'American Phoenix.' Child prodigy at the viceregal court of New Spain under patron Tomas de la Cerda, the Viceroy. Refused to eat cheese as a child because she heard it made people stupid. Eventually forced by Archbishop Aguiar y Seijas to sell her books and scientific instruments and cease writing. Signed 'I, the worst of all women' in her own blood. Subject of the 1990 film I, the Worst of All. Subject of Octavio Paz's intellectual biography The Traps of Faith. Jose Medina's The Epistemology of Resistance calls her an 'epistemic hero.' Her use of imagination creates 'epistemic friction.' Rosario Castellanos alluded to her. A 2019 Argentine literary prize for women writing in Spanish is named after her. Helen Edmundson's 2012 play The Heresy of Love is based on her life.

Summary of Facts

Sor Juana Ines de la Cruz is one of the most frequently clued non-English-language poets in quizbowl, anchored by two major works and a distinctive biography.

First Dream (Primero sueno) is her most important literary work, a long philosophical silva in which the soul is liberated from the body by Night and embarks on a quest to understand all of Creation. The poem opens with the instantly recognizable image of a 'pyramidal, funereal, death-born shadow of earth' aimed at heaven and ends with 'the world illuminated, and I awake' as the golden sun dispenses light. The soul travels through levels of knowledge often linked to Aristotle's ten categories, is 'roused by her own precipitous fall,' and 'crowns herself with the globe's abandoned half.' Octavio Paz described it as about 'the vertigo of being on the edge of the infinite' in The Traps of Faith.

The Reply to Sister Filotea is her most frequently referenced work overall, a letter defending women's right to education written in response to the Bishop of Puebla (Manuel Fernandez de Santa Cruz), who had published her Letter Worthy of Athena under the pseudonym 'Filotea.' The most commonly clued detail is her quip that 'if Aristotle had cooked, he would have written much more.' She argues that intellects have no gender since they all originate from the Divine, describes 'secrets of nature' learned from cooking, and thanks forebears like Aspasia and St. Gertrude. The letter quotes extensively from St. Jerome's letter To Leta.

'You Foolish Men' (Hombres Necios) is a satirical redondilla mocking men for their hypocrisy toward women, asking 'Why be outraged at the guilt that is of your own doing?' and accusing men of misting a mirror then complaining it's not bright. The Divine Narcissus is an auto sacramental whose loa features Religion sparing two Aztecs named America and Occident; Zeal complains they exalt 'The God of Seeds.' 'To Her Portrait' is a sonnet calling a portrait 'bright-colored deceit' and concluding ''Tis dead, 'tis dust, 'tis shadow, yea, 'tis nought.'

Biographically, Sor Juana was a child prodigy at the viceregal court, known as the 'Tenth Muse' and 'American Phoenix.' She wrote under the patronage of Tomas de la Cerda but was eventually forced by Archbishop Aguiar y Seijas to sell her books and cease writing. She signed 'I, the worst of all women' in her own blood. She refused to eat cheese as a child because she heard it made people stupid. She designed a triumphal arch described in the Allegorical Neptune and wrote a now-lost music theory treatise about the 'snail'-like spiral of justly-tuned fifths. She used Nahuatl in her villancicos, making 'horrible errors.' The Traps of Faith by Octavio Paz is her defining intellectual biography and appears in nearly every bonus and tossup about her.
