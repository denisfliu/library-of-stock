Turkish Nobel laureate (2006), known for novels that explore Istanbul, Ottoman history, and the tension between Eastern and Western culture. Major works include My Name Is Red (about Ottoman miniaturists and the murder of Elegant Effendi), Snow (the poet Ka investigates headscarf-related suicides in Kars), The Museum of Innocence (Kemal's obsessive love for Fusun, collecting her objects), The White Castle (an Italian scholar enslaved by the Ottoman Hoja), The Black Book (Galip searches for his missing wife Ruya), and The Red-Haired Woman (a well-digger's apprentice obsessed with Oedipus and Rostam/Sohrab). Prosecuted under Article 301 for discussing the Armenian Genocide.

Summary of Facts

Orhan Pamuk (born 1952) is a Turkish Nobel laureate (2006) whose novels explore Istanbul, Ottoman history, and the tension between Eastern and Western culture.

His most frequently clued novel is My Name Is Red, a metafictional mystery set in 16th-century Istanbul during the reign of Sultan Murat III. The miniaturist Elegant Effendi is murdered after preparing a secret illuminated manuscript in the Venetian style. Chapters are unusually narrated by a gold coin, Satan, a drawing of a tree (which proclaims 'I don't want to be a tree, I want to be its meaning'), and the color red itself. Black investigates three suspect miniaturists — Butterfly, Stork, and Olive — while pursuing his love for his cousin Shekure. Their love letters are delivered by the illiterate Jewish clothes-peddler Esther. The murderer Olive is identified using the 'courtesan method' of examining horse nostrils. Master Osman blinds himself with a plume needle, inspired by the legend of the miniaturist Bihzad, so he can paint from memory alone.

Snow follows the poet Ka, who returns from exile in Frankfurt to the border town of Kars to investigate a string of suicides among religious girls prompted by a headscarf ban. Ka falls in love with Ipek but she is also attracted to the terrorist Blue. An assassin steals 19 of Ka's 20 poems. Kadife shoots the actor Sunay Zaim during a performance of Kyd's The Spanish Tragedy. Ka finds poem titles like 'The Place Where God Does Not Exist' on a snowflake diagram. The narrator reveals himself to be 'Orhan Pamuk,' who discovers Ka was eventually assassinated in Germany for betraying Blue.

The Museum of Innocence follows the wealthy Kemal, who becomes obsessively devoted to the young salesgirl Fusun, his distant cousin, after first noticing her while buying a handbag for his fiancee Sibel. Kemal says 'no one recognizes the happiest moment of their lives' as he contemplates Fusun's butterfly-shaped earring. After Fusun dies in a car crash, Kemal collects 4,213 cigarette butts and countless other objects as relics of their relationship. He founds the failed Lemon Films. Pamuk established a real Museum of Innocence in the Cukurcuma district of Istanbul.

The White Castle, Pamuk's first novel translated into English, follows an Italian scholar kidnapped by pirates who becomes the slave of the Ottoman scholar Hoja, who looks exactly like him. Hoja is obsessed with creating a 'war engine' for the sultan. A character forces a village to confess their sins to discover 'the secret of the Frankish people's success.'

The Black Book follows the lawyer Galip, who searches for his missing wife Ruya and the journalist Jelal. Even-numbered chapters are Jelal's newspaper columns; Galip impersonates Jelal. The novel contains the story of a secluded Prince who spends ten years burning books.

The Red-Haired Woman contrasts the myths of Oedipus (patricide) and Rostam/Sohrab (filicide). A teenager apprentices with a well-digger, drops a bucket onto his boss's head thinking him dead, and flees. The title character narrates the epilogue.

Other works include The New Life (opens 'I read a book one day and my whole life changed'), Nights of Plague (set on the fictional island of Mingheria), The Silent House, and A Strangeness in My Mind (about the street vendor Mevlut). His essay collection Other Colors includes an introduction to Tristram Shandy and the Nobel lecture 'My Father's Suitcase.' Pamuk was prosecuted under Article 301 for 'insulting Turkishness' after discussing the Armenian Genocide in the Swiss magazine Das Magazin.
