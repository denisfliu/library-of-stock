John Brown's Body

An epic poem about the American Civil War, centered on the abolitionist John Brown. The poem won the Pulitzer Prize in 1928. It begins with an invocation to the 'American muse,' described as 'the buffalo-ghost, the broncho-ghost / with silver dollar in your saddle-horn.' The poem follows characters including Sally Dupre, who dances with a plantation owner's son and thinks 'Wingate Hall must tumble down,' and the Connecticut abolitionist Jack Ellyat. The poem's title derives from a marching song whose lyrics claim John Brown's 'body lies a-mouldering in the grave' while 'His soul's marching on.' The ending entreats the reader to say not that 'it is blest' but 'it is here.'

The Devil and Daniel Webster

A short story in which the New Hampshire farmer Jabez Stone sells his soul to Mr. Scratch (the Devil) in exchange for seven years of prosperity. The American orator Daniel Webster defends Stone in a trial. The jury includes historical villains such as Metacom (King Philip) and Blackbeard. Webster reads the palm of the Devil and ultimately the jury voids the contract. A character named Miser Stevens appears, his voice emerging from Mr. Scratch's handkerchief. John, the priest's son in a separate story, is a different work ('By the Waters of Babylon').

American Names

A poem in which the speaker declares 'I have fallen in love with American names' and praises places like Deadwood, Lost Mule Flat, Little French Lick, and Medicine Hat. The speaker laments 'I tired of loving a foreign muse' and describes American names as 'sharp' and 'never get fat.' The poem concludes with the famous line 'Bury my heart at Wounded Knee,' which provided the title for Dee Brown's book about the Indian history of the American West. The line 'I shall not be there. I shall rise and pass' appears near the ending.

By the Waters of Babylon

A post-apocalyptic short story in which a priest's son named John journeys to the 'Place of the Gods,' which is revealed to be New York City after 'The Great Burning.' John encounters landmarks with partially destroyed names, including a column that reads 'ASHING' (for George Washington). He realizes that 'the place newyork' was built by men, not gods, and concludes that 'we must build again.' The story takes its title from Psalm 137. John's ability to travel to the Dead Places and touch metal comes from his occupation as a priest.

Other Works and General

Benét wrote several other works that appear occasionally in clues. 'Western Star' is an unfinished narrative poem about the settling of the American frontier, for which he received a posthumous Pulitzer Prize in 1944. 'The King of the Cats' is a story about Monsieur Tibault, who becomes the finest conductor in Europe despite conducting with his tail. 'An End to Dreams' features James Rimington recalling his time as the 'quiet earthquake' of New York City business. The poem 'Difference' describes a lover's mind as 'a room where Peace and Honor talk like birds.' 'Nightmare Number Three' notes that 'The concrete-mixer may have made a mistake.' 'Elegy for an Enemy' asks 'Does that stupid earth / Bind still her sullen mirth.' A Civil War poem describes a man who 'had no gift for life' but 'knew how to die.'

Summary of Facts

Stephen Vincent Benét is an American poet and short story writer whose work is deeply rooted in American history, folklore, and landscape. He is most frequently identified through his three major works: the epic poem John Brown's Body, the short story 'The Devil and Daniel Webster,' and the poem 'American Names.'

John Brown's Body is Benét's most celebrated work, an epic poem about the American Civil War that won the Pulitzer Prize in 1928. The poem begins with an invocation to the 'American muse,' described with frontier imagery as 'the buffalo-ghost, the broncho-ghost.' It follows multiple characters including Sally Dupre and the Connecticut abolitionist Jack Ellyat, weaving together perspectives on the conflict centered around the figure of John Brown. The title derives from the marching song claiming John Brown's body 'lies a-mouldering in the grave.'

'The Devil and Daniel Webster' is a frequently clued short story about the farmer Jabez Stone, who sells his soul to Mr. Scratch (the Devil) for seven years of prosperity. The famous orator Daniel Webster defends Stone at trial before a jury of historical villains including Blackbeard and King Philip (Metacom). The jury ultimately voids the contract. Miser Stevens' voice emerging from Mr. Scratch's handkerchief is a notable detail.

'American Names' is a patriotic poem celebrating American place names. The speaker declares 'I have fallen in love with American names' and lists places like Deadwood, Lost Mule Flat, Little French Lick, and Medicine Hat. The poem's concluding line, 'Bury my heart at Wounded Knee,' became famous as the title of Dee Brown's influential book about Native American history.

'By the Waters of Babylon' is a post-apocalyptic short story in which a priest's son named John travels to the 'Place of the Gods,' revealed to be the ruins of New York City after 'The Great Burning.' He encounters partially destroyed inscriptions including 'ASHING' (George Washington) and realizes the gods were actually men. The title comes from Psalm 137.

Benét also received a posthumous Pulitzer Prize in 1944 for 'Western Star,' an unfinished narrative poem about the American frontier. Other minor works include 'The King of the Cats' (about Monsieur Tibault who conducts with his tail), 'An End to Dreams,' 'Nightmare Number Three,' and 'Elegy for an Enemy.'
