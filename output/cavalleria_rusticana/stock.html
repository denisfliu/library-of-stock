One-act verismo opera by Pietro Mascagni (1890), based on a short story by Giovanni Verga. Set in a Sicilian village on Easter morning, it depicts the tragic love triangle among Turiddu, Santuzza, and Lola, culminating in a duel with the teamster Alfio. Often considered the first verismo opera, it is almost always performed on a double bill with Leoncavallo's Pagliacci ('Cav/Pag'). Its celebrated intermezzo has been used prominently in film, most famously in Raging Bull and The Godfather Part III.

Cavalleria rusticana – Characters and Plot

The opera centers on Turiddu, a soldier who returns from war to find his fiancée Lola has married the teamster Alfio. Turiddu seduces Santuzza to make Lola jealous, leading to a confrontation. Santuzza informs Alfio of the affair; a duel between Alfio and Turiddu ensues (offstage), resulting in Turiddu's death. Lucia, Turiddu's mother, runs a wine shop and is a key witness to events.

Cavalleria rusticana – Arias and Musical Moments

The chorus 'Gli aranci olezzano' opens the opera proper.

Cavalleria rusticana – The Intermezzo

The celebrated orchestral intermezzo of Cavalleria rusticana accompanies the deserted village square during Easter as the townspeople are in church. It has been widely used in film, most notably in Martin Scorsese's Raging Bull (opening and closing credits) and in The Godfather Part III (finale, when Michael Corleone screams clutching his dead daughter Mary's body). The intermezzo is frequently performed as a stand-alone concert piece.

Summary of Facts

Cavalleria rusticana (Rustic Chivalry) is a one-act verismo opera by Pietro Mascagni that premiered in 1890. It won Edoardo Sonzogno's one-act opera competition that year, making Mascagni instantly famous. The opera is based on a short story (and a play) by Giovanni Verga; the same source material also served as the basis for Stanislao Gastaldon's Mala Pasqua!. Set entirely in the main square of a Sicilian village on Easter morning, it is often considered the first verismo opera — a genre defined by its realistic and often violent depictions of unromanticized everyday life, in contrast to Romantic opera's focus on gods, kings, and mythology.

The opera's plot centers on a love triangle with tragic consequences. Turiddu, son of Lucia who runs a wine shop, returns from military service to find that his fiancée Lola has since married the teamster Alfio. Turiddu responds by seducing Santuzza. When Santuzza informs Alfio of Turiddu's affair with Lola, a duel becomes inevitable. Turiddu accepts the challenge by biting Alfio's ear (the traditional Sicilian custom), then begs his mother for 'one kiss, Mother!' before going to the duel, which occurs offstage and results in Turiddu's death. At the beginning of the opera, the villagers sing the chorus 'Gli aranci olezzano' after Turiddu declares his love is 'lovely as the spring's bright blooms.' Alfio is characterized by the jolly aria 'Il cavallo scalpita,' in which he cracks his whip and claims snow and rain don't matter to him because his wife waits at home. During the prelude, the lead tenor sings an offstage love serenade/siciliana. A church chorus sings the Easter hymn Regina Coeli; Santuzza, who has been stood up and seduced by Turiddu, is barred from entering the church and sings to Lucia in the aria 'Voi lo sapete,' revealing the adultery. Turiddu later holds a party at Lucia's wine shop, singing 'Hail to the bubbling wine' before biting Alfio's ear.

The opera's celebrated orchestral Intermezzo accompanies the deserted village square while the townspeople attend Easter services. It has become one of the most recognized pieces of operatic music in popular culture. Martin Scorsese used it for the opening (and closing) credits of his 1980 film Raging Bull, featuring Jake LaMotta jumping in slow motion. Francis Ford Coppola famously used it in The Godfather Part III for the finale scene in which Michael Corleone screams while clutching his dead daughter Mary's body. The intermezzo plays before the climactic duel between Alfio and Turiddu and has frequently been excerpted as a concert piece.

Cavalleria rusticana is almost universally performed alongside Ruggero Leoncavallo's Pagliacci on what is known as a 'Cav/Pag' double bill, as both are short Italian verismo operas.
