Summary of Facts

Wilkie Collins (William Wilkie Collins) was a Victorian English novelist who pioneered both sensation fiction and detective fiction. He is most frequently clued for two novels: The Moonstone and The Woman in White.

The Moonstone is widely considered one of the first detective novels. The title diamond, originally stolen during the Siege of Seringapatam, is taken from Rachel Verinder at a birthday party. Sergeant Cuff, a gardening-obsessed detective who employs a boy nicknamed 'Gooseberry,' investigates. Godfrey Ablewhite is revealed as the thief, while Franklin Blake unknowingly took the diamond while under the influence of laudanum and later reenacts the events to prove his innocence. The novel features multiple narrators including the prim evangelist Drusilla Clack and the butler Betteredge. Three Brahmin 'jugglers' are sent to recover the gem. It ends with Rachel marrying Franklin and the diamond returned to an Indian statue.

The Woman in White created the sensation novel craze. Art teacher Walter Hartright discovers that Laura Fairlie resembles an asylum escapee named Anne Catherick. The obese Count Fosco and Sir Percival conspire against Laura. Marian Halcombe catches cold after climbing to a roof in the rain to overhear their plot. Laura is placed in an asylum in Anne's place. Walter restores Laura's identity by blackmailing Fosco, who is later murdered by an Italian 'Brotherhood.'

Collins had a laudanum addiction and believed he was accompanied by a doppelganger. He was a close friend and collaborator of Charles Dickens, co-writing 'A House to Let' and The Frozen Deep (about the Franklin Expedition). His other novels include No Name (about the Vanstone sisters losing their inheritance), Armadale, and The New Magdalen. Sheridan Le Fanu was called 'the Irish Wilkie Collins.'
