Brandenburg Concertos – The Set as a Whole

Six concertos composed by J.S. Bach and dedicated in 1721 to Margrave Christian Ludwig of Brandenburg, to whom Bach had sent them as part of a (fruitless) job application. The French dedication makes them also known as 'Six Concerts à plusieurs instruments.' They span BWV 1046–1051 and represent the concerto grosso form, each using a different combination of solo (concertino) instruments. Nikolaus Harnoncourt's 1964 recording was the first to use period instruments. In 1979, Wendy Carlos released a Moog synthesizer realisation as a double album. The first concerto's first movement was later recycled by Bach in his cantata 'Ich liebe den Höchsten von ganzem Gemüte.'

Brandenburg Concerto No. 5 (D major)

The fifth of the Brandenburg Concertos, in D major, features a concertino of flute, violin, and harpsichord. It is the first known instance of Bach using the transverse flute; an earlier version lacked both the flute and the cello part that appears in the final version. The most famous feature is a lengthy solo harpsichord cadenza (approximately 65 measures) in the first movement, occurring before the ritornello returns; the cadenza section is marked 'senza stromenti.' The harpsichord functions as part of both the ripieno and concertino groups — unusual for the time. The fifth concerto is thought to have been written to show off a new harpsichord delivered to Bach's court at Köthen by instrument maker Michael Mietke. It may also have been composed with a keyboard duel in mind against Louis Marchand, who reputedly fled Dresden rather than face Bach.

Brandenburg Concerto No. 3 (G major)

The third of the Brandenburg Concertos, in G major, is scored for three groups of string trios — three violins, three violas, and three cellos. Its most distinctive feature is the second movement, which consists of only a single bar containing two chords (an A minor chord and a B chord) forming a Phrygian half-cadence. Because the movement is so brief, performers typically improvise a keyboard cadenza over it, or substitute it with the slow movement of a Bach violin sonata. The first movement opens with a fast violin pickup motif of G–F-sharp–downbeat G.

Brandenburg Concerto No. 4 (G major)

The fourth Brandenburg Concerto, in G major, is in 3/8 time and functions essentially as a triple concerto for violin and two 'flauti d'echo' (echo flutes). In performance, the echo flute parts are generally played by alto recorders or flageolets. The solo violin plays the bass line when the ripieno is unaccompanied in the E-minor andante (second movement). The concertino instruments play together in an 82-bar ritornello. The work leads into its fugal presto finale via a Phrygian cadence.

Brandenburg Concerto No. 6

The sixth and final Brandenburg Concerto is notably scored without any violins. The instruments are violas, cello, viola da gamba, violone, and harpsichord, with two viola soloists. The gamba players have an unusual supporting rather than solo role. The third movement is a gigue consisting of two repeated sections, the second of which is three times as long as the first; this gigue is effectively a set of variations on the ritornello theme introduced in the first movement.

Brandenburg Concerto No. 1 (F major)

The first Brandenburg Concerto, in F major, is the only one of the six to have four movements. Its first movement lacks a tempo indication and opens in cut time with an eighth-beat pickup. The final movement consists of a polacca (polonaise) with two trios and a menuet repeated four times. The work features horn calls and may have been derived from, or is closely related to, Bach's Hunting Cantata ('Was mir behagt'). Bach later recycled this concerto's first movement in his cantata 'Ich liebe den Höchsten von ganzem Gemüte.'

Brandenburg Concerto No. 2

The second Brandenburg Concerto is notable for its difficult high trumpet (tromba) part, which debates whether Bach intended trumpet or horn. Both Johann Altenburg and Johann Ludwig Schreiber have been proposed as the intended trumpet soloist, as both were known for their clarino playing technique. The trumpet part is described as reaching extreme high registers.

Summary of Facts

The Brandenburg Concertos are a set of six concertos (BWV 1046–1051) composed by Johann Sebastian Bach and dedicated in 1721 to Margrave Christian Ludwig of Brandenburg. Bach sent them as part of a job application to the Margrave's court — an application that ultimately went nowhere, as he was already employed at Köthen. The French-language dedication gives the set an alternate title: 'Six Concerts à plusieurs instruments.' Each concerto uses a different combination of solo (concertino) instruments, making the set a showcase for varied instrumental textures within the concerto grosso form.

The fifth concerto (D major) is the most heavily clued individual work. Its defining feature is a lengthy solo harpsichord cadenza — approximately 65 measures — in the first movement, marked 'senza stromenti' (without other instruments). This was unprecedented: it is the first known instance of a keyboard instrument functioning as a true soloist rather than a continuo player. The concertino consists of flute, violin, and harpsichord, and the harpsichord uniquely participates in both the ripieno and concertino groups. The fifth concerto was also the first time Bach employed the transverse flute; an earlier version of the work lacked the flute and the cello part. The piece may have been composed to show off a new harpsichord delivered to the Köthen court by instrument maker Michael Mietke, and it may also have been connected to a proposed keyboard duel between Bach and Louis Marchand, who reputedly fled Dresden rather than compete.

The third concerto (G major) is scored for three groups of string trios — three violins, three violas, and three cellos. Its most distinctive trait is the second movement: just a single bar containing two chords (A minor and B major) forming a Phrygian half-cadence. Because the movement is so brief, performers typically improvise a keyboard cadenza over it or substitute a movement from a Bach violin sonata. The first movement opens with a fast violin pickup motif: G–F-sharp–downbeat G.

The fourth concerto (G major) begins in 3/8 time and is essentially a triple concerto for violin and two 'flauti d'echo' (echo flutes), which are almost universally performed on alto recorders or flageolets in modern practice. The solo violin plays the bass line when the ripieno is unaccompanied in the E-minor andante. The concertino instruments share a massive 82-bar ritornello, and the work leads into its fugal presto finale through a Phrygian cadence.

The sixth and final concerto stands apart by completely omitting violins. Its scoring calls for two violas (as soloists), cello, viola da gamba, violone, and harpsichord. The gamba players take an unusual supporting rather than solo role. The third movement is a gigue structured as two repeated sections, with the second three times as long as the first; it functions as variations on the ritornello theme from the first movement.

The first concerto (F major) is the only one in the set with four movements. Its first movement opens in cut time with an eighth-beat pickup and lacks a tempo indication. The final movement is a polacca (polonaise), flanked by two trios and a menuet repeated four times. The work features prominent horn calls and is related to — or derived from — Bach's Hunting Cantata ('Was mir behagt'). Bach later recycled the first movement's material in his cantata 'Ich liebe den Höchsten von ganzem Gemüte.'

The second concerto features a notoriously high and demanding 'tromba' (trumpet/horn) part. Scholars debate whether Bach intended a natural trumpet or a horn; both Johann Altenburg and Johann Ludwig Schreiber have been proposed as the intended performer, as both were renowned clarino players.

Performance history includes Nikolaus Harnoncourt's landmark 1964 recording, the first to use period instruments. In 1979, Wendy Carlos released a Moog synthesizer realization of the concertos as a double album (part of the 'Switched-On Bach' series), which helped bring Baroque music to wider popular audiences.
