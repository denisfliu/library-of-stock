Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood co-founder, best known for Ophelia (floating in a stream) and Christ in the House of His Parents (controversial carpentry shop scene). Married Effie Gray after her unconsummated marriage to John Ruskin. Also painted The Order of Release, Peace Concluded, and Bubbles.
Ophelia (1851–1852)

Depicts Shakespeare's Ophelia floating/singing in a stream as she drowns, surrounded by brightly colored flowers. Long purple flowers represent 'dead men's fingers'; a chain of violets around her neck; a bright poppy symbolizing death by her hand. Elizabeth Siddal posed in a cold bathtub; Millais had to pay her 50-pound doctor's bill. Landscape inspired by the Hogsmill River (artist worked beside Holman Hunt painting The Hireling Shepherd). Originally featured a water vole; John Ruskin described the setting as a 'rascally wirefenced garden-rolled-nursery-maid's paradise.' A botany professor used the detailed flora to instruct students. Displayed at the Royal Academy alongside Arthur Hughes's painting subtitled 'And He Will Not Come Back Again.'

Christ in the House of His Parents (1849–1850)

Controversial painting depicting Jesus as a red-headed boy in a lower-class carpentry shop with wood shavings on the floor. The boy holds up a bleeding hand and kisses his mother, who was modeled on Mary Hodgkinson. A boy holding a water bowl (John the Baptist) stares at the figure. A dove on a ladder represents the Holy Spirit. Charles Dickens attacked the depiction of Mary as 'so horrible in her ugliness' as to appear 'in the vilest cabaret in France, or the lowest ginshop in England'; ironically, this criticism boosted the PRB's popularity. John Ruskin defended it. William Holman Hunt created a companion piece.

Summary of Facts

John Everett Millais (1829–1896) was an English painter and co-founder of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood alongside Dante Gabriel Rossetti and William Holman Hunt. The Brotherhood published their ideas in the periodical The Germ and signed works with cryptic initials (as in Isabella). They sought to restore artistic aesthetics to those that existed before the Renaissance painter Raphael — critics like Reynolds ('Sir Sloshua') were mocked for their Raphael worship.

Ophelia (1851–52) depicts Shakespeare's character floating in a stream, hands held face-up, as she drowns, surrounded by meticulously detailed flowers. Long purple flowers represent 'dead men's fingers' in the corner; a chain of violets circles her neck; poppies symbolize death. The flora was so detailed a botany professor used the painting to instruct students. Elizabeth Siddal posed in a cold bathtub lit by lamps; Millais was forced to pay a 50-pound doctor's bill after she fell ill. The landscape was painted along the Hogsmill River, where Millais worked alongside Holman Hunt painting The Hireling Shepherd. John Ruskin described the setting as a 'rascally wirefenced garden-rolled-nursery-maid's paradise' and noted it originally featured a water vole. The painting was displayed at the Royal Academy alongside Arthur Hughes's painting of Ophelia subtitled 'And He Will Not Come Back Again.'

Christ in the House of His Parents (1849–50) controversially depicted Jesus as a red-headed boy in a grimy lower-class carpentry shop with wood shavings on the floor. The boy holds up a bleeding hand and kisses his mother (modeled on Mary Hodgkinson); John the Baptist brings water in a bowl; a dove on a ladder represents the Holy Spirit. Charles Dickens attacked the depiction of Mary as 'so horrible in her ugliness' as to appear 'in the vilest cabaret in France, or the lowest ginshop in England' — ironically, this criticism boosted the PRB's popularity. John Ruskin defended it. William Holman Hunt created a companion piece.

Millais married Effie Gray after her unconsummated marriage to Ruskin. She posed for The Order of Release (a woman handing the title document to a guard while her wounded husband with arm in sling embraces her and a dog jumps up) and Peace Concluded (two girls play with toy animals while their father holds a newspaper announcing the end of the Crimean War).

Other notable works: Bubbles (child blowing bubbles, sold to Pears Soap for advertisements, now housed at the Lady Lever Art Gallery in Port Sunlight); The North-West Passage (Trelawny as a wizened sailor being read old logbooks by his great-granddaughter, subtitled 'It might be done and England should do it' — teetotaler Trelawny was outraged by a glass of grog in the picture); The Boyhood of Raleigh (deconstructed on the Talk Talk album It's My Life cover); The Blind Girl (title woman fails to see a double rainbow in the background; a butterfly rests on her red cloak); The Ruling Passion (reclining ornithologist shows a stuffed bird of paradise to children); Sir Isumbras at the Ford (parodied by Frederic Sandys); Mariana (woman in blue robe by stained-glass Annunciation copied from Merton College, inspired by Tennyson); The Black Brunswicker (Napoleon Crossing the Alps visible in background); and portraits of John Ruskin standing by a waterfall in Glenfinlas, Cardinal Newman, and the Princes in the Tower. His unfinished portrait of Thomas Carlyle was attacked by suffragette Anne Hunt in protest of Emmeline Pankhurst's arrest. He illustrated Trollope's Framley Parsonage. He frequently hired Fanny Eaton, daughter of a Jamaican slave, as a model.
