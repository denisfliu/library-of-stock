Summary of Facts

Wilfred Owen was an English World War I poet, killed in action on November 4, 1918, one week before the Armistice. His posthumous collection, edited with help from Edith Sitwell, declares in its preface that the subject is not Poetry but war, and that 'the Poetry is in the pity.'

"Dulce et Decorum Est" describes a gas attack in which soldiers scramble in 'an ecstasy of fumbling' after the cry 'Gas! Gas! Quick, boys!' The speaker recalls a dying soldier who 'plunges at me, guttering, choking, drowning' in his dreams. Blood from 'froth-corrupted lungs' is described as 'obscene as cancer / bitter as the cud.' The poem calls Horace's phrase 'dulce et decorum est pro patria mori' (it is sweet and proper to die for one's country) 'the old Lie,' urging readers not to tell it 'with such high zest / to children ardent for some desperate glory.'

"Anthem for Doomed Youth" is a sonnet opening 'What passing-bells for those who die as cattle?' It describes 'the monstrous anger of the guns' and 'the stuttering rifles' rapid rattle,' and imagines 'shrill, demented choirs' mourning men in whose eyes 'shine the holy glimmers of good-byes.' The poem closes with the image 'each slow dusk a drawing-down of blinds.' Siegfried Sassoon suggested both the solemn title and the phrase 'tenderness of patient minds.'

"Strange Meeting" is set in a tunnel 'scooped through granites which titanic wars had groined,' where the speaker encounters a dead enemy who says 'I am the enemy you killed, my friend' and 'Let us sleep now.' It contains the line 'the pity of war, the pity war distilled' and 'Now men will go content with what we spoiled.' A character recounts 'courage was mine, and I had mystery / wisdom was mine, and I had mastery.'

"The Parable of the Young Man and the Old" reimagines the sacrifice of Isaac, with Abraham binding a boy 'with belts and straps.' An angel commands 'Offer the Ram of Pride instead of him,' but Abraham ignores the command and slays his son — an allegory for the old generation sacrificing the young in war.

Other poems include "Mental Cases" ('men whose minds the Dead have ravished'), "Insensibility" ('Happy are men who yet before they are killed can let their veins run cold,' cursing those who made themselves 'immune' to 'the eternal reciprocity of tears'), "Arms and the Boy" (teeth that 'seem for laughing round an apple'; 'God will grow no talons'), and "S.I.W." (the story of Tim whose face is so contorted at death that comrades tell his mother 'Tim died smiling').

Owen met Siegfried Sassoon at Craiglockhart Hospital, describing him as 'Keats + Christ + Elijah + my Colonel + my father-confessor + Amenophis IV.' They published in The Hydra journal and are fictionalized in Pat Barker's Regeneration, where they collaborate on 'The Dead-Beat.' Paul Fussell credits them with inventing 'modern memory.' Robert Graves described Owen's early poetry as 'Sassoon-ish' and suggested Owen was gay. Stephen MacDonald's play Not About Heroes takes its title from Owen's preface.
