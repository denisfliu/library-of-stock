Netherlandish Early Renaissance painter. Known for the Arnolfini Portrait, Ghent Altarpiece (with brother Hubert), Madonna of Chancellor Rolin, and Saint Barbara silverpoint drawing. Vasari claimed he invented oil painting. Signature motto 'Als Ich Kan' ('As I can') puns on his name. Likely 'Hand G' of the Turin-Milan Hours.
Arnolfini Portrait (1434)

Double portrait often interpreted as depicting a wedding. Features the artist's signature ('Jan van Eyck was here') on a wall above a convex mirror with Stations of the Cross on its frame; the couple clasps hands in an act of fides manualis. A small dog at the couple's feet symbolizes fidelity, and a wooden image of St. Margaret appears on the bedpost. Erwin Panofsky argued the painting constitutes a legal marriage contract. Wikipedia

Ghent Altarpiece (with Hubert van Eyck)

Massive polyptych created with brother Hubert, now in Saint Bavo's Cathedral in Ghent. Bottom center panel shows the Adoration of the Mystic Lamb, in which a golden chalice collects the Lamb's blood; the Lamb was restored in 2020 to reveal humanoid forward-facing eyes. Includes St. Cecilia (or Mary) playing organ, God/Mary/John the Baptist in a Byzantine deesis at center; Just Judges panel stolen in 1934 (Jef Van der Veken made a copy). Grisaille depictions of John the Baptist and John the Evangelist appear in the closed view. Red-robed Jesus wears a papal crown. Patrons Jodocus Vijd and Elisabeth Borluut depicted in the closed view. Recovered from an Austrian salt mine after Nazi seizure. Wikipedia

Madonna of Chancellor Rolin

Nicolas Rolin, the Burgundian chancellor, kneels in prayer opposite the Virgin Mary (being crowned by an angel) in a loggia; the middle ground is separated into three archways looking out onto a cityscape split by a river. Reflectography revealed van Eyck removed a large coin purse Rolin was wearing in the underdrawing. Panofsky's Early Netherlandish Painting featured this work on the cover. Rogier van der Weyden later borrowed the motif of two people peering over a wall for Saint Luke Drawing the Virgin. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Jan van Eyck (c. 1390–1441) was the leading Early Netherlandish painter, working primarily in Bruges. Giorgio Vasari credited him with inventing oil painting (along with brothers Lambert and Hubert), and he is widely recognized as a pioneer who replaced egg tempera with oil, enabling the luminous detail and layered glazes that define his style. David Hockney and physicist Charles Falco controversially theorized he used the camera obscura to improve perspective.

His signature motto 'Als Ich Kan' ('As I can,' a pun on his name) appears in Greek letters on several works, including carved on the frame of Man in a Red Turban — considered a possible self-portrait — and on a portrait of his wife Margaret. He is also likely the 'Hand G' who contributed to the Turin-Milan Hours manuscript.

The Arnolfini Portrait (1434) is his most celebrated single work. The couple clasps hands in a gesture of fides manualis to the left of a wooden carving of St. Margaret on a bedpost. The artist's signature 'Jan van Eyck was here' is rendered in lavish calligraphy on the wall above a convex mirror whose frame is surrounded by ten roundels depicting the Passion of Christ; amber prayer beads and a brush flank the mirror. A small dog at the couple's feet symbolizes fidelity. Erwin Panofsky famously argued in Early Netherlandish Painting (1953) that the painting served as a legal marriage contract. Craig Harbison also analyzed it in depth, and Jacques Paviot's discovery suggested the depicted event may have occurred after van Eyck's death. The convex mirror influenced the Werl Triptych four years later and Ford Madox Brown's Take Your Son, Sir!

The Ghent Altarpiece, co-designed with his brother Hubert, is housed in Saint Bavo's Cathedral, Ghent. Its bottom center panel depicts the Adoration of the Mystic Lamb: blood flows from the Lamb into a golden chalice, surrounded by 14 angels; a 2020 restoration revealed the Lamb originally had forward-facing humanoid eyes overpainted in the 16th century. The upper tier features a Byzantine deesis — unusual for showing the Virgin Mary reading a book — flanked by angels playing a pipe organ and stringed instruments, with inscriptions from Psalm 150 ('music in praise of God'). Grisaille depictions of John the Baptist and John the Evangelist appear on the closed view, with Cain and Abel above Adam and Eve; Adam's foot appears to reach beyond his architectural niche. The Just Judges panel, showing horsemen including Philip the Good, was stolen in 1934 and never recovered; on his deathbed, Arsene Goedertier confessed to knowing its location, and Jean-Baptiste Clamence in Camus's The Fall claims to have found it in a bar called Mexico City. Joseph Goebbels dispatched Heinrich Kohn to acquire it as a gift for Hitler. Jef Van der Veken made the famous copy. The altarpiece as a whole was recovered from an Austrian salt mine after Nazi seizure.

The Madonna of Chancellor Rolin depicts the Burgundian chancellor Nicolas Rolin kneeling in prayer opposite the Virgin Mary (being crowned by an angel) in a loggia; the middle ground opens through three archways onto a cityscape split by a river. Reflectography revealed van Eyck removed a coin purse from the underdrawing; Panofsky's Early Netherlandish Painting featured it on the cover. The Saint Barbara is a silverpoint-on-panel drawing showing the saint reading in front of an elaborate Gothic tower under construction.

Other works: Leal Souvenir — a portrait Panofsky claimed depicts the composer Gilles Binchois (though a letter suggests Niccolo Albergati); the Annunciation in which 'ecce ancilla Domini' is written upside-down and a Book of Wisdom quote ('she is more beautiful than the sun') is embroidered on Mary's robe; and a Madonna enthroned in which Mary breastfeeds. He visited the Portuguese court of the House of Aviz to paint Princess Isabella for Philip the Good.
