Summary of Facts

Alexander Pushkin (Alexander Sergeyevich Pushkin) is considered the founder of modern Russian literature and is its most canonical poet. He is most frequently clued through his verse novel Eugene Onegin, his narrative poem The Bronze Horseman, his short story 'The Queen of Spades,' and his Belkin Tales.

Eugene Onegin is by far Pushkin's most clued work, appearing in nearly every tossup. It uses a distinctive fourteen-line stanza of iambic tetrameter with alternating masculine and feminine rhymes (known as the 'Onegin stanza' or 'Pushkin sonnet'). The title character, the first literary 'superfluous man,' is a Byronic dandy who attends Tatyana's name-day celebration, where he flirts with Olga (Lensky's fiancee), provoking Lensky to challenge him to a duel. Onegin kills Lensky. Tatyana writes a love letter to Onegin, who gives her a condescending 'sermon' telling her marriage would bore him. Tatyana later realizes Onegin is 'a lexicon of words in vogue... mere parody and just a rogue' while reading his marginal notes. She reappears at a ball married to an old general named Gremin and rejects Onegin. The Night before the duel, Lensky rehearses a poem 'like drunken Delvik at a feast' after reading Schiller. Tatyana reads her fortune in hot wax and dreams of dining with monsters after a bear chases her through snow. Vladimir Nabokov produced a controversial literal translation that led to a falling-out with Edmund Wilson. Charles Johnston's form-preserving translation inspired Vikram Seth's The Golden Gate. Dostoevsky's 'Pushkin Speech' praised Tatyana as the apotheosis of Russian womanhood and claimed she 'stood solidly on her own native soil.'

The Bronze Horseman is a narrative poem about the 1824 flood of the Neva in St. Petersburg. The protagonist Yevgeny (Evgeni) falls in love with Parasha, whose house is destroyed by the flood. Yevgeny survives by sitting atop a marble lion. He later Goes mad and is chased through the streets by the title statue of Peter the Great. The poem contrasts Peter's 'eternal sleep' as nature-conqueror with the river's destructive power.

The Queen of Spades is Pushkin's best-known short story. The German engineer Hermann schemes to learn a secret gambling trick. The soldier Tomski tells of his grandmother, the 'Muscovy Venus,' who won a million francs on the advice of Count St. Germain. Hermann writes fake love letters to Lizaveta Ivanovna to gain access to the countess's house. He threatens the elderly countess with a pistol, causing her to die of fright. Her ghost forces Hermann to gamble his savings, and he Goes insane repeating 'three, seven, ace' after losing to Chekalinsky when the title card appears.

The Belkin Tales (Tales of Ivan Belkin) are a collection framed as stories of the mysterious recently-dead landowner Ivan Belkin, inspired by Irving and Scott. 'The Shot' features the expert marksman Silvio, who rushes to finish a long-deferred duel with the Count but calls it off when the Count's wife stumbles in, shooting a Swiss landscape painting instead (the painting has two bullet holes). 'The Blizzard' features Burmin falling to his knees before Maria after realizing she is the woman he jokingly married during the title event. 'The Coffin-Maker' features a coffin-maker insulted at a shoemaker's dinner, haunted by skeletons. 'The Station Master' has a house decorated with the Parable of the Prodigal Son. 'The Squire's Daughter' ends with a student realizing his peasant love is Liza in disguise. Prosper Merimee translated and plagiarized 'The Shot.'

Pushkin's lyric poetry is also heavily clued. 'I Loved You' ('I loved you; and perhaps I love you still') is his most famous lyric. 'I Remember a Wonderful Moment' was written for Anna Kern. 'Ode to Liberty' led to his exile at age 18. 'The Prophet' describes a snake depositing a 'blazing shard of coal' in the speaker's chest before being told to 'burn Man's heart' with God's word. 'Winter Morning' ('Frost and sun - the day is wondrous!') is memorized by Russian schoolchildren. 'The Devils' features a coachman saying 'we've lost the way.' His 'little tragedies' include The Stone Guest (based on the Don Juan legend/Mozart opera), Mozart and Salieri (Salieri poisons Mozart's drink, confirming 'villainy and genius can never go together'), Feast in the Time of Plague, and Boris Godunov (a monk woos the Polish noble Maryna while leading a rebellion). Ruslan and Lyudmila features a 'learned cat' on a 'golden chain' to a 'green oak' and a hill that turns out to be the massive head of the wizard Chernomor's brother.

The Captain's Daughter is a novel about Peter Grinev stationed at Bailogorsk fortress, who quarrels with Shvabrin and pursues Maria Mironova. He is rescued from a blizzard by Pugachev, the historical rebel leader.

Biographically, Pushkin branded himself 'The African' due to his great-grandfather Abram Gannibal. He died in a duel with the French officer Georges-Charles d'Anthes, precipitated by anonymous letters naming Pushkin 'Deputy Grand Master of the Order of Cuckolds' after d'Anthes flirted with Pushkin's wife Natalia. Lermontov's 'Death of The Poet' commemorates his death, decrying 'haughty ones, descendants of forebears known for shallowness' and saying his 'blood of blackness will not obliterate true and righteous blood.' Pushkin started the journal The Contemporary with Gogol as editor.
