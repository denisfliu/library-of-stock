Summary of Facts

Robert Browning is a Victorian English poet, primarily identified through his dramatic monologues collected in Men and Women and Dramatis Personae.

"My Last Duchess" is his most frequently clued poem, in which the Duke of Ferrara describes a painting by Fra Pandolf of his deceased wife (Lucrezia de' Medici), who had a heart 'too soon made glad' and equated the Duke's 'favour at her breast' with 'the dropping of the daylight in the West.' The Duke notes that 'all smiles stopped together,' implying he had her killed, and says 'I never stoop.' The painting shows 'depth and passion of its earnest glance' and 'a faint half-flush that dies along her throat.' The poem ends with the Duke showing the emissary a bronze statue of Neptune cast by Claus of Innsbruck. Margaret Atwood wrote a story named for this poem, and Maggie O'Farrell's novel The Marriage Portrait is based on it. Richard Howard wrote the response poem 'Nikolaus Mardruz to his Master Ferdinand.'

"Porphyria's Lover" opens with a storm that 'tore the elm-tops down for spite, and did its worst to vex the lake.' The speaker strangles his beloved with her yellow hair and concludes that God 'has not said a word.' Pippa Passes is a verse drama set in Asolo containing the famous line 'God's in his heaven, all's right with the world,' and was notably parodied in Nabokov's Pale Fire ('Here Papa pisses'). Browning mistakenly used the word 'twat' thinking it meant part of a nun's habit.

The 'Soliloquy of the Spanish Cloister' opens with 'Gr-r-r' and features a monk plotting against Brother Lawrence who drinks orange juice in three sips to frustrate the Arian. 'Rabbi Ben Ezra' begins 'Grow old along with me! The best is yet to be' and responds to the Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam. 'Andrea del Sarto' contains the line 'a man's reach should exceed his grasp.'

The Ring and the Book is a twelve-part epic based on a Yellow Book of legal testimony discovered in Florence, describing the murder trial of Count Guido Franceschini. 'Childe Roland to the Dark Tower Came,' from the collection Men and Women, takes its title from King Lear (Tom O'Bedlam's song), opens 'My first thought was, he lied in every word,' and ends with the knight blowing the 'slug-horn' (a term adopted from Thomas Chatterton). Harold Bloom calls the Dark Tower a metaphor for art's 'Scene of Instruction' in A Map of Misreading. Sordello is a notoriously difficult poem about a troubadour from Mantua that prompted Ezra Pound to write 'Hang it all, Robert Browning, there can be but one Sordello' in Canto II; Tennyson quipped he understood only the first and last lines — and both were lies.

Browning was married to Elizabeth Barrett Browning and lived with her at the Casa Guidi in Italy. Rudolf Besier's comedy The Barretts of Wimpole Street popularized the anecdote that only God understood a difficult passage Browning himself had forgotten. He inspired Ezra Pound and is the basis for Randolph Henry Ash in A.S. Byatt's Possession. Other notable poems include 'The Lost Leader' (attacking Wordsworth), 'The Bishop Orders His Tomb' (praised by Ruskin as summarizing Renaissance 'hypocrisy, ignorance, and love of luxury'), 'Fra Lippo Lippi' (which ends with the exclamation 'Zooks'), 'Home-Thoughts, from Abroad' ('Oh, to be in England / Now that April's there'), 'Two in the Campagna,' 'A Toccata of Galuppi's,' 'Caliban Upon Setebos,' and 'Paracelsus.' James Murray complained that Browning 'constantly used words without regard to their proper meaning,' complicating the OED.
