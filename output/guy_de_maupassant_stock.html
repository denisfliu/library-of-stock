Summary of Facts

Guy de Maupassant was a French short story writer and novelist, a protege of Gustave Flaubert and a leading figure of French Naturalism. The Goncourt brothers' journal started a rumor that he was Flaubert's illegitimate son. He went mad from syphilis late in life, a fact reflected in his horror story 'The Horla.' Isaac Babel wrote a story titled 'Guy de Maupassant' in which a translator recalls how he went mad and began eating his own feces.

His most famous story is 'The Necklace,' about Mathilde Loisel, the wife of a clerk, who borrows a piece of jewelry from Madame Forestier for a ball. She loses it and spends ten years working to pay off the debt for a replacement, only to discover in a twist ending that the original was made of paste and worth only 500 francs. Many stories were printed in Gil Blas. Henry James wrote 'Paste' as a reversal of 'The Necklace.'

'Boule de Suif' ('Ball of Fat') is about the prostitute Elisabeth Rousset, who shares her chickens (jellied, 'cut into joints') with fellow passengers on a stagecoach from Rouen to Le Havre during the Franco-Prussian War. The other passengers, including the Loiseaus, Count de Breville, and Cornudet, convince her to sleep with a Prussian officer so they can continue their journey. At the end, the red-bearded Cornudet whistles the Marseillaise while Boule de Suif weeps. It was among six stories collected in Evenings at Medan, alongside works by Huysmans and others.

'Mademoiselle Fifi' features a Prussian officer given a feminine nickname due to his use of the phrase 'fi donc.' He blows up a statue of Venus with a teapot filled with gunpowder, shoots out the eyes of a portrait on which he has drawn a mustache, and makes a speech mocking France. The Jewish prostitute Rachel stabs him through the neck with a dessert (cheese) knife and hides in a Church bell tower, ringing the bells until German troops depart.

Bel-Ami is Maupassant's most famous novel, about the handsome ex-soldier Georges Duroy, a journalist at La Vie Francaise who climbs the social ranks through affairs with wealthy women. Madeleine ghostwrites journal articles for him. He fights an incompetent duel with a gossip columnist from La Plume. He makes a fortune from insider trading in Morocco. Tolstoy claimed to find 'no moral relation' in it.

'The Horla' is written as diary entries beginning 'May 8th.' The narrator waves to a Brazilian ship and begins experiencing a vampire spirit's possession. He Burns down his house with servants inside to kill the creature, concluding 'He is not dead... I suppose I must kill myself.' A monk compares the supernatural to unseen wind. Maupassant's syphilitic madness may be reflected in the story.

'Two Friends' features an officer who emotionlessly cooks The Fish caught by two executed fishermen (a watchmaker and draper) who refused to give up a password; they are shot and left to float downstream. A.J. Greimas wrote a 300-page semiotic analysis of this six-page story.

Pierre et Jean is about two brothers; an inheritance from Marechal leads a doctor to suspect his brother's illegitimacy. It opens on a retired jeweler's boat. Maupassant considered it his best novel and wrote the preface 'The Novel,' listing 25 varied novels to refute critics.

Une Vie follows a baron's daughter from her teenage exit from a convent to her death; a jealous husband pushes a moveable hut containing his wife and her lover off a cliff.

'Mother Savage' tells of a woman who records Prussian soldiers' addresses, traps them in a loft, and sets it on fire after learning her son died in battle. 'The Piece of String' features Maitre Hauchecorne, who picks up the title object and is accused by a harness-maker of stealing a pocketbook. 'My Uncle Jules' involves discovering on a steamboat that a poor man selling oysters is the narrator's brother. 'Miss Harriet' features an English woman who drowns in a well after the narrator, a painter, breaks her heart. 'Madame Tellier's Establishment' has a group of prostitutes making an entire Church congregation cry at a First Communion ceremony. 'Useless Beauty' has a countess claiming her husband did not father one of her seven children. 'The Hand' features a judge visiting the reclusive Englishman John Howell, who has a severed hand chained in his gun room. 'Dr. Heraclius Gloss' features a man going insane from reading about metempsychosis. 'The Terror' has a narrator hallucinating a man in his armchair.
