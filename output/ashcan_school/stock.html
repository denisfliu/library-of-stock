Summary of Facts

The Ashcan School was an American realist art movement focused on depicting gritty scenes of poor urban life in New York City, closely associated with the group known as 'The Eight.' The name originated from a caption in the Philadelphia Record, disparaged by cartoonist Art Young (editor of The Masses, the first to apply the term 'Ashcan' to painting). The Eight's exhibition was organized by Charcoal Club members to protest the jury voting policies of the National Academy of Design and was held at the Macbeth Galleries in 1908, featuring works by Everett Shinn, William Glackens, Maurice Prendergast, and John Sloan. Many members grew up in Philadelphia and studied under Thomas Anshutz, who in turn studied under Thomas Eakins. The movement was connected to the 1913 Armory Show, staged by Arthur B. Davies, Walter Pach, and Walt Kuhn. Jacob Riis's How the Other Half Lives paralleled the Ashcan School's depictions of urban poverty.

Robert Henri was the leader of the movement, Philadelphia-born, and author of The Art Spirit (encouraging color and light as 'means of expression'). He painted Snow in New York (carriage on snowy brownstone street), The Masquerade Dress (portrait of his second wife Marjorie Organ), and Cumulus Clouds, East River. George Luks's rejection from a gallery exhibition prompted Henri to storm out and form the new group.

George Bellows, Henri's student, is the most frequently clued member, famous for Boxing Paintings including Stag at Sharkey's (referee pushed aside, fighters grapple, borrowed from the Belvedere torso, 'stags' are non-member spectators), Dempsey and Firpo (one fighter's legs form equilateral triangle, other tangled in ropes), and Both Members of This Club (white boxer vs. black fighter Joe Gans, title satirizes private sport clubs). His other major works include Cliff Dwellers (1913, laundry/clotheslines between tenement buildings, trolley heading to Vesey Street, used H.G. Maratta's color system based on musical notes), 42 Kids (naked boys jumping off dock into East River, critic said they looked 'more like maggots than humans'), The Barricade (German soldiers using naked Belgians as human shields; Bellows said Da Vinci 'didn't have a ticket to paint the Last Supper'), Paddy Flannigan (buck-toothed bare-chested Irish boy), Emma at the Piano (wife in cobalt blue gown), and seascapes inspired by Winslow Homer at Monhegan and Matinicus Islands. He served as editor/illustrator of The Masses and taught Archibald Motley.

John Sloan painted sympathetic portrayals of working-class New Yorkers including McSorley's Bar (he was a regular), Wake of the Ferry, Hairdresser's Window, Movies Five Cents, Sixth Avenue Elevated at Third Street, and three women drying hair on a rooftop. He was known for socialist themes and painted Salome in a green bra for the Macbeth Galleries. George Luks painted The Wrestlers, Houston Street, Hester Street, and Sulky Boy. William Glackens helped purchase initial paintings for Philadelphia's Barnes Foundation. Maurice Prendergast exhibited with the group but his Post-Impressionist style differed. Stuart Davis studied under Henri before gravitating toward cubism. Margarett Sargent was also an Ashcan School artist.
