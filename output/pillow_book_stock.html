A zuihitsu (miscellaneous essay) collection by the Heian-era lady-in-waiting Sei Shonagon, written circa 1002. A foundational work of Japanese literature, consisting of observations about court life, lists (such as 'Hateful Things,' 'Embarrassing Things,' 'Adorable Things'), diary passages, poetry, and anecdotes. Set during the reign of Empress Teishi. Opens with 'In spring, the dawn...' linking seasons to times of day. The key aesthetic concept is 'okashi' (roughly 'amusing' or 'delightful'). Sei Shonagon was a rival of Murasaki Shikibu, author of The Tale of Genji.
The Pillow Book (Lists and Zuihitsu Genre)

The most distinctive feature of The Pillow Book is its many lists (called 'dan'), cataloging things by common adjectives or nouns. Among the most famous are 'Hateful Things,' 'Embarrassing Things,' 'Pleasing Things,' 'Adorable Things,' 'Depressing Things,' 'Elegant Things,' 'Things That Make One's Heart Beat Faster,' and 'Things That Should Be Large.' The zuihitsu genre ('follow the brush') consists of loosely connected prose observations. Other zuihitsu works include Kamo no Chomei's An Account of My Hut (Hojoki) and Yoshida Kenko's Essays in Idleness (Tsurezuregusa). Wikipedia

The Pillow Book (Opening and Structure)

The book opens with the famous passage 'In spring, the dawn...' ('In spring it is the dawn that is most beautiful'), linking each of the four seasons to the best time of day to view it. The key aesthetic concept is 'okashi' (amusing/delightful), used in the opening to describe fireflies and rain. The work consists of essays, diary passages, lists (dan), and poetry.

General / Sei Shonagon and Rivalries

Sei Shonagon was a Heian-era lady-in-waiting. Her rival Murasaki Shikibu (author of The Tale of Genji) criticized her in her diary, calling her 'dreadfully conceited' for littering her writing with Chinese characters, and saying The Pillow Book 'leaves a great deal to be desired.' Sei Shonagon's name comes from the Kiyohara family. She may have written the Matsushima Diary after leaving court.

Summary of Facts

The Pillow Book (Makura no Soshi) is a foundational work of Japanese literature by the Heian-era lady-in-waiting Sei Shonagon, written circa 1002. It is the defining example of the zuihitsu genre ('follow the brush'), consisting of loosely connected prose observations, and is regularly clued alongside other zuihitsu works: Kamo no Chomei's An Account of My Hut (Hojoki) and Yoshida Kenko's Essays in Idleness (Tsurezuregusa).

The most frequently clued feature is the book's many lists (dan): 'Hateful Things,' 'Embarrassing Things,' 'Pleasing Things,' 'Adorable Things,' 'Depressing Things,' 'Elegant Things,' 'Things That Make One's Heart Beat Faster' (which includes 'sparrows feeding their young'), and 'Things That Should Be Large' (which includes 'Priests. Fruit. Houses.'). A baby dying in a lying-in room is categorized as a 'Dispiriting Thing.' The lists often serve as giveaway or mid-level clues.

The famous opening passage, 'In spring, the dawn...' links each of the four seasons to the best time of day to view it, and employs the aesthetic concept 'okashi' (amusing/delightful) to describe fireflies and rain.

Court anecdotes set during the reign of Empress Teishi include: a dog beaten nearly to death for attacking a popular cat but later pardoned by the Emperor when it whimpers; the narrator betting on how long a mound of snow will last (hiring a gardener to protect it); flirtation with the courtier Yukinari (Controller First Secretary), including a scene about a false cock's crow; the narrator joking about 'knotweed' and tigers; and a woman exchanging umbrella/rainfall drawings. The narrator glorifies the present over the past and dominates poetry contests against other ladies-in-waiting.

Sei Shonagon was famously the rival of Murasaki Shikibu, who called her 'dreadfully conceited' for littering her writing with Chinese characters and said The Pillow Book 'leaves a great deal to be desired.' Cross-references in clues include Anita Desai's Fire on the Mountain (Nanda Kaul reflects on Shonagon's claim about women living alone), Ruth Ozeki's My Year of Meats (Jane Takagi-Little inspired by The Pillow Book), and Suzanne Buffam's A Pillow Book.
