American author, journalist, and satirist known for his Civil War short stories and horror fiction. Best known for 'An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge,' The Devil's Dictionary, and stories like 'Chickamauga' and 'Moxon's Master.' Mysteriously disappeared in Mexico in 1914.
An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge

Bierce's most famous story about the Confederate sympathizer Peyton Farquhar, an Alabama farmer who is tricked by a disguised Federal scout into trying to sabotage a railroad using burnable driftwood. As Farquhar is hanged for treason, he hallucinates escaping: jumping into the river, dodging bullets, seeing 'great golden stars in strange constellations,' and walking home along a densely-wooded road to embrace his wife. At the exact moment he imagines embracing his wife, he feels a 'stunning blow' to the back of his neck. The story was adapted as a French film that was broadcast as a Twilight Zone episode. Edmund Wilson's Patriotic Gore describes it as 'an epitome of Bierce's strange doomed and constricted life.' The regularly recurring sound that disturbs Farquhar is his own watch, 'as slow as the tolling of a death knell.'

Moxon's Master

A story about a chess-playing robot that murders its inventor after losing at chess. The narrator is haunted by the phrase 'Consciousness is the creature of Rhythm' (quoting Herbert Spencer's definition of life) after a conversation about whether machines can think. The inventor Moxon uses the example of eucalyptus tree roots seeking water to prove that plants think. The narrator is saved from a burning house by the metal worker Haley. A fez-wearing creature strangles the inventor.

Chickamauga

A story about a deaf-mute child with a wooden sword who wanders into a forest and encounters retreating Civil War soldiers. The child's inherited warrior spirit allows him to ford a river, but fails him when he runs from a rabbit. He follows the soldiers back to his family's burning farm, which he cannot hear. The section heading 'One Does Not Always Eat What Is On the Table' refers to a corpse. Part of Tales of Soldiers and Civilians.

The Devil's Dictionary

Bierce's satirical reference work, originally called 'The Cynic's Wordbook.' It defines education as something that 'disguises from the foolish their lack of understanding' and says a cynic's 'faulty vision sees things as they are, and not as they ought to be.' Includes a satirical rephrasing of the Decalogue: 'Honor thy parents, and perchance / Their wills thy fortunes may advance.'

Horror Stories (Other)

Bierce wrote several proto-weird horror stories collected in Can Such Things Be? 'The Death of Halpin Frayser' features the title character writing an incomplete poem ending 'At last the viewless...' in blood, attacked by the zombified spirit of his mother Catherine Larue. 'The Damned Thing' involves an impromptu inquest ruling Hugh Morgan's death as a mountain lion attack. 'Haita the Shepherd' features the title character who worships Hastur and pursues a maiden named Happiness. 'An Inhabitant of Carcosa' describes a sick man wandering through the ruins of his former city. 'Killed at Resaca' involves a man returning a dead friend's blood-stained letter to a woman at Rincon Hill who throws it in the fire. 'A Horseman in the Sky' has a soldier shooting his father and horse off a cliff.

General / Biographical

Bierce was an American journalist and satirist. His newspaper column entries as 'The Town Crier' for the California Advertiser are said to be the first real newspaper column. He mysteriously disappeared in Mexico in 1914. Carlos Fuentes fictionalized him as 'the Old Gringo' (the 'Indiana General') in a novel, carrying a copy of Don Quixote and a Colt .44, seeking a glorious death during the Mexican Revolution alongside Harriet Winslow and General Thomas Arroyo.

Summary of Facts

Ambrose Bierce is a frequently clued American author known for Civil War stories, horror fiction, and satirical writing. His most famous story, 'An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge,' follows the Confederate sympathizer Peyton Farquhar, tricked by a disguised Federal scout into sabotaging a railroad. As Farquhar is hanged, he hallucinates an elaborate escape only to receive a 'stunning blow' to his neck at the moment he imagines embracing his wife. The sound of his own watch disturbs him 'as slow as the tolling of a death knell.' The story was adapted into a French film shown as a Twilight Zone episode.

'Moxon's Master' features a chess-playing robot that murders its inventor, with the narrator haunted by the phrase 'Consciousness is the creature of Rhythm.' 'Chickamauga' follows a deaf-mute child with a wooden sword who encounters retreating soldiers and discovers his family's burning farm. The Devil's Dictionary is his satirical reference work defining education as something that 'disguises from the foolish their lack of understanding.'

His proto-weird horror collection Can Such Things Be? includes 'The Death of Halpin Frayser' (poem written in blood, zombified mother Catherine Larue), 'The Damned Thing' (Hugh Morgan's death), and 'Haita the Shepherd' (introducing Hastur, later adopted by Lovecraft). Bierce disappeared in Mexico in 1914, and Carlos Fuentes fictionalized him as the 'Old Gringo' in a novel set during the Mexican Revolution.
