General / Biographical

Francisco Tárrega (1852–1909) was a Spanish classical guitarist and composer, widely regarded as the father of modern classical guitar technique. He was a student of Julián Arcas. Late in his career he suffered a right-side paralysis that severely limited his playing. He transcribed many works by Isaac Albéniz (including Asturias/Leyenda), Bach, and Mozart for guitar. His playing technique influenced his successor Andrés Segovia, who however broke from Tárrega's tradition by playing with a combination of fingernails and fingertips. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Francisco Tárrega (1852–1909) was a Spanish classical guitarist and composer, considered the founder of modern classical guitar technique and a central figure in establishing the guitar as a serious concert instrument. He studied under Julián Arcas and became a celebrated virtuoso, though a right-side paralysis near the peak of his career curtailed his playing. He wrote the paired preludes Endecha and Oremus shortly before his death. He wrote 14 standard preludes for guitar in total, the three named ones being 'Lagrima' (after audience members at a concert in England reported feeling sad), 'Endecha,' and 'Oremus.' He transcribed a great many works by Albéniz (including Asturias/Leyenda), Bach, and Mozart for guitar, doing much to establish the classical guitar repertoire. Andrés Segovia, the next generation's defining guitarist, built on but also departed from Tárrega's tradition (playing with a mix of fingernails and fingertips rather than fingertips alone).

His most famous original work is Recuerdos de la Alhambra (c. 1896), a tremolo étude inspired by the Moorish Alhambra palace in Granada. In the piece, the i, m, and a fingers rapidly alternate on the same melodic note (tremolo) while the thumb plays an accompanying counter-melody. The opening melody is an ornamented version of the pitches E – D – C – D – E (then transposed up), with an answering phrase from high C landing on G-sharp. His Gran Vals, a waltz for solo guitar, became famous worldwide when its main theme was adopted as the Nokia mobile phone ringtone in the 1990s; the Nokia melody consists of D–E (eighth notes), F–G (quarters), then the same figure transposed down twice, ending on a dotted half note on A. His Capricho Árabe, written after travels through Andalusia and North Africa, opens with a long D perfect-fifth natural harmonic and calls for extensive portamento. His Danza Mora was composed the morning after hearing a repetitive drum rhythm in Algiers.
