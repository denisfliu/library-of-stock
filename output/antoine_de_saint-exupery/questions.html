Questions: Antoine de Saint-Exupery

2 tossups · 3 bonuses

Tossups

T1 2024 COOT (2024) Diff 8 · Other Academic
At the end of a letter, this person wrote of “all tenderness, all memories, all reasons for living” being “more inaccessible than any Egyptian treasures locked away in the glass cages of a museum”. This person described their “useless work” of identifying locations from photographs in a letter to Paul Claudel’s (“claw-DELLS”) son. This person, who was described by Sartre (“SAHT”) as a precursor of “literature of the machine”, was the subject of an Adèle Breaux biography of them “in America”. An identity bracelet naming this person and their wife (*) Consuelo (“con-SWAY-low”) was discovered near the likely site of their death. This person, who addressed the citizens of occupied France in their Letter to a Hostage, dedicated their best-known work to their friend Léon Werth (“VERT”) “when he was a little boy”. For 10 points, name this author and aviator who wrote The Little Prince.
ANSWER: Antoine de Saint-Exupéry [or Antoine Marie Jean-Baptiste Roger, comte de Saint-Exupéry; accept Saint-Exupéry In America] (The first line is from “Letter to an American”.)
T2 2024 ACF Nationals (2024) Diff 9 · Literature
Note to moderator: Read the answerline carefully. Note to players: Description acceptable. The painter Hedda Sterne encouraged the use of these artworks, which include depictions of animals inspired by the creator’s lover Silvia Reinhardt and her black poodle. A character makes one of these artworks depicting a yellow-shaded figure falling “softly as a tree falls” in front of two hills, then reproduces it in the next chapter without that figure and calls it “the loveliest and the saddest landscape in the world.” After three failed attempts, the frustrated narrator creates one of these artworks depicting a box with three air holes that he claims contains a sheep. Adults mistake one of these artworks for a depiction of a hat, but the title character recognizes it as a boa constrictor swallowing an elephant. For 10 points, identify these artworks made by a pilot in a novella by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry (“SAN-teg-zew-pay-REE”).
ANSWER: illustrations in The Little Prince [accept watercolors, paintings, drawings, or art in place of “illustrations”; accept Le Petit Prince in place of “The Little Prince”; accept illustrations by the narrator of The Little Prince, the pilot in The Little Prince, the protagonist of The Little Prince, or equivalents; prompt on illustrations, paintings, drawings, or art by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry by asking “from what literary work?”; reject “drawings by the Little Prince” or equivalents]

B1 2022 Chicago Open (2022) Diff 10 · Literature
This author described feeling disgusted at a ten-year-old’s marriage ceremony in her autobiographical story “Child Bride.” For 10 points each:
[10] Name this Taiwanese author who wrote about her experience living in Western Sahara with her Spanish husband José in Stories in the Sahara. This author took her pen name from a fictional orphan in a mànhuà comic.
ANSWER: Sānmáo [or Echo Chan; or Echo; or Echo Chén Píng]
[10] Sānmáo’s writings about the Sahara may have been inspired by her love for this French book, whose narrator, a pilot, crashlands in the Sahara and meets a resident of Asteroid B-612.
ANSWER: The Little Prince [or Le Petit Prince]
[10] Shortly before her death, Sānmáo scripted an award-winning Ho Yim film whose title uses this adjective to describe “dust.” This adjective appears in the title of a novel whose narrator’s uncle Arhat is skinned alive by a Japanese butcher.
ANSWER: red [accept Red Dust or Swirling Red Dust or Gǔngǔn Hóngchén; accept Red Sorghum or Red Sorghum Clan or Hóng Gāoliáng Jiāzú] {The Little Prince is by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry; Red Sorghum is by Mò Yán (“mwoh YEN”).}
B2 2019 Fall Open (2019) Diff 9 · Literature
A novel by this author opens as its narrator imagines that his obituary will simply state that he was “SURVIVED BY AN APARTMENT FULL OF SHIT.” For 10 points each:
[10] Name this author who included that character’s obituary, titled “The Death of Leopold Gursky,” as the brief final chapter of her 2005 novel The History of Love.
ANSWER: Nicole Krauss
[10] In 2004, Krauss married this author, whose novel about Oskar Schell bears many similarities to Krauss’s The History of Love. More recently, this author wrote about Jews living in D.C. in the novel Here I Am.
ANSWER: Jonathan Safran Foer
[10] In Krauss’s The History of Love, Alma heaps admiration onto this pilot, who “risked his life to open mail routes to remote places.” He disappeared over the Mediterranean a year after he wrote The Little Prince.
ANSWER: Antoine de Saint-Exupéry (“SAN-teg-zoo-per-REE”) [or Antoine Marie Jean-Baptiste Roger, comte de Saint-Exupéry]
B3 2013 NASAT (2013) Diff 8 · Literature
This man detailed his time working for Aeropostale in Wind, Sand and Stars. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this author better known for a novella in which the title character lives on an asteroid called B-612, The Little Prince.
ANSWER: Antoine de Saint-Exupery
[10] This other twentieth-century French writer wrote about Fred Peloux in Chéri, as well as four novels about Claudine and the novella Gigi.
ANSWER: Colette [or Sidonie-Gabrielle Colette]
[10] Chéri was praised by this fellow French novelist of The Immoralist and The Counterfeiters.
ANSWER: Andre Gide
