The Little Prince

A novella in which a pilot crash-lands in the Sahara and meets the title character, a boy from Asteroid B-612. Adults mistake one of the narrator's drawings for a hat, but the Little Prince recognizes it as a boa constrictor swallowing an elephant. The narrator draws a box with three air holes after failing three times to draw a sheep. The novella is dedicated to Leon Werth 'when he was a little boy.' The narrator depicts a yellow-shaded figure falling 'softly as a tree falls' and then reproduces the drawing without the figure, calling it 'the loveliest and the saddest landscape in the world.' The painter Hedda Sterne encouraged the use of Saint-Exupery's watercolor illustrations. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Saint-Exupery was a French author and aviator. He worked for Aeropostale (detailed in Wind, Sand and Stars). He disappeared over the Mediterranean a year after writing The Little Prince. His wife was Consuelo (an identity bracelet naming them was discovered near the likely site of his death). Sartre described him as a precursor of 'literature of the machine.' He wrote Letter to a Hostage, addressing citizens of occupied France. Adele Breaux wrote a biography of him in America.

Summary of Facts

Antoine de Saint-Exupery is a French author and aviator best known for The Little Prince, a novella in which a pilot crash-lands in the Sahara and meets the title character from Asteroid B-612. The most iconic clue involves adults mistaking the narrator's drawing for a hat when it actually depicts a boa constrictor swallowing an elephant. After three failed attempts to draw a sheep, the narrator draws a box with three air holes. The novella is dedicated to Leon Werth 'when he was a little boy.' The narrator's illustrations include a yellow-shaded figure falling 'softly as a tree falls,' which is then reproduced without the figure as 'the loveliest and the saddest landscape in the world.' The painter Hedda Sterne encouraged the watercolor illustrations, which include animals inspired by Saint-Exupery's lover Silvia Reinhardt.

Biographically, Saint-Exupery worked for Aeropostale, an experience detailed in Wind, Sand and Stars. He disappeared over the Mediterranean a year after writing The Little Prince. An identity bracelet naming him and his wife Consuelo was found near the site. Sartre described him as a precursor of 'literature of the machine.' He wrote Letter to a Hostage, addressing occupied France. In Nicole Krauss's The History of Love, the character Alma admires him as a pilot who 'risked his life to open mail routes to remote places.'
