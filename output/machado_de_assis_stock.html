Machado de Assis

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Brazilian author (Joaquim Maria Machado de Assis), the mestizo grandson of slaves, often called the 'supreme black literary artist' by Harold Bloom. Best known for The Posthumous Memoirs of Bras Cubas, Dom Casmurro, and Quincas Borba. His works are set in and around Rio de Janeiro. He satirized Positivism and Social Darwinism as 'Humanitism.' His literary periods include an early Romantic period and a more widely read Fantastical period. Also wrote the novella The Alienist and numerous short stories including 'Midnight Mass.' A frequent translator of Shakespeare.
The Posthumous Memoirs of Bras Cubas

A novel narrated from the grave by the 'small winner' Bras Cubas, who opens by recounting his death from pneumonia despite developing a wonder elixir / 'anti-melancholy poultice.' Dedicated to 'the worm who first gnawed on the cold flesh of' the narrator's corpse. The narrator perceives himself turning into the Summa Theologica and traveling back in time on a hippopotamus (which he compares to Achilles's horse). He has an affair with Virgilia. One chapter consists entirely of punctuation marks and the names Bras Cubas and Virgilia ('Dialogue Between Adam and Eve'). Another chapter reads 'But, I'm either mistaken or I've just written a useless chapter.' He concludes that his failure to conceive a child was his contribution to mankind. His former classmate Quincas Borba teaches him the philosophy of Humanitism. The narrator discovers a prostitute has been ravaged by smallpox and now runs a jewelry store. Narrated in reverse structure. Wikipedia

Dom Casmurro

An 1899 novel narrated by Bento Santiago, infused with Othello themes. Bento suspects his wife Capitu of having an affair with his best friend Escobar. After Escobar drowns, Bento obsesses over his son Ezekiel's resemblance to the dead man and doubts the child is his. Dona Gloria promises God to have her child become a priest if it survives childbirth. Bento befriends Escobar at the seminary. The protagonist sees himself as Othello and Capitu as Desdemona; inspired by a performance of Othello. Bento's father quotes the proems of The Iliad and The Odyssey to his two children to contrast their character traits. A character is angered when his wife cannot remember the words a coconut sweet seller was singing when they first pledged their love. Machado frequently translated Shakespeare and drew heavily on Othello. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Joaquim Maria Machado de Assis (1839-1908), Brazilian author, the mestizo grandson of slaves. Called the 'supreme black literary artist' by Harold Bloom. Set most of his stories in and around Rio de Janeiro. A frequent translator of Shakespeare. Margaret Jull Costa and Robin Patterson published a 2018 collected stories volume. Also wrote the novel Esau and Jacob (structured as the seventh volume of Counselor Ayres's memoirs, about Natividade's two children feuding for Flora's affections). Short stories include 'Midnight Mass' (Nogueira stays up late talking with Conceicao Menezes; opens 'I have never quite understood a conversation I had with a lady many years ago, when I was seventeen and she was thirty'), 'Fame' (composer of polkas like 'Fine Words Butter No Parsnips' dreams of serious works), 'Life!' (dialogue between Prometheus and the Wandering Jew), 'The Case of the Stick,' and stories featuring a bonze from the Kingdom of Bungo (philosophy of Pomadism — reality is inferior to opinion), a feminine Thai king switching bodies, democracy among spiders, and summoning Alcibiades while reading Plutarch. His early Romantic period contrasts with his more widely read Fantastical period.

Summary of Facts

Machado de Assis (Joaquim Maria Machado de Assis, 1839-1908) is the most prominent Brazilian author in quizbowl, asked about primarily through his three major novels -- The Posthumous Memoirs of Bras Cubas, Dom Casmurro, and Quincas Borba -- as well as the novella The Alienist.

The Posthumous Memoirs of Bras Cubas is narrated from beyond the grave by the 'small winner' Bras Cubas, who recounts his death from pneumonia. The novel is dedicated to 'the worm who first gnawed on the cold flesh of' his corpse. In a delirium, the narrator turns into the Summa Theologica and travels back in time on a hippopotamus (compared to Achilles's horse). He has an affair with Virgilia, and one chapter ('Dialogue Between Adam and Eve') consists entirely of punctuation marks. He wants to invent an 'anti-melancholy poultice,' is taught Humanitism by his former classmate Quincas Borba, and concludes that his failure to conceive a child was his 'contribution to mankind.' The novel satirizes Positivism and Social Darwinism.

Dom Casmurro (1899) is narrated by Bento Santiago, who suspects his wife Capitu of having an affair with his best friend Escobar. After Escobar drowns, Bento obsesses over his son Ezekiel's resemblance to the dead man. The novel is infused with Othello -- Bento sees himself as Othello and Capitu as Desdemona, and is inspired to mistrust her by a performance of Shakespeare's play. Dona Gloria promises God her child will become a priest if it survives childbirth, and Bento befriends Escobar at the seminary.

Quincas Borba (Philosopher or Dog?) follows Rubiao, who inherits a fortune from the Humanitist philosopher Quincas Borba on condition he care for an identically-named dog. Borba praises war with a parable about two starving tribes, inspiring the refrain 'to the victor, the potatoes!' Rubiao pursues the married Sophia and becomes deluded into thinking he is Napoleon III. Christiano and Sofia steal his inheritance.

The Alienist follows Dr. Simao Bacamarte, an overzealous psychiatrist who commits nearly everyone to an asylum called the Green House, causes a revolt, and eventually locks himself in his own asylum. He explains to Soares that the soul is like a seashell from which he hopes to extract a pearl.

Biographically, Machado was the mestizo grandson of slaves, called the 'supreme black literary artist' by Harold Bloom. He set most of his stories in and around Rio de Janeiro and was a frequent translator of Shakespeare. His short stories include 'Midnight Mass' (opening with 'I have never quite understood a conversation...'), 'Life!' (dialogue between Prometheus and the Wandering Jew), 'Fame' (a polka composer dreams of serious works), and tales featuring Pomadism (reality inferior to opinion), democracy among spiders, and a Thai king switching bodies. His works span an early Romantic period and a more widely read Fantastical period.
