Italian film director, one of the towering figures of European cinema, best known for 8½, La Dolce Vita, La Strada, and Amarcord. Associated with the Italian Neorealist movement (early career) before developing a distinctly baroque and dreamlike personal style. Known for recurring motifs of circuses, memory, and the absurd. Many of his films draw on his own life in Rimini and Rome.

Summary of Facts

Federico Fellini is an Italian film director whose career spanned from Italian Neorealism to a highly personal, baroque, and impressionistic style. He is most frequently identified through his two signature films: 8½ (1963) and La Dolce Vita (1960). Fellini's early career is associated with the neorealist movement, though clues note he moved to a more dream-laden personal style.

La Dolce Vita (1960) follows gossip journalist Marcello Rubini through a week in Rome. Its most iconic scene shows Marcello and the Swedish-American actress Sylvia (played by Anita Ekberg) wading in the Trevi Fountain at dawn, a moment described across nearly every clue. The film also features a helicopter opening carrying a Christ statue over Rome, the character Paparazzo (from whom the word 'paparazzi' derives), and ends with a sea monster/leviathan washing ashore after a party. It stars Marcello Mastroianni and Anita Ekberg.

8½ (1963) is a semi-autobiographical film about director Guido Anselmi, who is creatively blocked while trying to make a science fiction film. It opens with the protagonist suffocating in a car and then floating up through the roof in a dream. Other key scenes include the clairvoyant Maya writing 'asa nisi masa' on a chalkboard triggering a childhood flashback, a man quoting the catechism to Guido in a spa steam room, and a final circus-like procession dancing on an incomplete rocket-ship film set. Guido imagines shooting himself under a table at a press conference. The film was originally titled The Beautiful Confusion and its title refers to the number of films Fellini had directed up to that point. The musical Nine by Maury Yeston is based on this film.

La Strada (1954) follows naive Gelsomina (Giulietta Masina) as she travels with brutish street performer Zampano (Anthony Quinn). The relationship breaks down when Zampano kills the clown Il Matto. Zampano's character was influenced by the Maciste figure from early Italian silent film. The film is considered neorealist.

Amarcord (1973) is a semi-autobiographical account of the coming-of-age of Titta under Fascism in a small Italian town. The title is a Romagnol dialect word meaning 'I remember,' which has since come to mean 'nostalgic memory' in Italian. The film contains a famous scene of a peacock unfurling its tail on a fountain during a snowfall.

Roma (1972) is a plotless semi-autobiographical film about Fellini's journey to the city of Rome. Its most clued scene involves subway workers discovering ancient frescoes that immediately fade and disintegrate upon exposure to fresh air. The film also features an ecclesiastical fashion show.

Nights of Cabiria (1957) stars Giulietta Masina as the prostitute Cabiria. The film ends with Cabiria walking along a road in tears before young musicians surround and cheer her. It is considered a neorealist work and was loosely adapted into the musical Sweet Charity (Cy Coleman, Neil Simon, Dorothy Fields).

Fellini's Casanova (1976) stars Donald Sutherland and opens with a Venetian carnival. It was filmed at Cinecittà; a prop of a giant head of Venus sinking into a canal from the film is still on the studio's grounds. I Vitelloni (1953) focuses on five unemployed young men and opens with a beachside 'Miss Mermaid' pageant. Satyricon (1969) contains a famous scene of a pig stuffed with birds and sausages cut open at Trimalchio's feast.

Biographically, Fellini's final interviews were compiled by Canadian filmmaker Damian Pettigrew into the book and documentary I'm a Born Liar. He co-wrote the screenplay for Roberto Rossellini's Rome, Open City. Circuses are a recurring motif across his career (La Strada, 8½, The Clowns). His frequent collaborators include Marcello Mastroianni and Anita Ekberg.
