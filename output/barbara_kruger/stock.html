American conceptual artist and feminist known for bold white text on red backgrounds, using the Futura Bold Oblique typeface over black-and-white photographs. Most famous works include Untitled (Your body is a battleground) and Untitled (I shop therefore I am). Her visual style was notoriously copied by the clothing brand Supreme.

Summary of Facts

Barbara Kruger is an American feminist conceptual artist whose signature style consists of bold white text in Futura Bold Oblique typeface set against red backgrounds, layered over black-and-white photographs appropriated from mass media. Her works typically feature provocative slogans that critique consumerism, patriarchy, and the politics of representation.

Her most frequently referenced work is Untitled (Your body is a battleground) (1989), created for a Women's March on Washington. It depicts a woman's face split into a photo positive and photo negative, with the titular phrase superimposed over it. Another iconic work, Untitled (I shop therefore I am), shows a hand grasping a red label or card bearing that phrase, and is held at the Hirshhorn Museum in Washington, D.C.

Kruger's Untitled (You invest in the divinity of the masterpiece) juxtaposes a detail from Michelangelo's Creation of Adam with the titular text behind black bars, attacking patriarchal narratives around artistic genius. She curated an exhibition called Picturing Greatness, selecting photographs shot solely by men by artists such as Aleksander Rodchenko and Robert Frank.

Other notable works include You Are Not Yourself (1981), depicting a woman looking in a mirror shattered by a bullet; We Won't Play Nature to Your Culture, showing a woman with leaves over her eyes; We Don't Need Another Hero; and When I Hear the Word Culture I Take Out My Checkbook, whose alternate title comes from a Hans Johst quote and divides a picture of Howdy Doody into three panels. Her 2004 video installation Twelve featured actors playing art critics and political strategists yelling slogans at each other.

Kruger's 2012 exhibition Belief + Doubt at the Hirshhorn Museum sprawled her works onto the floor and escalator of the museum. Her 2022 exhibition THINKING OF YOU. I MEAN ME. I MEAN YOU. included Hito Steyerl's Is the Museum a Battlefield? In 2011, her work Untitled (That's the Way We Do It) consisted of knock-off images in her signature style found online.

The clothing brand Supreme built its brand identity by directly copying Kruger's visual style of white Futura text on a red box background. Kruger famously referred to the resulting trademark case as "a ridiculous clusterfuck of totally uncool jokers." She also illustrated Stephen King's My Pretty Pony, with tiny red stopwatches in each illustration and a stainless-steel cover with a digital clock. Her book Remote Control: Power, Cultures, and the World of Appearances discusses the influence of TV and film on culture. Her first book paired photographs of California and Florida domestic architecture with typewritten musings on the people who lived inside.

Kruger uses sans-serif typefaces, primarily Futura Bold Oblique and sometimes Helvetica (particularly the Ultra Condensed variety). A recreation of one of her works served as the backdrop for Rage Against the Machine's Evil Empire tour, and another piece covered the breasts of an otherwise naked Kim Kardashian for a cover of W magazine.
