Opera by Giacomo Puccini, premiered in 1900. A melodramatic work set in Rome, following the soprano Floria Tosca, her lover the painter Mario Cavaradossi, and the villainous police chief Baron Scarpia. Known for its arias 'Vissi d'arte' and 'E lucevan le stelle,' its shocking plot of betrayal and death, and its climax atop the Castel Sant'Angelo.

Act I – The Church / Te Deum Scene

The first act is set in the church of Sant'Andrea della Valle in Rome. Key events include: Cavaradossi hiding the escaped prisoner Angelotti; the choir boys celebrating the presumed defeat at the Battle of Marengo by singing a Te Deum; and the villain Scarpia exclaiming 'You make me forget even God.' The first act opens with a character searching for a key hidden in the feet of a statue of the Virgin Mary.

Summary of Facts

Tosca is a melodramatic opera by Giacomo Puccini, set in Rome, centering on the soprano diva Floria Tosca, her lover the painter Mario Cavaradossi, and the corrupt police chief Baron Scarpia. The opera was described as a 'shabby little shocker' by musicologist Joseph Kerman in his 1956 book Opera as Drama — a characterization that conductor Thomas Beecham dismissed, saying anything Kerman said about the composer 'can safely be ignored.'

The opera opens with a recurring motif of three loud brass chords on B-flat, A-flat, and E. Act I is set in the church of Sant'Andrea della Valle: Cavaradossi is painting a portrait of Mary Magdalene modeled on the Marchesa Attavanti, and he sings the aria 'Recondita armonia' comparing Attavanti's blonde features with Tosca's dark ones. Tosca grows jealous when she notices the resemblance. Cavaradossi hides the escaped prisoner Angelotti (former consul of the Roman Republic), who ultimately kills himself when his hiding place is discovered. A church choir sings the Te Deum celebrating the supposed defeat of Napoleon at the Battle of Marengo, while the villain Scarpia exclaims 'You make me forget even God.' The tenor also sings 'Vittoria! Vittoria!' on the notes C-sharp, F-sharp, F-sharp and F-sharp, A-sharp, A-sharp.

In Act II, Scarpia blackmails Tosca: he will spare Cavaradossi's life if she sleeps with him. Tosca sings 'Vissi d'arte' — an aria beginning with the descending phrase E-flat, D-flat, B-flat, A-flat, moving from E-flat minor to major — in which she laments her fate, having 'donated jewels to the Madonna's mantle' and 'never harmed a living soul.' Tosca ultimately agrees to the bargain, then stabs Scarpia with his own knife.

In Act III, Cavaradossi sings 'E lucevan le stelle' (introduced by a clarinet solo with repeated C-sharp and F-sharp notes), recalling 'sweet kisses and languorous caresses' as he awaits execution and lamenting 'I die in desperation / And I never before loved life so much.' Tosca believes the execution will be a mock one, but Cavaradossi is really shot. Tosca, discovering the betrayal, leaps to her death off the Castel Sant'Angelo.

Puccini regretted not casting the young Enrico Caruso as Cavaradossi for the premiere, having chosen the older Emilio De Marchi instead. Famous performers of the title role include Maria Callas (during whose performance Tito Gobbi had to leap on her to extinguish her hair catching fire), Renata Tebaldi, and Maria Jeritza (who famously performed 'Vissi d'arte' entirely prone, prompting, according to impresario Giulio Gatti-Casazza, the longest ovation ever seen at the Met). A supposed 'bouncing Tosca' anecdote — that a mattress was once replaced with a trampoline — has been recounted by Gerald Durrell and others but never corroborated.
