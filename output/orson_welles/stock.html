American actor, director, and radio personality best known for directing Citizen Kane (1941), widely regarded as one of the greatest films ever made. Also directed The Magnificent Ambersons, Touch of Evil, The Lady from Shanghai, and The Third Man (as actor). Famous for the 1938 War of the Worlds radio broadcast that allegedly caused mass panic. Led the Mercury Theatre company and put on influential stage productions including the 'Voodoo Macbeth' and Julius Caesar.

Summary of Facts

Orson Welles was an American actor, director, and radio personality who dominated multiple entertainment fields from the late 1930s onward. He first came to prominence starring as McGafferty in Archibald MacLeish's play Panic, then rose to major influence through the Mercury Theatre, which he co-founded with John Houseman. His stage work included a landmark all-black production of Macbeth — the 'Voodoo Macbeth' — for the Federal Theatre Project's New York Negro Unit, in which Eric Burroughs played a bullwhip-wielding Hecate. He also directed an anti-fascist 1937 Mercury Theatre production of Julius Caesar (in which Cinna the Poet is killed by fascist secret police) and Marc Blitzstein's The Cradle Will Rock, whose original 1937 production was shut down by the federal government. He directed Canada Lee as Bigger Thomas in a Broadway production of Native Son, and created a play called Five Kings by rearranging Shakespeare dialogue, which later became the film Chimes at Midnight.

Welles's most notorious early work was his 1938 Halloween radio broadcast of H.G. Wells's War of the Worlds, staged by the Mercury Theatre on Air. The broadcast allegedly caused mass panic (an unusual object lands in Grover's Mill, New Jersey), though historians like Brad Schwartz have argued that newspapers deliberately exaggerated the hysteria to discredit radio as a commercial rival. The broadcast took place on October 30, 1938. Imitations in other countries had deadly consequences — a repeat in Quito caused a mob to set fire to a building, killing a pianist and violinist. Iowa Senator Clyde Herring introduced a never-passed bill requiring pre-clearance to prevent future such events.

Welles's debut feature film Citizen Kane (1941) is widely regarded as one of cinema's greatest achievements. He both acted and directed, playing newspaper magnate Charles Foster Kane — based on William Randolph Hearst — who dies at the opening, dropping a snow globe and whispering 'Rosebud.' The film is structured as a mystery to discover what 'Rosebud' means, revealed at the end to be a sled from Kane's childhood, shown burning in a fireplace. The film includes an opera scene (Susan Alexander performs Salammbo, which is panned), newsreel sequences describing Kane's run for Governor of New York, a shrieking cockatoo punctuating a butler's memory, and Kane's palatial Xanadu estate.

His second film, The Magnificent Ambersons (1942), suffered from studio interference: RKO cut 40 minutes while Welles was in Brazil filming a documentary. The film ends with a Joseph Cotten-played character providing for the fallen heir George. Agnes Moorehead's character Fanny breaks down in a famous 'boiler room' scene after admitting she is nearly broke — Welles reportedly filmed eleven false takes to build mounting hysteria. A scene in the film was inspired by Thornton Wilder's The Long Christmas Dinner.

The Lady from Shanghai (1947) starred Welles's wife Rita Hayworth as Elsa Bannister, a femme fatale. Welles played Michael O'Hara, who agrees to fake the death of George Grisby before Elsa murders Grisby herself. The film ends with a famous shootout in a hall of mirrors at a funhouse, with Welles playing Elsa navigates Chinatown in one sequence. Mr. Arkadin (1955) featured Guy Van Stratten hired to research a millionaire's mysterious past, of which he has no memory before 1927.

Touch of Evil (1958) is celebrated for its three-minute, twenty-second continuous opening tracking shot — shot by cinematographer Russell Metty — following two couples through a Mexican border town as a car bomb ticks. Welles plays corrupt police captain Hank Quinlan. Welles considered a separate longer tracking shot set inside an apartment in the film to be even superior. The film was the last Hollywood production Welles directed.

As an actor not directing, Welles's most famous role was Harry Lime in Carol Reed's The Third Man (1949). He delivers the celebrated 'cuckoo clock' speech on a Ferris wheel — comparing Switzerland's 500 years of democracy and peace to only producing the cuckoo clock — and refers to people below as 'dots.' He first appears memorably revealed by a streetlamp in a dark Vienna doorway after the protagonist believed him dead. He flees through the sewers of Vienna in the climax. Anton Karas's zither score plays throughout the film. Welles also played corrupt cop Hank Quinlan in Touch of Evil.

In his European period, Welles directed adaptations of Kafka's The Trial (1962), featuring Romy Schneider as Leni, and the Shakespeare compilation Chimes at Midnight (1965). His late film F for Fake (1973) was a docudrama about art forger Elmyr de Hory, Clifford Irving's dubious autobiography of Howard Hughes, and Welles's partner Oja Kodar.

Welles has been portrayed on screen by multiple actors: Liev Schreiber in RKO 281, Angus Macfadyen in Cradle Will Rock, and Christian McKay in Me and Orson Welles. Maurice LaMarche voiced him twice in animation (The Simpsons, The Critic) and claimed to have based the voice of 'the Brain' from Animaniacs on Welles. Peter Jackson's Heavenly Creatures uses Welles as an imaginary figure who chases the protagonists.
