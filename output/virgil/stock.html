Roman poet (Publius Vergilius Maro), author of the Aeneid, the Eclogues, and the Georgics. The Aeneid is Rome's national epic, opening with 'I sing of arms and the man.' The Eclogues are pastoral poems modeled on Theocritus, including the famous Fourth Eclogue with its 'messianic' child prophecy. The Georgics are four books of agricultural poetry. In medieval tradition, Virgil served as Dante's guide through Inferno and Purgatorio as a 'virtuous pagan,' and was also revered as a sorcerer in Neapolitan legends. He wished his manuscript burned after his death, but Augustus had it published. Patron was Maecenas.
Aeneid

Rome's national epic in twelve books of hexameter, beginning 'I sing of arms and the man' (Arma virumque cano). Follows the Trojan prince Aeneas from the fall of Troy to his founding of a new civilization in Italy. Ends with Aeneas killing Turnus in anger at the sight of Pallas's belt. Augustus had it published posthumously against Virgil's wishes. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Virgil (Publius Vergilius Maro, 70–19 BCE) is the preeminent Roman poet, author of the Aeneid, the Eclogues, and the Georgics. Born in Mantua, he died at Brundisium. His patron was the Roman statesman Maecenas.

The Aeneid, Rome's national epic, is twelve books of hexameter verse opening with the famous line 'I sing of arms and the man' (Arma virumque cano). It follows the Trojan prince Aeneas and ends with his killing of the Rutulian Turnus in anger at seeing the belt of his fallen ally Pallas — an act that may violate Aeneas's own piety. In Book VI, Aeneas sees a procession of future great Romans but is told not to look too long at Marcellus. Virgil allegedly caused Augustus's sister Octavia to faint by reading from the Aeneid. He wished the manuscript burned after his death, but Augustus had Lucius Varius Rufus and Plotius Tucca edit and publish it. Renaissance poet Maffeo Vegio wrote a thirteenth book (Supplementum) including an apotheosis scene. The Aeneid gave rise to the sortes Vergilianae, a medieval fortune-telling practice of picking random lines from the text. The golden bough from Book VI titles James Frazer's anthropological classic. Notable translations include the Earl of Surrey's (the earliest blank verse in English), John Dryden's landmark version, and Gavin Douglas's full Scots vernacular translation. The Aeneid contains a famous example of hysteron proteron: 'Let us die, and charge into the thick of the fight.' Lines were rearranged into centos praising Christ (e.g., by Faltonia Betitia Proba). Vondel's Dutch play Gijsbrecht van Aemstel mirrors the Aeneid's plot.

The Eclogues are ten pastoral poems modeled on Theocritus's Idylls. The collection contains the famous phrase 'omnia vincit amor' (love conquers all) in the tenth Eclogue, spoken by the poet Gallus who is dying of unrequited love for Lycoris. The Fourth Eclogue, addressed to Asinius Pollio, prophesies that a child ('great progeny of Jove') will end the 'iron race' and begin a golden age — a passage interpreted by early Christians as a prophecy of Jesus's birth. It invokes a 'Sicilian Muse' and instructs the child to 'begin to greet thy mother with a smile.' Other eclogue details include Corydon's love for the boy Alexis (Eclogue 2), Alphesiboeus using magic to attract Daphnis (Eclogue 8), and the amoebaean singing contest between Damoetas and Menalcas (Eclogue 3). Milton's 'Lycidas' closely imitates a poem from the Eclogues. The Eclogues are the first step on the 'Virgilian progression' — the career path from pastoral to agricultural to epic poetry that poets like Spenser tried to follow.

The Georgics are four books of hexameter agricultural poetry dedicated to Maecenas. They discuss horse breeding and beekeeping, including the bugonia theory that bees are born from the carcass of an ox. The phrase 'time flies' (fugit irreparabile tempus) comes from this work. The third book contains the Noric cattle plague. Virgil analogizes societies at war with colonies of bees.

In Dante's Divine Comedy, Virgil serves as Dante's guide through the Inferno and Purgatorio. He is a 'virtuous pagan' residing in Limbo (the first circle of Hell). He meets Dante after the poet encounters a leopard, a lion, and a she-wolf in a dark wood on Maundy Thursday night. Virgil explains 'Abandon hope, all ye who enter here' at the gates of Hell. He discusses the nature of Fortune with spendthrifts and hoarders, summons Geryon to reach Malebolge, throws dirt into Cerberus's 'rapacious gullets,' carries Dante past Big Pig and Ruby Face, negotiates with the giant Antaeus for passage, and is refused entry at the gates of Dis. He vanishes when Dante meets the flower-collecting Matilda, leaving him with Statius. In his final words, Virgil pronounces Dante 'Lord of yourself' and crowns and mitres him. Since Virgil cannot enter Paradise, he is replaced by Beatrice. Delacroix painted Dante and Virgil sharing a boat in The Barque of Dante. William Blake made seventeen wood engravings for Thornton's translation of Virgil.

In medieval legend, Virgil was revered as a sorcerer. He placed an egg under the Castel dell'Ovo in Naples to protect the city, conjured the Mergellina Tunnel, performed necromancy in the Colosseum after building it with demons, and was humiliated when an emperor's daughter left him hanging in a basket (the 'Power of Woman' genre). His name spelling changed from Vergilius to Virgilius due to a folk etymology connecting it to virga ('wand'). Gervase of Tilbury collected legends about Virgil as a sorcerer and claimed an English scholar and Roger II of Sicily tried to steal his bones.

Hermann Broch wrote The Death of Virgil, a novel structured as a string quartet with four sections named after the classical elements (Water, Fire, Earth, Air), set during Virgil's last days in Brundisium. The first incunable printed in Florence was a commentary on Virgil by Servius. Des Esseintes in Huysmans's A rebours calls Virgil 'one of the most terrible pedants ever produced by antiquity.' Virgil's tomb in Naples (in the grotta vecchia) featured a tree that supposedly died the year of Dante's death and was replanted by Petrarch.
