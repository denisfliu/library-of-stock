20th-century English poet known for 'This Be the Verse,' 'An Arundel Tomb,' 'Church Going,' and 'The Whitsun Weddings.' University of Hull librarian. Member of The Movement.
"This Be the Verse"

Larkin's most quoted poem, opening 'They fuck you up, your mum and dad.' 'They fill you with the faults they had / And add some extra, just for you.' Describes 'fools in old-style hats and coats' who were 'at one another's throats.' 'Man hands on misery to man. / It deepens like a coastal shelf.' Ends: 'Get out as early as you can, / And don't have any kids yourself.' Title from Robert Louis Stevenson's poem 'Requiem' ('This be the verse you grave for me').

"An Arundel Tomb"

Describes an earl and countess who 'lie in stone' in Chichester Cathedral. 'Their faces blurred.' 'Such plainness of the pre-Baroque hardly involves the eye.' 'Time has transfigured them into / Untruth.' A 'sharp tender shock' upon seeing the earl's hand still clasping his wife's. Concludes with the 'almost-instinct almost truth' that 'What will survive of us is love.' Inspired by Larkin's visit to Chichester with Monica Jones.

"Church Going"

Speaker visits an empty church, donates an Irish sixpence, reads 'Here endeth' much too loudly. Removes his 'cycle-clips in awkward reverence.' Describes a 'shape less recognizable each week, a purpose more obscure.' Wonders 'shall we avoid them as unlucky places?' and 'what remains when disbelief has gone?' Calls it 'A serious house on serious earth.' Considers 'dubious women' who 'make their children touch a particular stone.' Called religion a 'vast moth-eaten musical brocade.'

"The Whitsun Weddings"

Title poem of a collection. Speaker rides a 'three-quarters-empty train' on a sunlit Saturday, passing bridal parties at stations. Sees 'girls in parodies of fashion, heels and veils,' 'fathers with broad belts,' 'mothers loud and fat.' 'A sense of falling, like an arrow-shower / Sent out of sight, somewhere becoming rain.' Fifty minutes is 'just enough time to settle hats and say I nearly died.' Smells a fish market.

"High Windows"

Title poem of a collection. 'I see a couple of kids and guess he's fucking her and she's taking pills or wearing a diaphragm.' 'I know this is paradise.' "Aubade"

Opens 'I work all day, and get half-drunk at night.' 'Waking at four to soundless dark.' 'Death is no different whined at than withstood.' Death described as 'The anaesthetic from which none come round.' 'Postmen like doctors go from house to house.' Parodies the medieval French aubade form. Les Murray parodied it.

Summary of Facts

Philip Larkin is a 20th-century English poet whose giveaway is invariably 'This Be the Verse,' opening with 'They fuck you up, your mum and dad.' The poem describes how 'Man hands on misery to man' and advises 'don't have any kids yourself.' Its title comes from Stevenson's 'Requiem.'

'An Arundel Tomb' describes an earl and countess in Chichester Cathedral with the famous concluding line 'What will survive of us is love,' qualified as an 'almost-instinct almost truth.' 'Time has transfigured them into / Untruth.' 'Church Going' depicts visiting an empty church, donating an Irish sixpence, reading 'Here endeth' too loudly, and reflecting on what happens when belief dies — calling the building 'A serious house on serious earth.'

'The Whitsun Weddings' describes a 'three-quarters-empty train' ride past bridal parties, ending with 'an arrow-shower becoming rain.' 'High Windows' imagines 'everyone young going down the long slide to happiness' and ends with 'the deep blue air that shows nothing.' 'Aubade' opens 'I work all day, and get half-drunk at night' and confronts death as 'The anaesthetic from which none come round.'

Other key poems include 'Annus Mirabilis' (sexual intercourse began between 'the Chatterley ban / And the Beatles' first LP'), 'Mr Bleaney,' 'Toads,' and 'Dublinesque.' Larkin was a librarian at the University of Hull and a member of The Movement with Kingsley Amis. He turned down the Poet Laureateship (Ted Hughes accepted). His posthumous letters revealed casual racism. He was close to John Betjeman and his lover was Monica Jones.
