Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, 19th-century American poet. Best known for The Song of Hiawatha (Ojibwe epic in Kalevala meter with Minnehaha, Nokomis, "By the shores of Gitche Gumee"), Evangeline ("This is the forest primeval" in dactylic hexameter), "Paul Revere's Ride" ("one if by land, two if by sea"), and Tales of a Wayside Inn. Also wrote "A Psalm of Life," "Excelsior," "The Village Blacksmith," and "The Courtship of Miles Standish."
The Song of Hiawatha

An epic poem based on Ojibwe legends, drawn from the ethnography of Henry Schoolcraft. Written in Kalevala-esque trochaic tetrameter. The title hero is the grandson of Nokomis, who lives "By the shores of Gitche Gumee" (Lake Superior). Hiawatha fasts for seven days and wrestles Mondamin, from whose grave grows the first corn. He kills the evil magician Pearl-Feather with the help of a woodpecker. He is swallowed by the great fish Mishe-Nahma and escapes by punching it in the heart, with seagulls helping him escape. He marries Minnehaha ("Laughing Water"), the Arrow-maker's daughter, who dies hearing a waterfall. Pau-Puk-Keewis is a trickster character. Longfellow consulted George Copway's autobiography. Inspired Samuel Coleridge-Taylor's cantata trilogy.

Evangeline

An 1847 epic poem in dactylic hexameter beginning "This is the forest primeval." Set in Acadia (Grand-Pre), it tells of Evangeline's separation from her love Gabriel Lajeunesse during the Acadian expulsion. Father Felician buries Gabriel's father Benedict, who dies of a heart attack. Basil the blacksmith is revealed in Missouri wearing a "Spanish sombrero." Evangeline tends the sick in Philadelphia as a Sister of Mercy and reunites with Gabriel on his deathbed. The choice of dactylic hexameter was criticized by Whittier, who preferred prose. Longfellow's friend Jonathan Greenleaf Whittier questioned the meter.

Tales of a Wayside Inn

An 1863 collection framed as stories told by tavern patrons in Sudbury, Massachusetts. Includes "Paul Revere's Ride" (told by the Landlord), "The Saga of King Olaf," "The Birds of Killingworth," and "The Falcon of Ser Federigo" (adapted from the Decameron, told by the Student). Introduced the phrase "ships that pass in the night" in a narrative about Elizabeth Haddon.

A Psalm of Life

Concludes "Still achieving, still pursuing / Learn to labour and to wait" and urges the reader to "leave behind footprints on the sands of time."

General / Biographical

Other works include "Excelsior" (youth dies climbing Alps carrying banner with title Latin word for "ever higher"), "The Village Blacksmith" ("Under a spreading chestnut tree"), "Snow-flakes" ("the poem of the air"), "The Children's Hour," "Christmas Bells" (inspired "I Heard the Bells on Christmas Day"), "The Wreck of the Hesperus" (crashes on reef of Norman's Woe), "My Lost Youth" ("A boy's will is the wind's will"), and the novel Kavanagh (often cited for first lesbian relationship in American literature). His wife Fanny Appleton died in a house fire; he grew his beard after her death. "The Cross of Snow" was written 18 years later. First publication was a translation of Jorge Manrique; wrote travelogue Outre-Mer. Also translated Homer late in life. Robert Frost took the title A Boy's Will from "My Lost Youth." Emma Lazarus responded to his "The Jewish Cemetery at Newport."

Summary of Facts

Henry Wadsworth Longfellow is one of the most commonly clued 19th-century American poets. His most distinctive work is The Song of Hiawatha, an epic poem in Kalevala-esque trochaic tetrameter based on Ojibwe legends drawn from Henry Schoolcraft's ethnography. It opens "By the shores of Gitche Gumee" and follows Hiawatha, grandson of Nokomis, through his wrestling of Mondamin, killing of the magician Pearl-Feather, marriage to Minnehaha ("Laughing Water"), and other adventures.

Evangeline begins "This is the forest primeval" and is written in dactylic hexameter, a controversial choice that drew criticism from Whittier. It tells of Evangeline's separation from Gabriel Lajeunesse during the Acadian expulsion from Grand-Pre, her journey guided by Basil the blacksmith and Father Felician, and her reunion with the dying Gabriel in Philadelphia.

"Paul Revere's Ride," narrated by the Landlord of the title inn in Tales of a Wayside Inn, contains the famous signal "one if by land, two if by sea." Tales of a Wayside Inn also includes "The Saga of King Olaf," "The Birds of Killingworth," and "The Falcon of Ser Federigo." It introduced the phrase "ships that pass in the night."

"A Psalm of Life" contains "Life is real! Life is earnest!" and "footprints on the sands of time." "The Courtship of Miles Standish" features the love triangle of Standish, John Alden, and Priscilla Mullins, with her retort "Why don't you speak for yourself, John?" "Excelsior" describes a youth dying on the Alps with a banner reading the title Latin word. "The Village Blacksmith" is set "under a spreading chestnut tree."

Longfellow's wife Fanny Appleton died in a house fire, after which he grew his iconic beard and wrote "The Cross of Snow" eighteen years later. His novel Kavanagh is cited for possibly containing the first lesbian relationship in American literature. Emma Lazarus responded to his "Jewish Cemetery at Newport" with her own synagogue poem.
