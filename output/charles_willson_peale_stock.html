Summary of Facts

Charles Willson Peale (1741-1827) was an early American painter, best known for naming his sons after famous painters -- Raphaelle, Rembrandt, Titian (two sons with this name, including his youngest), Rubens, Linnaeus, and Franklin (frequency 4, giveaway). He also named a daughter Sophonisba after Sofonisba Anguissola.

His self-portrait The Artist in His Museum (1822) shows him lifting a red curtain to reveal a vast gallery of taxidermied objects and stuffed birds (frequency 3, giveaway), with a dead wild turkey in the bottom left corner. The Exhumation of the Mastodon (1806) depicts a pulley system going into a pit at Barber farm, with his sons Linnaeus, Franklin, and Titian and ornithologist Alexander Wilson present. He exhumed a full Mastodon skeleton in New York before bringing it to Pennsylvania. Peale painted The Exhumation after being introduced to Thomas Jefferson by Alexander von Humboldt. The Staircase Group is a trompe l'oeil showing two of his sons ascending stairs.

Peale painted many portraits of George Washington, including George Washington at Princeton (frequency 3), depicting the central figure leaning against a cannon and standing over a grounded flag. He exchanged a saddle for art lessons from John Hesselius.

His artistic dynasty is frequently clued. Son Raphaelle is considered the first professional American still-life painter. Son Rembrandt painted Court of Death and George Washington, Patriae Pater, and depicted brother Rubens wearing unusual eyeglasses next to a potted geranium. Other notable works include Rachel Weeping (a deceased child in a white bonnet as her mother wipes her tears) and a portrait of Yarrow Mamout (an allegedly 140-year-old man wearing a kufi). His slave Moses Williams became a silhouette maker.
