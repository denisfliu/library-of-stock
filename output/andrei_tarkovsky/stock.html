Soviet film director (1932–1986), known for long takes, philosophical themes, and visually poetic cinema. His major films are Solaris, Stalker, Andrei Rublev, Mirror, Nostalghia, and The Sacrifice. His father Arseny Tarkovsky was a poet whose verse appears as voiceover in several of his films. Wrote the book Sculpting in Time, advocating long takes over montage.
Solaris (1972)

Adaptation of Stanislaw Lem's novel about psychologist Kris Kelvin, who travels to a space station orbiting the ocean planet Solaris and encounters apparitions of his deceased wife Hari. A committee examines a man who claims to have seen a giant child's face in the ocean. Features close-ups of Brueghel's Hunters in the Snow. Ends with a reveal that Kelvin is on an island in the planet. Bondarchuk's daughter Natalya starred. Wikipedia

Stalker (1979)

Adaptation of the Strugatsky brothers' novel Roadside Picnic. A Stalker guides a Writer and a Professor through the dangerous 'Zone' to a wish-granting Room. A three-man team passes through a tunnel called 'the meat grinder.' Porcupine, a previous guide, committed suicide after wishing for wealth instead of resurrecting his dead brother. A character puts a crown of thorns on his head after reaching the Room. The film's score features flute and tar evoking 'space frozen in dynamic equilibrium.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Andrei Tarkovsky (1932–1986) was a Soviet film director known for slow, contemplative, visually poetic filmmaking. His father Arseny Tarkovsky was a poet whose verse appears as voiceover in both Mirror and Stalker; Arseny had a romantic relationship with Marina Tsvetaeva. Tarkovsky wrote the book Sculpting in Time, in which he theorized the 'time pressure' of long takes in opposition to Soviet Montage Theory, siding with critic André Bazin.

Solaris (1972) is his most frequently clued film. Adapted from Stanislaw Lem's novel, it follows psychologist Kris Kelvin, who travels to a space station orbiting the ocean planet Solaris and is visited by apparitions of his dead wife Hari. A committee examines a man who claims to have seen a giant child's face in the ocean. The film features close-ups of Brueghel's Hunters in the Snow and ends with the revelation that Kelvin is actually on an island in the planet. Bondarchuk's daughter Natalya starred.

Stalker (1979), adapted from the Strugatsky brothers' Roadside Picnic, follows a Stalker who guides a Writer and a Professor through the dangerous Zone (entering through a tunnel called 'the meat grinder') to a supposedly wish-granting Room. Porcupine, a former Stalker, committed suicide after wishing for wealth instead of resurrecting his dead brother. A character puts a crown of thorns on his head upon reaching the Room. The film's score features a theme for flute and tar evoking 'space frozen in a dynamic equilibrium'; it transitions from sepia to color when the men arrive at a field littered with abandoned military equipment.

Andrei Rublev (1966) is a historical epic about the 15th-century Russian icon painter, divided into eight parts. Shot in black and white, it ends with a color shot of horses grazing in a meadow. A horse falls down stairs and is impaled on a spear in one scene. Rublev is inspired to resume painting after watching a bell being cast and rung; Theophanes the Greek invites him to paint the Cathedral of The Annunciation.

Mirror (1975) is an autobiographical film from the perspective of Alexei; Arseny Tarkovsky narrates his own poetry in voiceover. It features a 'levitation' scene and close-ups of Brueghel's Hunters in the Snow while JS Bach plays; newsreel footage from the Spanish Civil War is interspersed with a scene of Ignat reading a letter; a woman sits on a well watching her family's barn burn. Nostalghia (1983), made after Tarkovsky moved to Italy, includes a nine-minute-long take of a man crossing a dry swimming pool without extinguishing a candle; it ends with a man and dog in a ruined cathedral. The Sacrifice (1986), his final film (made in Sweden), ends with 'In the beginning was the Word' spoken by the silent 'Little Man'; it opens with Alexander planting a tree with his son in a nine-and-a-half minute tracking shot; cinematography by Sven Nykvist. Ivan's Childhood (1962), his debut, follows a child scout for the Red Army and ends with the revelation that Ivan was captured and hanged by the Nazis; it influenced Sergei Parajanov's shift away from social realism.
