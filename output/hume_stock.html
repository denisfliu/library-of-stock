David Hume

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Scottish Enlightenment philosopher and empiricist. Author of A Treatise of Human Nature, An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, and Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion. Developed the copy principle, Hume's fork (relations of ideas vs. matters of fact), the problem of induction, the is-ought problem, the concept of constant conjunction, and skepticism about miracles. His moral philosophy characterizes reason as the 'slave of the passions.' Influenced Kant's 'dogmatic slumber,' David Lewis's Humean supervenience, and the Scottish Enlightenment alongside Adam Smith.
Epistemology: Copy Principle, Hume's Fork, and Causation

Hume's epistemology centers on the copy principle (all simple ideas are copies of simple impressions), Hume's fork (dividing all objects of inquiry into 'relations of ideas' like the Pythagorean theorem and 'matters of fact' like the sun rising tomorrow), and his theory of causation as 'constant conjunction' rather than necessary connection. The problem of induction argues that the uniformity of nature cannot be proven; Thomas Reid and Nelson Goodman (grue/bleen) responded. The 'missing shade of blue' is a famous counterexample to the copy principle. Mary Shepherd critiqued his theory of causation. Kant credited Hume with waking him from his 'dogmatic slumber.'

Moral Philosophy: Is-Ought Problem and Sentimentalism

Hume's moral philosophy argues that moral distinctions come from sentiment, not reason. 'Reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions.' The is-ought problem (Hume's guillotine) argues that normative claims cannot be derived from factual propositions — 'every system of morality' establishes its own God. John Searle's response considers promising $5 to derive ought from is. The 'sensible knave' exploits moral rules without being caught. Hume denounced 'monkish virtues' like celibacy and fasting. He distinguished artificial (socially constructed) from natural virtues. Simon Blackburn interprets Hume as a quasi-realist; Annette Baier called him the 'woman's moral theorist.'

Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion

Hume's posthumously published dialogues feature Cleanthes (experimental theist defending the design argument), Philo (skeptic, usually taken as Hume's voice), and Demea. Philo refutes the design argument by proposing alternative analogies — the universe could be compared to vegetation, generation, or an 'infinitely large spider.' The work discusses the problem of evil (architect metaphor: house subjects residents to noise and darkness) and argues against a 'future state' of life.

Aesthetics and Essays

Hume's essay 'Of the Standard of Taste' argues that beauty is subjective but identifies the 'joint verdict' of 'true judges/critics' as the standard. It uses a Don Quixote story about wine tasting (leather vs. iron) to illustrate 'delicacy of imagination.' Other Four Dissertations include 'Of Tragedy' (the paradox of tragedy — we gain pleasure from well-written tragedy) and 'The Natural History of Religion.' Hume also identified the 'paradox of tragedy.'

General / Biographical

Summary of Facts

David Hume (1711-1776) is the most heavily clued philosopher of the British empiricist tradition, with an extraordinarily deep and varied question set spanning epistemology, ethics, philosophy of religion, and aesthetics.

Hume's epistemology centers on the copy principle (simple ideas copy simple impressions), Hume's fork (relations of ideas vs. matters of fact), and causation as 'constant conjunction' rather than necessary connection (illustrated by billiard balls). The 'missing shade of blue' is a counterexample to the copy principle. The problem of induction argues that the uniformity of nature cannot be proven — making inferences is 'custom or habit,' not reason. Nelson Goodman's grue/bleen extends this. Kant credited Hume with waking him from his 'dogmatic slumber.' David Lewis's 'Humean supervenience' holds the world is 'a vast mosaic of local matters of particular fact.'

Hume's moral philosophy argues that moral distinctions come from sentiment, not reason — 'reason is the slave of the passions.' The is-ought problem (Hume's guillotine) says normative claims cannot be derived from factual ones. He preferred the destruction of the whole world to pricking his finger, denounced 'monkish virtues,' and distinguished artificial from natural virtues. Simon Blackburn interprets him as a quasi-realist; Annette Baier called him the 'woman's moral theorist.'

Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion features Cleanthes (design argument), Philo (skeptic/Hume), and Demea. Philo refutes the design argument with alternative analogies (vegetation, generation, infinite spider). The 'Of Miracles' chapter argues evidence for natural law always outweighs supernatural claims.

Hume's aesthetics include 'Of the Standard of Taste' (beauty is subjective; the Don Quixote wine story illustrates 'delicacy of imagination') and the 'paradox of tragedy.' He was a close friend of Adam Smith and a figure of the Scottish Enlightenment.
