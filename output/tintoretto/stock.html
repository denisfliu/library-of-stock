Summary of Facts

Tintoretto (Jacopo Robusti) was a Venetian Mannerist painter nicknamed 'Il Furioso' for his impetuous painting style, and also called 'little dyer' because his father was a silk dyer. He was briefly a student of Titian but was reportedly thrown out of his studio after just three days. Jean-Paul Sartre called him 'the first film director,' and Pierre-Jean Mariette noted a joke about his three pencils (gold, silver, and iron) reflecting his inconsistency.

His most frequently clued work is The Last Supper (1592-94) at San Giorgio Maggiore, famous for its diagonal table composition, lit by two sources: Christ's bright halo and a hanging oil lamp surrounded by ghostly translucent angels. A cat sniffs inside a basket in the foreground, and a woman reaches into a basket rather than watching the meal. It was his final work, with son Domenico helping with drapery.

The Miracle of the Slave (1548), painted for the Scuola Grande di San Marco, depicts Saint Mark swooping down from the sky (sometimes shown upside-down) to save a nude man from execution, with broken tools (hammer, axe) on the ground and a muscular red-clad figure reminiscent of Michelangelo's ignudi.

Il Paradiso (c. 1588-92), in the Doge's Palace, is often called the largest painting on canvas, depicting over 500 figures with Mary interceding before Christ in the Empyrean. His son Domenico helped complete it. The Origin of the Milky Way (c. 1575) shows stars shooting from Hera's breast as infant Hercules is pulled away, with an eagle carrying a crab and two peacocks in the lower right.

His St. Mark cycle includes Finding the Body of St. Mark (saint above his own pale corpse in an Alexandrian crypt) and other scenes. Other works include Miracle of the Bronze Serpent (for Scuola di San Rocco), Saint George and the Dragon (unusually centering on the corpse), The Deliverance of Arsinoe, and Bacchus and Ariadne with Venus (referencing Venice's wedding to the sea). His family workshop included daughter Marietta (whose work was indistinguishable from his) and sons Marco and Domenico. Multiple 19th-century painters depicted Tintoretto painting his dead daughter.
