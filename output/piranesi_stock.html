Summary of Facts

Giovanni Battista Piranesi was an Italian artist and architect, most famous for his Carceri d'invenzione (Imaginary Prisons), a series of 16 etchings depicting fantastical prisons with elaborate staircases, catapults, cables, and pulleys in impossible architectural compositions. The series was created by brushing sulfuric acid onto plates and includes individual works like The Round Tower, The Pier with a Lamp, and Smoking Fire. Thomas De Quincey described them in Confessions of an English Opium Eater, recounting how Coleridge spoke of these dream-like works. Aldous Huxley described their 'colossal pointlessness.' The works are considered 'paper architecture' or visionary architecture.

Piranesi's Vedute di Roma were extremely popular among Grand Tour visitors, depicting Roman ruins like the Pyramid of Cestius, the Colosseum, the Pantheon, and Villa Adriana at Tivoli. He distorted perspective to make ruins look bigger and defended the merits of Roman art against Pierre-Jean Mariette.

He restored ancient vases including the Boyd and Warwick Vases from fragments, earning the nickname 'Sir Pastiche.' The British Museum owns a 2.7-meter-tall krater he restored. He argued that Etruscans were the sole founders of Rome in Della magnificenza. He designed Santa Maria del Priorato (financed by Clement XIII) and wrote Diverse maniere on decorating chimneypieces.

Susanna Clarke named her 2020 novel Piranesi after him; the novel is set in an infinite House with halls, statues, and flooding. Le Corbusier called him 'horrible, ugly, and idiotic' but Piranesian space influenced his work. Piranesi inspired Boris Balinsky's poster for the film Metropolis.
