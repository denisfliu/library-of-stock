[B1] 2024 IQBT Undergraduate Championship (2024) | Diff: 8 | Literature
     Leadin: Much of the earliest surviving literature from the British Isles is in this language or in Latin. For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: Old English [or Anglo-Saxon; prompt on English]):
    Name this language used by the poets Caedmon ("KAD-mun") and Cynewulf ("KOO-nay-wolf"), which was also used to write the epic Beowulf.
  Part 2 (ans: hymns [accept "Caedmon's Hymn"; accept Olney Hymns]):
    Caedmon is known for an extant nine-line poem of this type written to "honour / heaven- kingdom's Ward." The later authors John Newton and William Cowper ("Cooper") wrote a collection of these poems named for the town of Olney.
  Part 3 (ans: Bede [or Saint Bede; or the Venerable Bede]):
    This writer is the namesake of an Old English "Death Song," which states "before the unavoidable journey," no one is wiser than he who ponders "good and evil within his soul."

[B2] 2017 WAO II (2017) | Diff: 8 | Literature
     Leadin: After this man left a banquet because he "did not know how to sing" and went to sleep, a figure in his dream told him "you can sing for me . . . Sing me creation." For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: Caedmon [accept "Caedmon's Hymn"]):
    Name this man, who sang a namesake "Hymn" about "the work of the Glory-Father," which consists of creating "for the children of earth / heaven as a roof," and "then middle-earth."
  Part 2 (ans: Bede):
    "Caedmon's Hymn," the oldest surviving Old English poem, survived through manuscripts by this author. This "Venerable" monk also wrote the Ecclesiastical History of the English People.
  Part 3 (ans: riddles):
    Aldhelm, a contemporary of Caedmon and Bede, wrote a collection of these things called the Aenigmata. The "Leiden" one of these works, which is another of the earliest extant Old English poems, begins "The wet ground, incredibly cold, first produced me from its innards."

[B3] 2017 Oxford Open (2017) | Diff: 8 | Literature
     Leadin: Objects in this text include the not-very-useful sword Hrunting, the somewhat-more-useful Nægling, and the mysterious "glof" belonging to the first of its three monstrous protagonists. For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: Beowulf):
    Name this Old English epic, in which the title warrior saves Hrothgar from the depredations of Grendel.
  Part 2 (ans: Nowell Codex [or Nowell manuscript]):
    The only surviving text of Beowulf is in this manuscript, the second part of Cotton Vitellius A.xv. ["A-fifteen"]. It has been suggested that heavy wear on one page of this manuscript, held at the British Library, is the result of readers' interest in the dragon.
  Part 3 (ans: hymn [accept "Caedmon's hymn"]):
    There may be some connection between a song sung in Hrothgar's hall and the Old English text of this kind often named for Caedmon, which begins "Now we must honour the guardian of heaven."

[B4] 2012 Michigan KABO (2012) | Diff: 8 | Literature
     Leadin: This work claims that we must "honour the guardian of heaven" and "the might of the architect." For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: Caedmon's Hymn):
    Name this work, split into eighteen half lines, which exalts the lord for creating middle earth as the "lands for men" and for making "heaven as a roof" for the "children of men."
  Part 2 (ans: Beowulf):
    Another early work of British literature is this epic poem, contained in the Nowell Codex, in which the titular hero slays Grendel and Grendel's Mother but dies in battle with a dragon.
  Part 3 (ans: Maldon [or "Battle of Maldon"]):
    Byrhtnoth refuses to pay the Vikings tribute and fails to corner them on the peninsula on which they have landed in a poem about this battle. Only slightly over 300 lines of the poem were preserved.
