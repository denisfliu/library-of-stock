French Enlightenment philosopher who co-edited the Encyclopedie with D'Alembert, wrote Rameau's Nephew and Jacques the Fatalist, and explored materialism in D'Alembert's Dream. He introduced the concept of the 'fourth wall' and was imprisoned for his atheism after the Letter on the Blind.
The Encyclopedie

Diderot's most famous project was co-editing the Encyclopedie with Jean Le Rond D'Alembert. It aimed to collect all human knowledge, was influenced by Ephraim Chambers' earlier work, and featured sections on every art and science. The Preliminary Discourse by D'Alembert served as its introduction, with a taxonomic chart dividing human knowledge into Memory, Reason, and Imagination. The most prolific contributor was Louis de Jaucourt, who wrote 17,266 articles (averaging eight per day) and passionately denounced slavery despite his nickname 'the slave of the Encyclopedia.'

D'Alembert's Dream

D'Alembert's Dream is a dialogue exploring materialist explanations for the unity of multicellular organisms. It compares a synchronized hive of bees to a multi-headed animal while considering how living beings emerge from networks of tiny cells. The last dialogue features a woman who runs a salon asking Theophile Bordeu whether bestiality should be legal. The work mainly consists of discussions between a doctor and a young woman about a man's transcribed statements.

Summary of Facts

Denis Diderot appears in quizbowl primarily as the co-editor of the Encyclopedie with Jean Le Rond D'Alembert. The Encyclopedie aimed to collect all human knowledge, was influenced by Ephraim Chambers' earlier work, and is introduced by D'Alembert's Preliminary Discourse, which features a chart dividing human knowledge into Memory, Reason, and Imagination. The most prolific contributor was Louis de Jaucourt (17,266 articles, averaging eight per day), who passionately denounced slavery. Other contributors included Baron d'Holbach and Montesquieu.

Diderot's philosophical dialogue D'Alembert's Dream explores materialist explanations for multicellular organisms, comparing a synchronized hive of bees to a multi-headed animal. Its last dialogue features a salon hostess asking Theophile Bordeu about the legality of bestiality.

Other common identifiers include Rameau's Nephew (a dialogue set in the Cafe de la Regence discussing Bossuet's morality), Jacques the Fatalist, and the Letter on the Blind (1749), for which he was imprisoned for atheism. He introduced the concept of the 'fourth wall,' may have contributed to d'Holbach's System of Nature, and argued for a solution to Molyneux's problem based on the eye's ability to learn after surgery. He also wrote a work featuring the Tahitian Orou discussing the 'all-knowing craftsman' with a chaplain.
