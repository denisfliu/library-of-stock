British high-tech architect (b. 1935), Baron Foster of Thames Bank. Key works: the Gherkin (30 St Mary Axe, London), Reichstag reconstruction (glass dome), HSBC Main Building (Hong Kong), Hearst Tower (Manhattan), Millau Viaduct (world's tallest bridge), Apple Park ('the Spaceship,' Cupertino). Founded Team 4 with Richard Rogers. Known for diagrid facades, glass domes, and sustainable design.

General / Biographical

Norman Robert Foster (b. 1935), Baron Foster of Thames Bank. Pioneer of high-tech architecture in Britain. Met Richard Rogers at Yale; together with their future spouses Su Brumwell and Wendy Cheesman, formed Team 4 (designed the Reliance Controls factory). Later founded Foster + Partners (Britain's largest architectural firm). Works in Astana, Kazakhstan: Palace of Peace and Reconciliation (pyramid with 62x62m base, stained glass apex, four 'hands of peace,' inspired by a congress of world religions) and Khan Shatyr Entertainment Center (tent-shaped, inspired by yurts). London City Hall: bulbous 'Glass Gonad' / 'glass testicle' shape to reduce direct sunlight exposure. Masdar City (UAE): planned zero-carbon, zero-waste city. Hong Kong International Airport. Wembley Stadium. Torre de Collserola (Barcelona): hollow concrete shaft, guy-wire-supported. Won Pritzker Prize 1999.

Summary of Facts

Norman Robert Foster (b. 1935), Baron Foster of Thames Bank, is a British architect and one of the leading figures of high-tech architecture. He met Richard Rogers at Yale University; together with their future spouses Su Brumwell and Wendy Cheesman, they formed the firm Team 4, which designed the Reliance Controls factory. Foster later founded Foster + Partners, Britain's largest architectural firm.

Foster's most recognizable London building is 30 St Mary Axe, known as 'the Gherkin' for its ovoid/cucumber shape. Swiss Re is the primary occupant. The building, with its diagrid structural facade, was constructed on the ruins of the Baltic Exchange. Foster's Hearst Tower in Manhattan incorporates a 1928 cast-stone base by Joseph Urban and features a diagrid facade with concave corner windows known as 'bird's mouths.'

Following German reunification, Foster reconstructed the Reichstag in Berlin, adding a glass dome. Inside the dome, a mirror-paneled cone surrounded by two helical ramps reflects and automatically adjusts sunlight downward. Christo and Jean-Claude wrapped the building in polypropylene fabric before the reconstruction.

Foster's HSBC Main Building in Hong Kong is a standard example of High-tech Modernism. Its exterior — which displays its steel skeleton — is supported by eight steel masts that free up the floor space. 'Coathanger' trusses suspend groups of floors the architect called 'social villages.' Computer-controlled 'sun scoops' track and reflect sunlight into the atrium. Two cannon-shaped maintenance cranes on the roof are aimed at the bad feng shui from the nearby I.M. Pei Bank of China Tower. A pair of bronze lions at the main entry — replicas of those outside HSBC Shanghai — are touched for good luck.

Foster designed the Millau Viaduct in France with engineer Michel Virlogeux: the world's tallest bridge, spanning the Tarn River (a tributary of the Garonne). In Cupertino, California, Foster designed the circular Apple Park ('the Spaceship') with Apple's chief designer Jony Ive, replacing Apple's 1 Infinite Loop campus and modeled on the quad at Stanford.

In Astana, Kazakhstan, Foster built the Palace of Peace and Reconciliation — a pyramid with a 62-by-62-meter base, a stained-glass apex, and four 'hands of peace,' inspired by a 2003 Congress of Leaders of World and Traditional Religions — and the Khan Shatyr Entertainment Center (tent-shaped, inspired by yurts, home to the world's largest marquee). London City Hall was built in a bulbous 'Glass Gonad' shape to minimize direct sunlight exposure. Masdar City in the UAE is his planned zero-carbon, zero-waste arcological city. Foster won the Pritzker Architecture Prize in 1999.
