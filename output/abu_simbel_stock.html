UNESCO Relocation

In preparation for flooding from the Aswan High Dam and the creation of Lake Nasser, the entire temple complex was relocated to higher ground in 1968, with the effort headed by UNESCO. This project motivated the International Campaign to Save the Monuments of Nubia and led to the designation of the first World Heritage Sites in 1972. Spain received the Temple of Debod as thanks for their participation.

Summary of Facts

Abu Simbel is a twin rock-cut temple complex in southern Egypt, built by the pharaoh Ramesses II (Ramesses the Great) during the New Kingdom. The site is most commonly identified by two features: four colossal seated statues of Ramesses flanking the entrance to the Great Temple (one of which has its entire upper body missing), and the relocation of the entire complex by UNESCO in 1968 to save it from flooding by Lake Nasser.

The Great Temple was constructed to commemorate Ramesses II's victory at the Battle of Kadesh. Its inner sanctuary is designed so that sunlight illuminates the back wall only twice a year, in February and October, while the fourth rock-cut figure—a sculpture of Ptah—remains in the dark year-round. The facade of the temple is topped by a row of twenty-two baboons with their hands raised. The smaller temple is dedicated to Ramesses's queen Nefertari, and its ceiling was decorated with thousands of five-pointed stars against a deep blue background.

The complex was originally covered by sand and rediscovered by Jean-Louis Burckhardt in 1813. Giovanni Battista Belzoni—a former Italian circus strongman turned archaeologist—cleared and blasted the sand from the entrance in 1817. The French scholar Champollion made a key breakthrough in deciphering Egyptian hieroglyphs while reviewing texts from this site.

In the 1960s, the construction of the Aswan High Dam (funded by the Soviet Union) threatened to submerge the complex beneath Lake Nasser. UNESCO led an international effort to relocate the temples to higher ground, completing the operation in 1968. This campaign motivated the International Campaign to Save the Monuments of Nubia and led to UNESCO designating the first World Heritage Sites in 1972. As thanks for their participation in the preservation effort, Spain was gifted the Temple of Debod, which now stands in Madrid.
