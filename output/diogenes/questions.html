Tossups

T1 2024 NASAT (2024) Diff 8 · Philosophy
In addition to himself, the character of Socrates in The Clouds parodies the philosophical views of a philosopher with this name, whom Theophrastus called the last of the physiologoi. Aristotle's History of Animals preserves a philosopher with this name's fragmentary writings on human blood vessels. Like Anaximenes, a philosopher with this name believed air was the fundamental substance but added it was intelligent. This name was shared by a philosopher from Apollonia and a third-century biographer who wrote Lives and Opinions of the Eminent Philosophers, nicknamed Laërtius. A philosopher with this name refuted Plato's definition of man as a "featherless biped" by plucking a chicken. For 10 points, a philosopher with what name founded Cynicism?
ANSWER: Diogenes [or Diogenes of Apollonia; or Diogenes Laërtius; or Diogenes of Sinope]
T2 2023 TELEOLOGY (2023) Diff 10 · Philosophy
A Nikolas Pappas article for The Stone argues though it appears that the man who created this thing “got the better of” another group, really that group simply used this thing to “fiddle with” and “improve” their definitions, benefiting both parties. In a Friedrich Georg Weitsch painting, a man in red with his back to the viewer gestures towards this thing; that painting of this thing appears on the cover of the first volume of the book version of Peter Adamson’s History of Philosophy Without Any Gaps series. In response to encountering this thing, one man supposedly revised a definition originally published in Statesman to include the term “with broad flat (*) nails.” The man who apocryphally created this thing entered The Academy, gestured at it, and declared “Behold, Plato’s man!” For 10 points, after Plato defined man as a “featherless biped” Diogenes the Cynic plucked what sort of animal to disprove him?
ANSWER: the plucked chicken Diogenes the Cynic supposedly brought to The Academy to own Plato

B2 2025 ACF Nationals (2025) Diff 9 · Philosophy
Answer the following about Epicurus’s Epistle to Herodotus, for 10 points each.
[10] The Epistle, like all of Epicurus’s fully extant works, is preserved in Book X of the Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers by this 3rd-century biographer of Greek philosophers.
ANSWER: Diogenes Laërtius (“die-AW-juh-neez lay-URR-shee-uss”) [prompt on Diogenes]
[10] In the Epistle, Epicurus reaffirms his belief that all bodies are composed of these indivisible components, a belief he got from Nausiphanes (“naw-SIFF-uh-neez”), who learned it from Democritus.
ANSWER: atoms [or atomon]
[10] Original Greek term required. The Epistle also contains Epicurus’s three criteria of truth: aisthēsis (“ess-THEE-siss”), pathē (“PATH-ee”), and these concepts, usually translated as “preconceptions.” Epicureans and Stoics both believed that these concepts were required for rational thought and the ability to connect names with objects.
ANSWER: prolēpseis [or prolēpsis]
