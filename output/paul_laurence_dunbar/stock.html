Sympathy

Dunbar's most famous poem, containing the refrain 'I know why the caged bird sings!' The caged bird has 'old, old scars' that 'pulse again with a keener sting,' beats its wings 'Till its blood is red on the cruel bars,' and sends up 'a prayer... from his heart's deep core' and 'a plea, that upward to Heaven he flings.' The song 'is not a carol of joy or glee.' The poem describes 'when the sun is bright on the upland slopes.' Dunbar's job at the Library of Congress informed the poem, as Alice Dunbar Nelson noted the iron grates of the book stacks inspired it. The poem's refrain provided the title of Maya Angelou's memoir I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings.

We Wear the Mask

A poem about the mask that 'grins and lies' and 'hides our cheeks and shades our eyes.' The speakers 'sing, but oh the clay is vile / Beneath our feet, and long the mile / But let the world dream otherwise.' The poem anticipates W. E. B. Du Bois's concept of double consciousness. It describes 'the debt we pay to human guile' and asks 'Why should the world be overwise, / In counting all our tears and sighs?'

Majors and Minors / Dialect Poetry

Dunbar's second collection divided poems into standard English 'majors' and dialect 'minors.' William Dean Howells preferred and popularized the dialect poems in a review. 'When Malindy Sings' tells 'Miss Lucy' to 'quit dat noise' as Malindy's singing 'makes its way in glory / To de very gates of God!' His first collection was Oak and Ivy, which he took out a loan to publish. Critics debated whether the dialect or standard poems were his more authentic voice.

Ships That Pass in the Night

A poem in which the speaker looks 'far out into the pregnant night' and hears 'a solemn booming gun.' His voice 'falls dead a foot from his own lips' as 'the ship I seek is passing, passing.' The poem echoes Longfellow's use of the phrase from Tales of a Wayside Inn.

Other Poems

'Ode to Ethiopia' declares 'Thy name is writ on Glory's scroll / In characters of fire' and 'Be proud, my race' -- its lines were used in William Grant Still's Afro-American Symphony. 'The Haunted Oak' is narrated by a tree haunted by 'the curse of a guiltless man' who was lynched. 'Douglass' pays homage to Frederick Douglass. 'Not They Who Soar' declares 'they who plod / Their rugged way, unhelped, to God / are heroes.' Alice Dunbar-Nelson was his wife, author of Violets and Other Tales.

General / Biographical

Dunbar was a high school friend of Orville Wright. He worked at the Library of Congress. James Whitcomb Riley championed him. He was championed by William Dean Howells. His collection Lyrics of Lowly Life is another major work. Robert Hayden included a homage to Dunbar in his collection American Journal. He is sometimes described as a Harlem Renaissance poet, though he predated the movement (died 1906).

Summary of Facts

Paul Laurence Dunbar is an African-American poet of the late 19th and early 20th century, most famous for 'Sympathy' with its refrain 'I know why the caged bird sings!' The caged bird has 'old, old scars' that 'pulse again with a keener sting,' beats its wings until 'its blood is red on the cruel bars,' and sends up 'a prayer from his heart's deep core.' This line provided the title for Maya Angelou's memoir I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings.

'We Wear the Mask' describes the mask that 'grins and lies' and 'hides our cheeks and shades our eyes,' with speakers who 'sing, but oh the clay is vile / Beneath our feet' and 'let the world dream otherwise.' The poem anticipated W. E. B. Du Bois's concept of double consciousness.

Dunbar's collection Majors and Minors divided poems into standard English 'majors' and dialect 'minors.' William Dean Howells preferred and popularized the dialect poems, though some critics argue the 'majors' represent Dunbar's more authentic voice. 'When Malindy Sings' tells 'Miss Lucy' to quit as Malindy's singing reaches 'de very gates of God.' 'Ships That Pass in the Night' describes looking into 'the pregnant night.'

'Ode to Ethiopia' declares 'Thy name is writ on Glory's scroll / In characters of fire' and was used in William Grant Still's Afro-American Symphony. 'The Haunted Oak' features a tree haunted by a lynching victim. Dunbar was a high school friend of Orville Wright, worked at the Library of Congress, and was championed by James Whitcomb Riley. His wife Alice Dunbar-Nelson was a poet who wrote Violets and Other Tales.
