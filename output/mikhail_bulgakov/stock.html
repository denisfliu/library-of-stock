Russian/Soviet author, best known for The Master and Margarita and Heart of a Dog. Also wrote The White Guard (adapted into the play The Days of the Turbins, which Stalin loved), The Fatal Eggs, A Young Doctor's Notebook, and The Cabal of Hypocrites. Trained as a doctor; faced heavy Soviet censorship throughout his career. The Master and Margarita was published posthumously, 26 years after his death.
The Master and Margarita

Bulgakov's most famous novel, published posthumously. Satan, disguised as Professor Woland, visits Moscow with his retinue (including the demonic cat Behemoth) and wreaks havoc. A parallel narrative follows Pontius Pilate's encounter with Yeshua ha-Notsri. The Master, an author who wrote a novel about Pilate, is reunited with his lover Margarita. Contains the famous line "manuscripts don't burn." Wikipedia

The White Guard / The Days of the Turbins

Summary of Facts

Mikhail Bulgakov was a Russian/Soviet author best known for his novel The Master and Margarita, which was published posthumously — twenty-six years after his death — due to heavy Soviet censorship. In that novel, Satan arrives in Moscow disguised as Professor Woland, accompanied by a retinue that includes the vodka-loving demonic cat Behemoth. Woland predicts the decapitation of Berlioz, the chairman of the literary organization MASSOLIT, by a streetcar. The poet Ivan 'Homeless' is sent to a lunatic asylum where he meets the Master, an author who has written a novel about Pontius Pilate and Yeshua ha-Notsri. The Master's lover Margarita flies naked over Moscow and uses a wish to free Frieda from her curse. Woland holds a midnight Walpurgis Night ball in Apartment 50 attended by denizens of hell. The famous line 'manuscripts don't burn' is spoken by Woland after the Master destroys his manuscript, and became a symbol of resistance to Soviet censorship. The Pilate chapters depict the Roman governor suffering continual headaches, served by the secret policeman Aphranius and the centurion Mark the Rat-Killer.

Bulgakov's other major work is Heart of a Dog, a satire on Communist theories of human perfectibility. In this novella, Dr. Philip Philippovich Preobrazhensky (whose name derives from the word 'transformation' and who is possibly based on the surgeon Serge Voronoff) transplants a human pituitary gland and testicles into a stray dog named Sharik, turning him into the human Polygraph Polygraphovich Sharikov. Sharikov gets a job catching and strangling stray cats and terrorizes Preobrazhensky's household. Dr. Bormenthal, Preobrazhensky's assistant, notes that the pituitary gives 'not rejuvenation but total humanization.' The novel also depicts Soviet apartment life during the New Economic Policy era.

The Fatal Eggs is a satirical science fiction novella in which Professor Vladimir Persikov discovers a red ray that causes rapid growth in organisms. Alexander Rokk accidentally uses the ray to create giant snakes, ostriches, and crocodiles instead of chickens during a poultry disease crisis. The plague of giant creatures attacking Moscow is stopped only by a mysterious August freeze.

The White Guard, Bulgakov's literary debut, follows the Turbin family — brothers Alexei and Nikolka, and sister Elena — during the Ukrainian Civil War, dealing with the aftermath of their mother's death amid political upheaval. It was adapted into the play The Days of the Turbins, which Stalin loved so much that he reportedly saw it 15-20 times. Stalin's affection for the play may have protected Bulgakov from persecution, and possibly even led to the reintroduction of Christmas trees in Russia in the 1930s, as the play includes a scene of decorating one.

Bulgakov also wrote The Cabal of Hypocrites, a play fictionalizing the life of Moliere that analogized Stalin to Louis XIV. The play's disastrous four years of rehearsals caused a rift between Bulgakov and Konstantin Stanislavski, co-founder of the Moscow Arts Theatre, whom Bulgakov later satirized in the novel Black Snow (also known as Theatrical Novel). Other works include the short story 'Diaboliad,' the play Bliss (set in the year 2222 with time travel), the play Adam and Eve (featuring Efrossimov's camera that protects people from gas attacks), and A Young Doctor's Notebook, a collection of stories inspired by Bulgakov's own medical experiences that includes 'Morphine.' Bulgakov was trained as a doctor, and his medical practice informed much of his fiction. Anna Akhmatova wrote an elegy in his memory. John Hodge's 2012 Olivier Award-winning play Collaborators depicts Bulgakov's fraught relationship with Stalin.
