Indian-born British-American novelist known for magical realism. Most famous for Midnight's Children (won the Booker and two 'Best of the Booker' awards) and The Satanic Verses (which prompted a fatwa from Ayatollah Khomeini in 1989). Frequently writes about Indian independence, partition, postcolonial identity, and the migrant experience. His essay collection Imaginary Homelands is a key text on postcolonial literature. Wrote the memoir Joseph Anton about his decade in hiding under that pseudonym (derived from Conrad + Chekhov). Stabbed in 2022 at a Chautauqua lecture; wrote the memoir Knife about the attack.
Midnight's Children

Rushdie's most famous novel, winner of the Booker Prize and two 'Best of the Booker' awards. Follows Saleem Sinai, born at the exact moment of India's independence, who has a large nose granting him telepathic powers. He connects with other 'midnight's children' — babies born with supernatural abilities during that hour. Features Mary Pereira swapping Saleem and Shiva at birth, the Braganza pickle factory, Parvati-the-Witch, 'the Widow' (Indira Gandhi), and the concept of 'chutnification' blending fiction and history. The Satanic Verses

Rushdie's most controversial novel, which prompted Ayatollah Khomeini to issue a fatwa calling for Rushdie's death in 1989. The novel opens with Saladin Chamcha and Gibreel Farishta miraculously surviving a fall from a hijacked plane over the English Channel, transforming into demonic and angelic figures respectively. Includes dream sequences about a prophet called 'the Messenger' (Mahound) and Ayesha leading pilgrims into the Arabian Sea. Gibreel murders Sisodia and his lover Allie Cone. Hal Valance calls Thatcher 'Mrs. Torture.' Wikipedia

The Fatwa / Joseph Anton (Memoir)

In 1989, Ayatollah Khomeini issued a fatwa calling for Rushdie's death over The Satanic Verses. Rushdie lived in hiding for a decade under the pseudonym 'Joseph Anton' (from the first names of Conrad and Chekhov). His memoir Joseph Anton describes this period, his marriages to Marianne Wiggins and Padma Lakshmi ('The Illusion'), and the aftermath. Translators were attacked: Hitoshi Igarashi (Japanese translator) was killed in 1991; Ettore Capriolo and William Nygaard were injured. Rushdie appeared at a U2 Wembley concert in 1993, telling Bono 'real devils don't wear horns.' Cat Stevens controversially said he'd harm Rushdie. Jimmy Carter wrote a NYT op-ed denouncing the fatwa but urging understanding. Rushdie wrote an apologetic essay 'In Good Faith' which he later called 'the biggest mistake of my life.' Wikipedia

Imaginary Homelands (Essay Collection)

Rushdie's major essay collection on postcolonial literature and the migrant experience. Contains 'Commonwealth Literature Does Not Exist,' which argues against the 'bogy of Authenticity' and attacks the idea of a unified 'commonwealth school of literature.' Also contains 'Outside the Whale,' which calls for 'rowdyism' against the quietism of Orwell's 'Inside the Whale.' The title essay discusses straddling two cultures and 'the migrant theory,' and declares 'we are translated men.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Salman Rushdie (born 1947) is an Indian-born British-American novelist who is one of the most frequently asked-about authors in quizbowl. His literary career and personal life are both heavily clued, with the fatwa issued by Ayatollah Khomeini in 1989 being one of the most recognizable giveaways in the game.

His most important work for quizbowl purposes is Midnight's Children, which follows Saleem Sinai, born at the exact moment of India's independence, whose large nose grants him telepathic abilities. He uses these powers to connect with other 'midnight's children' — babies born in that hour who all possess supernatural gifts. The novel features the nurse Mary Pereira swapping Saleem with Shiva of the Knees at birth, Parvati-the-Witch who helps the protagonist, the Braganza pickle factory, and 'the Widow' as a representation of Indira Gandhi (who actually sued Rushdie over the novel). The novel coined the term 'chutnification' for the blending of fiction and history. It won the Booker Prize and two 'Best of the Booker' awards, making Rushdie the first Indian author to win the Man Booker.

The Satanic Verses is his most controversial work. It opens with Saladin Chamcha (a London voice-actor who becomes a devil analogue) and Gibreel Farishta (an actor inspired by Amitabh Bachchan who becomes an angel figure) surviving a fall from a hijacked plane over the English Channel. The novel features dream sequences about the prophet Mahound ('the Messenger') and about Ayesha leading pilgrims into the Arabian Sea where they drown. Gibreel murders Sisodia and his lover Allie Cone. Hal Valance refers to Margaret Thatcher as 'Mrs. Torture.' The poet Baal writes 'assassination poems' and serves the Grandee of Jahilia.

The fatwa was issued on February 14, 1989 by Ayatollah Khomeini. Rushdie lived in hiding for a decade under the pseudonym 'Joseph Anton,' derived from the first names of Conrad and Chekhov. His memoir Joseph Anton describes this period, including his marriages to Marianne Wiggins (his second wife, whose marriage disintegrated during the fatwa) and Padma Lakshmi (referred to as 'The Illusion'). The fatwa's violence extended to translators: Japanese translator Hitoshi Igarashi was killed in 1991, and Ettore Capriolo and William Nygaard were injured. Rushdie made a surprise appearance at a U2 Wembley concert in August 1993, telling Bono 'real devils don't wear horns.' Cat Stevens controversially threatened him. Jimmy Carter wrote a NYT op-ed denouncing the fatwa but urging understanding. Rushdie wrote an apologetic essay 'In Good Faith' that he later called 'the biggest mistake of my life.' The fatwa also provoked the Sivas Massacre (mob burned a hotel killing 35 after failing to turn over translator Aziz Nesin), severed UK-Iran ties, and led to the bombing of Collets and Dillons bookshops on Charing Cross Road and the kidnapping of Jackie Mann. In 2022, Rushdie was stabbed by Hadi Matar at a Chautauqua lecture; he wrote about this in the memoir Knife (2024).

His essay collection Imaginary Homelands contains key postcolonial essays: 'Commonwealth Literature Does Not Exist' attacks the 'bogy of Authenticity' and argues this 'school' is the 'oddest of beasts' whose members deny belonging; 'Outside the Whale' calls for 'rowdyism' against Orwell's quietism in 'Inside the Whale'; the title essay discusses straddling two cultures and 'the migrant theory,' declaring 'we are translated men.' He also wrote 'The Empire Writes Back with a Vengeance' about post-colonial authors writing back against colonialism.

Haroun and the Sea of Stories is a children's novel where Haroun Khalifa meets Butt the Hoopoe (an AI bird) and Iff the Water Genie and helps the Guppee army defeat Khattam-Shud (a Khomeini analogue) who tries to poison the Sea of Stories. Set on the moon Kahani, divided into Gup and silent Chup. Inspired by Somadeva's Kathasaritsagara. His brother Luka stars in the sequel Luka and the Fire of Life, where the protagonist encounters a phantasm called 'Nobodaddy,' dies at the hands of the Old Man of the River, and realizes the world is like a video game.

Shame is a political satire set in an allegorical Pakistan with politicians Iskander Harappa and General Raza Hyder, and the fictional town of Q. Grimus, his first novel, follows the Axona Indian Flapping Eagle who searches for his sister Bird-Dog for 777 years; it was inspired by Attar's Conference of the Birds and Mount Qaf (transformed into 'Calf Mountain').

Other works include: Fury (professor-turned-dollmaker 'Little Brain' in New York); The Golden House (2017; Nero leaves Mumbai for NYC; filmmaker Rene; gender-questioning D.; 'The Joker' = Trump); Quichotte (title character hallucinates son 'Sancho'); The Moor's Last Sigh (won 1995 Whitbread); Shalimar the Clown (Kashmir chauffeur-turned-assassin); The Ground Beneath Her Feet (Ormus Cana and Vina Apsara as Orpheus/Eurydice); Two Years Eight Months and Twenty-Eight Nights (1001 Nights in NYC; lightning princess); Victory City (Pampa Kampana conjures Bisnaga); The Enchantress of Florence (claims descent from Nina Argalia); East, West (stories including 'Yorick,' agents Chekhov and Zulu, 'The Prophet's Hair,' 'At the Auction of the Ruby Slippers'); The Jaguar Smile (Nicaragua nonfiction); and his monograph on The Wizard of Oz (compared subject to 'authorless text'; the film inspired his career).

Rushdie is frequently referenced in bonuses about other authors (Zadie Smith's White Teeth, Azar Nafisi's Reading Lolita in Tehran, Aravind Adiga's The White Tiger) and in questions about Indian partition, postcolonial literature, and the One Thousand and One Nights tradition.
