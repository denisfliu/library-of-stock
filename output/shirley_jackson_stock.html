American author known for the horror novel The Haunting of Hill House and the short story 'The Lottery.' Also wrote We Have Always Lived in the Castle and the memoir Life Among the Savages. Her works explore themes of small-town cruelty, psychological horror, and the Gothic tradition.
"The Lottery"

Jackson's most famous short story, set on a clear and sunny June 27th in a small town. The villagers gather for an annual ritual using a shabby black wooden box from which paper slips are drawn. Traditional wood chips were replaced with paper by Mr. Summers. Old Man Warner, the oldest participant, admonishes younger critics as a 'pack of young fools' and repeats the saying 'corn be heavy soon' to defend the ritual. Bobby Martin and Dickie Delacroix (mispronounced by the townspeople) build a pile of stones. Tessie Hutchinson draws the slip with a black dot and protests 'it isn't fair, it isn't right' before being stoned to death, with even her son Davy participating.

The Haunting of Hill House

A Gothic horror novel about Hill House, a building that stood for eighty years with angles slightly off 90 degrees, making it confusing to navigate. Dr. John Montague investigates the haunting, bringing together Eleanor Vance, the possibly-lesbian artist Theodora, and Luke Sanderson (grandson of the owner). The house was built by Hugh Crain and is cared for by the old Dudley couple. Eleanor enters a trance and climbs an unstable iron staircase in a tower. The house displays strange messages pleading 'HELP ELEANOR COME HOME.' At the end, Eleanor wonders 'why don't they stop me?' before driving her car into a tree, committing suicide to 'stay' at Hill House.

We Have Always Lived in the Castle

A Gothic novel about the Blackwood family. The narrator Mary Katherine ('Merricat') Blackwood and her older sister Constance live with their wheelchair-bound Uncle Julian, who is writing a book about the family. Merricat selects 'Melody Gloucester Pegasus' as three magic words for protection, uses 'safeguards' such as a box of silver dollars buried by the creek, and has an affinity for the death cap mushroom. It is revealed that Merricat, not Constance, murdered most of the family by lacing berries/blackberries with arsenic. After a fire burns the house (described as 'turreted, and open to the sky'), the family is ostracized. Merricat dreams of living on the moon and feeds a cat named Richard Plantagenet, using 'long things' to remind herself to be kind to Uncle Julian.

"Charles"

A short story in which a kindergartener named Laurie tells his mother about a misbehaving classmate named Charles who encourages bad behavior in class. The twist is that Charles does not actually exist — Laurie invented him. This story is referenced in Jackson's memoir Life Among the Savages when a teacher says 'We don't have any Charles in the kindergarten.' In one version, the teacher made the boy color with red crayons instead of green, leading to a spanking.

"One Ordinary Day, with Peanuts"

A short story in which the protagonist, Mr. Johnson, gives a beggar enough money to buy a veal cutlet and gives money to Mildred Kent and Arthur Adams to go to Coney Island. Meanwhile, his wife spent her day accusing a woman of shoplifting and trying to get a bus driver fired. The story explores the idea of contrasting good and evil behavior as daily roles.

Other Works and General

Jackson's broader body of work includes stories featuring a recurring character named James Harris, who appears in 'The Daemon Lover' and 'Seven Types of Ambiguity.' Her debut novel is The Road Through the Wall, in which Carolina Desmond is beaten to death. She also wrote a short story called 'Pillar of Salt' in which Margaret feels increasingly suffocated in New York City, culminating in a breakdown at a drugstore. Jackson is frequently identified as the author of The Haunting of Hill House and 'The Lottery,' and her work is associated with the Gothic tradition.

Summary of Facts

Shirley Jackson is an American author best known for her Gothic-inflected fiction. Her most famous work is the short story 'The Lottery,' set on June 27th in a small town where villagers gather for an annual ritual. Slips of paper are drawn from a shabby black box (traditional wood chips having been replaced by Mr. Summers), and Old Man Warner defends the tradition against younger skeptics with the saying 'corn be heavy soon.' Bobby Martin and Dickie Delacroix build a pile of stones. Tessie Hutchinson draws the marked slip and protests 'it isn't fair, it isn't right' before being stoned to death by the community, including her own son Davy.

Her novel The Haunting of Hill House follows Dr. John Montague's investigation of the haunted Hill House, a structure built by Hugh Crain with angles slightly off 90 degrees, cared for by the old Dudley couple. Eleanor Vance, the possibly-lesbian artist Theodora, and Luke Sanderson participate in the investigation. Strange messages appear reading 'HELP ELEANOR COME HOME.' Eleanor enters a trance and climbs an unstable iron staircase in a tower. At the end, Eleanor wonders 'why don't they stop me?' and drives her car into a tree.

We Have Always Lived in the Castle centers on the Blackwood family. Merricat Blackwood selects 'Melody Gloucester Pegasus' as three protective magic words, buries silver dollars by the creek as safeguards, and has an affinity for the death cap mushroom. It is revealed that Merricat, not her sister Constance, murdered the family by lacing blackberries with arsenic. Their wheelchair-bound Uncle Julian writes a book about the family tragedy. After a fire, the house is described as 'turreted, and open to the sky.' The family is ostracized by the community.

Jackson also wrote the short story 'Charles,' in which kindergartener Laurie invents a misbehaving classmate named Charles who turns out not to exist, and 'One Ordinary Day, with Peanuts,' in which Mr. Johnson acts kindly while his wife does petty cruelties. Her debut novel The Road Through the Wall features Carolina Desmond being beaten to death. She used the recurring character James Harris in stories like 'The Daemon Lover' and 'Seven Types of Ambiguity.' The memoir Life Among the Savages references 'Charles.' Her novel The Sundial involves Orianna convincing her family the world is ending after a snake manifests from a fireplace.
