American Confessional poet who committed suicide in 1963. Known for the poetry collection Ariel (containing 'Daddy,' 'Lady Lazarus,' and the title poem), The Colossus, the novel The Bell Jar, and her marriage to Ted Hughes. Her work frequently engages with her father Otto Plath, Nazi imagery, and themes of death and rebirth.
Daddy

One of Plath's most famous and controversial poems, addressed to her father Otto Plath. It opens 'You do not do, you do not do / Any more, black shoe.' The father is described as 'a man in black with a Meinkampf look' and compared to a Nazi: 'Not God but a swastika.' The speaker claims 'Every woman adores a Fascist' and compares herself to a Jew: 'I think I may be a bit of a Jew.' The poem declares 'There's a stake in your fat black heart / And the villagers never liked you' before concluding 'You bastard, I'm through.' It also states 'the snows of the Tyrol, the clear beer of Vienna are not very pure or true' and 'The black telephone's off at the root' and 'If I've killed one man, I've killed two.' The speaker says 'I thought every German was you.' Anne Sexton's poem 'My Friend, My Friend' partly inspired this poem. The father's cleft chin is identified in a photograph at the blackboard.

Lady Lazarus

A poem about the speaker's repeated suicide attempts and resurrection. The speaker declares 'Dying / Is an art, like everything else. I do it exceptionally well' and 'like the cat I have nine times to die.' She mentions 'One year in every ten / I manage it' in reference to previous attempts. The poem addresses 'Herr Doktor' and 'Herr Lucifer' with 'Beware. / Beware.' The speaker states 'I am your opus, / I am your valuable' and 'there is a charge / For the hearing of my heart.' The poem concludes with the iconic lines 'Out of the ash / I rise with my red hair / And I eat men like air.' The speaker rocks 'shut as a seashell.'

Ariel (poem and collection)

Both the title poem and the posthumous collection it anchors. The poem describes a liberating horseback ride, opening with 'Stasis in darkness' and describing the speaker as 'the arrow, the dew that flies / Suicidal.' The speaker depicts 'white Godiva, I unpeel- / Dead hands, dead stringencies' and flies 'Into the red / Eye, the cauldron of morning.' The speaker states 'the child's cry melts in the wall.' William Davis argued the poem references 'God's lioness' rather than a Shakespearean character. The horse Ariel shares a name with the spirit in The Tempest. Ted Hughes wrote a poem about a 'Night Ride' on this horse in Birthday Letters and controversially moved the bee sequence from the end to the middle of the collection, defying Plath's wishes. The collection also includes 'Tulips,' 'The Munich Mannequins,' 'Morning Song,' 'Edge,' 'The Night Dances,' 'Stings,' and 'Fever 103.' Erica Wagner's book Ariel's Gift discusses the biographical background.

The Colossus

Both a poem and Plath's only poetry collection published during her lifetime. The poem addresses her father and opens 'I shall never get you put together entirely.' The father is described as 'pithy and historical as the Roman Forum.' The poem mentions 'A blue sky out of the Oresteia arches above.' The collection also contains 'Full Fathom Five' (in which the speaker tells the addressee 'You defy questions; You defy godhood' and concludes 'this thick air is murderous. I would breathe water'), 'The Beekeeper's Daughter,' and 'The Disquieting Muses' (inspired by a Giorgio de Chirico painting, ending 'no frown of mine will betray the company I keep').

The Bell Jar

Plath's semi-autobiographical novel, published under the pseudonym 'Victoria Lucas.' The protagonist Esther Greenwood works at Ladies' Day Magazine (based on Plath's time at Mademoiselle). Esther becomes violently ill from eating crab meat while staying at the Amazon hotel after meeting her boss Jay Cee. She sees her 'life branching out... like a fig tree' with different futures. She tries to write a thesis on twin imagery in Finnegans Wake while sponsored by novelist Philomena Guinea. Her boyfriend Buddy Willard is called a hypocrite after they watch Mrs. Tomolillo give birth. Esther masquerades as 'Elly Higginbottom.' She receives electroconvulsive therapy; Dr. Nolan advises her to get a diaphragm, freeing her from fear of sex and marriage. Her ex-boyfriend says a poem is 'a piece of dust.' She writes a novel beginning with the line 'Elaine sat on the breezeway in an old yellow nightgown.' Plath's unfinished second novel (working titles: The Interminable Loaf, Doubletake, Double Exposure) disappeared around 1970.

Other Works and General

Plath married Ted Hughes; their relationship is depicted in Hughes's collection Birthday Letters (1998). Robin Morgan's poem 'Arraignment' blames Plath's death on Hughes, who had an affair with Assia Wevill. Several biographies are clued: Heather Clark's Red Comet (2020, taking its title from Plath's line 'Scar in the sky, red comet'), Janet Malcolm's The Silent Woman (about biographical disputes, with Olwyn Hughes restricting access), Al Alvarez's The Savage God, Anne Stevenson's Bitter Fame, and Richard Wilbur's poem 'Cottage Street, 1953' about consoling a young Plath after a suicide attempt. Plath's children are Frieda and Nicholas Hughes; poems about them include 'Morning Song,' 'Nick and the Candlestick,' and 'Full Moon and Little Frieda.' Plath sealed the children's room before her suicide. Other poems include 'Cut' (about cutting a thumb instead of an onion: 'Little pilgrim, the Indian's axed your scalp'), 'Berck-Plage' (inspired by Percy Key's death at a hospice), 'Gulliver,' 'Edge' (a 'perfected' dead body with children 'coiled' like serpents), 'Metaphors' ('I'm a riddle in nine syllables'), 'Mad Girl's Love Song' (villanelle: 'I think I made you up inside my head'), 'Waking in Winter,' 'Stars Over the Dordogne,' 'Witch Burning,' 'The Eye-Mote,' and 'Electra on Azalea Path.' Helen Vendler wrote about Plath 'playing Pygmalion to her own Galatea.' Winter Trees is a posthumous volume. Crossing the Water is another posthumous collection. She worked at Mademoiselle magazine and taught at Smith College.

Summary of Facts

Sylvia Plath is an American Confessional poet who lived from 1932 to 1963, when she committed suicide by gas asphyxiation. She is one of the most heavily clued poets in quizbowl, primarily identified through her collection Ariel, the poems 'Daddy' and 'Lady Lazarus,' and her novel The Bell Jar.

'Daddy' is Plath's most iconic poem, addressed to her father Otto Plath. It opens 'You do not do, you do not do / Any more, black shoe' and describes the father as 'a man in black with a Meinkampf look.' The speaker claims 'Every woman adores a Fascist,' compares herself to a Jew, and declares 'There's a stake in your fat black heart' before concluding 'You bastard, I'm through.' The poem's Nazi imagery is its most distinctive feature.

'Lady Lazarus' is about the speaker's repeated suicide attempts and resurrections. It declares 'Dying / Is an art, like everything else' and warns 'Herr Doktor' and 'Herr Lucifer' to 'Beware.' The speaker states 'I am your opus, I am your valuable' and the poem ends with the famous lines 'Out of the ash / I rise with my red hair / And I eat men like air.'

The title poem 'Ariel' describes a horseback ride, opening with 'Stasis in darkness' and building to 'the red / Eye, the cauldron of morning.' The speaker is 'the arrow, the dew that flies / Suicidal' and becomes 'white Godiva.' The collection Ariel was published posthumously; Ted Hughes controversially moved the bee sequence from its intended position at the end to the middle. The horse Ariel shares a name with The Tempest's spirit.

'The Colossus' addresses Plath's father: 'I shall never get you put together entirely.' The father is 'pithy and historical as the Roman Forum.' This was Plath's only collection published in her lifetime. 'Full Fathom Five,' also in the collection, concludes 'this thick air is murderous. I would breathe water.'

The Bell Jar, published under the pseudonym 'Victoria Lucas,' follows Esther Greenwood, who works at a magazine, sees her life branching 'like a fig tree,' becomes ill from crab meat at the Amazon hotel, and receives electroconvulsive therapy. Her boyfriend Buddy Willard is called a hypocrite.

'Tulips' describes recovering from an appendectomy; the flowers 'eat my oxygen' and open 'like the mouth of some great African cat.' 'Morning Song' addresses her newborn: 'Love set you going like a fat gold watch.' 'The Munich Mannequins' opens 'Perfection is terrible, it cannot have children.'

Plath married Ted Hughes, whose collection Birthday Letters (1998) addresses her. Their relationship is the subject of multiple biographies: Heather Clark's Red Comet, Janet Malcolm's The Silent Woman (about biographical disputes), Al Alvarez's The Savage God, and Anne Stevenson's Bitter Fame. Robin Morgan's poem 'Arraignment' blames Plath's death on Hughes's affair with Assia Wevill. Plath sealed her children Frieda and Nicholas in their room before her suicide. She studied with Robert Lowell alongside Anne Sexton, and they drank at the Ritz-Carlton with George Starbuck after seminars.
