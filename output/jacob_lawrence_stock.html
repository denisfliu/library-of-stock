Summary of Facts

Jacob Lawrence was an African-American painter associated with the Harlem Renaissance, best known for his 60-panel Migration Series (1940-1941), which depicts the Great Migration of African Americans from the rural South to the urban North. The series was funded by the WPA/Federal Art Project, and its even and odd numbered panels are split between MoMA and the Phillips Collection. The first panel famously shows a crowd of silhouettes filing through doors labeled 'Chicago,' 'New York,' and 'St. Louis.' Lawrence prevented color variation by painting all 60 panels in parallel, applying unmixed casein tempera one color at a time, with the assistance of his wife, Barbadian artist Gwendolyn Knight, who also edited his often-didactic captions. Key panels include three girls in red, yellow, and blue writing numbers on a chalkboard, a segregated restaurant divided by a zigzagging yellow rope, a goggle-eyed judge under a bent lamp, and a scene of segregation outside the Dixie Belle Cafe.

Lawrence described his style as 'dynamic cubism,' influenced by Mexican muralism and the colorful tenements of New York. He also created a 22-panel series on The Legend of John Brown for the Detroit Institute of Arts in 1941, inspired by Horace Pippin's painting, later reworked into silkscreen prints with Lou Stovall. At age 20, he painted a 41-panel tempera series on The Life of Toussaint Louverture, now in the Amistad Research Center, featuring a portrait of Louverture in elaborate red and gold military costume. His War Series begins with 'Shipping out to Victory,' heavy on brown and black tempera. Other notable works include The Builders (displayed in the White House), Tombstones (1942, depicting a black child throwing a white doll), a Murano glass mural 'New York in Transit' at the Times Square subway station, and a New Jersey series for the Containers Corporation of America. Lawrence and Knight were excluded from the Spiral Group due to their renown. He also traveled to Nigeria where he spent time with members of the Mbari Club associated with the Negritude movement.
