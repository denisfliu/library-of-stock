Key concepts include epoche (suspension of judgment), ataraxia (freedom from disturbance), and the Problem of the Criterion.
Pyrrhonian Skepticism

The branch of ancient skepticism founded by Pyrrho and systematized by Sextus Empiricus in his Outlines of Pyrrhonism. Pyrrhonian skeptics advocated epoche (suspension of judgment) as the path to ataraxia (freedom from disturbance). This tradition heavily influenced Michel de Montaigne, who had quotes from it inscribed on the rafters of his tower study in Dordogne. Montaigne's personal medal bore the motto 'what do I know?' on one side and the Greek word 'epoche' on the other.

Academic Skepticism

The branch of skepticism that developed within Plato's Academy, inaugurated by Arcesilaus (the sixth scholarch) during the 'Middle' Academy phase and continued by Carneades in the 'New Academy.' Academic skeptics advocated for akatalepsia (the idea that nothing can be known). Carneades introduced probabilistic reasoning as a way to still achieve knowledge despite the absence of cognitive impressions.

Influence on Later Philosophy

Skepticism had a significant influence on later thinkers, particularly David Hume and Michel de Montaigne. Hume extended skepticism to encompass causation, arguing that 'constant conjunction' leads us to unjustly posit cause-and-effect out of habit. Montaigne's essay 'Apology for Raymond Sebond' drew heavily from Pyrrhonian skepticism, and Roderick Chisholm traced the 'Problem of the Criterion' through Montaigne's version of a skeptical argument named for a circle (diallelus).

Summary of Facts

Skepticism is a broad philosophical tradition that questions the certainty of knowledge. The clues reveal two main branches: Pyrrhonian skepticism, founded by Pyrrho and systematized by Sextus Empiricus in his Outlines of Pyrrhonism, and Academic skepticism, which developed within Plato's Academy under Arcesilaus and later Carneades.

Pyrrhonian skeptics advocate epoche, or the suspension of judgment, as the best path to ataraxia (freedom from disturbance). This Greek term epoche was later adopted by Edmund Husserl for phenomenological 'bracketing.' The tradition heavily influenced Michel de Montaigne, who had skeptical quotes inscribed on the rafters of his tower study in Dordogne and wore a personal medal with the motto 'what do I know?' on one side and 'epoche' on the other. Montaigne's essay 'Apology for Raymond Sebond' was particularly inspired by Sextus Empiricus.

Academic skepticism began with Arcesilaus, the sixth scholarch of the Academy, who inaugurated its 'Middle' phase and promoted akatalepsia (the idea that nothing can be known). Carneades continued this tradition in the 'New Academy,' introducing probabilistic reasoning. The Academic tradition ended during the First Mithridatic War when Philo of Larissa fled to Rome.

David Hume is a major figure in the skeptical tradition. In his Treatise of Human Nature, he noted that the coherence of an object leads 'vulgar' people to reject skepticism. In An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, he extended skepticism to causation, arguing that constant conjunction leads us to unjustly posit cause-and-effect out of habit. Hume's skeptical discussion of sympathy assumes the existence of other minds, a problem later addressed by Alvin Plantinga in his 1967 book God and Other Minds. Roderick Chisholm traced the Problem of the Criterion through Montaigne's circular skeptical argument (diallelus).
