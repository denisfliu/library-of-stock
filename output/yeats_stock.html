Irish poet, co-founder of the Abbey Theatre. Known for poems including "The Second Coming," "Sailing to Byzantium," "Easter, 1916," "The Wild Swans at Coole," "The Lake Isle of Innisfree," "Among School Children," "Leda and the Swan," "The Circus Animals' Desertion," and "An Irish Airman Foresees His Death." Also wrote plays and the mystical tract A Vision. Wrote the preface to Tagore's Gitanjali.
The Second Coming

Yeats's most famous poem, beginning "Turning and turning in the widening gyre." Contains the lines "Things Fall Apart; the centre cannot hold" (which titles Chinua Achebe's novel), "The ceremony of innocence is drowned," and describes a "rough beast" that "slouches towards Bethlehem to be born." Features a "vast image out of Spiritus Mundi" of "A shape with lion body and the head of a man." Collected in Michael Robartes and the Dancer.

General / Biographical

Irish poet who co-founded the Abbey Theatre with Lady Gregory and Edward Martyn. Unrequited lover of Maud Gonne. Member of the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn (feuded with Aleister Crowley). Made automatic writing with his wife Georgie Hyde-Lees, which informed A Vision (presents double gyres, phases of the moon, "Thirteenth Cone"). Wrote the preface to Tagore's Gitanjali ("stirred my blood as nothing has for years"). Told rioters at The Plough and the Stars "you have disgraced yourselves again." Epitaph from "Under Ben Bulben" reads "Horseman, pass by." Won the Nobel Prize in 1923. Christopher Okigbo's "Lament of the Masks" celebrates his centenary. W. H. Auden wrote "In Memory of W. B. Yeats" ("poetry makes nothing happen"). Adapted Noh theater in Four Plays for Dancers (including At the Hawk's Well). Also wrote The Countess Cathleen, Purgatory, and "When You Are Old." Called Blake a "too literal realist of the imagination." Coined "Bardolatry" to describe excessive Shakespeare worship. Wrote "Quintessence of Ibsenism" was not Yeats but rather often referenced alongside it.

Summary of Facts

William Butler Yeats (1865-1939) was an Irish poet, playwright, and Nobel laureate who stands as one of the most heavily clued figures in quizbowl literature. He co-founded the Abbey Theatre with Lady Gregory and Edward Martyn, and his work spans from early Celtic Revival poetry through modernist masterpieces.

"The Second Coming" is his most frequently clued poem, containing the lines "Things Fall Apart; the centre cannot hold" (which titles Chinua Achebe's Things Fall Apart) and "the rough beast... slouches towards Bethlehem to be born." It opens with "Turning and turning in the widening gyre" and features "a vast image out of Spiritus Mundi" of a "lion body with the head of a man."

"Easter, 1916" responds to the Easter Rising, repeating "A terrible beauty is born" while mourning "MacDonagh and MacBride / And Connolly and Pearse." The speaker recalls "polite meaningless words" and describes a "drunken, vainglorious lout" (John MacBride). "Sailing to Byzantium" opens "That is no country for old men" (titling Cormac McCarthy's novel) and describes an aged man as "a tattered coat upon a stick."

"The Wild Swans at Coole" observes "nine-and-fifty" swans that "paddle in the cold" in October twilight. "Among School Children" ends "How can we know the dancer from the dance?" and discusses Plato. "The Lake Isle of Innisfree" opens "I will arise and go now" and describes planting "nine bean rows" where "peace comes dropping slow."

"An Irish Airman Foresees His Death" features a speaker driven by "a lonely impulse of delight" who declares "Those that I fight I do not hate." "Leda and the Swan" describes Zeus's rape of Leda, with "a shudder in the loins" engendering "The broken wall, the burning roof and tower / And Agamemnon dead." "The Circus Animals' Desertion" ends at "the foul rag and bone shop of the heart." "Lapis Lazuli" describes carved Chinamen whose "ancient, glittering eyes, are gay."

Yeats was a member of the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn and feuded with Aleister Crowley over black magic. His mystical tract A Vision draws from automatic writing with his wife Georgie Hyde-Lees and posits "double gyres" and phases of the moon. He wrote the preface to Tagore's Gitanjali. He adapted Japanese Noh theater in plays like At the Hawk's Well. W. H. Auden's elegy for Yeats contains "poetry makes nothing happen." Yeats's epitaph from "Under Ben Bulben" reads "Horseman, pass by."
