Florentine High Renaissance painter, the 'faultless painter.' Known for Madonna of the Harpies, grisaille frescoes of John the Baptist in the Chiostro dello Scalzo, and his Last Supper at San Salvi. Subject of a Robert Browning dramatic monologue. Named for being the son of a tailor. Teacher of Rosso Fiorentino and Pontormo.

Summary of Facts

Andrea del Sarto (1486-1530) was a Florentine High Renaissance painter known as the 'faultless painter,' a title featured in Robert Browning's famous dramatic monologue in which the speaker tells his wife Lucrezia 'a man's reach should exceed his grasp, or what's a heaven for?' His name derives from being the son of a tailor (sarto).

His most famous painting is Madonna of the Harpies (1517), in which two angels cling to Mary's legs as she holds Christ atop a polygonal pedestal decorated with mythological monsters. It was originally supposed to contain St. Bonaventure instead of St. Francis. His largest undertaking was a grisaille fresco series of the life of John the Baptist for the Chiostro dello Scalzo in Florence. Madonna del Sacco, placed over a church doorway, is named for the sack Joseph leans on.

His wife Lucrezia del Fede modeled for his paintings. Rosso Fiorentino and Jacopo Pontormo trained in his studio in the 1510s.
