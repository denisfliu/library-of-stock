Summary of Facts

Duccio di Buoninsegna was a Sienese painter and the foremost artist of the Sienese School. His art was influenced by Byzantine styles and he was a possible student of Cimabue. He was the teacher of Simone Martini and probably the Lorenzetti brothers.

His masterpiece is the Maesta (1308-1311), the main altarpiece of the Siena Cathedral. Its front panel shows the Madonna enthroned with the child Jesus, surrounded by 20 angels and 19 saints, including four local saints — Sabinus, Ansanus, Crescentius (unusually in a red robe), and Victor — kneeling in the foreground. The back depicts 26 scenes of the Passion of Christ under a golden sky, with 43 scenes total from the life of Mary and Jesus. It was commissioned to replace a Madonna of the Large Eyes icon that had helped Siena win a battle, and its installation was celebrated with a holiday and parade. It was dismantled and sawn in half in 1771, with fragments now dispersed worldwide: the Temptation on the Mount (showing a gigantic Jesus rebuking a pure black-bodied winged devil) is at the Frick, and a Nativity fragment with Isaiah and Ezekiel flanking 16 angels is at the US National Gallery.

The Rucellai Madonna was commissioned by the Laudesi confraternity and depicts six angels holding up the Virgin's chair, painted with egg tempera and gold leaf. It was misattributed by Giorgio Vasari to Cimabue, an error repeated by Frederic Leighton in his painting Cimabue's Celebrated Madonna. It is displayed in Florence's Uffizi beside Giotto's Ognissanti Madonna. The Maesta and Rucellai Madonna are the only two dateable works by Duccio.

Other works include the Stoclet Madonna, Madonna of the Franciscans (three small monks praying at the feet of a black-robed Mary), and The Calling of Peter and Andrew (man in teal robe on a boat holding a fishnet).
