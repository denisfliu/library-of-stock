Duccio di Buoninsegna

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Sienese painter, foremost of the Sienese School. Known for the Maesta altarpiece (main altarpiece of the Siena Cathedral) and the Rucellai Madonna (misattributed by Vasari to Cimabue). Teacher of Simone Martini and probably the Lorenzetti brothers. Influenced by Byzantine styles but his Maesta was the first major work to break from that tradition.
Maesta (1308-1311)

Main altarpiece of the Siena Cathedral. Front panel shows Madonna enthroned with child surrounded by 20 angels and 19 saints. Back depicts 26 scenes of the Passion of Christ under a golden sky (43 scenes total from the life of Mary and Jesus). Dismantled and sawn in half in 1771; fragments dispersed around the world. Installation celebrated with a holiday and parade in Siena. Inscription on the marble base beseeches 'bring Peace... and life to [the artist], as he painted you this way.' Wikipedia

Rucellai Madonna

Panel painting commissioned by the Laudesi confraternity. Three angels on each side hold up the Virgin's chair. Egg tempera and gold leaf. Originally housed in the Church of Santa Maria Novella; now in the Uffizi in Florence. Misattributed by Vasari (and Frederic Leighton) to Cimabue. Made in 1285 in Siena. Named for the Rucellai family of wool merchants. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Duccio di Buoninsegna was a Sienese painter and the foremost artist of the Sienese School. His art was influenced by Byzantine styles, using egg tempera and gold leaf, and he was a possible student of Cimabue and contemporary of Giotto. He was the teacher of Simone Martini and probably the Lorenzetti brothers. Siena, his home city and rival of Florence, was also home to Ambrogio Lorenzetti, Il Sodoma, and Il Sassetta.

His masterpiece is the Maesta (1308-1311), the main altarpiece of the Siena Cathedral, described as the first major work to break from Byzantine tradition. Its front panel shows the Madonna enthroned with the child Jesus, surrounded by 20 angels and 19 saints (39 figures total), with four local patron saints -- Sabinus, Ansanus, Crescentius (unusually in a red robe), and Victor -- kneeling in the foreground. An inscription on the marble base beseeches 'bring Peace... and life to [the artist], as he painted you this way.' The back depicts 26 scenes of the Passion of Christ under a golden sky, with 43 scenes total from the life of Mary and Jesus. Notable back panels include: the Temptation on the Mount (now at the Frick -- a gigantic Jesus rebukes a pure black-bodied winged devil), Christ kissed by Judas against a yellow background, Zacchaeus climbing a tree in the Entry into Jerusalem, and two versions of the same blind man in the Healing scene. The front predella has scenes from the childhood of Christ, including a Nativity fragment at the US National Gallery with Isaiah and Ezekiel flanking 16 angels. It was commissioned to replace a Madonna of the Large Eyes icon that had helped Siena win a battle, and its installation was celebrated with a parade. It was dismantled and sawn in half in 1771, with fragments now dispersed worldwide; it had been removed to make way for Francesco di Giorgio sculptures.

The Rucellai Madonna was commissioned by the Laudesi confraternity in 1285, named for the Rucellai family of wool merchants, and depicts three angels on each side holding up the Virgin's chair, painted in egg tempera and gold leaf with mordant gilding on the throne. It was misattributed by Vasari to Cimabue, an error repeated by Frederic Leighton in his painting Cimabue's Madonna Carried in Procession Through the Streets of Florence. It is displayed in the Uffizi beside Giotto's Ognissanti Madonna. The Maesta and Rucellai Madonna are the only two dateable works by Duccio.

Other works include the Stoclet Madonna (sold in 2004, named for the Stoclet Palace in Brussels), Madonna of the Franciscans (three small monks at feet of black-robed Mary), and The Calling of Peter and Andrew (man in teal robe on a boat with fishnet).
