Summary of Facts

Zeno of Citium was the founder of Stoicism, one of the most influential philosophical schools of the ancient world. Originally from Citium in Cyprus, he was inspired by the Cynics before beginning to teach at the Stoa Poikile (Painted Porch) in Athens, giving his school its name.

Zeno's philosophical contributions include the concept of katalepsis (comprehension), which he demonstrated by gradually closing his hand in a fourfold process showing how perception moves from impression to assent to comprehension to knowledge. This Stoic epistemological concept was attacked by the Academic skeptic Arcesilaus, who challenged Zeno's causal theory of perception.

An anecdote about Zeno involves Dionysius the Renegade, who asked why Zeno didn't correct his mistakes. Zeno replied 'Because I mistrust you.' Dionysius later rejected Stoicism's forbearance in the face of suffering when his eye hurt and left to study with the Cyrenaics, a hedonistic school founded by Aristippus. Dionysius also pranked his other teacher Heraclides Ponticus with a fake play.

Zeno is frequently clued in the context of broader Stoic philosophy, including the Stoic belief in pneuma (breath of life) and apatheia. He shares his first name with Zeno of Elea, the philosopher known for paradoxes about motion (Achilles and the Tortoise, the flying arrow), and questions sometimes disambiguate between the two by accepting either.
