The Great Gatsby

Tender Is the Night

A semi-autobiographical novel about the marriage of Dick and Nicole Diver, modeled on Fitzgerald's own relationship with Zelda. Nicole's mental health struggles mirror Zelda's, and they fall in love at a mental asylum in Switzerland before moving to the French Riviera. Dick is a psychiatrist whose marriage decays over the course of the novel. Fitzgerald himself considered it his best novel.

This Side of Paradise

Fitzgerald's first novel about Monsignor Darcy's protege Amory Blaine at Princeton. The novel ends with a generation described as having 'grown up to find all gods dead, all wars fought, all faiths in man shaken.' Amory distinguishes between 'the slicker' and 'the big man,' and declares 'I know myself, but that is all' at the end. The character Thomas Parke D'Invilliers, based on John Peale Bishop, appears in this novel. Ginevra King inspired the character Isabella Borge.

Winter Dreams

A short story about the golf caddy Dexter Green who becomes infatuated with Judy Jones at the Sherry Island Golf Club. Dexter later learns his English method of washing wool golf socks and opens a laundry empire. The story ends with Dexter reflecting 'now that thing is gone' upon learning Judy married the philanderer Lud Simms. Fitzgerald described this story as a 'first draft' of The Great Gatsby, both inspired by his pursuit of Ginevra King. Appears in the collection All the Sad Young Men.

Short Stories (Other)

Fitzgerald wrote numerous short stories across several collections. 'The Diamond as Big as the Ritz' features Percy Washington boasting about his father's mountain; John Unger is nearly killed. 'Bernice Bobs Her Hair' features Bernice's scandalous haircut in Eau Claire and her revenge of cutting cousin Marjorie's pigtails. 'The Ice Palace' features Sally Carrol nearly freezing in the title structure after her northern beau Harry abandons her. 'The Cut-Glass Bowl' follows Evylyn Piper's misfortunes. 'The Curious Case of Benjamin Button' features a man aging backwards who is rejected from Yale. 'Babylon Revisited' has Charlie Wales traveling from Prague to Paris to regain custody of his daughter Honoria. 'Absolution' features Father Schwartz collapsing after hearing Rudolph Miller's confession and developed from a discarded early draft of Gatsby. These stories appear in collections including Flappers and Philosophers and Tales of the Jazz Age.

General / Biographical

Fitzgerald's biography and literary career as reflected in the clues. His romance with Ginevra King inspired multiple works. His wife Zelda Sayre and their tumultuous marriage is central to his fiction. His essay 'The Crack-Up' begins 'Of course all life is a process of breaking down' and proposes that 'the test of a first-rate intelligence is the ability to hold two opposed ideas in the mind.' His editor Maxwell Perkins played a key role in shaping The Great Gatsby. Edmund Wilson assembled his essays for posthumous publication. His notebooks contain the line 'Show me a hero and I'll write you a tragedy.' He was a friend of Nathanael West (who died the day after Fitzgerald) and was parodied in Zelda's novel Save Me the Waltz.

Summary of Facts

F. Scott Fitzgerald is one of the most frequently clued American authors in quizbowl, with his works spanning the Jazz Age and exploring themes of wealth, disillusionment, and the American Dream. His best-known novel, The Great Gatsby, is narrated by Nick Carraway and set in West Egg, New York, featuring the mysterious Jay Gatsby, his love for Daisy Buchanan, and the novel's famous closing line about 'boats against the current.' Key clue points include the Valley of Ashes with the eyes of Dr. T.J. Eckleberg, Meyer Wolfsheim's human molar cufflinks and his fixing of the 1919 World Series, the fatal hit-and-run killing Myrtle Wilson, and Gatsby's shooting in his swimming pool. The novel's epigraph is attributed to the fictional poet Thomas Parke D'Invilliers, a character from This Side of Paradise.

Tender Is the Night, which Fitzgerald himself considered his best novel, follows the semi-autobiographical marriage of Dick and Nicole Diver, modeled on Fitzgerald's relationship with Zelda Sayre. Nicole's mental health struggles mirror Zelda's, and the couple meets at a Swiss asylum before moving to the French Riviera.

This Side of Paradise, Fitzgerald's debut novel, follows Amory Blaine at Princeton and features the character Thomas Parke D'Invilliers (based on John Peale Bishop). It ends with Amory declaring 'I know myself, but that is all' and contains a famous passage about a generation that found 'all gods dead, all wars fought, all faiths in man shaken.'

Fitzgerald's short stories are extensively clued. 'Winter Dreams' features the golf caddy Dexter Green's infatuation with Judy Jones and is described by Fitzgerald as a 'first draft' of Gatsby, both inspired by his pursuit of Ginevra King. 'The Diamond as Big as the Ritz' is one of the 'Gatsby cluster' stories. 'Bernice Bobs Her Hair' centers on a scandalous haircut and revenge pigtail-cutting. 'The Ice Palace' follows Sally Carrol's near-freezing death. 'The Cut-Glass Bowl' traces Evylyn Piper's misfortunes.

Biographically, the clues emphasize Fitzgerald's relationship with Zelda, his essay 'The Crack-Up' (which proposes that 'the test of a first-rate intelligence is the ability to hold two opposed ideas'), his editor Maxwell Perkins, and his friendship with Nathanael West, who died the day after Fitzgerald.
