Thomas Hardy

Victorian/Edwardian English poet and novelist. Most commonly identified by "The Darkling Thrush" (leaning on a coppice gate), "The Convergence of the Twain" (sinking of the Titanic), "Channel Firing," "Hap," and novels set in the fictional county of Wessex including Jude the Obscure, Tess of the d'Urbervilles, Far from the Madding Crowd, The Return of the Native, and The Mayor of Casterbridge. Also worked as an architect (designed Max Gate).

Summary of Facts

Thomas Hardy was a Victorian/Edwardian English poet and novelist, also trained as an architect. He set virtually all his major novels in the fictional county of Wessex, an agglomeration of several southwestern English counties, with renamed towns such as Casterbridge, Christminster (Oxford), Budmouth, Weatherbury, Mellstock, and Wintoncester (Winchester).

His most frequently clued poem is "The Darkling Thrush," originally published as "By the Century's Deathbed" on December 19, 1900. The speaker leans upon a "coppice gate" and observes a bleak winter landscape where "The tangled bine-stems scored the sky / Like strings of broken lyres" and "The land's sharp features seemed to be the Century's corpse outleant." Despite "every spirit upon earth" seeming "fervourless," an aged thrush — "frail, gaunt, and small" — sings a "full-hearted evensong / of joy illimited," conveying "Some blessed Hope, whereof he knew / And I was unaware." The poem notes "Winter's dregs made desolate / The weakening eye of day" and the speaker sees "So little cause for carolings." The word "darkling" is the same unusual adjective used for a plain in Matthew Arnold's "Dover Beach."

"The Convergence of the Twain," subtitled "Lines on the loss of the Titanic," is written in eleven tercets. It describes the fated meeting of ship and iceberg, driven by "The Immanent Will that stirs and urges everything." The poem notes "The sea-worm crawls" on the wreck and culminates in "consummation comes, and jars two hemispheres." Simon Armitage wrote a response poem with the same title about 9/11.

"Channel Firing" is delivered from the perspective of corpses who hear "great guns" that "shook all our coffins as we lay." God reassures them: "It will be warmer when I blow the trumpet." The poem ends with images of Stourton Tower, Camelot, and "starlit Stonehenge." "Gunnery practice out at sea" broke "the chancel window-squares."

"Neutral Tones" describes the speaker learning "keen lessons that love deceives" and ends with the memorable list "Your face, and the God-curst sun, and a tree / And a pond edged with grayish leaves." A "grin of bitterness" sweeps "Like an ominous bird a-wing."

"Hap" is a sonnet in which the speaker wishes "some vengeful god would call to me" and laments "Crass Casualty obstructs the sun and rain / And dicing Time for gladness casts a moan." It imagines a figure warning "Know that thy sorrow is my ecstasy" and notably invents the noun "Powerfuller."

"The Man He Killed" reflects on war as "quaint and curious" since "You shoot a fellow down / You'd treat if met where any bar is." The speaker shot someone "because he was my foe." The poem was inspired by the Boer War. "The Pity of It" (1915) uses dialect phrases "Thu bist" and "Er war" to show the shared heritage of English and German.

"Ah, Are You Digging on My Grave" is told from a dead woman's perspective who wonders if the digging is her enemy or loved one, only to learn it's her dog burying a bone. Other poems include "The Blinded Bird" (protesting the blinding of birds in vinkensport), "The Caged Goldfinch" (a goldfinch left in a cage on a grave), "In Tenebris" ("death will not appal one who, past doubtings all, waits in unhope"), "He Never Expected Much" ("Well, World, you have kept faith with me" as a reflection on his 86th birthday), "The Subalterns" (sky, north wind, sickness, death talk to the speaker), "Shelley's Skylark" (the skylark that "moved a poet to prophecies"), and "The Ruined Maid" (ending "a raw country girl, such as you be, / Cannot quite expect that. You ain't ruined").

Hardy's Poems 1912-13 is an elegiac sequence for his dead first wife, Emma Gifford, with the epigraph from Dido's words "veteris vestigia flammae." "After a Journey" begins "hereto I come to view a voiceless ghost" as the speaker retraces a romantic trip along the cliffs of Cornwall, remembering "summer gave us sweets, but autumn wrought division." He burnt Emma's accusatory diary "What I Think About My Husband."

Among the novels, Jude the Obscure is set partly in "Christminster" (Oxford) and follows Jude Fawley, a stonemason (like Hardy's father) who aspires to a learned profession. His wife Arabella returns from Australia with the somber child "Little Father Time," who later murders two of Sue Bridehead's children and hangs himself, leaving a note: "Done because we are too menny." Sue reads Swinburne's line "Thou has conquered, O pale Galilean" and later turns to religion. The novel ends with Jude's wife leaving his fresh corpse to join a Remembrance Week party.

Far from the Madding Crowd features Bathsheba Everdene and three suitors: Gabriel Oak (a rejected shepherd), Farmer Boldwood (led on by a Valentine saying "Marry Me"), and Sergeant Troy. Bathsheba is horrified to find Fanny Robin's child inside Fanny's coffin. The Return of the Native is set on Egdon Heath, where Clym Yeobright returns from Paris (hated selling diamonds), marries Eustacia Vye (who still loves Damon Wildeve), and reads so much he nearly goes blind. The novel opens and ends on Guy Fawkes Day and includes a sheep-dye salesman perpetually stained red. Damon and Eustacia drown at the end.

A Pair of Blue Eyes is said to have originated the term "cliffhanger" through a scene where Henry Knight hangs from a cliff, thinks about dinosaurs, and is rescued by Elfride's underwear. "A Mere Interlude" tells of Baptista who elopes with a former lover who drowns an hour after their wedding. Hardy was also an architect who designed Max Gate and worked with John Hicks. He married Emma Gifford after the financial success of Far from the Madding Crowd, calling her an "encyclopedia in petticoats." He supported anti-vivisection and regarded Charlotte Mew as "far and away the best living woman poet." John Fowles called Hardy "the first to try to break the Victorian middle-class seal over the supposed Pandora's box of sex" in The French Lieutenant's Woman.
