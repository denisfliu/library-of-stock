Russian Romantic composer (1844–1908), member of the Mighty Five, and one of the great orchestrators in Western music history. Best known for the symphonic suite Scheherazade, the Capriccio Espagnol, the Russian Easter Festival Overture, and the operatic interlude 'Flight of the Bumblebee.' His operas include Sadko, The Tale of Tsar Saltan, The Snow Maiden, and The Golden Cockerel. He taught Stravinsky and completed Mussorgsky's Night on Bald Mountain. His orchestration textbook Principles of Orchestration uses only examples from his own works.
General / Biographical

Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov (1844–1908) was a Russian Romantic composer and one of the Mighty Five (with Balakirev, Mussorgsky, Borodin, and Cui). He served as Inspector of Naval Bands and professor at the Saint Petersburg Conservatory. He was celebrated as one of the greatest orchestrators of his era, publishing the treatise Principles of Orchestration (posthumous), which uses only examples from his own works. He taught Igor Stravinsky and provided Stravinsky with Russian folk melodies for The Firebird. He completed Mussorgsky's Night on Bald Mountain and helped complete Borodin's Prince Igor (with Glazunov). He attended 'Quartet Fridays' hosted by the music patron Mitrofan Belyayev. Wikipedia

Scheherazade

Rimsky-Korsakov's four-movement orchestral suite (Op. 35, 1888), inspired by the One Thousand and One Nights. The four movements are: (I) 'The Sea and Sinbad's Ship,' (II) 'The Kalendar Prince,' (III) 'The Young Prince and the Young Princess,' and (IV) 'Festival at Baghdad / The Sea.' Scheherazade herself is represented by a solo violin (with harp) playing a high sixteenth-note triplet theme; the Sultan is represented by an E-D-C-B-flat (E-D-C-A-sharp) motif in the low brass. The finale depicts a shipwreck. A 'fanfare motif' for trombones and muted trumpets is reprised in the last movement. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov (1844–1908) was a Russian Romantic composer and a member of the Mighty Five (the Mighty Handful), the nationalist school that also included Mily Balakirev, Modest Mussorgsky, Alexander Borodin, and César Cui. He served as Inspector of Naval Bands and professor at the Saint Petersburg Conservatory. He was celebrated above all as an orchestrator of extraordinary skill, and his posthumously published Principles of Orchestration — which uses only examples from his own works (Capriccio Espagnol, Scheherazade, etc.) — remains a landmark text. He described the bassoon as 'an atmosphere of senile mockery.'

His most famous orchestral works are the symphonic suite Scheherazade (Op. 35, 1888) and the Capriccio Espagnol (Op. 34, 1887). In Scheherazade, the storyteller is represented by a solo violin with harp accompaniment playing high sixteenth-note triplets, while the Sultan is represented by an E-D-C-B-flat bass motif in the low brass. The suite's four movements are 'The Sea and Sinbad's Ship,' 'The Kalendar Prince,' 'The Young Prince and the Young Princess,' and 'Festival at Baghdad / Shipwreck.' His wife protested its 1910 adaptation as a ballet by Michel Fokine and Léon Bakst. The Capriccio Espagnol's five movements include two Alborada movements (identical except for key and orchestration), 'Variations,' 'Scena e canto gitano' (with five successive cadenzas over a snare drum for horn/trumpets, violin, flute, clarinet, and harp), and a closing 'Fandango asturiano' with castanets.

His Russian Easter Festival Overture ('Overture on Liturgical Themes') opens in the unusual 5/2 time with a Lento mystico liturgical melody, includes a violin cadenza, and ends with exclamations of 'Resurrexit!' shifting into 2/1 time. It is based on Obikhod chants and was dedicated to Mussorgsky and Borodin.

His operas include: Sadko (1898) — featuring three 'Guest Songs' for a Varangian, Indian (the 'Song of India,' later adapted by Tommy Dorsey as a jazz standard), and Venetian visitor; The Tale of Tsar Saltan (1900) — famous for the 'Flight of the Bumblebee' interlude depicting Prince Gvidon's transformation into an insect by the Swan-Bird; The Snow Maiden (1882) — in which Snegurochka melts after declaring her love for Mizgir; and The Golden Cockerel (1909, his last opera) — immediately banned, calling for a rare tenor altino voice as the eunuch Astrologer.

Rimsky-Korsakov taught Igor Stravinsky; Stravinsky borrowed Russian folk melodies from his collection 100 Russian Folk Songs for The Firebird. Rimsky-Korsakov completed Mussorgsky's Night on Bald Mountain (the version most commonly heard today) and helped Glazunov complete Borodin's Prince Igor. He attended 'Quartet Fridays' at the home of music patron Mitrofan Belyayev, whose circle (unlike the Five) emphasized Western craftsmanship over nationalist distinctiveness.
