Percy Bysshe Shelley

View source questions · Make cards
English Romantic poet. Most commonly identified by "Ozymandias," "Ode to the West Wind," "To a Skylark," "Adonais" (elegy for Keats), Prometheus Unbound, The Cenci, "The Masque of Anarchy," and "A Defence of Poetry." Also wrote "Mont Blanc," "Hymn to Intellectual Beauty," and The Revolt of Islam. Husband of Mary Shelley; friend of Thomas Love Peacock (satirized as Scythrop in Nightmare Abbey). Said to have predicted his death by shipwreck in "Adonais."

Summary of Facts

Percy Bysshe Shelley was an English Romantic poet, the husband of Mary Shelley, and a close friend of Thomas Love Peacock. He is one of the most frequently clued English Romantic poets in quizbowl.

"Ozymandias" is a sonnet in which a traveller from an antique land describes "Two vast and trunkless legs of stone" standing in the desert beside a shattered visage. The pedestal inscription reads "Look on my works, ye Mighty, and despair!" while nothing beside remains. The sculptor well understood "The hand that mock'd" and "the heart that fed."

"Ode to the West Wind" addresses the wind as the "breath of Autumn's being" and compares it to "the bright hair uplifted from the head / Of some fierce Maenad." The speaker, weighed down by "a heavy weight of hours" though once "tameless, and swift, and proud," urges the wind to "Drive my dead thoughts over the universe" to "quicken a new birth." The poem closes with the famous question "If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?"

"Adonais" is a pastoral elegy on the death of John Keats, repeatedly lamenting "I weep for Adonais — he is dead!" It describes seasons passing as "the green lizard, and the golden snake, like unimprisoned flames" awake from their trance while "grief returns with the revolving year." The dead man's soul is "like a star, / beacons from the abode where the Eternal are." The poem calls for "the most musical of mourners" to "weep again!" and states "some yet live, treading the thorny road, / Which leads, through toil and hate, to Fame's serene abode." The "Go thou to Rome" passage declares "Life, like a dome of many-coloured glass / Stains the white radiance of Eternity." Chatterton is mentioned as one who "rose pale, his solemn agony... not yet faded from him." The poem is said to have predicted Shelley's own death by shipwreck with the lines "My spirit's bark is driven, / Far from the shore." Allen Ginsberg recalled reading the "last triumphant stanzas" in "Kaddish," and Jack Kerouac frequently quoted the opening lament.

"To a Skylark" opens "Hail to thee, blithe Spirit! / Bird thou never wert" and describes the skylark as a "Poet hidden / In the light of thought" that lets out "profuse strains of unpremeditated art." The speaker asks "Teach me half the gladness / That thy brain must know" and notes the bird "lovest, but ne'er knew love's sad satiety." Noel Coward titled his play Blithe Spirit from this poem.

"The Masque of Anarchy" was written in response to the Peterloo Massacre of 1819. A figure rides "on a white horse, splashed with blood" declaring "I AM GOD, AND KING, AND LAW!" A "maniac maid" named Hope who "looked more like Despair" appears. The poem depicts Murder, Fraud, and Anarchy, and delivers a rousing speech urging countrymen to "Rise like lions" and "shake your chains to earth, like dew," closing with "Ye are many — they are few."

Prometheus Unbound is a closet drama riffing on Aeschylus. Hercules frees Prometheus from being chained to a precipice, and a fourth act appended features a masque for his wedding to Asia. Jupiter is destroyed by Demogorgon, who declares "this is alone Life, Joy, Empire and Victory." The preface describes the protagonist as "exempt from the taints of ambition," unlike Satan in Paradise Lost. The play opens with Prometheus surrounded by Ione and Panthea.

"A Defence of Poetry" was written in response to Thomas Love Peacock's "The Four Ages of Poetry." It argues that poetry improves society by enlarging the imagination, refers to history as "that cyclic poem written by Time upon the memories of men," and ends with the famous declaration that "poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the world."

"Mont Blanc" opens "The everlasting universe of things / Flows through the mind, and rolls its rapid waves" and was written in the Vale of Chamouni about the "Ravine of Arve." The mountain is told "Thou hast a voice, great Mountain" and can "repeal large codes of fraud and woe." "England in 1819" opens "An old, mad, blind, despised, and dying King" (George III). "Hymn to Intellectual Beauty" addresses "the awful shadow of some unseen Power" and recalls "while yet a boy I sought for ghosts."

The Cenci is Shelley's most-performed play, in which Beatrice hires Olimpio and Marzio to kill her tyrannical father Count Francesco, then walks to her own execution. The Revolt of Islam (originally Laon and Cythna), in Spenserian stanzas, is an allegory of the French Revolution in which Laon and Cythna overthrow the tyrant Othman.

Other works include "Mutability" ("Naught may endure but Mutability"), "Music, When Soft Voices Die" ("Love itself shall slumber on"), "Stanzas Written in Dejection, Near Naples," the novel St. Irvyne about Wolfstein and Ginotti, Queen Mab (dedicated to Harriet Westbrook), "Alastor" (a man rejects an "Arab maiden" for a "veiled maid"), "A Lament" ("O World! O Life! O Time!"), and "To Wordsworth" (lamenting Wordsworth's turn from "songs consecrate to truth and liberty"). Shelley is modeled as Scythrop Glowry in Peacock's Nightmare Abbey. Gregory Corso's ashes were buried at the foot of Shelley's grave. At the Villa Diodati, Shelley wrote a "Fragment of a Ghost Story" while Mary wrote Frankenstein.
