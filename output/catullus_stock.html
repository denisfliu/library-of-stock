Catullus

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Roman lyric poet of the late Republic, known for his 116 carmina. Most commonly identified by his love poems to Lesbia (likely Clodia Pulchra, sister of Publius Clodius Pulcher), the elegy to his dead brother ending 'ave atque vale' (Carmen 101), his longest poem — the epyllion on the wedding of Peleus and Thetis (Carmen 64), and the obscene Carmen 16 threatening to sodomize Furius and Aurelius. Also known for poems about Lesbia's sparrow and the kiss poem 'Vivamus, mea Lesbia' (Carmen 5).

Summary of Facts

Catullus (Gaius Valerius Catullus) was a Roman lyric poet of the late Republic, a Neoteric poet associated with the Hellenistic/Alexandrian literary tradition. He is most commonly identified as the author of 116 carmina (the numbering goes to 116 due to Marc-Antoine Muret's 1554 edition, which wrongly attributed three poems), and as the lover of Lesbia, a pseudonym (in honor of Sappho) for what is likely Clodia Pulchra, sister of Publius Clodius Pulcher.

His most frequently clued poem is Carmen 5 ('Vivamus, mea Lesbia, atque amemus'), which opens 'Let us live, my Lesbia, and let us love,' demands 'a thousand kisses, then a hundred' (totaling 3300), tells Lesbia to value the 'rumors of old men' at 'just one penny,' and warns that 'night is one long everlasting sleep.' The poem also uses 'the stars in the tacit night' and 'sandgrains in the desert' to measure affection, and instructs the addressee to 'confuse' the number of kisses so no enemy may 'cast an evil eye.'

Carmen 101 ('Ave Atque Vale' / 'Hail and Farewell') is an elegiac poem in elegiac couplets (hexameter + pentameter) addressed to Catullus's dead brother. The speaker is 'carried through many nations and over many seas' to 'wretched funeral rites,' places gifts 'dripping much with fraternal weeping' on the grave, addresses 'silent ashes,' and notes that 'Fortune has taken your own self away from me.' The final line begins 'atque in perpetuum' ('and for eternity'). Anne Carson's Nox, a poetry box/accordion book created as a personal epitaph for her brother, draws centrally on this poem — a word-by-word translation renders the first line as 'Many the peoples many the oceans I crossed' and Carson repeats the word 'wrongly' twice in parentheses. Swinburne titled his elegy for Baudelaire 'Ave Atque Vale' in reference to this poem. Aeneas quotes from it in Book XI of the Aeneid while lamenting Pallas.

Carmen 64 is Catullus's longest poem, an epyllion in dactylic hexameter about the wedding of Peleus and Thetis. It opens with pine trees from Mount Pelion's summit swimming through Neptune's waters (describing the Argo). The poem's central feature is an extended ekphrasis on a bedspread/coverlet depicting Ariadne abandoned by Theseus, in which Ariadne is compared to a 'Bacchante's stone statue' with exposed 'milk-white breasts.' The Three Fates sing a gloomy epithalamium with the repeated line 'fly, guiding threads, fly spindle,' predicting Achilles's birth, life, and death. The poem ends with commentary on humanity's sins convincing the gods no longer to visit, 'nor endure the touch of clear daylight.' Ariosto drew on this poem for his depiction of Bireno ditching Olympia in Orlando Furioso.

Carmen 16 ('Pedicabo ego vos et irrumabo') is the notoriously obscene poem addressed to Furius and Aurelius, opening with the threat to 'sodomize and face-fuck' them. The addressees think the narrator 'less of a man' for writing about kisses, while he claims his poems can arouse 'hairy old men who can't get it up.' Kenneth Quinn called it a prime example of urbanitas wit; Michael Broder argued it is an early example of camp. NPR bleeped out even the Latin in a 2009 discussion with Mary Beard.

The sparrow poems (Carmen 2 and 3) describe Catullus envying Lesbia's pet sparrow and later mourning its death. The sparrow is called 'sweeter than honey,' and Catullus curses the 'evil Shades of Orcus' as 'destroyers of beauty.' Ovid's elegy on Corinna's parrot was inspired by these poems, and John Skelton's Phyllyp Sparowe pays homage to them.

Carmen 85 ('Odi et Amo') is a famous two-line poem: 'I hate and I love... I feel it happening and I am tortured.' Carmen 51 translates Sappho 31 — 'He seems to me to be equal to a god' — using the Sapphic stanza. The Juventius poems address a young man; Catullus feels guilty for stealing 'a kiss more sweet than sweet ambrosia' and being 'crucified on the highest cross.' Carmen 63 describes the priest Attis's self-mutilation out of devotion to Cybele in galliambic meter. Catullus translated Callimachus's Lock of Berenice into Latin, and his first poem acknowledges the Hellenistic tradition: 'to whom shall I dedicate this new slim little book?' Louis and Celia Zukofsky created a controversial homophonic translation of Catullus. Catullus used hendecasyllabic meter in 43 of his poems. His surviving works descend from a lost Verona manuscript (the O source is one of three direct descendants). He was popularized in 17th-century France via Jean de la Chapelle's novel. Yeats's 'The Scholars' ends with the possibility of Catullus 'walk[ing] that way!' and Swinburne addresses him as 'my brother.' Robinson Jeffers references him in 'Be Angry at the Sun': 'you are not Catullus you know, to lampoon these crude sketches of Caesar.'
