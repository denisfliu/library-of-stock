Elizabethan English playwright and poet, contemporary of Shakespeare. Most commonly identified by Doctor Faustus ('Was this the face that launched a thousand ships'), The Jew of Malta (Barabas, Machiavel prologue), Tamburlaine, Edward II, "The Passionate Shepherd to His Love" ('Come live with me and be my love'), and Hero and Leander. Known for his 'mighty line' of blank verse. Stabbed to death in 1593 under suspicious circumstances.

Summary of Facts

Christopher Marlowe was an Elizabethan English playwright and poet, a contemporary of Shakespeare and a 'University Wit' known for his 'mighty line' of blank verse. He was stabbed to death in 1593 at a house in Deptford in the company of Robert Poley, Nicholas Skeres, and Ingram Frizer, all associates of Thomas Walsingham. His death may have been connected to the 'Baines note' (which quoted him saying 'they that love not Tobacco and Boys are Fools'), the 'Dutch Church Libel,' and his reputed atheism. Charles Nicholl analyzed his death in The Reckoning, and Anthony Burgess's last novel fictionalized it.

Doctor Faustus is about a scholar who sells his soul to Mephistopheles. Faustus conjures Helen of Troy and exclaims 'Was this the face that launch'd a thousand ships' and 'make me immortal with a kiss.' The Latin words 'homo fuge' appear on his arm. He plays tricks on the Pope (stealing items invisibly, punching him), puts cuckold horns on knights, and has the Seven Deadly Sins paraded for his entertainment. Valdes and Cornelius assist him. The play contains the first notable English use of 'che sera, sera.' T.S. Eliot wrote an essay on the blank verse of Marlowe.

The Jew of Malta opens with a prologue by Machiavel, who declares 'I count religion but a childish toy / And hold there is no sin but ignorance.' The scheming merchant Barabas, with his servant Ithamore, poisons a convent of nuns including his daughter Abigail. Barabas drinks 'poppy and cold mandrake juice' to fake his death and ultimately dies falling into a boiling cauldron — the trap he had set for Calymath.

Tamburlaine the Great is a two-part play about a Scythian shepherd who conquers western Asia, woos Zenocrate, and uses the Turkish emperor Bajazeth as a footstool or keeps him in a cage. Bajazeth 'brains himself against' the cage and his wife commits suicide similarly. Tamburlaine burns the Qu'ran in an act of religious defiance and dies afterward. The prologue attacks 'jigging veins of rhyming mother-wits.' Tamburlaine declares 'Nature, that framed us of four elements... doth teach us all to have aspiring minds.' A captain's widow tricks her captor into stabbing her in the throat.

Edward II depicts the king's relationship with his favorite Piers Gaveston, who is killed by Warwick. Gaveston observes 'the mightiest kings have had their minions.' Edward is murdered by Lightborn (name anglicized from 'Lucifer') with a red-hot poker. The title of Aldous Huxley's Antic Hay comes from this play.

'The Passionate Shepherd to His Love' begins 'Come live with me and be my love' and promises pleasures including 'a gown made of the finest wool,' 'a cap of flowers, and a kirtle / Embroidered all with leaves of myrtle,' and 'a belt of straw and Ivy buds, With Coral clasps and Amber studs.' Walter Raleigh wrote 'The Nymph's Reply to the Shepherd' in response. John Donne's 'The Bait' and Dorothy Parker also wrote replies. The milkmaid Maudlin sings it in Izaak Walton's The Compleat Angler.

Hero and Leander is an unfinished erotic narrative poem completed by George Chapman in four additional sestiads. It opens 'On Hellespont, guilty of true-love's blood,' describes Leander's beauty ('how smooth his breast was, and how white his belly'), and contains the famous line 'Who ever lov'd, that lov'd not at first sight?' (quoted in Shakespeare's As You Like It). Neptune mistakes Leander for Ganymede.

Other works include Dido, Queen of Carthage (based on the Aeneid; Jupiter dandles Ganymede), The Massacre at Paris (Duke of Guise), and a Latin elegy for Roger Manwood. The New Oxford Shakespeare (2016) attributed the Henry VI plays to Marlowe and Shakespeare using 'big data.' Peter Whelan's play The School of Night depicts Marlowe's death.
