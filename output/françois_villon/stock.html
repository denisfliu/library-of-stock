Medieval French poet and criminal active in 15th-century Paris. His masterwork Le Grand Testament (c. 1461) is a long collection in octosyllabic verse containing sixteen ballades, presented as a dying man's reflection on misspent youth. Its most famous poem, 'The Ballad of Dead Ladies' (Ballade des Dames du temps jadis), is the archetypal ubi sunt poem, ending each stanza with 'Where are the snows of yesteryear?' — a refrain translated by Dante Gabriel Rossetti, who coined the English word 'yesteryear.' Another embedded poem, 'The Ballad of the Hanged Men,' is spoken by executed men asking the living for pity. Swinburne called him 'our sad bad glad mad brother.' Villon's works influenced Ezra Pound, Bertolt Brecht (who lifted four songs from Villon translations for The Threepenny Opera), and Jonathan Littell. He killed the priest Philippe Sarmoise and disappeared after banishment in 1463.

Summary of Facts

François Villon was a medieval French poet and criminal active in 15th-century Paris, known for writing about the Parisian underworld. He was arrested after killing the priest Philippe Sarmoise, attended the Sorbonne (where a lost poem 'The Romance of the Devil's Fart' describes a prank he played), and composed Le Grand Testament shortly before his banishment and disappearance in 1463. Swinburne called him 'our sad bad glad mad brother' and 'Prince of All Ballad-Makers.'

His most frequently clued work is 'The Ballad of Dead Ladies' (Ballade des Dames du temps jadis), an ubi sunt poem — the archetypal example of the genre — that ends each stanza with 'Where are the snows of yesteryear?' (Mais où sont les neiges d'antan?). The poem asks about famous women including Héloïse ('the learned nun' who made Abelard 'lose manhood and put priesthood on'), Joan of Arc (the last figure mentioned), Flora ('the lovely Roman'), Hipparchia, Thais, and Queen Blanche, as well as Charlemagne. It references Jean Buridan's apocryphal drowning in the Seine. Dante Gabriel Rossetti's English translation is the most famous version — he coined the English word 'yesteryear' to translate 'antan.' The famous refrain is projected on screen at the start of Tennessee Williams's The Glass Menagerie, and Madame Arcati recites it in French in Noel Coward's Blithe Spirit. Part of the line supplies the title of a Gregor von Rezzori memoir about life in Czernowitz.

'The Ballad of the Hanged Men' (Ballade des Pendus) is spoken by executed men who ask the living for pity: 'Brothers, men who live after us, let not your hearts be hardened against us.' The bodies are 'rinsed and laundered by the rain, and by the sunlight dried and blackened too,' swaying 'at the wind's fantasy,' 'pitted like thimbles,' with 'skin has more bird peckings than if it were sewed.' Each stanza ends with 'But pray God that he wills to absolve us all!' This poem was used as the epigraph to Truman Capote's In Cold Blood, and Charlotte Mandell suggested the opening of Jonathan Littell's The Kindly Ones echoes it.

Le Grand Testament is Villon's masterwork, a long collection in octosyllabic (eight-syllable) verse containing sixteen ballades. Based on the earlier poem Le Petit Testament (The Legacy / Le Lais), it is presented as the work of an old, dying man reflecting on his misspent youth, and contains 'Item' stanzas bequeathing possessions to real and hypothetical people. It opens by describing Villon's humiliation at the hands of Bishop Thibault d'Aussigny. Ezra Pound's 'With Usura' Canto quotes a phrase from Le Testament describing 'harps and lutes' adorning an image of heaven on a church wall. The ballade form used throughout is one of three formes fixes (along with the rondeau and virelai) commonly set to music in medieval France.

Other notable poems include 'The Regrets of the Beautiful Heaulmière,' in which an old 'Armouress' wishes to 'be a girl again' and declares 'this is the end of human beauty' — this poem inspired Rodin's sculpture 'She Who Was Once the Helmet-Maker's Beautiful Wife' in The Gates of Hell; and 'The Ballad of Fat Margot,' in which the speaker threatens to pawn a prostitute's clothes. Villon wrote a paradoxical self-description of 'dying of thirst beside the fountain,' and published a joking quatrain before his death: 'my neck will learn the weight of my ass.' Four songs in Brecht's Threepenny Opera were lifted from Karl Anton Klammer's translations of Villon. Rabelais included anecdotes about him in Gargantua and Pantagruel, Clément Marot edited his works, and Robert Louis Stevenson wrote 'A Lodging for the Night' about him. Osip Mandelstam published an essay on Villon in the magazine Apollon. The artists Jacques Duchamp and Raymond Duchamp-Villon adopted the surname Villon in his honor.
