West German filmmaker (1945–1982), a central figure of New German Cinema. Bisexual; died at 37 having made over 40 films. Signed the Oberhausen Manifesto ('Papa's cinema is dead'). Major films: Ali: Fear Eats the Soul (1974, homage to Douglas Sirk), The Marriage of Maria Braun (1979, first of the BRD Trilogy), The Bitter Tears of Petra von Kant (1972), and the 15-hour Berlin Alexanderplatz TV miniseries (1980). Frequent cinematographer: Michael Ballhaus.
Ali: Fear Eats the Soul (1974)

Elderly German cleaning woman Emmi (Brigitte Mira) falls in love with and marries young Moroccan guest worker Ali (El Hedi ben Salem). The couple face ostracism and prejudice. In an outdoor cafe scene, the staff stare at them in an alienating manner. Emmi's son kicks in her television set at a family gathering — one of several references to Douglas Sirk's All That Heaven Allows, which the film directly homages. Ali is told by Emmi he must stop getting couscous. The final shot shows Emmi at Ali's hospital bedside — he has a stomach ulcer. Fassbinder himself plays Eugen, Emmi's bitter son-in-law. At the opening, a character tells the old man she is dancing with: 'Think much, cry much.' The film was also a reference point for Todd Haynes's Far From Heaven and John Waters's Polyester.

The Bitter Tears of Petra von Kant (1972)

Set in a bedroom dominated by a large reproduction of Poussin's Midas and Bacchus. The fashion designer Petra lures Karin (a fashion model) and begins a lesbian relationship. The silent assistant Marlene packs her suitcase and leaves at the end. Marlene and Petra dance disconsolately to 'Smoke Gets In Your Eyes' by The Platters. The film inspired the fictional play 'Maloja Snake' in Oliver Assayas's film Clouds of Sils Maria. The American Soldier, another Fassbinder film, ends with two men writhing on the ground to his co-written song 'Too Much Tenderness.'

The Marriage of Maria Braun (1979) and BRD Trilogy

Maria Braun (Hanna Schygulla) kills the African-American soldier Bill as he brawls with her husband Hermann; Hermann takes the blame and goes to prison. Maria becomes the mistress of industrialist Karl Oswald. The film ends with a gas explosion. It is the first film of Fassbinder's BRD (Federal Republic of Germany) Trilogy, together with Lola (1981) and Veronika Voss (1982). Lola adapts the same source material as The Blue Angel: building commissioner von Bohm is corrupted by the prostitute Lola. Phoenix (2014, Christian Petzold) was influenced by The Marriage of Maria Braun.

Berlin Alexanderplatz (1980)

A 15-hour TV miniseries (sometimes described as 13 parts and an epilogue) based on Alfred Doblin's novel. Begins in 1928 when Franz Biberkopf is released from Tegel prison after killing his wife — the first section is titled 'The Punishment Begins.' Franz loses his arm when his brutish frenemy Reinhold pushes him out of a getaway car. Barbara Sukowa plays Mieze, who is murdered. The hallucinatory epilogue includes: a rear-projected atom bomb explosion with Glenn Miller's 'In the Mood,' Franz hoisted on a crucifix, and two ostentatiously-dressed angels.

Career, Style, and New German Cinema

Fassbinder was bisexual and died at age 37 having already made over 40 films. He was a major figure of New German Cinema alongside Werner Herzog and Wim Wenders. He signed the Oberhausen Manifesto ('Papa's cinema is dead'), a founding document of New German Cinema. His melodramatic period began with The Merchant of Four Seasons (fruit peddler protagonist). His cinematographer was Michael Ballhaus. He was heavily influenced by Douglas Sirk's American melodramas. His final film was Querelle (1982), adapted from Jean Genet's novel. Tom Stoppard adapted Nabokov's Despair for Fassbinder's first English-language film (Despair, 1978). In The Third Generation, the phrase 'the world as will and idea' activates a terrorist cell secretly funded by an industrialist who profits from increased demand for security systems.

Summary of Facts

Rainer Werner Fassbinder (1945–1982) was a West German filmmaker and a central figure of New German Cinema alongside Werner Herzog and Wim Wenders. Bisexual and relentlessly prolific, he died at age 37 having made over 40 films. He signed the Oberhausen Manifesto ('Papa's cinema is dead'). His cinematographer was Michael Ballhaus. Deeply influenced by Douglas Sirk's American melodramas.

Ali: Fear Eats the Soul (1974) is a direct homage to Sirk's All That Heaven Allows: elderly German cleaning woman Emmi (Brigitte Mira) marries young Moroccan guest worker Ali (El Hedi ben Salem). A son kicks in her TV set; an outdoor cafe scene depicts the staff staring at the couple in alienating fashion. Fassbinder himself plays bitter son-in-law Eugen. The final shot shows Emmi at Ali's hospital bedside (stomach ulcer). The film also inspired Todd Haynes's Far From Heaven and John Waters's Polyester.

The Bitter Tears of Petra von Kant (1972): set in a bedroom dominated by a large reproduction of Poussin's Midas and Bacchus. Petra lures Karin to become a fashion model; lesbian relationship. Silent assistant Marlene packs her suitcase and leaves at the end; Marlene and Petra dance disconsolately to 'Smoke Gets In Your Eyes.' The film inspired the fictional play 'Maloja Snake' in Oliver Assayas's Clouds of Sils Maria.

The Marriage of Maria Braun (1979, BRD Trilogy part 1): Maria kills the African-American soldier Bill; husband Hermann takes the blame. Maria becomes industrialist Karl Oswald's mistress; ends with a gas explosion. The BRD Trilogy also includes Lola (1981, building commissioner von Bohm corrupted by prostitute Lola — same source as The Blue Angel) and Veronika Voss.

Berlin Alexanderplatz (1980): 15-hour TV miniseries (13 parts + epilogue) based on Alfred Doblin's novel. Franz Biberkopf released from Tegel prison in 1928 (first section: 'The Punishment Begins'); loses arm when Reinhold pushes him from a getaway car; Barbara Sukowa plays Mieze (murdered). Hallucinatory epilogue: rear-projected atom bomb + Glenn Miller's 'In the Mood' + Franz on a crucifix.

Fassbinder's final film was Querelle (1982, adapted from Jean Genet). Tom Stoppard adapted Nabokov's Despair for Fassbinder's first English-language film (Despair, 1978). The melodramatic period began with The Merchant of Four Seasons. The Third Generation features 'the world as will and idea' as a terrorist cell's activation phrase.
