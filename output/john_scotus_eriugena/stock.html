General / Biographical

Eriugena was an Irish philosopher active in the 9th century, part of the Carolingian Renaissance. He succeeded Alcuin as leader of the Palace School, served Charles the Bald, and is legendarily said to have been stabbed to death by his students with pens. Hegel praised him as the beginning of 'true philosophy,' and he quipped 'only a table' when a king asked what separates a Scot from a fool.

Summary of Facts

John Scotus Eriugena is a 9th-century Irish Neoplatonist philosopher whose Gaelic origin earned him the name 'Scotus,' the same nickname later given to John Duns Scotus. He was part of the Carolingian Renaissance, succeeded Alcuin of York as leader of the Palace School in Aachen, and served under Charles the Bald.

His magnum opus, the Periphyseon or The Division of Nature, is a five-book dialogue between a master and a student that defines nature as universitas rerum, 'the totality of all things,' including things that do not exist. It divides nature into four categories based on axes of creating and being created: that which creates and is not created (God as origin), that which is created and creates (primordial causes), that which is created and does not create (temporal effects), and that which neither creates nor is created (God as end). The work was suppressed for its pantheistic implications, as Eriugena claimed 'God is everything that truly is because he himself makes all things and is made in all things.' He also proposed five modes in which things can either be or not be.

Eriugena's rare knowledge of Greek allowed him to produce the first complete Latin translations of the works of Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, including The Divine Names and The Celestial Hierarchy. He believed these were by an Athenian disciple of Saint Paul. These translations helped revive Neoplatonic thought in the Latin West centuries after Justinian I closed the Platonic Academy, causing Damascius and his followers to flee to the court of Khosrau.

Hegel praised Eriugena as the beginning of 'true philosophy,' and he is famous for quipping 'only a table' when a king seated across from him asked what separates a Scot from a fool. He compared interpretations of scripture to the innumerable colors in a peacock's tail feathers and declared that 'the true philosophy is the true religion.' A legend recorded by William of Malmesbury claims he was stabbed to death by his students with their pens, though this is probably apocryphal and may result from confusion with another person.
