Italian Renaissance polymath (1452–1519). Student of Verrocchio, employed by Ludovico Sforza in Milan. Known for the Mona Lisa (portrait of Lisa del Giocondo, at the Louvre), The Last Supper (Santa Maria delle Grazie, Milan), Virgin of the Rocks (two versions), Vitruvian Man, Lady with an Ermine (Cecilia Gallerani), and Salvator Mundi (sold for $450M). Used sfumato technique. Notebooks in left-handed mirror script include the Codex Leicester (owned by Bill Gates). Modeled as Plato in Raphael's School of Athens.

Summary of Facts

Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519) was an Italian Renaissance polymath, painter, sculptor, architect, and inventor. He trained under Andrea del Verrocchio in Florence — reportedly modeled as the Archangel Raphael in Verrocchio's Tobias and the Angel — and assisted Verrocchio in creating the gold-leafed copper orb atop Florence's cathedral. He was later employed by Ludovico Sforza, Duke of Milan, a relationship that produced his most celebrated works.

The Mona Lisa, a portrait of Lisa del Giocondo (a female member of the Giocondo family) using his signature sfumato technique, resides in its own room at the Louvre, where it draws millions annually. It is being moved out of the Salle des Etats as of January 2025. It was stolen in 1911 by Vincenzo Peruggia; Guillaume Apollinaire implicated Picasso. Marcel Duchamp's 1919 L.H.O.O.Q. added a mustache and goatee to a postcard of it (letters mean 'she has hot booty'). Fernando Botero parodied the subject at 'Age Twelve'; Salvador Dali was photographed as its subject by Philippe Halsman. Pascal Cotte's scans revealed the subject originally had visible eyebrows.

The Last Supper, commissioned by Ludovico Sforza for the refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie in Milan, depicts all thirteen figures on the same side of the table. Leonardo used Jesus's cheek as the vanishing point. He painted it in an oil/tempera mixture on plaster — not true fresco — and it began decaying within months; Vasari, writing less than 60 years later, described it as a 'muddle of blots.' Henry James called it 'the saddest work of art in the world.' It has a 15-minute viewing limit. It was struck by bombs in 1943 (protected by sandbags). Judas's face is in the dark and he knocks over a salt cellar. Andy Warhol's last artwork was sixty silkscreen prints of it. Marisol sculpted herself looking at it; Mary Beth Edelson replaced its figures with women artists.

The Virgin of the Rocks exists in two versions (Louvre and London National Gallery), both showing the angel Uriel, an infant John the Baptist, and the Virgin Mary in a triangular composition set in a rocky grotto. The London version features intricately rendered Stars-of-Bethlehem flowers; the grotto was inspired by Leonardo's study of Lake Arno's fossil shoreline.

The Vitruvian Man depicts an idealized figure inscribed in two superimposed positions inside a square and circle, drawn after Vitruvius's De architectura. Backward notes describe a line through the navel as '24 palms' long. Le Corbusier's Modulor anthropometric system was inspired by it. Hutan Ashrafian has argued the figure was modeled on a corpse.

The Battle of Anghiari is a lost painting depicting four soldiers fighting for a standard, painted in the Salone dei Cinquecento of the Palazzo Vecchio, Florence. Leonardo abandoned it when his oil-on-wall technique failed — the same problem that plagued The Last Supper. It may have been painted over by Vasari's Battle of Marciano (identified by the phrase 'Cerca Trova' on a green flag). Rubens's Hippopotamus Hunt is modeled on it.

Lady with an Ermine depicts Cecilia Gallerani, Ludovico Sforza's mistress, wearing a blue and red coat and holding an ermine symbolizing her pregnancy with Sforza. The Czartoryski family named it to distinguish it from La Belle Ferronnière (a portrait of Lucretia Crivelli wearing a ferronnière headband). The earliest description of the painting is a sonnet by Bernardo Bellincioni that opens "Why are you angry? Whom do you envy, nature?" A 1497 letter requested the painting be loaned to Isabella d'Este. The background was repainted black in the 18th century; because of this and the subject's thin dark chain, it resembles La Belle Ferronnière. A 20th century restoration removed the footprint of an SS soldier. The London version of Virgin of the Rocks was part of an altarpiece flanked by Bernardino Luini (a green angel) and Ambrogio de Predis (a red angel holding a lute).

Salvator Mundi, a portrait of Jesus holding a crystal orb and raising two fingers in blessing, sold for $450.3 million in 2017 to Prince Badr for the Louvre Abu Dhabi — the most expensive painting ever sold. Its attribution to Leonardo is controversial; Walter Isaacson argued the orb is rendered unrealistically, while Martin Kemp defends attribution. The earliest known photograph of the painting was posted on a Baton Rouge real estate agency's website. Yves Bouvier was arrested for dealing it to Dmitry Rybolovlev. Paintings in the National Gallery London by Benedetto Rusconi and Andrea Previtali closely resemble it.

His notebooks run to over 7,000 pages in left-handed mirror script. The Codex Leicester was purchased by Bill Gates in 1994. The notebooks were inherited by Francesco Melzi. Leonardo was the model for the figure of Plato in Raphael's School of Athens (holding Timaeus, pointing upward). Ingres painted his deathbed attended by Francis I. He was teacher to Salai (who modeled for both John the Baptist and the homoerotic Angel in the Flesh drawing). His portrait of Ginevra de' Benci is the only Leonardo publicly viewable in the Americas (National Gallery, Washington). His earliest known commissioned painting was the Annunciation, in which a figure holds a white lily representing Florence. He drew two sketches of Leda with a Swan and produced grotesque head caricatures — the Ugly Duchess by Quentin Matsys may derive from a lost Leonardo grotesque head.
