Nicaraguan poet and founder of the modernismo literary movement in Latin America. Real name Felix Ruben Garcia Sarmiento. His most frequently clued poem is 'To Roosevelt,' an anti-imperialist poem calling the U.S. president an 'Alexander-Nebuchadnezzar' and 'professor of Energy' who is 'missing one thing: God!' His landmark 1888 collection Azul... (Blue...) kickstarted modernismo, heavily influenced by French Parnassians and Symbolists. Other major collections include Prosas Profanas and Songs of Life and Hope (Cantos de Vida y Esperanza). Used the swan as a recurring symbol. Known for 'Symphony in Gray Major,' 'Sonatina,' 'The Bourgeois King,' 'Autumnal,' and 'Decalogue of the Artist.'
"To Roosevelt"

Dario's most frequently clued single poem, an anti-imperialist address to Theodore Roosevelt. Calls him 'Alexander-Nebuchadnezzar,' 'professor of Energy,' and 'the deadly Rifleman and the dreadful Hunter.' Ends with 'although you count on everything, you lack one thing: God!' Invokes 'the voice of the Bible, or the verse of Walt Whitman.' Warns that 'a thousand cubs of the Spanish lion are roaming free.' Describes 'the future invader / of the naive America who has Indian blood.' Says the addressee 'joins the cult of Hercules and the cult of Mammon.' First appeared in Songs of Life and Hope. Azul... (Blue...)

Dario's landmark 1888 collection that kickstarted the modernismo movement in Latin American literature. Heavily influenced by French Parnassians and Symbolists. Sections include 'Cuentos en Prosa' (prose tales), 'En Chile,' and 'El Ano Lirico' (The Lyrical Year, containing four seasonal poems). Contains stories like 'The Bourgeois King' (a hungry poet forced to crank a music box), 'The Ruby,' and the 'Veil of Queen Mab.' Includes characters from fairy tale: Walt Whitman, Queen Mab, and Puck. Two letters from Juan Valera praising the collection (noting 'mental Gallicism') were included as a prologue. Five 'medallions' praising authors like Leconte de Lisle and Catulle Mendes were added in the second edition. Opens with a Victor Hugo epigraph. Wikipedia

"Symphony in Gray Major"

A poem that opens with 'The sea like a vast silvered mirror reflects the sky like a sheet of zinc.' Describes an 'old sea-dog' sleeping in 'the siesta of the tropics,' thinking of distant beaches. The sea-dog's 'curly hair' and 'athletic biceps' are known by the 'iodine and saltpeter foam.' Features an 'old cicada' with its 'senile, raucous guitar.' Found in Prosas Profanas. Incorporated assonant rhyme with words stressed on the antepenultimate syllable as a modernist element. Wikipedia

Swan Poems

Dario frequently used the swan as a recurring symbol for beauty, poetry, and himself. 'The Swan' (El Cisne) describes how 'the new Poetry' is born under the swan's wings and begins 'It happened in a divine moment for the human species.' 'I Pursue a Form' describes 'the question posed by a white swan's neck.' A sonnet explains the swan 'used to sing only to die' but a 'Wagnerian' one sings 'in order to live again.' 'Swans' asks 'Is it our fate that millions of us will speak in English?' and notes the 'question mark' of the swan's neck. Lohengrin is referenced. Enrique Gonzalez Martinez wrote a poem criticizing the swan as 'deceptive,' suggesting an owl would be better.

General / Biographical

Nicaraguan poet, father/founder of modernismo in Latin American literature. Real name Felix Ruben Garcia Sarmiento. Influenced by French Symbolists and Parnassians. Claimed 'I think in French!' after memorizing a Dictionary of Gallicisms. Said his style was 'Spanish verse... passed through the sieve of French verse.' Said his wife was Spanish but 'his mistress is in Paris.' Described by Octavio Paz as more like Victor Hugo than his idol Paul Verlaine in 'The Siren and the Seashell.' Leopoldo Lugones and Jose Marti joined the modernismo movement. In Garcia Marquez's The Autumn of the Patriarch, the dictator asks Dario how he can write so beautifully. Giannina Braschi uses his epigraph 'If Segismundo grieves, Hamlet feels it' in United States of Banana. Crossed paths with Antonio Machado in Paris. Parra's poem jokingly lists 'Chile's four great poets / are three: / Alonso de Ercilla' and Dario. Wrote about Lautreamont in The Misfits. Tried to commit suicide in Cuba in 1910.

Summary of Facts

Ruben Dario (real name Felix Ruben Garcia Sarmiento) is a Nicaraguan poet universally recognized as the founder of the modernismo literary movement in Latin America. His two most important works for quiz purposes are the poem 'To Roosevelt' and the collection Azul... (Blue...), which together account for the vast majority of giveaway and mid-level clues.

'To Roosevelt' is Dario's most frequently clued individual poem, an anti-imperialist address to Theodore Roosevelt. It calls the president 'Alexander-Nebuchadnezzar' and 'professor of Energy,' ends with the accusation 'although you count on everything, you lack one thing: God!,' and invokes 'the voice of the Bible, or the verse of Walt Whitman.' It warns that 'a thousand cubs of the Spanish lion are roaming free,' describes the U.S. as 'the future invader / of the naive America who has Indian blood,' and accuses Roosevelt of joining 'the cult of Hercules and the cult of Mammon.' The poem first appeared in Songs of Life and Hope.

Azul... (1888) is the landmark collection that kickstarted modernismo. Heavily influenced by French Parnassians and Symbolists, it contains sections titled 'Cuentos en Prosa,' 'En Chile,' and 'El Ano Lirico' (The Lyrical Year, with four seasonal poems). Stories include 'The Bourgeois King' (a hungry poet forced to crank a music box), 'The Ruby,' and the 'Veil of Queen Mab,' with characters including Walt Whitman, Queen Mab, and Puck. Two letters from Juan Valera noting Dario's 'mental Gallicism' were included as a prologue. Five 'medallions' praising authors like Leconte de Lisle and Catulle Mendes were added in the second edition.

'Symphony in Gray Major' opens with 'The sea like a vast silvered mirror reflects the sky like a sheet of zinc' and describes an 'old sea-dog' sleeping in 'the siesta of the tropics,' thinking of distant beaches, with the 'iodine and saltpeter foam' knowing his 'curly hair' and 'athletic biceps.' It appears in Prosas Profanas and incorporated assonant rhyme.

Songs of Life and Hope (Cantos de Vida y Esperanza) contains 'To Roosevelt,' the 'Swans' section (related to Leda), 'In Autumn,' 'To King Oscar,' and 'Poets! Towers of God!' It includes 'there is no pain greater than living / nor sorrow greater than the conscious life.'

Dario frequently used the swan as a symbol for beauty, poetry, and himself. 'The Swan' (El Cisne) describes 'the new Poetry' being born under the swan's wings. 'I Pursue a Form' describes 'the question posed by a white swan's neck.' 'Swans' asks 'Is it our fate that millions of us will speak in English?' Enrique Gonzalez Martinez later criticized the swan as 'deceptive.'

Other poems include 'Autumnal' (fairy maiden guides the speaker through a landscape; repeated cries of 'More!'), 'In Autumn' ('an aged tree'; 'let the hurricane swirl my heart to song'), 'Sonatina' (princess guarded by hound, dragon, and hundred lancers; adapted French alexandrines to 14-syllable meter), 'To Columbus' ('droplets of refined French sap' with 'half-Spanish, half-indigenous mouth'), 'Leda' ('sparkle-eyed Pan watches'), and 'Margarita Debayle.'

Biographically, Dario was influenced by French Symbolists and claimed 'I think in French!' He said his style was 'Spanish verse passed through the sieve of French verse' and described himself in the introduction to Prosas Profanas as having his wife be Spanish but 'his mistress in Paris.' Octavio Paz described him as more like Victor Hugo than his idol Verlaine in 'The Siren and the Seashell.' Leopoldo Lugones and Jose Marti joined the modernismo movement he sparked. In Garcia Marquez's The Autumn of the Patriarch, the dictator asks Dario how he writes so beautifully. Giannina Braschi uses his epigraph 'If Segismundo grieves, Hamlet feels it' in United States of Banana.
