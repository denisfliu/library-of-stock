A Ming Dynasty novel attributed to Wu Cheng'en, one of the Four Great Classical Novels of China. It follows the Buddhist monk Xuanzang (also called Tang Sanzang or Tripitaka) on a pilgrimage to India (Vulture Peak) to retrieve Buddhist scriptures, accompanied by Sun Wukong (the Monkey King), Pigsy (Zhu Bajie), and Sandy. Based on the historical monk Xuanzang's real journey to India in the 7th century. Arthur Waley's abridged English translation (30 of 100 chapters) is titled Monkey.
Sun Wukong (the Monkey King)

The central heroic figure of the novel. Born from a stone egg, he becomes king of the monkeys after jumping through a waterfall to discover a cave behind it at Flower Fruit Mountain. He wields a shape-changing iron staff obtained from the Dragon King of the Eastern Sea (Ao Guang). He possesses seventy-two transformations and the ability to cloud-somersault. He once angered his master Subodhi by transforming into a pine tree. Subodhi conferred the name 'Sun Wukong' upon him to reward his awareness of the vacuity of the universe. He loses a bet when he cannot somersault out of Buddha's hand. He is captured during a fight when hit in the head by a bracelet thrown from heaven. He is freed from beneath a mountain after agreeing to help the monk retrieve Buddhist texts. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Journey to the West is a Ming Dynasty novel attributed to Wu Cheng'en, and one of the Four Great Classical Novels of China. It has 100 chapters and follows the Buddhist monk Xuanzang (also known as Tang Sanzang or Tripitaka) on a pilgrimage to Vulture Peak in India to obtain Buddhist scriptures. He is accompanied by Sun Wukong (the Monkey King), Pigsy (Zhu Bajie), and Sandy, the latter two being demoted demigods. The novel originates in popular tales about the real 7th-century monk Xuanzang's journey to India, which is also documented in the Great Tang Records on the Western Regions, preserving descriptions of Nalanda, Tashkent, Samarkand, and Bactria. Arthur Waley's abridged English translation, covering 30 of 100 chapters, is titled Monkey.

Sun Wukong is the central hero. Born from a stone egg, he becomes king of the monkeys after jumping through a waterfall at Flower Fruit Mountain, discovering a cave behind it with a stone tablet describing 'the blessed land of Flower Fruit Mountain.' He acquires his shape-changing iron staff (which can shrink and grow to any size) from the Dragon King of the Eastern Sea (Ao Guang), whose palace is guarded by shrimps and crabs. He possesses seventy-two transformations and the cloud-somersault ability. His master Subodhi conferred the name 'Sun Wukong' upon him to reward his awareness of the vacuity of the universe, but the Monkey King angered Subodhi by transforming into a pine tree. Sun Wukong loses a bet when he fails to somersault out of Buddha's hand. He is captured during a fight when struck in the head by a bracelet thrown from heaven, and is freed from beneath a mountain after agreeing to help the monk on his quest. He also journeys to the Chinese underworld, meeting the Ten Yama Kings at the capital Youdu.

Pigsy uses a nine-toothed rake as a weapon and is tasked with cleaning altars. He is characterized as the hard-working suitor who kidnaps the daughter of a village elder and consumes more food than the farm produces. Tang Sanzang rides a white horse that is actually a dragon, and demons attempt to eat his flesh multiple times throughout the journey. The novel also features the Ox King.

A 17th-century supplement to the novel features bizarre dreams inflicted by a spirit called the 'fish spirit of sexual desire,' including time travel through a fish demon's dream world. In one notable episode from the main novel, Sun Wukong transforms into a fly and enters a demon's body to beat her up, forcing her to give up a banana leaf fan that allows the group to pass flaming mountains.

A Chinese aphorism states 'when you're young, you shouldn't read this work,' quoted in Xuan Juliana Wang's short story collection Home Remedies. The novel has been referenced in various cultural contexts, including the Dragon Ball character Son Goku, who is based on Sun Wukong.
