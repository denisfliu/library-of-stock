Cold Comfort Farm

A comic novel following Flora Poste as she moves to the titular rural property in the Sussex village of Howling to stay with her relatives, the Starkadder family. It is a parody of the 'loam and lovechild' tradition of rural English fiction by authors like Sheila Kaye-Smith and Mary Webb. Flora efficiently reorganizes the chaotic lives of the Starkadders.

Summary of Facts

Stella Gibbons is a British author clued in quizbowl almost exclusively through her comic novel Cold Comfort Farm. That novel follows Flora Poste, whose education is described as 'expensive, athletic, and prolonged,' as she moves to the Sussex village of Howling to stay with her relatives, the Starkadder family. Flora efficiently solves the problems of the Starkadders during her stay. The tyrannical matriarch Aunt Ada Doom repeatedly claims she once 'saw something nasty in the woodshed,' one of the novel's most iconic and frequently clued lines. The preacher Cousin Amos delivers a sermon to the Church of the Quivering Brethren declaring 'there'll be no butter in Hell.' The farmhand Adam Lambsbreath uses twigs for 'clettering' and is too proud of the mop Flora buys him. Some residents of the farm bear nicknames including Aimless, Feckless, Graceless, Pointless, and Big Business. A pompous writer named Mr. Mybug works on a biography arguing that Branwell Bronte was the true genius of the Bronte siblings. Flora meets Earl Neck, an American movie producer, at a screening of the Norwegian-Japanese film 'Yes,' and Neck turns Flora's cousin Seth into the star of 'Small-Town Sheik.' Near the climax, Neck retorts 'Did it see you?' while dragging Seth away from a wedding. Rooms in the farmhouse are labeled 'Quiete Retreate' and 'Greate laundrie,' prompting Flora to compare the place to 'being locked in the Victoria and Albert Museum after closing time.' The novel frequently mentions the plant 'sukebind.' Especially turgid passages are preceded by Baedeker-style star ratings of their prose quality. Cold Comfort Farm is a parody of the rural 'loam and lovechild' literary tradition, specifically targeting authors like Sheila Kaye-Smith and Mary Webb, and it may also parody John Cowper Powys's novel Wolf Solent. Flora says her idea of hell is 'a large party in a cold room where everybody has to play hockey properly,' and she draws strength from The Pensees of the Abbe Fausse-Maigre. The novel is set in the fictional Sussex village of Howling.
