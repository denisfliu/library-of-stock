Summary of Facts

Isak Dinesen (pen name of Karen Blixen) was a Danish author known for her collection Seven Gothic Tales and the memoir Out of Africa. She was pseudonymous, using a male pen name. She originally wrote in English.

'Babette's Feast' is her most frequently clued story, serving as the primary giveaway. The title character is a former French Communard who fled Paris (her husband and son were killed in the uprising) and works as a cook for the elderly Norwegian sisters Martine and Philippa in the town of Berlevaag. The dean's daughters are named after Martin Luther and Philipp Melanchthon. The opera star Achille Papin introduces Babette via letter; he had previously given one sister singing lessons but stopped after impulsively kissing her during a duet from Don Giovanni. Babette wins ten thousand francs in the lottery and spends all of it on an extravagant French dinner celebrating the Dean's 100th birthday. A turtle arriving in the kitchen frightens the devout Protestant sisters. General Lorens Loewenhielm attends and excitedly exclaims he is eating 'Blinis Demidoff.' Babette declares 'a great artist, Madame, is never poor' and 'Give me leave to do my utmost!' She is told 'how you will enchant the angels.' In another story, a sailor-boy is described as her 'red-haired familiar.'

'The Deluge at Norderney' is the first story in Seven Gothic Tales. A great flood causes travelers to shelter in a granary, where they exchange stories (compared to Scheherazade). Miss Malin Nat-og-Dag (who almost cut off her own breasts as a girl but was stopped by a painting of nymphs in a mirror), Jonathan Maersk, and Countess Calypso tell tales. Miss Malin convinces Calypso and Maersk to marry. A cardinal reveals he is actually the valet Kasparson, who tells 'The Wine of the Tetrarch.' Kasparson kisses Miss Nat-og-Dag. Timon of Assens learns his singing is only popular because he is a baron's illegitimate son and is told that salt water (sweat, tears, or sea) is 'the cure for everything.'

Out of Africa describes Dinesen's time on a coffee plantation in the Ngong Hills of Kenya with her husband. She details her romance with Denys Finch Hatton, who gave her a gramophone and died in a plane crash (his brother placed an obelisk on his grave with lines from 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner'). Characters include Kamante, a sick boy she sends to the hospital whose leg sores won't heal, who later becomes her cook and prepares a meal for The Prince of Wales. Berkeley Cole drinks champagne every morning. Kinanjui leads the laborers on her failing estate. The book discusses The Trial of a man who killed Kitosch and a Kyama determining responsibility for Wamai's death. Ngugi wa Thiong'o condemned a passage comparing Kamante to a 'civilized dog' and called it 'one of the most dangerous books ever written.' Dinesen adopted an antelope she named Lulu.

'The Supper at Elsinore' features the De Coninck sisters Fanny and Eliza conversing with the ghost of their brother Morton, who tells them of his career as a pirate. 'The Monkey' features the Prioress of Cloister Seven (Abbey Six) scheming to marry her nephew Boris (who is homosexual) to the unwilling Athena Hopballehus. The prioress magically switches places with the title animal; her lace cap is torn off revealing her true identity. Boris fights and rapes his fiancee after discussing the King of Ava's elephants at dinner. A character sends a woman to seduce Saufe, who tries to build wings to talk to angels.

'The Roads Round Pisa' features the impotent Prince Potenziani who asks Count Nino to impregnate his wife Rosina; unfortunately, both wives switched. The cross-dressing Agnese interrupts a duel. 'Sorrow-Acre' tells of a peasant woman who dies saving her son Goske from prison by mowing a field in one day. 'The Dreamers' features Pellegrina Leoni, an opera singer whose various identities (a revolutionary milliner, monarchist Madame Rosalba, and an Englishman's lover Olalla) are revealed. 'The Old Chevalier' features Baron von Brackel recalling sleeping with a woman he didn't know was a prostitute; fifteen years later an artist shows him a skull he recognizes as hers. 'The Poet' features a councilor/struggling writer killed by a stone thrown by Fransine. 'The Revenge of Truth' is a marionette play where a witch curses an inn so every lie is revealed as truth. Dinesen classified women as housewives, guardian angels, dancers, or witches in the essay 'Daguerreotypes.' She described Sophus Claussen as the only 'free' Danish poet. Dorothy Canfield Fisher wrote the introduction to Seven Gothic Tales.
