Gottfried Leibniz

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
German polymath and rationalist philosopher of the 17th-18th centuries. Co-discovered calculus independently of Newton. Author of Monadology, Discourse on Metaphysics, Theodicy, and New Essays on Human Understanding. Developed the concepts of monads ('windowless' simple substances), the identity of indiscernibles, pre-established harmony, the principle of sufficient reason, and the claim that we live in the 'best of all possible worlds' (satirized as Pangloss in Voltaire's Candide).
Monads and the Monadology

The Monadology (1714) describes the universe as composed of 'windowless' simple substances called monads, which have perception and appetite but no spatial extension. They exist in a three-tiered hierarchy of entelechies, souls, and spirits, and interact only through God's pre-established harmony. Leibniz borrowed the term 'entelechies' from Aristotle to describe entities with an inner self-directed nature. The 'mill argument' imagines enlarging a thinking machine enough to walk inside it, and David Chalmers claims it inspired the philosophical zombie thought experiment. Anne Conway's 'spirit particles' from her Principles of the Most Ancient and Modern Philosophy are believed to have inspired the concept of monads. Deleuze interpreted monads as 'folds' in time and space in his book The Fold.

Identity of Indiscernibles (Leibniz's Law)

Leibniz's Law states that if two objects share all of the same properties, they must be identical. It was first explicitly formulated in the Discourse on Metaphysics and defended via the principle of sufficient reason in correspondence with Samuel Clarke. Max Black's counterexample of two identical iron spheres in a perfectly symmetric universe is the most famous objection. The 'masked man' fallacy is an incorrect application of the principle. Quine developed a 'maxim of the identification of indiscernibles' based on it. Kant argued against it using raindrops in the 'Amphiboly' section of the Critique of Pure Reason.

Best of All Possible Worlds and the Theodicy

Leibniz's Theodicy (Theodicee, 1710) defends philosophical optimism using the principle of sufficient reason, arguing God created the 'best of all possible worlds' — one that is 'simplest in hypotheses and richest in phenomena.' The work ends with a goddess guiding Theodorus to the top hall of an infinite pyramid. Satirized by Voltaire as Dr. Pangloss in Candide. Alvin Plantinga called his assumption that this is the best world 'Leibniz's lapse.' The Discourse on Metaphysics clarifies the claim.

Discourse on Metaphysics and Truth Theory

The Discourse on Metaphysics (1686) introduces the 'complete individual concept' — every substance has a concept containing everything that will ever happen to it. Leibniz illustrated this with Alexander the Great's soul and Julius Caesar crossing the Rubicon. The work also distinguishes between necessary truths (grounded in non-contradiction) and contingent truths (grounded in the principle of sufficient reason). Haecceity (from Duns Scotus) is invoked to argue something is only a substance if it has a complete concept.

New Essays on Human Understanding

Leibniz's New Essays on Human Understanding is a chapter-by-chapter refutation of John Locke's Essay Concerning Human Understanding, written as a dialogue between Theophilus (Leibniz) and Philalethes (Locke). It defends innate ideas against Locke's tabula rasa, arguing the mind is not a 'blank tablet' but a 'veined block of marble.' It also contains his exploration of 'petites perceptions' (unconscious perceptions) and the claim 'nothing is in the intellect which was not first in the senses, except the intellect itself.'

General / Biographical and Calculus

Common identifiers: German philosopher/polymath, co-discovered calculus independently of Newton, author of Monadology. The Newton-Leibniz calculus controversy involved accusations that Leibniz plagiarized Newton's work on quadrature. Leibniz published the relevant material in Nova Methodus pro maximis et minimis and created the integral symbol. He corresponded with Samuel Clarke (a Newton ally) until his death in 1716, debating absolute vs. relational space. His characteristica universalis was a universal language project drawing on Chinese characters. Bertrand Russell argued (now rejected) that Leibniz kept his 'true philosophy' to himself.

Summary of Facts

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716) is among the most heavily clued philosophers in quizbowl, with a rich and varied set of questions spanning metaphysics, logic, mathematics, and political philosophy. The three universal giveaways are: monads ('windowless' simple substances), the best of all possible worlds, and co-discovering calculus with Newton.

The Monadology describes all reality as composed of simple, indivisible, extensionless substances called monads that have perception and appetite but are 'windowless' — they do not interact causally. Instead, God has synchronized them through pre-established harmony. Leibniz borrowed the term 'entelechies' from Aristotle for entities with self-directed inner nature, and organized monads in a hierarchy of entelechies, souls, and spirits. Anne Conway's 'spirit particles' are believed to have inspired the concept. The 'mill argument' (imagine enlarging a thinking machine to walk inside it) inspired David Chalmers's philosophical zombie.

The identity of indiscernibles (Leibniz's Law) states that two objects sharing all properties must be identical. Max Black's famous counterexample posits two iron spheres in a perfectly symmetric universe. Leibniz defended it via the principle of sufficient reason in his correspondence with Samuel Clarke, where he also argued against Newton's absolute space using 'static shift' and 'kinematic shift' arguments.

The Theodicy defends philosophical optimism: God created the best of all possible worlds, one that is 'simplest in hypotheses and richest in phenomena.' Voltaire satirized this as Dr. Pangloss in Candide. Plantinga called the assumption 'Leibniz's lapse.'

The Discourse on Metaphysics introduces the 'complete individual concept' (every substance's concept contains everything that will ever happen to it), illustrated by Judas, Alexander the Great, and Caesar. It distinguishes necessary truths (non-contradiction) from contingent truths (sufficient reason).

New Essays on Human Understanding refutes Locke chapter by chapter, with Leibniz as Theophilus and Locke as Philalethes. It defends innate ideas, comparing the mind to a 'veined block of marble' rather than a blank slate, and introduces 'petites perceptions' (unconscious perceptions).

The calculus priority dispute with Newton is a frequent bonus topic. Leibniz published in Nova Methodus and created the integral symbol. His correspondence with Clarke debated relational vs. absolute space. His characteristica universalis project envisioned a universal language, captured in his exclamation 'Calculemus!' David Lewis's modal realism builds on Leibniz's possible worlds framework.
