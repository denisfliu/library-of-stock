American short story writer and novelist nicknamed the 'Chekhov of the Suburbs.' Known for his New Yorker stories including 'The Swimmer,' 'The Enormous Radio,' 'Goodbye, My Brother,' and 'The Five-Forty-Eight,' as well as novels The Wapshot Chronicle and Falconer. His fiction explores suburban discontent and alcoholism in mid-century America.
"The Swimmer"

Cheever's most famous story, in which Neddy Merrill decides to travel home across the county by swimming through all his neighbors' backyard pools, calling this route the 'Lucinda River' after his wife. He lives in Bullet Park. Along the way he crashes a party at the Biswangers' where Grace calls him a 'gate-crasher,' and is refused a drink by his former mistress Shirley Adams. The story is set on a midsummer day when 'everyone says I drank too much.' At the end, Neddy finds his house abandoned, locked, and empty, and smells autumnal fragrances while observing constellations, suggesting time has passed in a way he didn't notice.

"The Enormous Radio"

A story about the middle-class couple Jim and Irene Westcott, who have a 'satisfactory average of income, endeavor, and respectability.' Irene discovers that their new enormous radio allows her to listen in on their neighbors' private conversations, hearing 'demonstrations of indigestion, carnal love, abysmal vanity, faith, and despair.' A Chopin prelude is interrupted by a couple arguing. Irene becomes distressed and hysterically concludes 'Life is too terrible, too sordid and awful' before asking 'We're happy, aren't we, darling?' First published in The New Yorker in 1947. The radio first mixes in hums of electronics to Mozart's music.

"Goodbye, My Brother"

A story about the Pommeroy family vacation at their summer home at Laud's Head, which is disrupted by the pessimistic brother Lawrence, nicknamed 'Tifty.' Lawrence remarks to the backgammon player Chaddy that the house will soon fall into the sea. The narrator calls him 'a gloomy son of a bitch.' The story ends with the narrator hitting Lawrence with a root on the beach, then watching the naked bodies of his wife and sister walking out of the sea. Several characters attend a ball dressed as brides and football players.

The Wapshot Chronicle

Cheever's first novel, set in the sleepy New England fishing town of St. Botolphs. The patriarch Leander, a retired captain, lost his ship the Topaze while crossing the Nagansakit. His sons Moses and Coverly try to impress their aunt Honora, who purchases a boat named the Topaze. Sarah converts the wrecked Topaze into 'The Only Floating Gift Shoppe in New England.' The bisexual Coverly works as a Taper on Island 93. The novel ends with Coverly discovering a letter titled 'Advice to my sons' by Leander, who drowned himself. Coverly is also a Eugenio Montale translator.

While in prison, he engages in a love affair with another prisoner named Jody and suffers from methadone withdrawal. The heroin-addicted Farragut is sent to the title prison.

"The Five-Forty-Eight"

A story about Blake, a businessman who is stalked by his former secretary Miss Dent on the title commuter train. Miss Dent compares herself to Chicken-Licken while accosting Blake at gunpoint. A letter addressed 'Dear Husband' includes the image of a volcano erupting with blood. The story ends with Blake being forced to rub his face in the dirt after getting off the train. Miss Dent recognizes her own insanity but feels sorry for Blake.

Other Stories and Works

Cheever wrote several other notable stories. 'The Country Husband' features Francis Weed (or Moon) falling in love with a babysitter in the fictional suburb of Shady Hill. 'O Youth and Beauty!' tells of Cash Bentley, a former athlete accidentally shot by his wife while hurdling over a couch. 'The Lowboy' opens with 'Oh I hate small men' and involves a struggle over a dressing table. Oh What a Paradise It Seems features pollution of Beasley's Pond leading Lemuel Sears to begin a relationship with Renee. Bullet Park follows Eliot and Tony Nailles, including a teenager cured by Swami Rutuola. Cheever also wrote 'Boy in Rome' and his work set in Shady Hill includes 'The Housebreaker of Shady Hill.'

General / Biographical

Cheever was known as the 'Chekhov of the Suburbs' for his depictions of suburban life. He was an alcoholic who published many of his stories in The New Yorker. He taught at the Iowa Writers' Workshop alongside Raymond Carver. Colm Toibin discussed Cheever in New Ways to Kill Your Mother, arguing that for Cheever 'the suburban house' was 'a sort of hell.' Cheever won the Pulitzer Prize.

Summary of Facts

John Cheever was an American short story writer and novelist nicknamed the 'Chekhov of the Suburbs' for his depictions of mid-century American suburban discontent. He published prolifically in The New Yorker and won the Pulitzer Prize.

Cheever's most frequently clued story is 'The Swimmer,' in which Neddy Merrill decides to swim home across the county through all his neighbors' backyard pools, calling the route the 'Lucinda River' after his wife. Along the way he is called a 'gate-crasher' at the Biswangers' party and is refused a drink by his former mistress Shirley Adams. The story ends with Neddy finding his house locked and abandoned, smelling autumnal fragrances despite starting on a midsummer day. 'The Enormous Radio' follows Jim and Irene Westcott, whose new radio allows Irene to eavesdrop on her neighbors, hearing their private miseries; the story was first published in The New Yorker in 1947.

'Goodbye, My Brother' depicts the Pommeroy family vacation at Laud's Head, disrupted by the pessimistic Lawrence ('Tifty'), whom the narrator calls 'a gloomy son of a bitch' before hitting him with a root on the beach. 'The Five-Forty-Eight' follows the businessman Blake, who is stalked by his former secretary Miss Dent on a commuter train and forced to rub his face in the dirt. 'The Country Husband' features Francis Weed falling in love with a babysitter in Shady Hill.

Cheever's first novel, The Wapshot Chronicle, is set in St. Botolphs and follows the family of Captain Leander (who lost the ship Topaze) and his sons Moses and Coverly. Falconer, inspired by Cheever's teaching at Sing Sing prison, follows Ezekiel Farragut, imprisoned for murdering his brother with a poker, who has an affair with fellow prisoner Jody.

Cheever was an alcoholic who taught at the Iowa Writers' Workshop alongside Raymond Carver. Colm Toibin discussed Cheever in New Ways to Kill Your Mother, arguing that 'the suburban house' was 'a sort of hell' in his fiction.
