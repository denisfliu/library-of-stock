Summary of Facts

Delmore Schwartz (1913–1966) is an American poet and short story writer best known for two works: the short story "In Dreams Begin Responsibilities" and the poem "The Heavy Bear Who Goes With Me."

"In Dreams Begin Responsibilities" is a story in which the narrator dreams he is sitting in a movie theater watching a black-and-white film of his parents' courtship on his twenty-first birthday. The parents visit Coney Island's boardwalk (though the mother disdains its amusement parks) and argue over entering a fortune teller's booth. The narrator watches with growing anger and shouts "What are they doing? Don't they know what they're doing?" The story describes ocean waves "somersaulting and arching their backs so beautifully, showing white veins in the green and black." The final line has the narrator wake into "the bleak winter morning of my 21st birthday" to see "the windowsill shining with its lip of snow." The title is taken from an epigraph to a W.B. Yeats poetry book, and the story was inspired by the broken marriage of Schwartz's parents. It appears in a collection that also includes "Coriolanus and His Mother."

"The Heavy Bear Who Goes With Me" describes an "inescapable animal" that "moves where I move, distorting my gesture" — the "hungry beating brutish one / In love with candy, anger, and sleep." The epigraph, "the withness of the body," comes from his teacher Alfred North Whitehead. Susan Bordo's body theory work analyzes this poem in discussing the "crystallization of culture."

"In the Naked Bed, in Plato's Cave" speaks of "the mystery of beginning again and again, while History is unforgiven."

Schwartz served as the inspiration for Von Humboldt Fleisher in Saul Bellow's Humboldt's Gift (1976 Nobel Prize winner); the fictionalized Schwartz is named Charlie Citrine's mentor. He was the dedicatee of the second half of John Berryman's Dream Songs (His Toy, His Dream, His Rest), which contains twelve elegies for him. Robert Lowell wrote "To Delmore Schwartz" in Life Studies, remembering when they "drank and eyed the chicken-hearted shadows of the world" and recalling Schwartz's stuffed duck and a kitten named Oranges.

Schwartz was the youngest winner of the Bollingen Prize for his 1959 collection Summer Knowledge. He also wrote the drama Shenandoah; poems "America, America!" and "Chaplin Upon the Cliff, Dining Alone"; a ballad about "the children of the czar"; and a response to Marlowe's 'The Passionate Shepherd to His Love' warning readers that 'like Molière's bourgeois gentleman, you may discover you have been speaking blank verse all your life.' His unsuccessful epic Genesis was an early example of confessional poetry (a term coined by critic M.L. Rosenthal). He taught Lou Reed, who dedicated "European Song" on The Velvet Underground and Nico to him. Donald Margulies's play Collected Stories features a student writing a novel based on her teacher's affair with Schwartz. He died of alcoholism in 1966 at the Chelsea Hotel.
