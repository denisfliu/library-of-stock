Russian composer (1804–1857), considered the father of Russian classical music and the first Russian composer to achieve broad European recognition. His two operas — A Life for the Tsar (1836) and Ruslan and Lyudmila (1842) — are cornerstones of the Russian operatic repertoire. General / Biographical

Mikhail Glinka (1804–1857), from Smolensk, was the first Russian composer to gain wide recognition. He reportedly acquainted himself with dissonance by listening to the out-of-tune bells of his native Smolensk. His piano teacher in Russia was the Irish composer John Field. He traveled to Spain, inspiring two overtures; his 'Patriotic Song' served as Russia's temporary national anthem from 1990 to 2000. He founded a namesake annual prize in 1884; early winners included César Cui, Anatoly Lyadov, and Tchaikovsky (for his Romeo and Juliet overture-fantasy). His music inspired the Mighty Five (Balakirev, Mussorgsky, Borodin, Rimsky-Korsakov, Cui). Wikipedia

A Life for the Tsar (Ivan Susanin)

Glinka's first opera (1836), originally titled A Life for the Tsar and later renamed Ivan Susanin. Based on the legend of Ivan Susanin, who led a Polish army into the deep forest to their deaths in order to save Tsar Mikhail Romanov at the beginning of the Time of Troubles. The opera was retitled Hammer and Sickle in the Soviet era. Its second act is largely ballet and contains a set of four Polish dances. Balakirev wrote a piano fantasia inspired by this opera. It was the earliest Russian-language opera in the standard repertoire. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Mikhail Glinka (1804–1857) is considered the father of Russian art music and the first Russian composer to achieve broad European recognition. Born near Smolensk, he reportedly developed an ear for dissonance by listening to the out-of-tune village bells as a child; his early piano teacher in Russia was John Field, the Irish inventor of the nocturne. After extensive travel in Italy and Berlin, Glinka returned to Russia and composed his two major operas.

His first opera, A Life for the Tsar (1836), later renamed Ivan Susanin, depicts the legend of Ivan Susanin, who led a Polish army into the deep Smolensk forests to their deaths in order to protect the newly crowned Tsar Mikhail Romanov. The opera was the earliest Russian-language opera in the standard repertoire. During the Soviet era it was censored and retitled 'Hammer and Sickle.' It features a set of four Polish dances in a largely ballet-driven second act. Mily Balakirev wrote a solo piano fantasia inspired by it.

His second opera, Ruslan and Lyudmila (1842), was based on Pushkin's poem and depicts the knight Ruslan's quest to rescue his abducted bride Lyudmila, including an encounter with a giant talking head. The overture uses piano and harp to imitate the Russian folk instrument the gusli; Act II contains Farlaf's Rondo. The opera's merits were famously debated by the critics Vladimir Stasov and Alexander Serov.

Among his orchestral works, Kamarinskaya (1848) — based on a slow bridal folk song ('Izza gor') and the folk dance tune Kamarinskaya — was described by Tchaikovsky as 'the acorn from which the oak of Russian music grew.' It was the first orchestral work based entirely on Russian folk songs. Glinka also wrote two Spanish overtures — 'Capriccio Brilliante on the Jota Aragonesa' and 'A Night in Madrid' — after travels in Spain. His 'Patriotic Song' served as Russia's temporary national anthem from 1990 to 2000.

In 1884 Glinka established a namesake annual prize; early winners included César Cui and Anatoly Lyadov; Tchaikovsky's Romeo and Juliet overture-fantasy also won it. Glinka's song cycle Farewell to St. Petersburg contains 'The Lark,' which Balakirev arranged for solo piano. His music was the principal inspiration for the Mighty Five (or Mighty Handful) — the nationalist school of Balakirev, Mussorgsky, Borodin, Rimsky-Korsakov, and Cui.
