Armenian-born Soviet composer best known for his ballets Gayane (featuring the iconic "Sabre Dance") and Spartacus (featuring a famous Adagio). Also composed the Masquerade Suite, a Violin Concerto for David Oistrakh, a Toccata in E-flat minor, and three symphonies. Studied under Gliere and Myaskovsky. Targeted in the 1948 Zhdanov decree for formalism.
Gayane (ballet, 1942)

Khachaturian's most famous work, a ballet set on a collective farm in Soviet Armenia. The title character Gayane's husband Giko is a drunkard who burns down a cotton warehouse, participates in a smuggling plot, and attempts to kill Gayane. Kazakov nurses her back to health and they marry. The ballet ends with a triple wedding. Adapted from an earlier ballet titled Happiness. Contains the universally recognized "Sabre Dance" and an adagio depicting carpet weavers. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Aram Khachaturian (1903-1978) was an Armenian-born Soviet composer whose works are deeply rooted in Armenian folk music traditions. He is overwhelmingly identified in quizbowl by his ballet Gayane, which contains the iconic "Sabre Dance" — a piece whose musical details are extremely well-clued: it features piercing F-sharp eighth notes in the xylophone and flute, followed by loud trombone glissandi from F down to D, with a timpani ostinato and a cymbal clash interrupting one of the A section's repetitions. The middle section of the Sabre Dance has cellos and saxophone playing a sinuous melody with rising pickup quarter notes F, A-flat, B-flat landing on B-natural. Gayane is set on a collective farm in Soviet Armenia and tells the story of the title character whose drunkard husband Giko burns a cotton warehouse, participates in smuggling, and tries to kill her. Kazakov nurses Gayane back to health, and the ballet ends with a triple wedding (couples include Karen and Nune, and Kazakov and Gayane). The ballet was adapted from an earlier work titled Happiness and uses folk instruments like the daira drum.

Khachaturian's other major ballet, Spartacus, is best known for its Act Two Adagio depicting the escape and love of the title character and his wife Phrygia. This Adagio is frequently performed as a standalone concert piece.

The Masquerade Suite is extracted from Khachaturian's incidental music for Mikhail Lermontov's play, consisting of five movements: a waltz, nocturne, mazurka, romance, and galop. The opening waltz in A minor is the most famous movement and is often described as grim.

Khachaturian's Violin Concerto in D minor was written for and premiered by David Oistrakh. Its first movement is marked Allegro con fermezza and opens with the soloist playing two sixteenth-note Ds followed by eighth-notes F-E-F. The concerto unusually calls for tambourine and piccolo snare drum. The second movement opens with a mournful bassoon solo repeated by clarinet. Jean-Pierre Rampal transcribed it into a flute concerto.

His Toccata in E-flat minor is a virtuosic piano piece written while studying under Myaskovsky, popularized by classmate Lev Oborin. It features a central Andante espressivo episode between outer Allegro marcatissimo sections, with four-beat rhythmic units that accelerate from quarter notes to sixteenth notes.

The Symphony No. 2, nicknamed "The Bell," opens with the piercing motif E, G-sharp, F and uses a bell to open and close the work. It was written for the 25th anniversary of the Russian Revolution. Khachaturian controversially swapped the order of the Allegro risoluto and Andante sostenuto middle movements when revising it. One movement contains the Dies Irae at half speed on strings, based on a folk song about a mother learning of her son's death.

The Symphony No. 3, subtitled "Symphony-Poem," is a single movement calling for 15 trumpets and a solo organ.

Khachaturian's Piano Concerto in D-flat major was written for Lev Oborin and unusually calls for a musical saw (flexatone). Performances of it made pianist William Kapell famous. He wrote three concertos over ten years for each member of a renowned piano trio.

Biographically, Khachaturian studied under Reinhold Gliere and Nikolai Myaskovsky. He was targeted alongside Shostakovich, Prokofiev, and Myaskovsky in the 1948 Zhdanov decree, which condemned their supposed formalism. His Cello Concerto was specifically criticized for Western intrusions such as quoting the Dies Irae.
