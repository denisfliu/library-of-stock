Summary of Facts

Persona (1966) is an experimental psychological film written and directed by Ingmar Bergman. It centers on the relationship between nurse Alma (played by Bibi Andersson) and her patient Elisabet Vogler (played by Liv Ullmann), a stage actress who has suddenly and inexplicably stopped speaking. The two women retreat to a sea cottage, where their identities gradually merge.

The film's most famous element is an iconic shot in which the two women's faces are superimposed into a single composite image — achieved with a split-screen technique that Bergman described as using 'smoke and mirrors.' This image has been widely reproduced and directly inspired the cover art and title of Camille Paglia's 1990 book Sexual Personae. David Lynch paid tribute to Persona's doubling theme in Mulholland Drive.

The film opens with a rapid, disorienting montage: a sheep being butchered, a nail hammered into a hand, and a boy in a morgue who wakes up and reaches out to touch a projected image of a woman. The UK and US releases censored an erect penis from this montage. Other notable scenes include Alma's explicit monologue about a beach sexual encounter; Elisabet briefly speaking after being threatened with boiling water; and a moment where a torn photograph of a boy appears under a hand (triggering a dissonant chord and white fade). The film ends with a camera crew filming one of the women boarding a bus to leave the cottage.

Near the film's climax, after the face-merge shot, Elisabet breaks her silence only once, saying 'nothing' (ingenting) when commanded to by Alma. Susan Sontag wrote that the film's form 'resists being reduced to a story.' The film stars two of Bergman's most frequent collaborators, Bibi Andersson and Liv Ullmann, and is paired with The Seventh Seal as the most-clued Bergman films in quizbowl.
