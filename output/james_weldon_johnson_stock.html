African-American poet, novelist, and civil rights leader of the Harlem Renaissance. Known for the poem 'Lift Ev'ry Voice and Sing' (called the 'Black National Anthem'), the poetry collection God's Trombones ('Seven Negro Sermons in Verse'), and the novel The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man about racial passing. First Black leader of the NAACP.
The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man

Johnson's 1912 novel, first published anonymously, about an unnamed biracial narrator who decides to 'pass' as white after witnessing a lynching. The narrator first learns he is biracial when his principal asks 'all of the white scholars' to stand. He plays ragtime piano for a rich white gentleman (his 'millionaire benefactor') and travels to Europe, but after witnessing a man burned alive in Georgia decides to change his identity and marry a white woman. The novel ends with the narrator wondering if he has 'traded his birthright for a mess of pottage.' His valedictorian classmate 'Shiny' becomes a professor at an HBCU; the narrator is inspired to tell his singer fiancee a secret after encountering Shiny in New York. While in Paris, he sees his father and half-sister at a performance of Gounod's Faust. His absentee father was described as having 'the best blood of the South.' In School, the narrator helped 'Red Head' cheat on a spelling test. He drops out of Atlanta University, hides in the closet of a Pullman car to get to Jacksonville, and earns a job as a reader in a cigar factory; the theft of his $400 causes him to take up cigar rolling. He gives money to men who wore linen dusters on their feet because they gambled away their shoes. He plays ragtime renditions of Mendelssohn's Wedding March while touring Europe. He wishes to 'gather up all the little tragedies of his life, and turn them into a practical joke on society.' An Argument about race occurs between 'the old soldier' and 'the Texan.' The narrator had youthful feelings for a violin-playing character called 'she of the brown eyes.'

God's Trombones

A poetry collection subtitled 'Seven Negro Sermons in Verse,' illustrated by Aaron Douglas in a silhouette style (specific illustrations include 'Let My People Go' and 'The Judgement Day'). Includes 'The Creation' (in which God says 'I'm lonely -- I'll make me a world'), 'Go Down, Death' (a funeral sermon about Sister Caroline being taken to heaven from her 'big bed of pain,' drawing from the spiritual 'Go Down, Moses'), and 'The Prodigal Son' (warning 'your arm's too short to box with God'). A dialect poem by Johnson proclaims Brer Rabbit to be the cutest of all creatures. Johnson's poem 'O Black and Unknown Bards' asks 'At the creation, ever heard a theme / Nobler than Go Down, Moses?'

Lift Ev'ry Voice and Sing

Known as the 'Black National Anthem,' this poem/song asks 'let our rejoicing rise, high as the listening skies' and states 'yet with a steady beat, have not our weary feet come to the place for which our fathers sighed?' The first stanza ends 'let us march on till victory is won.' It instructs listeners to perform its title action 'Till earth and heaven ring, / Ring with the harmonies of Liberty.' It describes the bitterness of the 'chastening rod.' Maya Angelou's eighth-grade graduation class sang it in I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings, making her realize she was 'a member of the proud, graduating, negro race.' Augusta Savage created a harp-shaped sculpture named for this song, in which a boy kneels in front of a series of people arranged in descending height. A saxophone cover of this song precedes the opening credits of the film Do the Right Thing. It was performed before 'The Star-Spangled Banner' to open the 2020 NFL season.

General / Biographical

Johnson was the first Black leader of the NAACP, succeeded by Walter White. He was a central figure of the Harlem Renaissance. He served as consul to Venezuela. He tried to find a producer for his comic opera Tolosa. He used the phrase 'Jes Grew' to describe the earliest ragtime songs in his preface to The Book of American Negro Poetry; Ishmael Reed borrowed this phrase for the central conceit of his novel Mumbo Jumbo. Gertrude Stein declared that Johnson's praise of her story 'Melanctha' (from Three Lives) made her the first white writer to write a love story about Black people treating them as 'normal members of the human family.' Johnson also wrote a dialect poem proclaiming Brer Rabbit to be the cutest of all creatures.

Summary of Facts

James Weldon Johnson is a central figure of the Harlem Renaissance and one of the most frequently clued African-American writers in quizbowl. His novel The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man (1912, first published anonymously) follows an unnamed biracial narrator who decides to pass as white after witnessing a man burned alive in Georgia. The narrator first learns he is biracial when his principal asks 'all of the white scholars' to stand, and his absentee father is described as having 'the best blood of the South.' He plays ragtime piano for a 'millionaire benefactor' and tours Europe, performing ragtime renditions of Mendelssohn's Wedding March. While in Paris, he sees his father and half-sister at a performance of Gounod's Faust. His classmate 'Shiny' becomes an orator; encountering Shiny in New York inspires the narrator to tell his singer fiancee a secret. In School, he helped 'Red Head' cheat on a spelling test and had youthful feelings for a violin-playing character called 'she of the brown eyes.' After dropping out of Atlanta University, he hides in a Pullman car closet to reach Jacksonville, where he works as a reader in a cigar factory. The novel ends with the narrator wondering if he has 'traded his birthright for a mess of pottage' and wishing to 'gather up all the little tragedies of his life, and turn them into a practical joke on society.'

His poetry collection God's Trombones ('Seven Negro Sermons in Verse'), illustrated by Aaron Douglas in a silhouette style (including illustrations titled 'Let My People Go' and 'The Judgement Day'), is frequently clued for individual poems: 'The Creation' features God saying 'I'm lonely -- I'll make me a world'; 'Go Down, Death' is a funeral sermon about Sister Caroline being taken to heaven from her 'big bed of pain,' drawing from the spiritual 'Go Down, Moses'; and 'The Prodigal Son' warns 'your arm's too short to box with God.' His poem 'O Black and Unknown Bards' asks 'At the creation, ever heard a theme / Nobler than Go Down, Moses?'

'Lift Ev'ry Voice and Sing,' known as the 'Black National Anthem,' instructs listeners to sing 'till earth and heaven ring' and describes the bitterness of the 'chastening rod.' Maya Angelou's I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings describes her eighth-grade class singing it after a demeaning speech, making her realize she was 'a member of the proud, graduating, negro race.' Augusta Savage created a harp-shaped sculpture named for this song. A saxophone cover of it precedes the opening credits of Do the Right Thing, and it was performed before 'The Star-Spangled Banner' to open the 2020 NFL season.

Johnson was the first Black leader of the NAACP, succeeded by Walter White. He served as consul to Venezuela and tried to produce his comic opera Tolosa. He used the phrase 'Jes Grew' to describe the earliest ragtime songs in his preface to The Book of American Negro Poetry; Ishmael Reed borrowed this phrase for his novel Mumbo Jumbo. Johnson also praised Gertrude Stein's story 'Melanctha,' declaring her the first white writer to treat Black people as 'normal members of the human family' in a love story.
