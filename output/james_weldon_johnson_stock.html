James Weldon Johnson

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
African-American poet, novelist, and civil rights leader of the Harlem Renaissance. Known for the poem 'Lift Ev'ry Voice and Sing' (called the 'Black National Anthem'), the poetry collection God's Trombones ('Seven Negro Sermons in Verse'), and the novel The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man about racial passing. First Black leader of the NAACP.
The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man

Johnson's 1912 novel, first published anonymously, about an unnamed biracial narrator who decides to 'pass' as white after witnessing a lynching. The narrator first learns he is biracial when his principal asks 'all of the white scholars' to stand. He plays ragtime piano for a rich white gentleman and travels to Europe, but after the lynching decides to marry a white woman and have white-passing children. The novel ends with the narrator wondering if he has 'traded his birthright for a mess of pottage.' His valedictorian classmate 'Shiny' becomes a professor at an HBCU. While in Paris, he sees his father and half-sister at a performance of Gounod's Faust. An argument about race occurs between 'the old soldier' and 'the Texan.'

God's Trombones

A poetry collection subtitled 'Seven Negro Sermons in Verse,' illustrated by Aaron Douglas. Includes 'The Creation' (in which God says 'I'm lonely -- I'll make me a world'), 'Go Down, Death' (a funeral sermon about Sister Caroline being taken to heaven from her 'big bed of pain'), and 'The Prodigal Son' (warning 'your arm's too short to box with God'). The collection drew from the spiritual 'Go Down, Moses.'

Lift Ev'ry Voice and Sing

Known as the 'Black National Anthem,' this poem/song asks 'let our rejoicing rise, high as the listening skies' and states 'yet with a steady beat, have not our weary feet come to the place for which our fathers sighed?' The first stanza ends 'let us march on till victory is won.' Maya Angelou's eighth-grade graduation class sang it in I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings, making her realize she was 'a member of the proud, graduating, negro race.' The song instructs listeners to perform its title action 'Till earth and heaven ring, / Ring with the harmonies of Liberty.'

General / Biographical

Johnson was the first Black leader of the NAACP. He was a central figure of the Harlem Renaissance. He served as consul to Venezuela. He tried to find a producer for his comic opera Tolosa. He also wrote a poem inspired by Moses, 'Let My People Go.'

Summary of Facts

James Weldon Johnson is a central figure of the Harlem Renaissance and one of the most frequently clued African-American writers in quizbowl. His novel The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man (1912, first published anonymously) follows an unnamed biracial narrator who decides to pass as white after witnessing a lynching. The narrator plays ragtime piano for a wealthy white patron and travels to Europe, but ultimately concludes by wondering if he has 'traded his birthright for a mess of pottage.' The novel is a key text in the literature of racial passing.

His poetry collection God's Trombones ('Seven Negro Sermons in Verse'), illustrated by Aaron Douglas, is frequently clued for individual poems: 'The Creation' features God saying 'I'm lonely -- I'll make me a world'; 'Go Down, Death' is a funeral sermon about Sister Caroline being taken to heaven; and 'The Prodigal Son' warns 'your arm's too short to box with God.'

'Lift Ev'ry Voice and Sing,' known as the 'Black National Anthem,' is perhaps Johnson's most famous work in popular culture. Maya Angelou's I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings describes her eighth-grade class singing it, making her realize she was 'a member of the proud, graduating, negro race.'

Johnson was the first Black leader of the NAACP and served as consul to Venezuela. He attempted to find a producer for his comic opera Tolosa.
