Summary of Facts

Paul Signac was a French Neo-Impressionist painter who developed pointillism alongside Georges Seurat. His best-known work is the Portrait of Félix Fénéon (1890), depicting the anarchist art critic holding a top hat and a white cyclamen flower before a swirling, colorful background of green-and-red ripples and yellow stars on blue, demonstrating his Divisionist style. His major painting In the Time of Harmony (subtitled 'the Golden Age is not in the Past, it is in the Future') shows an idyllic scene of people relaxing by the sea, including men playing boules, a man picking a fig from a tree, a woman feeding her child, dancers under a tree, and a painter with an easel. Henri Edmond Cross contrasted its 'dreamed-of age of happiness' with the 'poignant misery' of other anarchist works. Another painting, Sunday, depicts a brown-and-white cat on a Persian rug as a woman in violet stands at the window.

Signac wrote From Eugène Delacroix to Neo-Impressionism, in which he criticized 'muddy mixtures' and echoed a Romantic painter's claim that 'the enemy of all painting is grey.' He purchased Matisse's Luxe, Calme et Volupté, inspiring Matisse toward 'color structure' and the beginning of Fauvism. He co-founded the Salon des Indépendants with Seurat and Redon, served as a juror for the Prix Blumenthal, and frequently depicted scenes at Saint-Tropez. His works were deliberately barred from the 1889 Volpini Exhibition, which instead debuted Gauguin's Post-Impressionist works.
