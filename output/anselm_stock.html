The Ontological Argument (Proslogion)

Anselm's Proslogion contains the ontological argument for God's existence, arguing that one can imagine 'the greatest possible conceivable being,' and that such a being must exist because existence is more perfect than non-existence. Anselm wrote the argument to persuade the 'Fool of the Psalms.' Gaunilo of Marmoutiers responded by postulating a 'Lost Island' to show the argument could prove the existence of anything perfect. Later critics and elaborators include David Lewis (argued actuality is indexical), Descartes (fifth Meditation), Alvin Plantinga (modal version), and Charles Hartshorne.

Satisfaction Theory of Atonement (Cur Deus Homo)

In Cur Deus Homo, framed as a dialogue with his student Boso, Anselm criticized the dominant ransom theory of atonement. He argued instead that Christ's crucifixion was necessary not as a 'ransom' but to bring 'satisfaction' to God for humanity's offenses. This satisfaction theory became a major influence on Western Christian theology.

General / Biographical

Archbishop of Canterbury, one-time Abbot of Bec. His motto was 'faith seeking understanding' (fides quaerens intellectum); he famously wrote 'I believe so that I may understand.' He also wrote the Monologion. R.W. Southern wrote two biographies of him and argued he originated a genre of 'ardent and effusive self-disclosure.' At the Council of Bari, he compared the Father-Son relationship to man's relationship to himself through memory. Considered the 'father of Scholasticism.'

Summary of Facts

Saint Anselm of Canterbury is an 11th-century philosopher and theologian best known for the ontological argument for God's existence and the satisfaction theory of atonement. He served as Archbishop of Canterbury and was formerly Abbot of Bec.

The ontological argument, presented in the Proslogion, begins with the definition of God as 'that which none greater can be imagined' and argues that such a being must exist, because existence is more perfect than non-existence. Anselm wrote the argument to persuade the 'Fool of the Psalms.' Gaunilo of Marmoutiers famously responded with the 'Lost Island' objection, arguing that the same logic could prove the existence of any perfect thing. Gaunilo's response was titled In Behalf of the Fool. Later thinkers engaged with the argument extensively: Descartes's variation makes up most of his fifth Meditation, David Lewis argued in 1970 that the argument is invalid because actuality is indexical, and Alvin Plantinga developed a 'victorious' modal version in The Nature of Necessity, building on Charles Hartshorne and Norman Malcolm. Duns Scotus's arguments for the univocity of being were also based on Anselm's work.

In Cur Deus Homo, framed as a dialogue with his student Boso, Anselm criticized the dominant ransom theory of atonement and argued that Christ's crucifixion was necessary to bring 'satisfaction' to God for humanity's offenses, not as a ransom. Peter Abelard later opposed this, claiming Christ's death was to exemplify perfect love.

Anselm's philosophical motto was 'faith seeking understanding,' and he famously wrote 'I believe so that I may understand.' R.W. Southern wrote two biographies of him and argued in The Making of the Middle Ages that Anselm originated a genre of 'ardent and effusive self-disclosure.' At the Council of Bari, Anselm compared the Father-Son relationship to man's relationship to himself through memory. He also wrote the Monologion and is considered the 'father of Scholasticism.'
