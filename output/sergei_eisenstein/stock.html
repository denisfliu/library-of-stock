Soviet film director and film theorist, pioneer of montage theory. Best known for Battleship Potemkin (1925), featuring the 'Odessa Steps' sequence, and Alexander Nevsky (1938). Also directed Strike (1925), October (1928), and the unfinished ¡Que Viva México! (1932). Developed five types of montage and argued that 'montage is conflict.' Associated with Soviet cinema of the 1920s–1940s.

Montage Theory

Eisenstein's central theoretical contribution to cinema — the idea that the collision of two shots creates a new meaning beyond either alone. He identified five types: metric, rhythmic, tonal, overtonal, and intellectual. Key essays collected in Film Form (1949). He collaborated with Vsevolod Pudovkin on montage theory. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Sergei Eisenstein is a Soviet film director and theorist, the central figure of Soviet montage cinema. He is most identified by the 'Odessa Steps' sequence in Battleship Potemkin (1925), in which a baby carriage rolls down steps while soldiers fire on crowds — a canonical example of his montage theory.

Eisenstein's first film was the short Glumov's Diary. His debut feature, Strike (1925), climaxes with cows being slaughtered intercut with soldiers massacring workers in a muddy field. Battleship Potemkin (1925) followed, containing the iconic Odessa Steps Sequence. October (1928), about the 1917 Russian Revolution, ended with a ruler's bearded head towering over a parade — but was criticized by Soviet leaders as 'formalist.' The film inspired the name of the arts journal October, founded by Rosalind Krauss and Annette Michelson.

Alexander Nevsky (1938) restored Eisenstein's political standing with a propaganda film dramatizing the Battle of the Ice against The Teutonic Knights; a bishop wears a hat with a swastika. His planned trilogy Ivan the Terrible ended with Part II being condemned by the Soviet Central Committee as 'anti-historical'; Eisenstein died of a heart attack in 1948 after refusing to revise it. He also abandoned ¡Que Viva México! (1932), which Upton Sinclair financed at Charlie Chaplin's request, after shooting 50 hours of footage.

As a theorist, Eisenstein developed five types of montage — metric, rhythmic, tonal, overtonal, and intellectual — in collaboration with peers like Vsevolod Pudovkin. His essay collection Film Form argues 'montage is conflict' and analyzes the emotions induced by haiku and kabuki in a piece on cinema's 'fourth dimension.' Another essay argues Charles Dickens was D.W. Griffith's strongest influence. Early in his career he staged Enough Stupidity in Every Wise Man, drawing on commedia dell'arte. In a lecture he insisted Paul Robeson should star in a film about Haitian revolutionary Jean-Jacques Dessalines.
