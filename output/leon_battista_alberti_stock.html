Summary of Facts

Leon Battista Alberti was a Florentine Renaissance polymath — architect, painter, and theorist — who articulated the ideal of the Renaissance man with the phrase 'a man can do all things if he will.' His most important written work is Della Pittura / De Pictura (1435), the first post-classical treatise on painting, which introduced the concept of using a vanishing point for illusionistic perspective and discussed istoria (narrative composition) as painting's highest achievement. The second book opens with the story of Narcissus, comparing painting to 'embracing the surface of the pool.' The treatise discusses populating works with 'a variety of things' and cites the story of a cobbler antagonizing Apelles. A challenge he posed led Renaissance artists to create versions of The Calumny of Apelles. His treatment of perspective was later iterated upon by Piero della Francesca.

Alberti also wrote De Re Aedificatoria, a ten-book treatise on architecture updating Vitruvius's De Architectura, in which he proclaimed columns to be the most noble building element. His major architectural works include the facade of Santa Maria Novella in Florence (featuring scrolls linking upper and lower registers, flat pilasters, and an oriel window), the Tempio Malatestiano in Rimini (remaking a Franciscan church with three equal arches, a chapel of the planets, and sarcophagi for patron Sigismondo Malatesta and wife Ixotta, proclaimed 'full of pagan gods'), the Basilica of Sant'Andrea in Mantua (deep-arched entrance within a temple front, colossal and Corinthian columns, original design lacking transept and dome), and the Palazzo Rucellai in Florence. Ironically, while he called columns the noblest element, they are absent from his facade of San Sebastiano in Mantua.

Beyond architecture and painting theory, Alberti wrote Philodoxius, a satirical Latin comedy still thought to be ancient a century after his death, and invented the first polyalphabetic cipher (De Cifris). He was also a gifted athlete. According to Vasari, Luca Pacioli plagiarized Alberti's ideas on geometric proportions in the human form.
