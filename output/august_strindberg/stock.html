Summary of Facts

August Strindberg is a Swedish playwright and novelist, best known for the plays Miss Julie, The Father, A Dream Play, and The Ghost Sonata. He is one of the most frequently clued dramatists in quizbowl.

Miss Julie is by far Strindberg's most clued work. Set on Midsummer's Eve, it follows the Count's daughter Miss Julie and the valet Jean. Julie breaks off her engagement when her fiance refuses to jump over a riding whip. She flirts with Jean, who cheats on the cook Christine/Kristine. Julie and Jean plot to open a hotel on Lake Como, but Jean decapitates Julie's pet canary (or greenfinch) with a razor. Julie's father the Count returns, and Jean gives Julie the razor with which she presumably commits suicide offstage. Julie tells Jean about a recurring dream of sitting on a pillar but finding no rest. The preface to Miss Julie is a naturalist manifesto: it calls the title character a 'man-hating half-woman,' discusses Jean's 'slave mentality' expressed through worship of the Count (symbolized by the Count's boots present on stage), and criticizes canvas stage doors for not being capable of expressing the 'anger of an irate pater familias.' Strindberg intended the title role for his wife Siri von Essen. Julie orders the cook to prepare an abortifacient for her dog Diana. A character makes up a story about a 'Turkish pavilion' and attempting suicide in an oat bin.

The Father features Laura goading her military officer husband (the Captain) into throwing a burning lamp at her, so she can have him declared insane and control the career of their daughter Bertha. Laura tells a lie about the Captain using a microscope (rather than a spectroscope) to discover extraterrestrial life. The Captain dies from a stroke after being put in a straitjacket. Strindberg wrote The Father as a response to Ibsen's A Doll's House, arguing that Nora is the true villain.

A Dream Play features Agnes, the daughter of the god Indra, descending to Earth to Experience human hardships. The phrase 'men are to be pitied' is frequently repeated. Settings include Foulstrand, Fairhaven, and Fingal's Cave. A rapidly aging Officer pines for the opera singer Victoria. The deans of four university faculties argue about whether a door should be opened. A scene changes from a lawyer's office to a commencement ceremony. The play ends with a castle burning and a flower bud on its roof bursting into a giant chrysanthemum. A character describes stealing apples and entering a building that turns out to be an outhouse.

The Ghost Sonata is a chamber play set in a boarding house home to the wheelchair-bound Jacob Hummel and the student Arkenholtz ('Sunday child' who can see apparitions, including a milkmaid). An ogre-like cook sucks nourishment from food. Amalia (a parrot-like madwoman/mummy) convinces Hummel to hang himself. Arkenholtz's love interest (the Girl/Hyacinth Girl) dies behind a 'death screen' as Arkenholtz sings. Ingmar Bergman controversially depicted Hummel as Jewish. It is one of five three-scene 'chamber plays' premiered at the Intima Theater.

The Red Room is Strindberg's novel about the journalist Arvid Falk, who meets with bohemian Artists and intellectuals at the titular location. The Triton marine insurance company turns out to be fraudulent. Falk's friend the painter Sellen loses his idealism.

Other works: The Stronger consists of a monologue by Frau X as Mademoiselle Y (revealed as Amelia) remains silent. The Pelican: Frederik commits arson. The Dance of Death: Alice hopes to be murdered during husband Edgar's saber dance. The Great Highway is Strindberg's final play, following a Hunter through seven 'stations' of life. Inferno is an autobiographical novel about practicing alchemy at the Hotel Orfila and believing oneself in hell. Married led to a blasphemy trial.

Biographically, Strindberg had a bitter rivalry with Henrik Ibsen -- he kept a portrait of Ibsen in his study and called it 'Madness Incipient.' He wrote 'Psychic Murder (Apropos Rosmersholm),' calling Rebecca West an 'unconscious cannibal.' Chekhov called him a misogynist 'who would like to see women enslaved.' He was virulently antisemitic, causing The Critic Edvard Brandes to stop corresponding with him. He praised Otto Weininger's Sex and Character for 'solving the woman problem.' He wrote passionate letters to Nietzsche. Karl Jaspers wrote a 'pathographic analysis' of Strindberg and Van Gogh. He called himself 'the Zola of the Occult.' He founded the Intima Theater. Mauriac's Nobel lecture imagines the 'world of a novelist without hope' led by Strindberg.
