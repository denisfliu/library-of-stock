Moshe Safdie is an Israeli-Canadian architect who apprenticed with Louis Kahn and became known for his modular and prefabricated designs. His most celebrated work is Habitat 67, a residential complex of 354 stacked concrete boxes built for Montreal's Expo 67. Other major works include the Marina Bay Sands resort in Singapore, the Crystal Bridges Museum in Arkansas, and the Kauffmann Center for the Performing Arts in Kansas City.
Habitat 67

Habitat 67 consists of 354 stacked prefabricated 90-ton concrete boxes forming 158 apartments, built near the St. Lawrence River for Montreal's Expo 67. The design grew from Safdie's master's thesis 'A Case for City Living' and was guided by the motto 'For Everyone a Garden.' August Komendant supervised the structural engineering.

Summary of Facts

Moshe Safdie is an Israeli-Canadian architect who apprenticed with Louis Kahn and developed a career defined by innovative modular design and civic ambition. His master's thesis — 'A Case for City Living' — was developed into Habitat 67, a residential complex of 354 prefabricated 90-ton concrete boxes arranged into 158 apartments, built on a manmade island near the St. Lawrence River for Montreal's Expo 67 in 1967. The complex was inspired by the motto 'For Everyone a Garden,' emphasizing terraced gardens above lower units. August Komendant supervised the structural engineering. Habitat 67 has been compared to Ricardo Bofill's Kafka Castle and inspired the Nakagin Capsule Tower. Safdie later proposed rebuilding a version of Habitat for Golden Dream Bay in Qinhuangdao, China.

The Marina Bay Sands resort in Singapore is another signature Safdie work: three towers whose shapes resemble a deck of cards are connected at their tops by a 340-meter 'SkyPark,' which contains the world's largest infinity pool. The complex appears prominently in Crazy Rich Asians.

The Crystal Bridges Museum of American Art in Bentonville, Arkansas was commissioned by Alice Walton. Safdie dammed a river to create two pools which divide eight shell-like pavilions of a forested art museum such that two buildings act as bridges over the pond. The museum owns Asher Durand's Kindred Spirits.

The Kauffmann Center for the Performing Arts in Kansas City is designed with symmetrical half-shells of concentric vertical arches and resembles, from the back, a pair of connected sliced-up onions. Safdie's other works include the Yad Vashem Holocaust memorial museum in Jerusalem (opened 2005), the Yitzhak Rabin Center in Tel Aviv, the National Gallery of Canada, the Jewel Changi airport terminal in Singapore (a five-story indoor garden with a tropical forest), a chapel on Harvard's campus covered by a pyramidal glass roof over a koi pond, the United States Institute of Peace (white roof resembling a dove's wing), and the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms, and Explosives headquarters.
