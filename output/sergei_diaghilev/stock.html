Russian ballet impresario and founder of the Ballets Russes (1909, Paris), which became the most influential ballet company of the early 20th century. Famous for commissioning Stravinsky's The Firebird, Petrushka, and The Rite of Spring, employing Vaslav Nijinsky, Anna Pavlova, Tamara Karsavina, Michel Fokine, and Leonide Massine, and hiring artists like Picasso, Bakst, Benois, Goncharova, and Chanel to design sets and costumes. A flamboyantly gay impresario whose lovers included Nijinsky and Massine. Co-founded the journal Mir iskusstva (World of Art). His death in 1929 ended the Ballets Russes entirely.

Summary of Facts

Sergei Pavlovich Diaghilev (1872-1929) was a Russian art critic, patron, and ballet impresario who founded the Ballets Russes in Paris in 1909 and single-handedly transformed it into the most influential ballet company of the early 20th century. Before turning to dance, Diaghilev co-founded the journal Mir iskusstva (World of Art) with his friends Alexandre Benois and Leon Bakst, both of whom would later design costumes and sets for his ballets. His career as an impresario is inseparable from his discovery of Igor Stravinsky: after being impressed by Stravinsky's Scherzo fantastique and Fireworks, he commissioned the unknown composer to write The Firebird (1910), which launched one of the 20th century's most important musical careers. Diaghilev and Stravinsky would go on to collaborate on Petrushka (1911), The Rite of Spring (1913) — whose 1913 Paris premiere, choreographed by Vaslav Nijinsky with costumes by Nicholas Roerich, caused a near-riot — Les Noces (1923), and Pulcinella. Diaghilev's most famous productions are repeatedly clued by commission details: he paired Nijinsky with Debussy for The Afternoon of a Faun (1912), provoking Michel Fokine's departure from the company and a Le Figaro review that called Nijinsky "incontinent, with vile movements of erotic bestiality." He produced Parade (1917), a landmark collaboration between Erik Satie, Jean Cocteau, Pablo Picasso (Cubist costumes), and Leonide Massine that Guillaume Apollinaire's program notes dubbed "Surrealist." A flamboyantly gay man whose major lovers included Nijinsky and Massine, Diaghilev fired Nijinsky after Nijinsky's marriage to the Hungarian stalker Romola de Pulszky and reportedly "gave himself to a wild orgy of dissipation." He was the inspiration for Boris Lermontov in Powell and Pressburger's The Red Shoes. Diaghilev had a voracious appetite for commissioning modernist artists: Natalia Goncharova, Mikhail Larionov, Sonia Delaunay, Coco Chanel, Joan Miro, Giorgio de Chirico, Andre Derain, and Matisse all designed for him. His commissions ranged across composers from Stravinsky to Prokofiev (The Prodigal Son; he rejected the Scythian Suite) to Manuel de Falla (The Three-Cornered Hat), Reynaldo Hahn (Le dieu bleu), Respighi (La Boutique fantasque), and Germaine Tailleferre (La nouvelle Cythere, never performed because Diaghilev's 1929 death cancelled the whole season). Constant Lambert termed his Scarlatti/Goldoni adaptation The Good-Humored Ladies the first of his "time-travelling" pastiche works. Diaghilev famously quarrelled with Maurice Ravel over La Valse, refusing to choreograph it — Ravel challenged him to a duel. In the world of literature, W. H. Auden's poem "September 1, 1939" immortalized the line "What mad Nijinsky wrote / About Diaghilev / Is true of The Normal Heart," and Larry Kramer borrowed from it for the title of his AIDS play The Normal Heart; Frank Bidart also made him a subject in "The War of Vaslav Nijinsky."
