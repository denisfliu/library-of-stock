Swedish filmmaker (1918–2007), one of cinema's most celebrated directors. Known for existential and metaphysical explorations of faith, death, and human psychology. His frequent collaborator and lead actor was Max von Sydow; his longtime cinematographer was Sven Nykvist. Major works include The Seventh Seal, Persona, Wild Strawberries, Autumn Sonata, and Fanny and Alexander.

Bergman's most iconic film. Bergman's most experimental film. A nurse named Alma tends to a mute actress named Elisabet Vogler (or Elizabet). Their faces merge together in a single close-up shot described as using 'smoke and mirrors' / a split-screen technique. Near the end, Elisabet breaks her silence by saying 'nothing' (ingenting) when commanded. After being threatened with boiling water, the previously mute actress begins speaking. General / Biographical

Swedish director Ingmar Bergman (1918–2007). His recurring lead actor was Max von Sydow; his longtime cinematographer was Sven Nykvist, who later worked with Louis Malle (Black Moon) and Andrei Tarkovsky (The Sacrifice). Bergman lived and worked for many years on the island of Faro, which inspired Mia Hansen-Love's 2021 film Bergman Island. In Woody Allen's Manhattan, Mary calls Bergman 'overrated.' The rock band Sparks wrote a 'radio musical' about his 'seduction' (The Seduction of Ingmar Bergman), imagining him magically transported to 1950s Hollywood. Bergman as a child staged operas in a toy marionette theatre; he later directed a TV adaptation of The Magic Flute. Concert pianist Kabi Laretei was one of his wives. Andrei Tarkovsky frequently mimicked his usage of sparse sound, and later hired Nykvist for The Sacrifice. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Ingmar Bergman (1918–2007) is Sweden's most celebrated filmmaker, internationally recognized for existential and metaphysical explorations of faith, death, the silence of God, and human psychology. He worked primarily in Swedish-language cinema and shot many of his later films on the island of Faro, which inspired Mia Hansen-Love's 2021 homage film Bergman Island.

His most iconic work is The Seventh Seal (1957), which follows a knight named Antonius Block (played by Max von Sydow) who returns from the Crusades during the Black Death and engages Death in a game of chess. The film ends with a celebrated Danse Macabre — the cast parading in silhouette on a hill, led by a shrouded figure with a scythe. The knight's squire Jons saves a girl from being raped by a man named Raval. A Scott Walker song recounts the chess scene from the knight's perspective. The film was funded by the success of Smiles of a Summer Night at Cannes.

Smiles of a Summer Night (1955) is a romantic comedy about Fredrik Egerman, ending with a game of Russian roulette where the gun is revealed to be loaded with soot. It directly inspired Stephen Sondheim's musical A Little Night Music (which includes 'Send in the Clowns' and 'The Glamorous Life'); Woody Allen made a loose film adaptation called A Midsummer Night's Sex Comedy.

Persona (1966) is Bergman's most experimental film, depicting the merging identities of a nurse named Alma and a mute actress named Elisabet Vogler. The film uses smoke and mirrors (a split-screen technique) to blend half of each woman's face into a single close-up. It opens with jarring imagery — a crawling spider, a nail driven into a hand, and a morgue boy who wakes when a phone rings. Threatened with boiling water, the mute actress eventually begins to speak; near the film's end she says 'nothing' (ingenting) when commanded to. Susan Sontag described how the film 'resists being reduced to a story' and noted the 'calculated frustration' of the ending (which depicts a woman boarding a bus alone). Sven Nykvist's cinematography was central to the film's visual identity.

Wild Strawberries (1957) follows elderly Professor Isak Borg as he journeys to Lund to receive an honorary Doctor Jubilaris degree, picking up hitchhikers who inspire memories and nightmares. A key dream sequence features clocks without hands and a man being dragged into a coffin by his own corpse. Borg is told he is 'guilty of guilt.' His daughter-in-law Marianne is planning to divorce his son Evald. Victor Sjostrom, director of The Phantom Carriage, gave his final screen performance as Borg.

Autumn Sonata (1978) concerns a concert pianist mother named Charlotte and her daughter Eva. After listening to Eva play Chopin's Prelude No. 2 in A Minor, Charlotte gives her a car and a wristwatch. Bergman's own concert pianist wife Kabi Laretei was associated with this film.

Through a Glass Darkly (1961), the first of Bergman's 'Silence of God' trilogy, shows a woman named Karin writhing before a window overlooking the sea after hearing voices from the wallpaper; she has a vision of God as a malevolent spider. Her father David later stands at the same window and tells his son that love and God may be the same. The saraband from J.S. Bach's Cello Suite No. 2 plays in four versions across the film. Winter Light (1963), another part of this trilogy, features a priest comforting a fisherman frightened by nuclear war; it directly inspired Paul Schrader's First Reformed, and Bergman praised Robert Bresson's Diary of a Country Priest. The Silence (1963) depicts two sisters — the sensual Ester and her sickly intellectual sister Anna — and opens on a train as a boy named Johan watches tanks pass.

Fanny and Alexander (1982), Bergman's last theatrical feature, includes a boy named Ismael who foretells a fire that kills the protagonist's abusive step-father. It was also made as a miniseries; HBO later remade Bergman's Scenes from a Marriage in 2021.

Biographically: Bergman's recurring lead actor was Max von Sydow, who appeared in The Seventh Seal, The Virgin Spring, Through a Glass Darkly, and others. His longtime cinematographer was Sven Nykvist, who later worked with Louis Malle (Black Moon) and Andrei Tarkovsky (The Sacrifice); Tarkovsky frequently imitated Bergman's sparse use of sound. In Woody Allen's Manhattan, the character Mary puts Bergman in her 'Academy of the Overrated.' The band Sparks wrote The Seduction of Ingmar Bergman, a radio musical imagining Bergman transported to 1950s Hollywood. As a child, Bergman staged operas in a toy marionette theatre; he later directed a TV adaptation of Mozart's The Magic Flute. A New Order B-side to 'Everything's Gone Green' titled 'Cries and Whispers' was named after his 1972 film.
