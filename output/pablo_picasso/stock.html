Spanish artist (1881–1973) and co-founder of Cubism with Georges Braque. Most famous for Guernica (anti-war mural, Spanish Civil War), Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (proto-Cubist brothel scene), and The Old Guitarist (Blue Period). Key periods: Blue Period (1901–04, sparked by Casagemas suicide), Rose Period (Family of Saltimbanques), Analytic Cubism, Synthetic Cubism. Major works include Portrait of Gertrude Stein, Three Musicians, Still Life with Chair Caning (first collage), Women of Algiers (15 versions), Las Meninas variations (58 paintings). Sculptural works include Bull's Head (bicycle parts) and the Chicago Picasso (Daley Plaza). Muses/lovers: Fernande Olivier, Dora Maar (Weeping Woman series), Marie-Thérèse Walter (Le Rêve).
Guernica (1937)

Anti-war mural depicting bombing of Basque town by Condor Legion during Spanish Civil War. Screaming woman holds dead baby below a bull; flower grows by the hand of a dead man with a sword. Eye-like light bulb above a screaming horse. Painted in muted/monochrome palette using Ripolin enamel paint. Photographed during creation by Dora Maar, who documented raised fist and wallpaper scraps. Preceded by etching series "The Dream and Lie of Franco." Analyzed in Rudolf Arnheim's book on "visual thinking." A tapestry copy hangs at UN; covered with blue tarp before Colin Powell's 2003 Iraq War presentation. Wikipedia

Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907)

Five nude prostitutes from Barcelona's red-light district. Left three figures influenced by Iberian sculpture; right two wear African masks (from Trocadero/Ethnographic Museum). Matisse showed Picasso a Vili figurine which prompted his Trocadero visit. Blue curtains drape two rightmost figures. Triangular table of gray-shaded fruits in foreground. Limb positions inspired by El Greco's Opening of the Fifth Seal; one figure influenced by Gauguin's Oviri goddess sculpture. Early studies included a sailor and a medical student holding a skull; Andre Salmon removed a word from title for first public showing at 1916 Salon d'Antin. Painted at Le Bateau-Lavoir in Montmartre. Working title: 'The Philosophical Brothel' (Leo Steinberg essay). Self-described 'exorcism painting.' Eyes gaze directly at viewer; noses in profile. Summary of Facts

Pablo Picasso (1881–1973) was a Spanish artist who co-founded Cubism with Georges Braque, working through analytic and synthetic phases. He is associated with several distinct periods: his Blue Period (1901–04), sparked by the suicide of his friend Carles Casagemas at L'Hippodrome Cafe after being spurned by Germaine Pichot; and his Rose Period (c. 1904–06). He befriended and hosted a notorious 1908 banquet for Henri Rousseau at Bateau-Lavoir, where a sarcastic 'Glory To Rousseau' banner was hung, Andre Salmon faked an epileptic seizure and ate a hat, and Marie Laurencin smeared cream on people—the event is documented in Gertrude Stein's Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas.

Guernica (1937) is his anti-war mural responding to the Condor Legion's bombing of a Basque town. It features a screaming woman with a dead baby below a bull, a flower by a dead man's broken sword, and an eye-like light bulb above a screaming horse. Painted in muted tones using Ripolin enamel paint, its creation was documented in photographs by Dora Maar, who captured Picasso painting over a raised fist and adding wallpaper scraps. A tapestry copy hangs at the UN; it was covered with a blue tarp before Colin Powell's 2003 Iraq War presentation. Rudolf Arnheim's book on 'visual thinking' analyzed Guernica's genesis.

Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907), painted at Le Bateau-Lavoir, shows five nude prostitutes from Barcelona's red-light district. The left three figures were influenced by Iberian sculpture; the right two wear African masks from the Trocadero. Matisse had shown Picasso a Vili wooden figurine, prompting the Trocadero visit that marks the start of Picasso's proto-Cubist African Period. One figure was modeled on Gauguin's Oviri goddess sculpture. Limb positions came from El Greco's Opening of the Fifth Seal. Early studies included a sailor and a medical student holding a skull. The working title was 'The Philosophical Brothel' (Leo Steinberg essay). Andre Salmon removed a word from the title before its first public showing at the 1916 Salon d'Antin. Figures' eyes gaze directly at the viewer; noses are in profile. Picasso called it his 'exorcism painting' to 'obliterate all lessons of the past,' and created it as a rebuttal to Matisse's The Joy of Life.

The Old Guitarist (c. 1903–04) is a Blue Period work depicting a blind aged musician sitting cross-legged with head bent left, with El Greco-influenced elongation. Infrared analysis revealed an earlier image of a mother, child, and calf on the right side underneath. It inspired David Hockney's The Blue Guitar etchings.

Family of Saltimbanques (1905), the Rose Period masterpiece at the National Gallery of Art, shows circus performers in a barren desert. Picasso depicted himself as a harlequin in a diamond-patterned costume holding a young girl's hand. The fat man in red is probably Guillaume Apollinaire; the seated woman in a red skirt is probably Fernande Olivier. A girl in pink with her back to the viewer holds a flower basket; a youth in briefs carries a drum. The background was originally a racetrack. The figures were said to 'trace a vague capital C for Creation,' inspiring Rilke's 5th Duino Elegy.

Three Musicians (1921), a late Synthetic Cubist work, exists in two versions. The better-known MoMA version features a hidden brown dog under the table; the Philadelphia version does not. The central Harlequin (Picasso) plays guitar, flanked by Pierrot (Apollinaire) and a monk (Max Jacob). Fernand Léger muttered 'Pauvre Picasso' when comparing his own version to Picasso's.

His Portrait of Gertrude Stein (Rose Period) required 80–90 sittings; its face was modeled on Iberian sculpture after Picasso returned from a trip to Gosol. Stein wrote it 'is the only reproduction of me which is always I.' It hung alongside Matisse's The Joy of Life (purchased by Leo Stein). Picasso's remark: 'everybody says she does not look like it… she will.' The portrait is frequently discussed in Stein's Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas.

Still Life with Chair Caning is the first intentional collage, using oilcloth with a chair-caning pattern on an oval canvas framed with rope. Ma Jolie (1911–12) is analytic Cubist, containing guitar strings for lover Marcelle Humbert and a musical staff; the title is a refrain from a popular Parisian dance hall song meaning 'my pretty girl.' The Weeping Woman depicts Dora Maar with her face partially obscured by a jagged blue-white handkerchief. Le Rêve depicts Marie-Thérèse Walter; Steve Wynn accidentally put his elbow through it.

His sculptures include Bull's Head (bicycle seat and handlebars welded together), and the Chicago Picasso in Daley Plaza (50-foot-tall, flat oblong face, inspired by an Afghan hound). Julio Gonzalez taught Picasso oxy-fuel welding at the Renault Factory; Robert Hughes called Picasso's 1912 series of guitar sculptures 'the most radical change in sculpture since bronze-casting.' His Guitar (cardboard/sheet metal) is also significant.

Other key works: Women of Algiers (15 versions A–O after Delacroix; Version O sold for record $179M after Matisse's death); Las Meninas variations (58 paintings including 9 of doves, held entirely in a Barcelona museum); Massacre in Korea (borrowing firing squad composition from Goya's Third of May); Vollard Suite (100 Neoclassical etchings exploring minotaur obsession—minotaur used as self-symbol in 70+ artworks); analytical Cubist portrait of art dealer Daniel-Henry Kahnweiler (wavy hair, pocket watch chain visible). He designed costumes for the Ballets Russes, including boxy building-shaped costumes for Satie's Parade and the Le Tricorne curtain (later housed in the Seagram Building). He used doves as a peace symbol for the 1949 World Peace Conference. Gertrude Stein reported that Picasso proclaimed camouflage was 'Cubism.' Catalan poet Jaime Sabartes was a close friend and later Picasso's secretary; Picasso made many portraits of him, including one titled alternately as a 'beer' portrait.
