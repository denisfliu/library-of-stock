Summary of Facts

William-Adolphe Bouguereau was a French Academic painter of the 19th century, one of the leading practitioners of Academic art alongside his contemporary Alexandre Cabanel. He is most commonly identified by his idealized depictions of the human figure and genre scenes of innocent young girls.

His most notable works include The Birth of Venus (1879), in which the title red-haired goddess stands on a conch shell surrounded by cupids and mermen, and Nymphs and Satyr, depicting four women dragging a faun into a pool. He painted many portraits of young girls including The Young Shepherdess, The Bohemian, Alone in the World, and The Knitting Girl.

Bouguereau won the Prix de Rome at age 25 with Zenobia Found by Shepherds on the Banks of the Araxes. His wife, the American-born salon painter Elizabeth Jane Gardner, imitated his style, stating she'd 'rather be known as the best imitator of [him] than be nobody.' His painting First Mourning (Adam and Eve discovering Abel's body) was painted after the death of his own son.

Bouguereau and Cabanel refused to allow the Impressionists into the Paris Salon of 1863, leading to the Salon des Refuses. Academic art was derisively called 'l'art pompier' (fireman art) because Attic helmets in academic paintings resembled firemen's headgear. Other practitioners included Paul Delaroche and Jean-Leon Gerome.
