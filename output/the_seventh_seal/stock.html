Summary of Facts

The Seventh Seal (Det sjunde inseglet, 1957) is a Swedish black-and-white film written and directed by Ingmar Bergman, adapted from his own one-act morality play Wood Painting. It stars Max von Sydow as Antonius Block, a medieval knight returning to a plague-ravaged Sweden from the Crusades.

The central premise — Block challenging Death to a game of chess on a rocky beach to prolong his life — is the most frequently clued element and essentially serves as the giveaway identification. Block picks white pieces; Death picks black and says 'it becomes me well.' During the game, Block visits a confessional where he inadvertently reveals his strategy to Death, who has disguised himself as a monk wearing whiteface. Death admits 'perhaps no one is there' when asked about God, and his final lines are 'I have no secrets' and 'I am unknowing.'

Block's 'meaningful deed' — his stated purpose for wanting to survive — is to distract Death long enough for the traveling actor Jof, his wife Mia, and their infant son to escape. He does this at the chess board, allowing the family to flee. The film ends with the iconic Danse Macabre: Death, hourglass in hand, leads a silhouetted procession of Block and his companions across a hilltop, watched from afar by Jof. This finale recreates a Danse Macabre fresco Block had observed being painted in a church earlier in the film.

Other key scenes: the jester Skat fakes a theatrical suicide and hides in a tree, but Death saws it down and kills him (a squirrel then hops on the stump); the thief Raval dies of the plague while begging for water, with sunlight shining on him the moment he collapses; a girl is burned at the stake for allegedly sleeping with the Devil; a mute servant girl whispers 'It is finished' when Death arrives at Block's castle; a traveling troupe's performance is interrupted by flagellants. Block also shares a peaceful meal of strawberries and milk with Jof and Mia, declaring he will remember 'this moment, these strawberries, this bowl of milk.'

The film is shot in black-and-white — Bergman's first color film was the later All These Women. Smiles of a Summer Night's success at Cannes provided the funding for The Seventh Seal. Bergman's frequent collaborator and cinematographer was Sven Nykvist. Death was played by Bengt Ekerot, who later directed the world premiere of Long Day's Journey into Night. In quizbowl, The Seventh Seal almost always appears alongside Persona and/or Wild Strawberries when the answer is Ingmar Bergman.
