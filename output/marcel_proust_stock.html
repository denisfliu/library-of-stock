French modernist author of the seven-volume novel In Search of Lost Time (also known as Remembrance of Things Past). The novel's most iconic scene involves involuntary memories triggered by eating a tea-soaked madeleine. Key characters include Charles Swann, Odette de Crecy, Albertine Simonet, the Baron de Charlus, and the fictional composer Vinteuil. C.K. Scott Moncrieff's English translation took its title from Shakespeare's Sonnet 30. Proust wrote the critical essay Against Sainte-Beuve. He had a relationship with composer Reynaldo Hahn and lived in a cork-lined room.
In Search of Lost Time (general / cross-volume)

Proust's seven-volume masterpiece, also known as Remembrance of Things Past. The narrator's involuntary memories are triggered by a tea-soaked madeleine at Combray. C.K. Scott Moncrieff's English translation was named after a line from Shakespeare's Sonnet 30. Features fictional artists: the composer Vinteuil, the painter Elstir, and the writer Bergotte (based on Anatole France). Andre Gide rejected the manuscript at the NRF. Won the 1919 Goncourt Prize for Within a Budding Grove. The early unfinished novel Jean Santeuil was later expanded into this work. Wikipedia

Albertine Simonet

A major character in In Search of Lost Time. The narrator meets Albertine among a group of girls on a beach at Balbec in Within a Budding Grove. The narrator jealously imprisons her in his house over her lesbian affairs in The Prisoner. She dies in a horse-riding accident in The Fugitive (also called Albertine Disparue). A theory (Andre Gide's 'transposition theory') holds that Albertine is actually male and based on Proust's chauffeur (or secretary Albert Nahmias). Anne Carson wrote a poem about her called 'The Albertine Workout.'

Summary of Facts

Marcel Proust is one of the most frequently clued authors in quizbowl literature, always identified through his seven-volume novel In Search of Lost Time (also known as Remembrance of Things Past). The most iconic giveaway is the madeleine episode: the narrator dips a madeleine in tea, triggering involuntary memories of his childhood at Combray. This appears in virtually every tossup and many bonuses about Proust.

The novel's English translation by C.K. Scott Moncrieff, titled after a line from Shakespeare's Sonnet 30, is another very frequent clue. The fictional composer Vinteuil's 'little phrase' in a violin sonata, which symbolizes Charles Swann's love for Odette de Crecy, is equally central — appearing in about half of all tossups. Competing theories hold that this fictional music was modeled on works by Franck, Brahms, Saint-Saens, or a Gabriel Perne sonata.

The novel opens with an 'overture' beginning 'for a long time I went to bed early,' in which the narrator cannot sleep without his mother's goodnight kiss. The Baron de Charlus's homosexual encounter with the tailor Jupien — observed by the narrator while watching a bee fertilize an orchid — opens the volume Sodom and Gomorrah. Swann's obsessive love for Odette de Crecy is a major plot thread; they use the euphemism 'to do a cattleya' for sex.

Albertine Simonet is a major character with her own cluster of clues. The narrator meets her at Balbec beach in Within a Budding Grove. He imprisons her jealously over her lesbian affairs in The Prisoner. She dies in a horse-riding accident in The Fugitive. Andre Gide's 'transposition theory' holds that Albertine is actually male, based on Proust's chauffeur or secretary Albert Nahmias. Anne Carson wrote 'The Albertine Workout' about her.

Other notable clues involve the writer Bergotte (based on Anatole France), who dies in front of Vermeer's View of Delft while reflecting on a 'patch of yellow wall.' The kitchen-maid is nicknamed 'Giotto's Charity,' and a woman is compared to Botticelli's Zipporah. The narrator is obsessed with the actress Berma's Racine performances. Bloch badgers Madame de Norpois about the Dreyfus Affair. The novel ends with a party thrown by the Prince of Guermantes in Time Regained.

Proust's critical essay Against Sainte-Beuve attacks the biographically-oriented criticism of Charles Augustin Sainte-Beuve, arguing against the importance of biographical information in literary criticism.

Biographically, Proust's lover was the Venezuelan composer Reynaldo Hahn. Jozef Czapski famously delivered lectures on Proust in a Soviet prison camp. The Proust Questionnaire (a confession album he filled out at age 14) became famous through James Lipton's Inside the Actors Studio and Vanity Fair magazine. Alain de Botton wrote How Proust Can Change Your Life. Samuel Beckett wrote a monograph on Proust. Andre Gide initially rejected the manuscript at the NRF. Robert de Montesquiou inspired the Baron de Charlus. Proust lived in a cork-lined room in his last years and translated Ruskin's Sesame and Lilies.
