Anne Bradstreet

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Colonial-era Puritan poet, the first female author published in the British colonies in North America. Best known for "To My Dear and Loving Husband" ("If ever two were one, then surely we"), "The Author to Her Book" ("ill-form'd offspring of my feeble brain"), "Verses upon the Burning of Our House" ("My hope and treasure lies above"), and the collection The Tenth Muse Lately Sprung Up in America. Subject of John Berryman's "Homage to Mistress Bradstreet."
To My Dear and Loving Husband

Bradstreet's most frequently clued poem, addressed to her husband Simon. Opens "If ever two were one, then surely we." Declares "I prize thy love more than whole Mines of gold, / Or all the riches that the East doth hold." States "My love is such that rivers cannot quench" and "Thy love is such I can no way repay." Concludes "let's so persevere that when we live no more, we may live ever."

The Author to Her Book

Poem addressing Bradstreet's collection The Tenth Muse as "thou ill-form'd offspring of my feeble brain" and a "rambling brat." Complains the book was published prematurely "by friends, less wise than true" (John Woodbridge published it without her knowledge). The speaker "wash'd thy face, but more defects I saw" and describes rubbing off a spot that makes a new flaw. Describes finding "rags" to clothe the book: "In this array, 'mongst vulgars may'st thou roam." Calls the book "unfit for light."

Verses upon the Burning of Our House

Poem about the 1666 fire that destroyed Bradstreet's home. The speaker is awakened by "thund'ring noise / And piteous shrieks of dreadful voice." Laments "no pleasant talk shall here be told / nor things recounted done of old." Reflects on items lost: "Here stood that Trunk, and there that chest." Declares "Adieu, Adieu, all's vanity" and finds consolation in a heavenly house "purchased and paid for too / by Him who hath enough to do" that is "high erect; Framed by that mighty Architect, With glory richly furnished." Ends "The world no longer let me love / My hope and treasure lies above."

The Prologue (to The Tenth Muse)

Opens "To sing of Wars, of Captaines, and of Kings" — claims these are "too superior things" for the author's "mean pen." References reading Du Bartas and admiring his "sugar'd lines." The fifth stanza declares "I am obnoxious to each carping tongue, / Who sayes, my hand a needle better fits." Ends with "Give wholsome Parsley wreath, I aske no Bayes." Echoed in the image of "glistering gold" that returns in "To My Dear and Loving Husband."

General / Biographical

The Tenth Muse Lately Sprung Up in America was the first book of poetry by a woman published in the British colonies. Contains the "Quaternions" (groups of four poems) including "The Four Monarchies" and "The Four Ages of Man" (inspired by Jacques' "All the world's a stage" speech). Also wrote "A Dialogue between Old England and New" ("Farewell, dear mother; Parliament, prevail"), "A Letter to Her Husband, Absent upon Publick Employment" ("My chilled limbs now nummed lye forlorn" / "My Sun" / uses astronomical metaphors; asks "how stayest you there whilst I at Ipswitch lye?"), "Before the Birth of One of her Children" ("How soon, my Dear, death may my steps attend"). Subject of John Berryman's "Homage to Mistress Bradstreet" (57 stanzas; "Love has no body and presides the sun" / "moved you not, restless, waiting for him?" / "my lost candle like the firefly loves"). Also subject of an Eavan Boland poem. Married to Simon Bradstreet, governor of the Massachusetts Bay Colony. Elaine Showalter's A Jury of Her Peers references the Prologue. "The Greeks did naught but play the fools and lie. Let Greeks be Greeks."

Summary of Facts

Anne Bradstreet is the first female author published in the British colonies, with her collection The Tenth Muse Lately Sprung Up in America. Her most frequently clued poem is "To My Dear and Loving Husband," which opens "If ever two were one, then surely we" and declares "I prize thy love more than whole Mines of gold, / Or all the riches that the East doth hold."

"The Author to Her Book" addresses the collection itself as "thou ill-form'd offspring of my feeble brain" and a "rambling brat," complaining it was published prematurely by John Woodbridge. "Verses upon the Burning of Our House" describes the 1666 fire that destroyed her home, with the speaker awakened by "thund'ring noise / And piteous shrieks" and ultimately finding consolation: "The world no longer let me love / My hope and treasure lies above."

The Prologue to her collection opens "To sing of Wars, of Captaines, and of Kings" and sarcastically claims these are "too superior things" for her "mean pen." It references her admiration for Du Bartas and defends her right to write against those who say "my hand a needle better fits."

Bradstreet also wrote "A Dialogue between Old England and New," "A Letter to Her Husband, Absent upon Publick Employment" (with its solar metaphors), and "Before the Birth of One of her Children." She was married to Simon Bradstreet, governor of the Massachusetts Bay Colony. John Berryman wrote a 57-stanza "Homage to Mistress Bradstreet" and Eavan Boland also wrote a poem about her.
