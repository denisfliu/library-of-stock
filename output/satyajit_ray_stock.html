Bengali Indian filmmaker, writer, and artist (1921–1992). Best known for the Apu Trilogy (beginning with Pather Panchali, 1955), inspired by Vittorio de Sica's Bicycle Thieves and scored by Ravi Shankar. Protege of Jean Renoir (served as location scout for The River). Also a prolific writer, creating the detective Feluda, the scientist Professor Shonku, and science fiction stories. Won an Honorary Academy Award in 1992.
The Apu Trilogy (1955–1959)

Three films: Pather Panchali (1955), Aparajito (1956), and Apur Sansar (The World of Apu, 1959). The first was inspired by Vittorio de Sica's Bicycle Thieves. Scored by Ravi Shankar. Cinematography by Subrata Mitra. In Pather Panchali, the young protagonist (son of a temple priest) runs through gaps in a train rolling through a field of pampas grass; a brother and sister run through a grassy field to see a passing train. The trilogy follows Apu from childhood in rural India to his life in Varanasi (in the second film) to his aspirations as a writer (in the third). The films are set in and around Kolkata and Tollygunge (the city's film hub). Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Bengali Indian filmmaker Satyajit Ray (1921–1992). He scouted locations for Jean Renoir's The River and described this in his books Speaking of Films and Our Films, Their Films. He edited the children's magazine Sandesh and wrote two popular fictional series: detective Feluda (accompanied by Topshe) and scientist Professor Shonku. He also wrote the script for a film called The Alien, which Spielberg may have plagiarized for E.T. His collection of nonsense poems Today Bandha Ghorar Dim includes a Bengali translation of 'Jabberwocky.' In the Sonar Kella story, Mukul is kidnapped; the fictional writer Jatayu impresses characters on a train with his knife collection. Ganashatru (1990) was an adaptation of Ibsen's An Enemy of the People. Frequently worked with cinematographer Subrata Mitra. Praised The Rules of the Game for refusing to 'wear its innovations on its sleeve.' Two parks in Kolkata are themed on his characters Professor Shonku and Feluda. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Satyajit Ray (1921–1992) was a Bengali Indian filmmaker, writer, and artist who transformed Indian art cinema. He began as an assistant/location scout for Jean Renoir's The River (1951) and went on to direct the Apu Trilogy — Pather Panchali (1955), Aparajito (1956), and Apur Sansar (1959) — which was heavily inspired by Vittorio de Sica's Bicycle Thieves. The trilogy was scored by Ravi Shankar and filmed by his frequent cinematographer Subrata Mitra, who pioneered bounce lighting by using light-colored surfaces to reflect light during storm scenes.

Pather Panchali's most iconic scene shows the young Apu (son of a temple priest) and his sister running through a field of pampas grass to see a passing train. The film's climax cuts from the distraught parents to a zoom on the young protagonist holding an umbrella in shock. In the third installment, Apur Sansar (1959), a jobless aspiring novelist marries his cousin on a whim; the film ends with a shot of the protagonist's back as he walks toward a train under a cloud, deliberately avoiding his eyes.

Charulata (1964) depicts a bored woman in Kolkata who peers through her house's blinds and spies on her oblivious husband through opera glasses; the camera tracks quickly to maintain a close-up on the glasses as she runs to the mansion windows. The Chess Players (1977) depicts two nobles so obsessed with chess that they ignore the British annexation of their territory.

Ray also had a distinguished literary career. He edited the Bengali children's magazine Sandesh and created two beloved fictional series: the detective Feluda (accompanied by his companion Topshe), and the scientist Professor Shonku. In the Sonar Kella story, Mukul is kidnapped in pursuit of hidden treasure. His collection of nonsense poems Today Bandha Ghorar Dim includes a Bengali translation of 'Jabberwocky.' He wrote film criticism books Speaking of Films and Our Films, Their Films, and praised Renoir's Rules of the Game for refusing to 'wear its innovations on its sleeve.' He also wrote an early script for The Alien — a story about a friendly alien — that Spielberg has been accused of plagiarizing for E.T. His final major work was Ganashatru (1990), an adaptation of Ibsen's An Enemy of the People. Two parks in Kolkata are themed after his fictional characters Professor Shonku and Feluda.
