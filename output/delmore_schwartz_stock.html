Summary of Facts

Delmore Schwartz is an American poet and short story writer best known for "In Dreams Begin Responsibilities," a story in which the narrator dreams he is sitting in a movie theater watching a film of his parents' courtship on his twenty-first birthday. The story describes a lyrical passage of ocean waves "somersaulting and arching their backs so beautifully, showing white veins in the green and black." The parents visit Coney Island's boardwalk, and the boy grows increasingly angry watching the film. The title is taken from an epigraph to a W.B. Yeats poetry book.

His major poem "The Heavy Bear Who Goes With Me" describes an "inescapable animal" that "moves where I move, distorting my gesture" — the "hungry beating brutish one / In love with candy, anger, and sleep." The poem's epigraph, "the withness of the body," comes from his teacher Alfred North Whitehead. Another notable poem, "In the Naked Bed, in Plato's Cave," speaks of "the mystery of beginning again and again, while History is unforgiven."

Schwartz served as the inspiration for Von Humboldt Fleisher, the title character of Saul Bellow's Humboldt's Gift. He was the dedicatee of the second half of John Berryman's Dream Songs (His Toy, His Dream, His Rest), which contains twelve elegies for him. Robert Lowell wrote "To Delmore Schwartz" in Life Studies, remembering when they "drank and eyed the chicken-hearted shadows of the world" and recalling Schwartz's stuffed duck and a kitten named Oranges.

Schwartz became the youngest winner of the Bollingen Prize for his 1959 collection Summer Knowledge, which includes "The Bear Who Goes With Me" (alternate title). He also wrote the drama Shenandoah, poems "America, America!" and "Coriolanus and His Mother," the failed epic Genesis, and "Chaplin Upon the Cliff, Dining Alone." He died of alcoholism in 1966 at the Chelsea Hotel.
