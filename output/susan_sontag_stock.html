Susan Sontag

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
American writer and public intellectual (1933–2004). Known for the essay collections Against Interpretation ('Notes on Camp,' 'Against Interpretation'), On Photography, and the critical texts Illness as Metaphor and AIDS and Its Metaphors. Also wrote the historical novel The Volcano Lover (about William and Emma Hamilton) and fiction including 'The Way We Live Now' (about AIDS). Long-term partner of photographer Annie Leibovitz. Staged a candlelit performance of Waiting for Godot during the Siege of Sarajevo.
"Notes on 'Camp'" (1964)

Essay published in Partisan Review (and collected in Against Interpretation) that analyzes 'Camp' sensibility — defined as seeing 'everything in quotation marks,' characterized by the 'triumph of the epicene style,' and described as 'good because it's awful.' The 'pure' form of Camp is 'always naive.' Uses epigrams from Oscar Wilde. Lists examples including Ronald Firbank's novels, Flash Gordon comics, Tiffany lamps, Bellini's operas, and Aubrey Beardsley drawings. Traces Camp to Christopher Isherwood's The World in the Evening. Inspired the 2019 Met Gala theme. Wikipedia

On Photography (1977)

Essay collection opening with 'In Plato's Cave,' which claims 'to collect photographs is to collect the world.' Contains the essay 'Melancholy Objects' (connecting the 'joys of watching' to Baudelaire's flaneur). Contrasts FSA photography (called 'propagandistic') with Diane Arbus. The essay 'Freak Show' (also titled 'America, Seen Through Photographs, Darkly') criticizes Arbus's photography for its absence of beauty. Sontag states 'there is an aggression implicit in every use of the camera' and a 'non-intervention.' Followed by the book Regarding the Pain of Others (on war photography). Wikipedia

Illness as Metaphor (1978) and AIDS and Its Metaphors (1989)

Illness as Metaphor (1978) was written while Sontag was being treated for breast cancer (without mentioning it). The book traces how tuberculosis and cancer were romantically associated with psychological traits and argues against 'blame the victim' language around illness. Notes that cancer was described as a disease of 'repression.' Compares the glorification of TB patients with the victim-blaming directed at cancer patients. Notes that in Stendhal's Armance, a mother won't say the word 'tuberculosis' for fear of worsening her son's illness. Near-total historical amnesia about the 1918 influenza pandemic is discussed. AIDS and Its Metaphors (1989) is a sequel applying the same arguments to AIDS. Wikipedia

Against Interpretation (1966)

Essay collection including 'Notes on Camp' and the title essay. 'Against Interpretation' ends with the statement 'In place of a hermeneutics, we need an erotics of art.' It contrasts content-based and formalist hermeneutics and cites Randall Jarrell's essay on Walt Whitman as a rare example of accurate, loving art criticism. Another essay is 'The Aesthetics of Silence.' Also contains 'On Style,' which notes in a footnote that the Greek notion of form was spatial rather than temporal. Wikipedia

The Volcano Lover (1992)

Historical novel fictionalizing the lives of William Hamilton (a vulcanologist) and his wife Emma Hamilton (who became Lord Nelson's mistress). Protagonist is Lady Hamilton. Ends with Eleonora da Fonseca Pimentel cursing the main characters for their selfishness, declaring 'Damn them all.' Accused of plagiarizing Willa Cather's My Mortal Enemy by Ellen Lee. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

American writer and public intellectual (1933–2004). Long-term partner of photographer Annie Leibovitz (photographed by Leibovitz in a bear suit). Wrote the play Alice in Bed (features a tea party with Emily Dickinson and Margaret Fuller). Staged a candlelit production of Waiting for Godot during the 1990s Siege of Sarajevo. Wrote a controversial New Yorker essay five days after 9/11 arguing that replacing 'candor' with 'psychotherapy' was wrong and calling for not being 'stupid together.' Wrote that 'the white race is the cancer of human history' in Partisan Review. Stated in 1982 that Communism was 'the most successful variant of Fascism.' Her son David Rieff (policy analyst) wrote the memoir Swimming in a Sea of Death about her 2004 death. Benjamin Moser wrote a 2019 biography emphasizing her relationships with women like Maria Irene Fornes and Annie Leibovitz. Wrote a classic foreword to Pedro Paramo. Summary of Facts

Susan Sontag (1933–2004) was an American writer and public intellectual whose essays redefined cultural criticism. Her essay 'Notes on Camp' (1964, collected in Against Interpretation) defined Camp as a sensibility that 'sees everything in quotation marks' and is 'good because it's awful,' traces it to Christopher Isherwood's The World in the Evening, uses epigrams from Oscar Wilde, and cites Tiffany lamps, Aubrey Beardsley, Bellini's operas, Flash Gordon comics, and Ronald Firbank as canonical Camp examples. The essay inspired the 2019 Met Gala theme. The title essay of Against Interpretation ends with the famous line 'In place of a hermeneutics, we need an erotics of art.'

On Photography (1977) opens with 'In Plato's Cave,' claiming 'to collect photographs is to collect the world.' It contains 'Freak Show' (also known as 'America, Seen Through Photographs, Darkly'), which contrasts the FSA photography (called 'propagandistic') with the work of Diane Arbus, criticizing Arbus's absence of beauty. Sontag states 'there is an aggression implicit in every use of the camera.' On Photography was followed by Regarding the Pain of Others (on war photography). Illness as Metaphor (1978) was written while Sontag was being treated for breast cancer (which she did not mention in the book). It argues against victim-blaming language around illness and against treating disease metaphorically as a reflection of personality — contrasting the romantic glorification of tuberculosis patients with the blaming of cancer patients. She noted near-total historical amnesia about the 1918 influenza pandemic because it was less useful as metaphor. AIDS and Its Metaphors (1989) applied the same analysis to AIDS.

Sontag's major fiction includes The Volcano Lover (1992), a historical novel fictionalizing William and Emma Hamilton, ending with Eleonora da Fonseca Pimentel cursing the main characters ('Damn them all'); the novel was accused of plagiarizing Willa Cather. Her short story 'The Way We Live Now' (1986) about the AIDS crisis is told through dialogues of friends of a hospitalized man, opening with Max saying 'At first he was just losing weight.' She also wrote the play Alice in Bed, featuring a tea party with Emily Dickinson and Margaret Fuller as characters.

Biographically, Sontag was the long-term partner of photographer Annie Leibovitz (who photographed her in a bear suit). She staged a candlelit production of Waiting for Godot during the 1990s Siege of Sarajevo to draw attention to the conflict. Five days after the 9/11 attacks she wrote a controversial New Yorker essay urging 'Let's by all means grieve together. But let's not be stupid together.' Her son David Rieff wrote the memoir Swimming in a Sea of Death about her 2004 death. Marina Abramovic dedicated Seven Easy Pieces to her.
