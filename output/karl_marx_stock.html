German philosopher and economist who wrote Das Kapital and The Communist Manifesto (with Engels). Known for historical materialism, the labor theory of value (use-value vs. exchange-value, surplus value), the base-superstructure model, commodity fetishism, the theory of alienation, and the Theses on Feuerbach. Louis Althusser and Jacques Derrida are key interpreters.
Das Kapital and Political Economy

Das Kapital (Capital), subtitled 'A Critique of Political Economy,' is Marx's magnum opus, left unfinished. It distinguishes between 'use-value' (tied to physical properties) and 'exchange-value' (a measure of 'congealed labour-time'), theorizes that surplus value arises from the exploitation of labor by capital, and introduces commodity fetishism (attributing value to objects rather than their production labor). Marx called Bentham a leather-tongued 'arch-philistine.' The reserve army of labor (unemployed) depresses wages. G.A. Cohen defended Marx's theory of history in Karl Marx's Theory of History, founding the analytical Marxism / September Group.

Historical Materialism and Alienation

Marx's theory of historical materialism argues that economic processes and class conflict drive history. Societies progress through stages beginning with primitive accumulation and including capitalism. He argued history repeats itself, 'first as tragedy, then as farce' (from The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Napoleon). His concept of alienation holds that workers become estranged from their 'species-being' and the products of their labor through 'objectification.' The 1844 manuscripts introduced alienation and were analyzed by Althusser as the 'Young Marx.'

Theses on Feuerbach and The German Ideology →

Marx wrote 11 short Theses on Feuerbach, the last of which states 'Philosophers have only interpreted the world; the point is to change it.' They criticize Feuerbach for restricting human essence to the individual rather than seeing it as a product of social relations, and for privileging theoretical activity. The Theses served as an outline for The German Ideology, co-written with Engels, which attacks Bruno Bauer, Feuerbach, and Max Stirner. The German Ideology contains the 'hunting in the morning, fishing in the afternoon' passage and calls Stirner a 'saint.'

The Communist Manifesto

The Communist Manifesto (1848), co-written with Friedrich Engels, opens with 'a spectre is haunting Europe' and ends with 'workers of the world, unite.' It emphasizes class struggle. The phrase 'from each according to his ability, to each according to his need' comes from the Critique of the Gotha Program.

Althusser and Derrida (Reception)

Louis Althusser led 'structural' Marxism, co-writing Reading Capital and publishing For Marx (which identified an 'epistemological break'). He borrowed base/superstructure and relations of production, and wrote on Ideological State Apparatuses. Jacques Derrida's Specters of Marx coined the term 'hauntology' and argued Marx's ideas still haunt Western society. Mark Fisher's Capitalist Realism and Ghosts of My Life extended hauntology. Slavoj Zizek's Sublime Object of Ideology combined Marx and Freud. Gyorgy Lukacs founded Western Marxism (reification, History and Class Consciousness). Kojin Karatani's Transcritique examined Kant and Marx together.

General / Biographical

Marx is a German philosopher and economist, author of Das Kapital and The Communist Manifesto (with Engels). He was a Young Hegelian and his dialectics built on Hegel, twisting it from spiritual to economic processes. Peter Singer wrote the Very Short Introduction on Marx. The Grundrisse is another major text. The Critique of the Gotha Program criticizes legal equality as inherently unequal. He also wrote Scorpion and Felix (a novel) and The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Napoleon.

Summary of Facts

Karl Marx is one of the most frequently clued philosophers in quizbowl, with 10 tossups and 20 bonuses. His universal giveaway is as the German philosopher/economist who wrote Das Kapital and The Communist Manifesto with Friedrich Engels.

Das Kapital (subtitled 'A Critique of Political Economy') distinguishes between use-value and exchange-value (a measure of 'congealed labour-time'), theorizes surplus value from labor exploitation, introduces commodity fetishism, and presents the base-superstructure model. The reserve army of labor depresses wages. G.A. Cohen's Karl Marx's Theory of History founded analytical Marxism / the September Group with its 'Primacy Thesis' and 'Development Thesis.'

Historical materialism argues economic processes and class conflict drive history through progressive stages. 'First as tragedy, then as farce' comes from The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Napoleon. His theory of alienation (from the 1844 manuscripts) holds that workers become estranged from their 'species-being' through objectification. Althusser identified an 'epistemological break' between the Young and Mature Marx.

The 11 Theses on Feuerbach culminate in 'Philosophers have only interpreted the world; the point is to change it.' The German Ideology (with Engels) attacks Bauer, Feuerbach, and Stirner, containing the 'hunt in the morning' passage. The Communist Manifesto opens with 'a spectre is haunting Europe' and ends with 'workers of the world, unite.' The Critique of the Gotha Program includes 'from each according to his ability, to each according to his need.'

Key interpreters: Althusser (structural Marxism, Reading Capital, ISAs, For Marx); Derrida (Specters of Marx, hauntology); Mark Fisher (Capitalist Realism, hauntology); Lukacs (reification, History and Class Consciousness); Zizek (Sublime Object of Ideology). Marx was a Young Hegelian who built on Hegel's dialectic, twisting it from spiritual to economic processes.
