"Vesti la giubba" – Canio's Act I Aria

The central tenor aria of I Pagliacci, sung by Canio at the end of Act I as he dons his clown costume. Having just discovered his wife Nedda's infidelity, he commands himself to 'put on the costume' and perform for the audience even though his heart is breaking — 'turn your distress and tears into jokes.' Enrico Caruso's 1902 recording was the first record to sell over a million copies. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

I Pagliacci (The Clowns) is a two-act verismo opera composed and librettized by Ruggero Leoncavallo, premiered in 1892. It is nearly always performed on a double bill with Pietro Mascagni's Cavalleria rusticana. The opera is set on the Feast of the Assumption in a small southern Italian village and centers on a traveling troupe of commedia dell'arte performers.

The opera opens with a prologue in which Tonio, dressed in his character costume as Taddeo, steps in front of the curtain and addresses the audience with 'Si puo?' — reminding them that the performers are 'men... like you' with real feelings. The Intermezzo between acts echoes the baritone solo of this prologue. Act I includes a 'Bell Chorus' in which the villagers sing 'don, din, don' to imitate vesper bells, followed by Nedda's aria 'Stridono lassù' (also called a 'ballatella'), in which she imitates birds in the sky and longs for freedom. When the hunchbacked Tonio attempts to declare his love to Nedda, she rebuffs him with a bullwhip. Tonio overhears Nedda planning with her lover Silvio to run away at midnight, and furiously demands to know his name ('il nome! il nome!'). Having discovered Nedda's infidelity, Canio — the troupe's leader and Nedda's husband — closes Act I with the aria 'Vesti la giubba' ('Put on the costume'), in which he commands himself to perform despite his heartbreak, because 'people have paid money and want to be amused.' The aria's tradition of a sob-note at its climax made it famous; Enrico Caruso's 1902 recording of it was the first record in history to sell over a million copies.

In Act II, the troupe performs its commedia dell'arte play-within-the-play: Canio plays the clown Pagliaccio, Nedda plays Colombina/Columbine, and Arlecchino (another clown) has earlier summoned Nedda offstage with the serenade 'O Colombina.' As the performance continues, Canio — tortured by the parallel to his real situation — demands Nedda name her lover. When she refuses, he stabs her. Silvio rushes onto the stage and is killed as well. The opera closes with the declaration 'La commedia è finita!' ('The comedy is over!'), echoing the traditional closing of commedia dell'arte; whether Canio or Tonio delivers this line varies by production (in Franco Zeffirelli's 1982 film, it was sung by Juan Pons as Tonio, not Placido Domingo as Canio; in the original 1892 production, Victor Maurel, who also sang the prologue, delivered it). After the line, the score calls for three strokes on a tam-tam.

The soprano role of Nedda was created at the 1893 Covent Garden premiere by Nellie Melba. The role of Canio is a dramatic tenor part; among the most celebrated interpreters are Enrico Caruso and Luciano Pavarotti. The opera also appeared in a Seinfeld episode titled 'The Opera,' in which 'Crazy' Joe Davola weeps while listening to 'Vesti la giubba.'
