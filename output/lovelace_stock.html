To Lucasta, Going to the Wars

Speaker apologizes for leaving his lover to fight for Charles I in the English Civil War. Famous ending: 'I could not love thee, Dear, so much, / Lov'd I not Honour more.' Speaker declares 'a new mistress now I chase' and 'with a stronger faith embrace / A sword, a horse, a shield.' Lucasta is probably based on Lucy Sacheverell, nicknamed 'Lux Casta' ('pure light'). Inspired by Lovelace's service under George Goring in the Bishops' Wars.

Other Poems

'The Grasshopper' is dedicated to his 'Noble Friend' Charles Cotton, depicting an insect getting drunk on an 'oaten beard.' 'To Amarantha, That She Would Dishevel Her Hair' is a carpe diem poem. 'The Snail' calls the title animal 'wise emblem of our politic world.' 'The Scrutinie' notes 'I loved the addressee for twelve hours but now must be sated with Varietie.' 'To Lucasta: Her Reserved Looks' describes Lucasta's face as 'an angel here, the devil there.' Andrew Marvell commended Lovelace's poems.

Summary of Facts

Richard Lovelace (1617-1657) was an English Cavalier poet, one of the 'Sons of Ben' (followers of Ben Jonson), known primarily for two poems written during the English Civil War.

'To Althea, from Prison' was written while Lovelace was imprisoned for his royalist sympathies. Its most famous lines are 'Stone walls do not a prison make, / Nor iron bars a cage.' The speaker sings of 'the sweetness, mercy, majesty / And glories of my King,' and declares that 'fishes that tipple in the deep' and 'birds that wanton in the air' 'know no such liberty' as he does. Only 'Angels alone that soar above / Enjoy such liberty.'

'To Lucasta, Going to the Wars' is addressed to his lover as the speaker departs for battle, concluding 'I could not love thee, Dear, so much, / Lov'd I not Honour more.' The speaker embraces 'a sword, a horse, a shield.' Lucasta is probably based on Lucy Sacheverell, whose nickname means 'pure light.' The poem was inspired by Lovelace's service under George Goring in the Bishops' Wars.

Other poems include 'The Grasshopper' (dedicated to Charles Cotton), 'To Amarantha, That She Would Dishevel Her Hair' (a carpe diem poem), and 'The Snail.' Andrew Marvell wrote a commendatory poem for Lovelace.
