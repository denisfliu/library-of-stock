Hellenistic philosophical school founded by Epicurus, arguing that pleasure (especially the absence of pain) is the highest good. The Epicureans advocated atomism, the clinamen (swerve), ataraxia (freedom from distress), aponia (freedom from pain), and the tetrapharmakos (fourfold remedy). Lucretius popularized the school in De Rerum Natura. The school taught at 'the Garden.'
Atomism and the Clinamen (Swerve)

Epicurean physics is grounded in atomism inherited from Democritus. The key innovation is the clinamen or 'swerve' -- atoms falling through the void spontaneously deviate, creating randomness and explaining free will. Lucretius expounded this in the six-book poem De Rerum Natura, which Stephen Greenblatt titled his book The Swerve after. The Epicureans believed the gods occupied areas devoid of atoms called metakosmia. All sense perception is considered true, illustrated by the example of a square tower appearing round from a distance.

Ataraxia, Aponia, and the Tetrapharmakos

The goal of Epicurean life is ataraxia (freedom from distress/disturbance) and aponia (absence of pain). The tetrapharmakos ('fourfold remedy') is a daily recitation from the 'principal doctrines' arguing: don't fear gods, don't fear death, what is good is easy to get, what is terrible is easy to endure. The founder's argument against fearing death: 'when we are, death is not come, and when death is come, we are not.' Gravestones bore 'Non fui, fui, non sum' ('I was not, I was, I am not'). Followers celebrated the holiday Eikas on the twentieth of every month.

The Garden and Epicurean Community

Epicurus taught at a school called 'the Garden,' whose gate inscription reportedly read 'Stranger, here you will do well to tarry.' He enjoyed a simple diet of barley bread and water. In Naples, Philodemus of Gadara led a circle of Epicureans as a client of Lucius Calpurnius Piso. Diogenes of Oenoanda carved Epicurean doctrines on a portico wall. The school was opposed to both Stoicism and Platonism; Plutarch wrote that it is impossible to live well according to Epicureanism. Pierre Gassendi led an early-modern revival.

Problem of Evil and Reception

Epicurus is credited with introducing the problem of evil (his 'namesake paradox'), popularized in David Hume's Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion as a trilemma. The school's materialist, atomist worldview was compared to the Indian Carvaka school. In modern reception, Tolstoy discussed adopting Epicureanism in A Confession, and Ninon de Lenclos is considered a key figure in the 17th-century revival. Santayana's Three Philosophical Poets begins with Lucretius as an Epicurean.

Summary of Facts

Epicureanism is a Hellenistic philosophical school founded by Epicurus, centered on the pursuit of pleasure understood primarily as the absence of pain. The school's physics derives from Democritus's atomism, with the crucial innovation of the clinamen or 'swerve' -- atoms spontaneously deviating from their path, which explains both randomness and free will. Lucretius's six-book poem De Rerum Natura is the primary surviving text expounding Epicurean physics, and Stephen Greenblatt's The Swerve discusses its rediscovery.

The ethical goal is achieving ataraxia (freedom from distress) and aponia (absence of pain). The tetrapharmakos ('fourfold remedy'), drawn from the 'principal doctrines,' was recited daily. Epicurus argued that fearing death is irrational ('when we are, death is not come'). Followers' gravestones bore 'Non fui, fui, non sum.' The school celebrated the holiday Eikas on the twentieth of each month.

Epicurus taught at 'the Garden' in Athens, living simply on barley bread and water. He argued that all sense perception is true, using the example of a square tower appearing round. He posited that gods occupy the metakosmia (spaces between atoms) and have no concern for mortals. The school established three criteria of truth: feelings, preconceptions, and sense-perceptions.

Philodemus of Gadara led a circle in Naples (writings found at Herculaneum). Diogenes of Oenoanda carved doctrines on a wall. The school opposed Stoicism and Platonism. Epicurus is credited with the problem of evil, popularized through Hume. Pierre Gassendi revived the school in the early modern period. The Epicureans were compared to India's Carvaka school for shared hedonistic and atomistic doctrines.
