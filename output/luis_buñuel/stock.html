Spanish-born surrealist film director, active primarily in Mexico and France. Pioneering figure of surrealism in cinema. Best known for collaborating with Salvador Dalí on Un Chien Andalou (1929, opens with an eyeball being sliced) and L'Age d'Or (1930). Major later films include The Discreet Charm of the Bourgeoisie, Belle de Jour (starring Catherine Deneuve), The Exterminating Angel, Viridiana, and Los Olvidados. Un Chien Andalou inspired the Pixies song 'Debaser.'
Un Chien Andalou (1929)

Iconic silent surrealist short film co-written and co-directed with Salvador Dalí. Opens with a close-up of a razor slicing a woman's eyeball (intercut with a cloud crossing the moon). Other images: ants crawling out of a man's hand; a man laboriously pulling two pianos containing decaying donkeys and two priests. A young man in a nun's outfit also appears. Inspired the Pixies song 'Debaser' (which misquotes the title as 'Un Chien Andalusia'). Wikipedia

L'Age d'Or (The Age of Gold, 1930)

Second Buñuel/Dalí surrealist collaboration. Opens with documentary footage of scorpions paired with intertitles describing their life. The lovers Gaston Modot and Lya Lys are repeatedly sexually thwarted by bourgeois French society. A woman erotically sucks on the toes of a religious statue. Caused a riot when far-right Frenchmen destroyed paintings being shown with it. Several scalps on a cross appear in the film. Features a montage beginning with a tilt up the Eiffel Tower with a voice-over about problems plaguing capitals. Wikipedia

Belle de Jour (1967)

Catherine Deneuve plays a sexually repressed housewife who secretly works as a prostitute in the afternoons. Recurring sounds of horse hooves and jingling bells symbolize her sexual desires that her doctor husband cannot satisfy. Co-written by Jean-Claude Carrière. The title puns on 'daytime liaisons.' Roger Vivier's metal buckle pump shoes were popularized by Deneuve's character. William Friedkin's casting agent accidentally cast Fernando Rey (from Belle de Jour) as the villain of The French Connection when asked for 'that Spanish actor from Belle de Jour.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Luis Buñuel (1900–1983) was a Spanish-born surrealist film director who worked primarily in Mexico and France. His most iconic work is Un Chien Andalou (1929), a silent surrealist short co-created with Salvador Dalí. The film opens with a close-up of a razor slicing a woman's eyeball (intercut with a cloud crossing the moon) and includes ants crawling from a man's hand and a man laboriously dragging pianos containing decaying donkeys across the floor. Buñuel created the famous Liebestod/Argentine tango alternation by hiding behind the screen with two records — described in his autobiography My Last Sigh. The film inspired the Pixies song 'Debaser' from Doolittle, whose lyrics intentionally mispronounce the title as 'Un Chien Andalusia' (because the songwriter thought 'andalou' sounded too French). David Bowie screened the film instead of hiring an opening act for his 1976 Station to Station tour.

L'Age d'Or (1930), the second Buñuel/Dalí collaboration, opens with documentary footage of scorpions. The lovers Gaston Modot and Lya Lys are repeatedly sexually thwarted by bourgeois society. A woman erotically sucks the toes of a religious statue. The film caused a riot in which far-right French extremists destroyed paintings being shown alongside it.

Belle de Jour (1967), co-written with Jean-Claude Carrière, stars Catherine Deneuve as Séverine, a sexually repressed housewife who secretly becomes a prostitute in the afternoons. Recurring horse hooves and jingling bells symbolize her unsatisfied desires. Alfred Hitchcock complimented Buñuel on the scene revealing the heroine's leg amputation. William Friedkin's agent accidentally cast Fernando Rey (from this film) as the villain of The French Connection. The Discreet Charm of the Bourgeoisie (1972) follows bourgeois friends who are constantly prevented from dining together; the walls of the set fall as characters realize they are being watched by a live audience. A recurring shot shows six people walking silently down a country road (Buñuel reportedly said this was good precisely because it appeared in so many other films).

The Exterminating Angel (1962) was inspired partly by Sartre's No Exit: bourgeois dinner guests find themselves inexplicably unable to leave the music room and eventually smash a wall to reach a water pipe. Thomas Adès later adapted it as an opera. Viridiana (1961) follows a would-be nun who turns her uncle's estate into a home for beggars; the beggars end up holding a Last Supper-style photo after a leper plays Handel's 'Hallelujah Chorus.' The film includes a pointed sight gag where a man buys a dog to free it from a cart, only to immediately see another dog tied to another cart. Los Olvidados (1950) and El (1952) are among his Mexican-period films: Los Olvidados follows children in a Mexico City slum (Pedro's body is thrown onto garbage at the end); El follows Francisco, who falls in love with Gloria at a foot-washing ceremony and later stands in a bell tower contemplating strangling his wife.
