Alvar Aalto was a Finnish architect of the twentieth century known for his humanist approach to modernism, his use of natural materials such as red brick and wood alongside organic curving forms, and his furniture and design work through the company Artek. His most prominent buildings include Finlandia Hall in Helsinki, the Baker House dormitory at MIT, the Saynatsalo Town Hall, and the Villa Mairea.
Finlandia Hall

Finlandia Hall is Aalto's concert and congress hall in Helsinki, faced with Carrara marble to suggest a connection to the Mediterranean. The marble warped and had to be replaced — a mistake not repeated in Essen. The building was meant to be viewed by traffic on Freedom Avenue and includes a congress wing.

Baker House (MIT)

Baker House is a dormitory Aalto designed at MIT while teaching there. He gave it a serpentine, curving shape to maximize each resident's view of the Charles River. It also featured a moon garden roof. Aalto described it as a cross between 'a ski lodge and a ship.'

Saynatsalo Town Hall

Saynatsalo Town Hall exemplifies Aalto's 'red period,' featuring a Renaissance-inspired courtyard with rows of grass stairs between its red brick buildings. It is located in Jyvaskyla, the city considered Aalto's home.

Villa Mairea

The Villa Mairea was designed for the Gullichsen family and is notable for its open living room with irregularly-spaced, thin black steel columns wrapped in bark to echo surrounding birch trees. Aalto designed a kidney-shaped pool here that influenced Thomas Church's Donnell Garden and inadvertently provided the template for vert skateboarding.

Paimio Sanatorium

The Paimio Sanatorium is one of Aalto's early functionalist works, associated with the International Style. He designed a cantilevered plywood chair there to help tuberculosis patients, which is still marketed today by Artek.

Artek and Design Work

Aalto and his wife Aino co-founded the furniture company Artek. The company still markets his cantilevered Paimio chair and other pieces. He also designed an undulating glass vase for the Savoy Restaurant in Helsinki, now known as the Aalto vase. The Ikea Poang chair was inspired by Aalto's work.

World's Fair Pavilion

Aalto designed a wooden World's Fair Pavilion that used airplane propellers as fans for the ceiling. It is considered one of his better-known critical regionalist projects.

German Buildings

Aalto's German works include a Cultural Center built around a roof terrace in Wolfsburg, a 200-foot highrise in Bremen, and a performing arts center (opera house) in Essen. Robert Venturi praised the Wolfsburg Cultural Center in Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture for combining 'standardization with irrationality.'

Other Works

Other works include the Vyborg Library, the library at Mount Angel Abbey in Oregon (with a brick exterior fan and curving interior steel frame lit by natural light), a summer house begun after his wife's death featuring an external fire pit and wooden beam loft, the Rautatalo office buildings in Helsinki, a copper and marble bookstore in Helsinki, and the Muuratsalo Experimental House.

Summary of Facts

Alvar Aalto was a Finnish architect whose work spanned functionalist modernism and a more humanistic, regionalist approach. His brick-obsessed 'red period' produced some of his most acclaimed buildings, including the Saynatsalo Town Hall, which featured a Renaissance-inspired courtyard with grass-covered staircases between its red brick buildings. The Saynatsalo Town Hall is located in the city of Jyvaskyla, considered Aalto's home city, where he designed many buildings including several for its namesake university.

Aalto's Finlandia Hall in Helsinki stands as one of his most iconic works. Originally clad in Carrara marble to suggest a connection to the Mediterranean, the marble warped and had to be replaced — a mistake Aalto did not repeat in his design for the performing arts center in Essen. In the 1990s, the public voted against using granite to re-clad the building. Finlandia Hall was meant to be viewed by traffic proceeding down Freedom Avenue, in accordance with Aalto's desire to build on the work of Eliel Saarinen. It includes a congress wing and faces Toolo Bay.

The Baker House dormitory at MIT demonstrates Aalto's sensitivity to human experience: he gave the building a serpentine, curving shape so that each resident would have a view of the Charles River. Aalto described the Baker House as a cross between 'a ski lodge and a ship.' It also featured a moon garden roof. Aalto taught at MIT while designing this building.

The Villa Mairea, designed for the Gullichsen family, is another major work. Its open living room features irregularly-spaced, thin black steel columns wrapped in bark, echoing the surrounding birch forest. Aalto designed a kidney-shaped pool for the Villa Mairea that has been cited as an influence on Thomas Church's Donnell Garden and, inadvertently, on the development of vert skateboarding in Southern California, as the kidney-shaped pools Aalto designed proved ideal for the activity.

The Paimio Sanatorium was an early functionalist work where Aalto designed a cantilevered plywood chair to aid tuberculosis patients by supporting their posture. That chair is still marketed today by Artek, the furniture company Aalto co-founded with his wife Aino. Along with Aino, he designed buildings in a 'total work of art' style, integrating all aspects of a building's design into a unified whole.

Aalto designed an undulating glass vase — the Aalto vase — for the Savoy Restaurant in Helsinki. Robert Venturi praised Aalto in Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture for combining 'standardization with irrationality,' particularly in his Cultural Center in Wolfsburg. His German buildings also include a 200-foot highrise in Bremen. He designed the library at Mount Angel Abbey in Oregon, notable for its brick exterior fan and curving interior steel frame illuminated by natural light. His summer house, begun after his wife's death, features an external fire pit and wooden beam loft reflecting architectural whimsy. He also designed the Vyborg Library and the Aalto Hochhaus (highrise) in Bremen. The Finnish Association of Architects gives an award named after Aalto; Jorn Utzon received it in 1982.
