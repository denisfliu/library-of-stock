Summary of Facts

Iris Murdoch was an Irish-born British novelist and philosopher. Her most frequently asked-about novels are The Sea, The Sea and Under the Net.

The Sea, The Sea won the Man Booker Prize and follows the retired playwright Charles Arrowby, who moves to a coastal house called Shruff End and obsesses over his first love Mary Hartley Fitch. Titus drowns, and Arrowby blames the husband of his childhood crush. He is saved from a whirlpool by his levitating Buddhist cousin and sees a dragon on LSD. In quizbowl, this novel is frequently paired with John Banville's The Sea, which also won the Booker Prize.

Under the Net, her first novel, follows writer Jake Donaghue, who kidnaps movie star dog Mr. Mars with Finn O'Finney and adapts his conversations with Hugo Belfounder into the book The Silencer. Sammy Starfield and film star Sadie Quentin plot to steal Jake's manuscript; Jake retrieves his translation of Le Rossignol de Bois. Jake searches for his ex Anna Quentin in Paris on Bastille Day and visits pubs like the 'Skinner's Arms' searching for Hugo. Jake takes Beckett's Murphy with him when evicted from Madge's flat. Hugo ultimately leaves everything to the political activist Lefty Todd's party. The novel is dedicated to Raymond Queneau.

The Black Prince follows Bradley Pearson, who is convicted of the murder of Arnold Baffin after falling in love with Baffin's daughter Julian. The novel ends with 'Post-Scripts' by the characters, who read the main text and offer their own versions of the story.

The Bell is set at Imber Court, a lay religious community near an enclosed convent. Toby Gashe discovers the title object at the bottom of a lake. The prospective nun Catherine Fawley is driven mad.

A Severed Head features Palmer Anderson stealing Martin Lynch-Gibbon's wife Antonia. Nearly every possible heterosexual pairing of the characters occurs over the course of the novel.

Other novels include The Green Knight (1993), in which the historian Lucas accidentally hits a man with a baseball bat while trying to murder his brother, and Nuns and Soldiers, in which Christ gives Anne Cavidge a pebble in a scene based on Julian of Norwich's writings.

As a philosopher, Murdoch wrote The Sovereignty of Good, which revived Platonism in analytic moral philosophy. She drew on Simone Weil's prioritization of moral 'attention' over will. In The Fire and the Sun, she defends Plato's banishment of the poets on the grounds that it is easier to copy a bad man than a good man. She distinguished between the 'current view' and the natural law 'view' of morality in two early essays.

Murdoch suffered from Alzheimer's disease, and her last novel Jackson's Dilemma showed vocabulary decline. Her husband John Bayley wrote Elegy for Iris. She had a lifelong affair with Philippa Foot and an affair with Elias Canetti (documented in his memoir Party in the Blitz). Her favorite painting was Titian's Apollo Flaying Marsyas; Tom Phillips set his portrait of her against a backdrop of that painting. She wrote a poem about a 'class' on Aeschylus's Agamemnon that teases Eduard Fraenkel's three-volume commentary on the play.
