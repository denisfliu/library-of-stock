General / Group Identity

The Section d'Or (Golden Section) was a Cubist and Orphist artist collective that formed around 1912, also known as the Puteaux Group. They are distinguished from the Picasso-Braque strand of Cubism by their public salon exhibitions and theoretical engagement with the golden ratio (phi). Jacques Villon suggested the name 'Section d'Or' due to the group's fascination with phi; Josephin Peladan is also credited with coining the name. They met at the home of the Duchamp brothers (Marcel and Jacques). Unlike Picasso and Braque, they lacked an exclusive deal with dealer Daniel Kahnweiler and so exhibited in open salons.

Summary of Facts

The Section d'Or (French for 'Golden Section') was a French artist collective active around 1912, also known as the Puteaux Group. The name refers to the golden ratio (phi) that fascinated the group's members; Jacques Villon (born Gaston Duchamp) is credited with suggesting the name, while one source attributes its coinage to Josephin Peladan. The group met at the home of the Duchamp brothers — Marcel and Jacques — though at least one source places their meetings at Albert Gleizes's home. Unlike Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque, who had an exclusive arrangement with dealer Daniel Kahnweiler, the Section d'Or lacked such a deal and instead exhibited their work publicly in salons. The group was associated with both Cubism and Orphism.

Albert Gleizes and Jean Metzinger were the leading theorists and key figures. They co-authored 'Du Cubisme' (1912), the first major theoretical treatise on Cubism. Gleizes's paintings include Man on a Balcony, Woman with Phlox, and La Chasse (depicting a man with a golden hunting horn and a group of hunting dogs). Metzinger's Soldier at a Game of Chess — representative of his Crystal Cubism era — depicts a central poilu (French soldier) playing chess against a blue background, with the number '24' embedded on his collar.

The Duchamp brothers formed the core of the group. Jacques Villon organized the Section d'Or and designed stained glass windows for the Metz Cathedral; he also painted L'Acrobate. Raymond Duchamp-Villon created the large bronze Cubist sculpture The Horse and a proposed Maison Cubiste facade for the Salon d'Automne; he died of typhoid fever contracted during World War I. Marcel Duchamp (the most famous of the family) is associated with the group's formation, with two brothers (Jacques and Raymond) having adopted the surname of French poet François Villon to distinguish themselves.

Other notable members include Robert Delaunay — one of the original members, and the leading Orphist painter — known for Simultaneous Contrasts: Sun and Moon and Simultaneous Windows on the City, as well as Cubist paintings of the Eiffel Tower. Guillaume Apollinaire coined the term 'Orphism' for Delaunay's work. Francis Picabia showed Dances at the Spring at the 1912 Section d'Or exhibition (and the Armory Show). Fernand Léger was a Puteaux Group member whose early obsession with cylindrical forms led critic Louis Vauxcelles to nickname his style 'Tubism.'
