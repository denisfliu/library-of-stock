Migration Series (1940–1941)

60-panel series depicting the Great Migration of African Americans from the rural South to the urban North. Funded by the WPA/Federal Art Project. Even and odd numbered panels split between MoMA and the Phillips Collection. Featured in the 'One-Way Ticket' exhibition. Edith Halpert sold half to the Phillips Collection against the artist's wishes. Leah Dickerman curated a major exhibit commissioning poems by Natasha Trethewey. Helen Zughaib created a stylistically inspired Syrian Migration Series in 2019. The series catapulted Lawrence to fame after a 1941 solo exhibition at the Manhattan Downtown Gallery.

Summary of Facts

Jacob Lawrence (1917-2000) was an African-American painter associated with the Harlem Renaissance, best known for his 60-panel Migration Series (1940-1941), which depicts the Great Migration of African Americans from the rural South to the urban North. He studied under Augusta Savage and Charles Alston at the Harlem Community Art Center and was championed by Alain Locke, the so-called 'Dean' of the Harlem Renaissance.

The Migration Series was funded by the WPA/Federal Art Project, and Lawrence prevented color variation by painting all 60 panels in parallel, applying unmixed casein tempera one color at a time, with the assistance of his wife, Barbadian artist Gwendolyn Knight, who helped transfer drawings to panels and edited his often-didactic captions. The first panel famously shows a crowd of silhouettes filing through doors labeled 'Chicago,' 'New York,' and 'St. Louis.' Key panels include: three girls in red, yellow, and blue writing numbers on a chalkboard (panel 58); a segregated restaurant divided by a zigzagging yellow rope (panel 49); an empty noose and huddled figure with the caption 'There were lynchings' (panel 15, also described as a figure in orange bowing before a lone branch with a noose); a goggle-eyed judge under a bent lamp (panel 14); a bell, headlamp, and smokestack representing the night train (panel 5); a couple at a bare table beneath a pot hanging from a nail, captioned 'They were very poor' (panel 10); a woman in a red shirt reading 'letters from relatives in the North'; three faceless pallbearers carrying a coffin topped with tulips (died of tuberculosis); a man in blue standing in an X to block a train entrance; and a scene of segregation outside the Dixie Belle Cafe in black and white. In his 70s, Lawrence went back and edited all but five of the captions. The even and odd numbered panels are split between MoMA and the Phillips Collection after dealer Edith Halpert sold half to Phillips against the artist's wishes. The series was reunited for the 'One-Way Ticket' exhibition at MoMA, curated by Leah Dickerman, who commissioned poems by Natasha Trethewey for the catalogue. In 2019, Helen Zughaib created a Syrian Migration Series stylistically inspired by Lawrence's work. The series catapulted Lawrence to fame after a 1941 solo exhibition at the Manhattan Downtown Gallery.

Lawrence described his style as 'dynamic cubism,' influenced by Mexican muralism and the colorful tenements of New York. He and Paul Cadmus contributed to a 20th-century revival of tempera painting. He also created a 22-panel gouache and tempera series on The Legend of John Brown for the Detroit Institute of Arts in 1941, inspired by Horace Pippin's 'John Brown Going to His Hanging,' later reworked into 15 silkscreen prints with Lou Stovall. One panel shows John Brown placed between a cross and rifles; another depicts a figure in red on a rock before a noose on a long branch. At age 20, he painted a 41-panel tempera series on The Life of Toussaint Louverture, now in the Amistad Research Center, featuring a portrait of Louverture in elaborate red and gold military costume; he revisited the subject over 40 years later.

Other notable works include The Builders (displayed in the White House), Tombstones (1942, depicting a black child throwing a white doll), a War Series beginning with 'Shipping out to Victory' (heavy on brown and black tempera), a Murano glass mural 'New York in Transit' at the Times Square subway station, a New Jersey series for the Containers Corporation of America, a self-portrait showing himself scratching his chin while holding paintbrushes, and a version of Washington Crossing the Delaware (#10 in a 1954 series, titled from Tench Tilghman's account of an 'excessively severe' night). Lawrence and Knight were excluded from the Spiral Group due to their renown, though Lawrence studied under Charles Alston, who cofounded the Spiral. He also traveled to Nigeria where he spent time with members of the Mbari Club.
