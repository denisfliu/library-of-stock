13th-century Persian Sufi poet (full name Jalal al-Din Muhammad Rumi; also known as Mawlana/Mevlana). Author of the Masnavi (Spiritual Couplets), a six-book work that opens with the 'Song of the Reed Flute.' Also wrote the Divan-e Shams (Great Divan), dedicated to his teacher Shams of Tabriz. Often cited as the 'best-selling poet in America' due to Coleman Barks's controversial free-verse translations, which are criticized for erasing Islamic references from Rumi's poetry. His tomb is a shrine for the Mevlevi order (whirling dervishes), named after him.
The Masnavi (Spiritual Couplets)

Rumi's magnum opus, a six-book poetic work of approximately 50,000 lines. Opens with the 'Song of the Reed Flute,' instructing the reader to 'listen to the story told by the reed / of being separated.' The reed flute 'complains, lamenting its banishment from its home,' inspired by 'the fire of love.' The Masnavi opens by describing itself as the 'explainer' of the Quran. Contains quasi-Biblical stories about 'Moses and the Shepherd' (Moses erroneously rebukes a shepherd for offering to comb God's hair) and 'Pharaoh and His Wife's Advice,' as well as fables like the mouse and the frog. Includes Rumi's version of the blind men and the elephant parable, ending with the metaphor of distinguishing foam from the sea. Scholars identify seven 'voices' in the work. Also contains the biography of Ibrahim ibn Adham, who gave up the crown of Bactria to become an ascetic. The first two books discuss the carnal self. Wikipedia

Divan-e Shams (Divan of Shams of Tabriz)

A collection of approximately 90 ghazals dedicated to Rumi's teacher Shams of Tabriz, inspired by Shams's disappearance. Contains poems including 'Out beyond ideas of rightdoing and wrongdoing, there is a field' (which in the original references 'religion' and 'infidelity'), 'The Guest House' ('This being human is a guest house / Every morning a new arrival'), 'Like This' (asking the 'dissolver of sugar' to 'dissolve me'), 'That Quick' (comparing self-destructive love to a person with dropsy), and a poem about Jesus running from cynics. Coleman Barks Translations (Controversy)

Coleman Barks's free-verse English 'translations' have made Rumi the 'best-selling poet in America,' but are widely criticized for erasing Islamic references, removing mentions of the Quran, and turning specific Sufi concepts into 'generic New Age-y platitudes.' Barks neither reads nor writes Persian; he works from previous English literal translations. He was introduced to Rumi by Robert Bly, who gave him a copy of A.J. Arberry's literal translation and told him the poems needed to be 'released from their cages.' A 2017 New Yorker article discussed 'The Erasure of Islam from the Poetry of Rumi.' Omid Safi called it 'spiritual colonialism.' Barks read Rumi's 'The Guest House' on Coldplay's 'Kaleidoscope.'

Summary of Facts

Rumi (Jalal al-Din Muhammad Rumi) is a 13th-century Persian Sufi poet, consistently identified as the author of the Masnavi (Spiritual Couplets) and as a student of Shams of Tabriz. He is frequently cited as the 'best-selling poet in America' due to Coleman Barks's translations.

The Masnavi (Spiritual Couplets) is Rumi's magnum opus, a six-book poetic work of approximately 50,000 lines. It opens with the 'Song of the Reed Flute,' which instructs the reader to 'listen to the story told by the reed / of being separated.' The reed flute 'complains, lamenting its banishment from its home,' inspired by 'the fire of love.' The Masnavi describes itself as the 'explainer' of the Quran. It contains quasi-Biblical stories including 'Moses and the Shepherd' (Moses erroneously rebukes a shepherd for offering to comb God's hair and pick his lice) and 'Pharaoh and His Wife's Advice,' as well as Rumi's version of the blind men and the elephant parable, which ends with his distinctive metaphor of distinguishing foam from the sea. Other content includes the biography of Ibrahim ibn Adham (who gave up the crown of Bactria to become an ascetic), a story of a holy man ordering dessert for his deathbed creditors, and the fable of the mouse and the frog. Scholars identify seven 'voices' in the work, including the 'Hiatus' voice. The first two books discuss the carnal self.

The Divan-e Shams (Divan of Shams of Tabriz) is a collection of ghazals dedicated to Rumi's teacher Shams of Tabriz, inspired by Shams's mysterious disappearance. It contains the widely quoted 'Out beyond ideas of rightdoing and wrongdoing, there is a field' (which in the original references 'religion' and 'infidelity' — words Barks omits). Other poems include 'Like This' (asking the 'dissolver of sugar' to 'dissolve me, if this is the time'), 'The Guest House' ('This being human is a guest house / Every morning a new arrival'), 'That Quick' (comparing self-destructive love to a person with dropsy), a poem where Jesus runs from cynics, and lines like 'Silence is the language of god. All else is poor translation' and 'Anyone apart from someone he loves / understands what I say.'

Coleman Barks's controversial free-verse 'translations' are one of the most heavily clued aspects. Barks neither reads nor writes Persian; he was introduced to Rumi by Robert Bly, who gave him A.J. Arberry's literal translation and told him the poems needed to be 'released from their cages.' Barks removes Islamic references, ignores Quranic interpolations, and turns Sufi concepts into 'generic New Age-y platitudes.' A 2017 New Yorker article discussed 'The Erasure of Islam from the Poetry of Rumi,' and scholar Omid Safi called it 'spiritual colonialism.' A misspelling of 'blind' as 'blond' in a previous translation was propagated by Barks. Better translators include Franklin Lewis and Jawid Mojaddedi. Barks read 'The Guest House' on Coldplay's 'Kaleidoscope.'

Biographically, Rumi was taught by Shams of Tabriz, who supposedly taught him in seclusion for 40 days and 40 nights. In one legend, Shams threw Rumi's books in a pool; they remained dry, and Shams said 'Something you do not know.' Shams later disappeared, and Rumi searched for him in Damascus before realizing 'His essence speaks through me.' Rumi then befriended a goldsmith (Salah al-Din Zarkub) to replace Shams. Rumi is called Mawlana/Mevlana, and his tomb in Konya is a shrine for the Mevlevi order. He changed his name upon relocating to Anatolia. The Forty Rules of Love by Elif Shafak is inspired by Rumi and Shams, featuring the homemaker Ella Rosenberg. Kaveh Akbar's novel Martyr! also features Rumi in a dream sequence.
