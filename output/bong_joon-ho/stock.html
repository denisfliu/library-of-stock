South Korean film director known for blending genre filmmaking with social commentary. His breakthrough international hit was Parasite (2019), the first non-English film to win the Oscar for Best Picture and the Palme d'Or at Cannes. Other major works include Memories of Murder (2003), The Host (2006), Mother (2009), Snowpiercer (2013), and Okja (2017). Frequent collaborator is actor Song Kang-ho.
Parasite (2019)

Bong's most acclaimed film. A poor South Korean family (the Kims) systematically infiltrate the wealthy Park household by posing as unrelated professionals. Ends in violence involving a man living secretly in the basement. Won the 2019 Palme d'Or at Cannes and became the first non-English film to win the Academy Award for Best Picture. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Bong Joon-ho is a South Korean director celebrated for blending genre filmmaking with pointed social critique. His frequent collaborator is actor Song Kang-ho, who has appeared in Memories of Murder, The Host, Parasite, and others.

Parasite (2019) is Bong's greatest international success. A poor family — the Kims — systematically infiltrate the wealthy Park household by each posing as an unrelated professional: English tutor, art therapist, and so on. Before ringing the Parks' doorbell, two Kim siblings practice the jingle 'Jessica, only child, Illinois, Chicago' as a mnemonic for fake credentials. The Kims exploit the existing housekeeper Moon-gwang's peach fuzz allergy to get her fired. An aria from Handel's Rodelinda plays over a montage of them expelling her. Mr. Park (the patriarch) comments on driver Ki-taek's 'subway smell.' The family's past failures at opening fried chicken restaurants and cake shops are repeatedly mentioned. Moon-gwang's husband has been secretly living in the Parks' underground bunker, flickering a stairway light to communicate with his wife. The violence comes to a head at a birthday party where the chauffeur Ki-taek dresses as a Native American chief. Parasite won the 2019 Palme d'Or at Cannes and became the first non-English film to win the Academy Award for Best Picture. In his Oscar acceptance speech, Bong described the 'one-inch tall barrier of subtitles' and encouraged audiences to overcome it. Song Kang-ho plays Ki-taek, the Kim patriarch.

Memories of Murder (2003) is a thriller based on South Korea's first serial murder case. The killer targets women wearing red on rainy nights; a pop song plays on the radio each time a murder is committed, and a policewoman discovers the radio station link. In one scene, a detective grasps a suspect's hands to feel their softness, then beats him. The film ends with a former detective peering down a farm drainpipe/culvert before staring directly into the camera after a child tells him about an ordinary man who had returned to a crime scene.

The Host (2006) is a monster film set near the Han River. A giant mutated creature — nicknamed 'Steve Buscemi' by the film's crew — uses its tail to snatch the daughter of a narcoleptic character played by Song Kang-ho. The film was inspired by the real-life 'McFarland incident' and references the US Army's Rainbow Herbicides through the fictional bioagent 'Agent Yellow.' Near the end, a woman shoots a flaming arrow she ignited with her brother's Molotov cocktail. A character nicknamed 'Fat Guevara' asks whether his reward for turning in a friend will be taxed.

Mother (2009) opens with a dreamlike scene of an old woman dancing in a wheat field to a guitar tune. Its best-known song is 'The Belt of Faith.' A mentally ill man is run over by a train after being coaxed down from a telephone pole. Snowpiercer (2013) is Bong's English-language debut, set on a train perpetually circling a frozen post-apocalyptic Earth. Tilda Swinton's character exhorts the poor tail-section passengers to 'be the shoe' and freezes the arm of a man who throws a shoe at her. Bong's debut feature was Barking Dogs Never Bite (2000).
