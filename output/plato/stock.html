Ancient Greek philosopher, author of the Republic and numerous dialogues featuring Socrates. Associated with the Theory of Forms, the Allegory of the Cave, the ideal city Kallipolis, the tripartite soul, and the founding of the Academy. His work gave rise to Neoplatonism (Plotinus, Porphyry) and the concept of mathematical Platonism.
The Republic and the Ideal City (Kallipolis)

The Republic is Plato's most frequently clued work, centering on justice and the ideal city. Kallipolis has three classes (workers, auxiliaries, guardians) based on a 'noble lie' dividing citizens into gold, silver, and iron/brass castes. Philosopher-kings rule; poets are expelled. The guardian class shares wives and receives training in gymnastics and music. The Allegory of the Cave imagines prisoners watching shadows on a wall, with one escaping to see the sun (representing the Form of the Good). The Republic ends with the Myth of Er, featuring the Spindle of Necessity and the choice of lives before reincarnation.

The Tripartite Soul

Plato's theory that the soul has three parts is clued through the Republic and the Phaedrus. In the Republic, Leontius's simultaneous desire and disgust upon seeing corpses demonstrates that the soul undergoes 'opposites in relation to the same thing.' Homer's line about Odysseus striking his chest and speaking to his heart concludes the argument. In the Phaedrus, the tripartite soul is illustrated by a charioteer controlling two horses of opposing natures.

Neoplatonism

Neoplatonism, the revival and reinterpretation of Plato's ideas, is heavily clued. Plotinus wrote the Enneads (compiled by Porphyry), conceiving a spiritual cosmology of the One, the Intelligence (nous), and the Soul. Porphyry wrote the Isagoge and Against the Christians. Iamblichus advanced theurgy. Proclus wrote the Elements of Theology. The tradition spread through the Islamic world via Arabic translations and the pseudo-Aristotelian Theology of Aristotle. al-Kindi, al-Farabi, and Suhrawardi were influenced. Marsilio Ficino revived it at the Florentine Academy, coining 'Platonic love' and writing Three Books on Life. Cambridge Platonists (Cudworth, More, Conway) continued the tradition in the 17th century.

The Academy and Academic Skepticism

Plato founded the Academy in a grove outside Athens. Under later scholarchs Arcesilaus and Carneades, it became the center of Academic Skepticism, promoting reliance on the 'probable' (pithanon) in the face of acatalepsia (incomprehensibility). Carneades opposed Stoic katalepsis and gave contradictory lectures on justice in Rome. The Academy ended when Philo of Larissa fled to Rome during the First Mithridatic War. Justinian later closed its Neoplatonic successor. A plucked chicken was famously brought to the Academy by Diogenes to ridicule Plato's definition of man as a 'featherless biped.'

Mathematical Platonism

Mathematical Platonism (or mathematical realism) is the position that mathematical objects like numbers have mind-independent existence, analogous to Plato's Theory of Forms. The Quine-Putnam indispensability argument is the main argument for it: we should have ontological commitments to entities necessary for our best scientific theories. Paul Benacerraf argued against it in 'What Numbers Could Not Be' using the educations of Johnny and Ernie. Stewart Shapiro proposed a 'working' version. Frege assumed this position in the Grundlagen. Formalism directly opposes it.

Timaeus

The Timaeus is one of Plato's most influential dialogues, discussing cosmology and the creation of the universe. The Demiurge creates the world because 'nothing becomes or changes without cause.' The four elements correspond to geometric bodies (Platonic solids). The Demiurge creates the world-soul by combining Existence, Sameness, and Difference. The Timaeus was the only Platonic dialogue known in medieval times through Calcidius's Latin translation, where it influenced the equivocation of the Holy Spirit with the 'World-Soul.' The story of Atlantis appears in Timaeus and Critias.

Plato's Unwritten Doctrines and Letters

Plato's unwritten or esoteric doctrines (agrapha dogmata) are debated by the Tubingen School (Hans Kramer, Konrad Gaiser), who argue these theories underlie all of Plato's thought. According to Aristotle's Metaphysics, they posit two ultimate principles: the One and the Indefinite Dyad. Giovanni Reale defends them; Harold Cherniss rejects them. Plato's letters include the Seventh Letter, describing his disastrous attempt to advise Dionysius II of Syracuse, and are numbered 309a-363e in the Stephanus pagination.

Summary of Facts

Plato is one of the most heavily clued philosophers in quizbowl, with his influence spanning the Republic, the Academy, Neoplatonism, and mathematical Platonism. The Republic is the central text, featuring the ideal city Kallipolis with its three-class system of workers, auxiliaries, and guardians under philosopher-kings. The 'noble lie' assigns citizens to gold, silver, or iron/brass castes. Poets are famously expelled in Book X, and the Allegory of the Cave depicts prisoners watching shadows on a wall. The Republic ends with the Myth of Er, where souls choose their next lives at the Spindle of Necessity before reincarnation.

The tripartite soul, argued in the Republic and illustrated in the Phaedrus with the charioteer allegory, divides the soul into rational, spirited, and appetitive parts. Leontius's simultaneous desire and disgust at corpses demonstrates this division.

Plato founded the Academy, which later became the center of Academic Skepticism under Arcesilaus and Carneades. The Academy was closed by Justinian. Diogenes the Cynic's plucked chicken incident at the Academy, refuting Plato's definition of man as a 'featherless biped,' is one of the most frequently clued anecdotes.

Neoplatonism is an enormous cluster of clues. Plotinus's Enneads (compiled by Porphyry) establish a cosmology of the One, nous, and the Soul. The tradition spread through Islam via the pseudo-Aristotelian Theology of Aristotle, influencing al-Kindi, al-Farabi, and Suhrawardi. Marsilio Ficino revived it at the Florentine Academy, coining 'Platonic love' and using the concept of prisca theologia. Cambridge Platonists (Cudworth, More, Conway) continued the tradition. Iamblichus advanced theurgy; Proclus wrote the Elements of Theology.

Mathematical Platonism holds that numbers and mathematical objects exist independently. The Quine-Putnam indispensability argument supports it; Benacerraf's Johnny and Ernie thought experiment opposes it. Frege, Shapiro, and Godel have all engaged with this position.

The Timaeus discusses cosmology through the Demiurge, Platonic solids, and the world-soul. It was the only Platonic dialogue known in medieval times through Calcidius's translation. Plato's unwritten doctrines, debated by the Tubingen School, posit the One and the Indefinite Dyad. The Seventh Letter describes Plato's failed attempt to advise Dionysius II of Syracuse.
