September 1, 1939

One of Auden's most famous poems, set 'in one of the dives / On Fifty-second Street' as World War II begins. It contains the line 'We must love one another or die' (later changed to 'and die,' then considered 'trash' by Auden). The speaker watches 'the clever hopes expire / of a low dishonest decade' and declares 'the lights must never go out / the music must always play.' It describes 'children afraid of the night who have never been happy or good.' Larry Kramer's The Normal Heart takes its title from a line about 'what mad Nijinsky wrote about Diaghilev.'

Funeral Blues (Stop All the Clocks)

A mournful poem beginning 'Stop all the clocks, cut off the telephone' with commands to 'bring out the coffin.' The deceased was 'my North, my South, my East and West.' The speaker asks to 'Pack up the moon and dismantle the sun' and declares 'The stars are not wanted now: put out every one.' Originally written for the play The Ascent of F6 (co-written with Christopher Isherwood), with lines about 'crepe bows round the white necks of the public doves' and 'traffic policemen' wearing 'black cotton gloves.' An original version mentioned the fictional 'Ian Shawcross.'

The Shield of Achilles

An ekphrastic poem based on the shield description in Book 18 of the Iliad, with the repeating line 'She looked over his shoulder.' A boy throws a stone at a bird and 'had not heard / Of any world where promises were kept.' Instead of 'marble well-governed cities,' the figure finds 'an artificial wilderness / And a sky like lead.' 'Barbed wire enclosed an arbitrary spot' in place of 'vines and olive-trees.' A 'voice without a face' sends soldiers to war. Achilles' mother 'of the shining breasts / Cried out in dismay.'

In Memory of W. B. Yeats

An elegy for Yeats who died on a 'dark cold day.' Contains the famous declaration 'poetry makes nothing happen.' The speaker urges the subject to 'sing of human unsuccess / in a rapture of distress' while noting 'all the dogs of Europe bark.' The poem declares 'Ireland has her madness and her weather still.'

As I Walked Out One Evening

A poem about a stroll 'down Bristol Street' where the speaker hears someone declare 'The years shall run like rabbits' and 'I'll love you / Till Africa and China meet.' It advises 'Let not Time deceive you, / You cannot conquer time' and contains 'You shall love your crooked neighbour / With your crooked heart.' The poem ends 'the lovers they were gone / the clocks had ceased their chiming / And the deep river ran on.' A glacier knocks 'in the cupboard.'

Other Works and General

Auden wrote many other poems and was connected to many other cultural figures. 'The More Loving One' opens 'Looking up at the stars, I know quite well / That, for all they care, I can go to hell' and declares 'If equal affection cannot be / Let the more loving one be me.' 'Night Mail' describes a train 'bringing the cheque and the postal order' and ends 'for who can bear to feel themselves forgotten?' (written for a GPO Film Unit documentary). 'The Fall of Rome' imagines 'cerebrotonic Cato' and ends with reindeer moving 'silently and very fast.' 'In Praise of Limestone' describes a landscape 'we, the inconstant ones, are consistently homesick for.' 'Law Like Love' proposes law is 'Like love we often weep / Like love we seldom keep.' He co-wrote plays with Christopher Isherwood (The Ascent of F6, The Dog Beneath the Skin, On the Frontier) set in the fictional country of Ostnia. He was Professor of Poetry at Oxford (1956); delivered 'Making, Knowing, Judging' (from The Dyer's Hand). 'Moon Landing' mocked Apollo 11 as a 'phallic triumph.' 'The Unknown Citizen' describes an employee of Fudge Motors Inc. 'The Sea and the Mirror' is a commentary on The Tempest. The Age of Anxiety is a 'baroque eclogue.' He befriended Benjamin Britten while working for the GPO Film Unit. John Ashbery named Auden as his greatest influence; Auden awarded Ashbery the Yale Younger Poets Prize.

Summary of Facts

W. H. Auden (Wystan Hugh Auden) was an English-born American poet, one of the most important literary figures of the 20th century. His poems are extremely frequently clued in quizbowl.

'September 1, 1939' is set 'in one of the dives / On Fifty-second Street' as WWII begins. It contains the iconic line 'We must love one another or die' (which Auden later changed to 'and die,' then disavowed entirely). The speaker watches 'the clever hopes expire / of a low dishonest decade' and declares 'the lights must never go out.' Larry Kramer's The Normal Heart takes its title from a line about 'what mad Nijinsky wrote about Diaghilev.'

'Funeral Blues' (or 'Stop All the Clocks') is a mournful poem with commands to 'stop all the clocks, cut off the telephone' and 'bring out the coffin.' The deceased was 'my North, my South, my East and West.' Originally written for The Ascent of F6 (co-written with Christopher Isherwood), with lines about 'crepe bows' and 'traffic policemen' wearing 'black cotton gloves.'

'The Shield of Achilles' is an ekphrastic poem based on Book 18 of the Iliad, with the repeating line 'She looked over his shoulder.' It contrasts 'marble well-governed cities' with 'an artificial wilderness / And a sky like lead' and describes 'Barbed wire' enclosing 'an arbitrary spot.'

'Musee des Beaux Arts' opens 'About suffering they were never wrong, / The Old Masters' and describes Brueghel's Icarus painting, where a ploughman 'may have heard the splash.' 'In Memory of W. B. Yeats' declares 'poetry makes nothing happen.' 'As I Walked Out One Evening' features promises like 'I'll love you / Till Africa and China meet' and advice to 'Let not Time deceive you.' 'The More Loving One' declares 'If equal affection cannot be / Let the more loving one be me.'

Other notable works include 'Night Mail' (for the GPO Film Unit), 'The Unknown Citizen' (about an employee of Fudge Motors Inc.), 'The Fall of Rome,' 'In Praise of Limestone,' and 'Law Like Love.' The Age of Anxiety is a 'baroque eclogue.' He co-wrote plays with Isherwood set in fictional Ostnia. 'The Sea and the Mirror' is a commentary on The Tempest. 'Moon Landing' mocked Apollo 11. He was Professor of Poetry at Oxford and authored The Dyer's Hand. John Ashbery named him as his greatest influence.
