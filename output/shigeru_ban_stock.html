Humanitarian / Paper Tube Work

Shigeru Ban is renowned for using paper tubes as cheap, recyclable building materials after natural disasters. Following the 1995 Kobe earthquake, he built refugee shelters and the Takatori Catholic Church ('Paper Dome') in Kobe (later moved to Taiwan). He also substituted aluminum rods with cardboard tubes for refugee camps in Byumba, Rwanda. He designed an Expo 2000 pavilion (with Frei Otto) whose roof was made entirely of paper. The New York Times critic Michael Kimmelman called him an 'accidental environmentalist.'

Summary of Facts

Shigeru Ban (born 1957) is a Japanese architect known for using paper tubes and other recycled or lightweight materials for humanitarian disaster relief housing and temporary structures. He won the 2014 Pritzker Prize.

His most internationally recognized project is the Cardboard Cathedral in Christchurch, New Zealand, built after the 2011 earthquake destroyed the original Anglican cathedral. The transitional building uses eight shipping containers as its walls and a triangular polycarbonate roof.

For the Centre Pompidou-Metz in northeastern France, Ban designed a distinctive hexagonal roof of glue-laminated timber, inspired by the shape of a Chinese bamboo hat. A paper tube studio he used during the design process was incorporated into the final building. The museum also features a glass elevator inspired by a gondola, from which visitors 'ski' down to the galleries.

Ban's humanitarian work is central to his reputation. After the 1995 Kobe earthquake, he designed refugee houses using paper tubes and built the Takatori Catholic Church ('Paper Dome') in Kobe, which was later moved to Taiwan. He substituted aluminum rods with cardboard tubes for refugee camps in Byumba, Rwanda. He collaborated with German architect Frei Otto on the Japanese Pavilion at Expo 2000 in Hanover, which had a roof made entirely of paper.

Other notable works include transparent toilets for the 2020 Tokyo Olympics (whose glass walls turn opaque when occupied), the Aspen Museum of Art (his first US museum, housed in a converted hydroelectric plant), and the Nomadic Museum for photographer Gregory Colbert's Ashes and Snow exhibition. Some of his structures are anchored by sand-filled Kirin beer crates. The New York Times critic Michael Kimmelman called him an 'accidental environmentalist.'
