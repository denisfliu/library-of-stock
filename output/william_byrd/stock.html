English Renaissance composer (c. 1543–1623), student of Thomas Tallis at the Chapel Royal. Catholic composer who prospered under the Protestant Elizabethan regime. With Tallis, held a 21-year royal monopoly on music printing. Prolific composer of keyboard music (especially for the virginal), motets, masses, and consort songs. Known as 'Britanniae Musicae Parens' (Father of British Music).
My Ladye Nevells Booke

A collection of 42 keyboard works dedicated to "My Ladye Nevell," including ten paired pavanes and galliards. One of the most important collections of keyboard music from the English Renaissance, alongside the Fitzwilliam Virginal Book. General / Biographical

William Byrd (c. 1543–1623) was an English Renaissance composer, organist at the Chapel Royal, and devout Catholic who nonetheless prospered under Protestant Queen Elizabeth I. He was a student of Thomas Tallis, and the most notable composer of consort songs. Known as 'Britanniae Musicae Parens.' Byrd's Catholicism is reflected in his liturgical works despite his precarious position.

Summary of Facts

William Byrd (c. 1543–1623) was the foremost English composer of the Renaissance, known by the honorific 'Britanniae Musicae Parens' (Father of British Music). He studied under Thomas Tallis at the Chapel Royal in London, and together the two were granted a 21-year royal monopoly on music printing by Queen Elizabeth I — the first-ever letters patent on music printing — leading to their jointly published Cantiones Sacrae (1575), a collection of Latin motets dedicated to the queen. Despite being a devout Catholic in Protestant England, Byrd managed to prosper under Elizabeth I's patronage. His keyboard works are central to his legacy: My Ladye Nevells Booke (1591) is a collection of 42 keyboard pieces dedicated to Lady Nevell, containing ten paired pavanes and galliards, variations on popular tunes like 'Carman's Whistle' and 'Sellinger's Round,' the song 'Will You Walk the Woods so Wild,' a hexachord fantasia, and the nine-movement battaglia 'The Battle' (whose fourth section includes 'the marche of horsemen' and 'the flute & the drome'). Alongside the Fitzwilliam Virginal Book (in which Byrd appears with John Bull, Peter Philips, and Giles Farnaby), My Ladye Nevells Booke is considered the most important collection of English Renaissance keyboard music. Byrd also contributed two pavanes and four galliards to Parthenia, the first printed collection of keyboard music in England. He composed three complete Mass settings (for three, four, and five voices), which circulated without title pages under the dangerous Catholic climate. His Gradualia (two volumes, 1605–07) provided 109 pieces of Mass Propers for the major feasts of the Roman liturgy. His motet Ave Verum Corpus is written in G Aeolian (G minor) and stresses 'verum' rather than 'corpus.' Its opening moves through G minor, D major, and then the unexpected F major, creating an F-sharp/F-natural cross-relation between the superius and bass; a similar false relation appears at the close on the repeated words 'Miserere mei.' The motet sets a 14th-century Latin Eucharistic text whose opening words translate as 'Hail, true body.' On Tallis's death, Byrd composed the elegy Ye Sacred Muses, concluding with 'Tallis is dead, and Music dies.' He preceded John Bull and Jan Sweelinck in composing the earliest known hexachord fantasia, he wrote a lullaby (berceuse), and he taught Thomas Morley, author of the Plain and Easy Introduction to Practical Music. A pair of dances was inspired by the death of Robert Cecil, Earl of Salisbury. Gordon Jacob later composed a suite 'freely transcribing' six of Byrd's pieces, more famous in its version for symphonic wind band.
