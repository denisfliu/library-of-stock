German-born Surrealist and Dada painter. Known for Two Children Are Threatened by a Nightingale, The Elephant Celebes, Ubu Imperator, and collage novels (A Week of Kindness, La Femme 100 Têtes). Invented frottage and grattage techniques. Alter ego named Loplop ('Superior of the Birds'). Third husband of Peggy Guggenheim; husband of Dorothea Tanning; lover of Leonora Carrington.
Two Children Are Threatened by a Nightingale (1924)

Painting/collage hybrid. A grisaille man balances on the tip of his foot on a pitched roof while reaching for a red and blue knob. An open gate is nailed to the frame; a girl brandishes a knife at a bird. A second girl has fainted on the grass; a small nightingale appears above a running woman. Based on a measles-induced childhood fever dream. Ernst's sisters, whose deaths prompted his art, are one interpretation of the title figures. Held at MoMA. Wikipedia

The Elephant Celebes (1921)

A large, dark grey boiler-shaped mechanical elephant (inspired by a photo of a Sudanese corn bin) dominates the canvas. A headless female statue wearing a surgical/yellow-orange glove gestures toward it in the lower right corner. The title derives from a German children's nursery rhyme. The back of the canvas contains an enigmatic sketch with the words 'HA HA.' Held at the Tate Modern. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Max Ernst (1891–1976) was a German-born Surrealist and Dada painter, sculptor, and collagist. His most frequently clued work is Two Children Are Threatened by a Nightingale (1924), a painting-collage hybrid held at MoMA. A grisaille man balances on tiptoe on a pitched roof reaching for a red and blue knob, while a girl brandishes a knife toward a small nightingale and another girl has fainted on the grass. An open gate and a knob are physically nailed to the frame. Ernst said it was inspired by a measles-induced childhood fever dream and called it 'a kind of farewell' to collage. Two figures derive from a popular illustration of a child rescued by a fireman in an asbestos suit.

The Elephant Celebes (1921), held at the Tate Modern, depicts a large dark grey mechanical elephant — its body based on a photo of a Sudanese corn bin — in front of which a headless female statue in a yellow-orange surgical glove gestures. The title comes from a German nursery rhyme; the back of the canvas contains a sketch with 'HA HA.' Ubu Imperator (1923) portrays the literary character Ubu from Alfred Jarry's Ubu Roi as a giant spinning top.

The Fireside Angel (1937), also called The Triumph of Surrealism, was inspired by Franco's defeat of the Spanish Republic. It depicts a red, blue, and orange dragon-like monster celebrating; Ernst described it as 'a clumsy oaf which destroys everything,' and its limbs form a swastika.

Ernst created collage novels by cutting up Victorian pulp fiction illustrations. A Week of Kindness (Une Semaine de Bonté) is split into seven sections using Easter Island moai and lion heads. La Femme 100 Têtes (whose French title is a double entendre meaning 'headless' or 'hundred-headed') introduced his alter ego Loplop, 'Superior of the Birds.' Loplop's origin is traced to the death of his pet parrot Hornebom on the same day his sister Loni was born. Loplop appears in gold and red holding a framed picture, and introduces members of the Surrealist group in collage works.

Ernst invented frottage (rubbing pencil over rough surfaces to create chance textures) and grattage (applying paint to a canvas then scraping it off); Joan Miró co-pioneered grattage. He fled Nazi Germany with the help of Peggy Guggenheim, who became his third wife. He later married Dorothea Tanning; earlier he had a ménage à trois with Gala and Paul Éluard. He collected Kachina dolls during eight years in Arizona. He appeared in Luis Buñuel's L'Age d'Or and starred in Hans Richter's Dreams That Money Can Buy. He also created chess objects for the 1944 exhibition The Imagery of Chess and won a competition (against Dalí) for the best painting of The Temptation of St. Anthony for an Albert Lewin film.
