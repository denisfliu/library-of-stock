Contemporary African American novelist. Two-time Pulitzer Prize winner for The Underground Railroad (2016) and The Nickel Boys (2019). Also wrote The Intuitionist (debut, about rival schools of elevator inspection), Zone One (zombie novel), Harlem Shuffle (crime novel), John Henry Days, and nonfiction including The Noble Hustle.
The Underground Railroad

An alternate-history novel (2016 Pulitzer) that reimagines the Underground Railroad as a literal subway beneath the earth. The slaves Cora and Caesar escape a Georgia plantation and are pursued by the slave catcher Ridgeway, who had previously failed to capture Cora's mother Mabel. Cora works under Mr. Fields in the Museum of Natural Wonders. In South Carolina, she leaves to avoid forced sterilization by Dr. Stevens, who uses Black men as syphilis test subjects. She is hidden in an attic in North Carolina by Martin and Ethel (both killed). Royal works for the Railroad before being killed in a raid on an Indiana farm. The novel ends with Cora reaching the end of the railroad and hitching a ride on Ollie's wagon to California.

The Nickel Boys

A 2019 Pulitzer Prize-winning novel inspired by the 2012 discovery of unmarked graves at the Arthur G. Dozier School for Boys. Elwood Curtis, a driven Black student inspired by MLK's speeches and his teacher Mr. Hill, is falsely accused and sent to a Florida reform school. There he meets Turner (later revealed to be Jack Turner). The school's White House is nicknamed the 'Ice Cream Factory' due to the bruising. Griff is killed for failing to throw a boxing match. Elwood wins fake encyclopedias in a dish-drying competition. At the end, Elwood is shot during an escape attempt, and Turner adopts his name and returns 50 years later to give testimony.

The Intuitionist

Whitehead's debut novel (1999) about Lila Mae Watson, the first Black female elevator inspector in an unnamed city. She belongs to the 'Intuitionist' school of elevator inspection (which uses psychic/intuitive methods) opposed by the 'Empiricists' (who use established instruments). She searches for the 'black box' theorized by James Fulton, who was inspired by the fictional visionary Roland the Carpenter. The novel has been called a contender for the Great American Novel.

Zone One

A post-apocalyptic zombie novel in which 'sweepers' clean up zombies ('skels') in Manhattan. The protagonist, nicknamed after the swimmer Mark Spitz, picks off harmless zombies and suffers from Post-Apocalyptic Stress Disorder. He is part of Omega Unit. Whitehead stated his foray into zombie fiction was partly inspired by reading Stephen King in junior high.

Summary of Facts

Colson Whitehead (Arch Colson Chipp Whitehead) is a contemporary African American novelist and one of only four authors to win the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction twice, for The Underground Railroad (2016) and The Nickel Boys (2019).

The Underground Railroad is his most frequently clued work, an alternate-history novel that reimagines the titular network as a literal subway system. The slaves Cora and Caesar escape a Georgia plantation and are hunted by the terrifying slave catcher Ridgeway, who travels with a boy named Homer and had previously failed to capture Cora's mother Mabel. In South Carolina, Cora discovers forced sterilization by Dr. Stevens and syphilis experiments on Black men. She hides in an attic in North Carolina (where all runaways face execution) with Martin and Ethel, both of whom are killed. Royal works for the Railroad before dying in a raid on an Indiana farm. Caesar is killed while escaping. The novel ends with Cora reaching the end of the railroad and hitching a ride toward California.

The Nickel Boys is inspired by the 2012 discovery of unmarked graves at the Arthur G. Dozier School for Boys in Florida. Elwood Curtis, a driven Black student inspired by MLK's speeches and his teacher Mr. Hill, is falsely accused and imprisoned at a reform school where a work shed called the White House is nicknamed the 'Ice Cream Factory' due to the bruising inflicted there. Griff is killed for failing to throw a boxing match ('I thought it was the second round!'). Elwood wins fake encyclopedias in a dish-drying competition. He is shot during an escape attempt, and his friend Jack Turner adopts his name, returning 50 years later to testify.

Whitehead's debut novel The Intuitionist follows Lila Mae Watson, the first Black female elevator inspector, in a conflict between Intuitionists and Empiricists. Zone One is a zombie novel about sweepers cleaning up 'skels' in Manhattan, featuring a protagonist nicknamed Mark Spitz. Other works include Harlem Shuffle (Ray Carney fencing goods), John Henry Days, and The Noble Hustle (poker nonfiction). He parodied critic James Wood in 'Wow, Fiction Works!' and wrote about the 2012 Olympics with a W.G. Sebald companion app for Grantland.
