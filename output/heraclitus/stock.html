Heraclitus of Ephesus was a Pre-Socratic Greek philosopher known as 'the weeping philosopher' and 'the obscure.' He is best known for his doctrine of flux, summarized in the aphorism that one cannot step into the same river twice, and his belief that fire was the fundamental element of the universe. He introduced the concept of logos as a universal governing principle and developed the idea of the unity of opposites, illustrated through examples like The Bow and the Lyre, and the road up being the same as the road down.
Doctrine of Flux and the River

The single most commonly clued aspect of Heraclitus, his doctrine of flux holds that the universe is in a state of constant change. This is summarized by the phrase panta rhei ('everything flows') and most famously illustrated by the statement that no man can step into the same river twice. The original fragment more precisely states that 'upon those who step into the same rivers, different and again different waters flow.' This doctrine appears as the giveaway in virtually every Heraclitus tossup. Cratylus, a radical disciple of Heraclitus, strengthened this claim to the seemingly nonsensical position that one cannot step into the same river even once. In the Platonic dialogue Theaetetus, Socrates argues that Heraclitus's doctrine of flux renders knowledge through perception impossible. Jorge Luis Borges praised this statement's 'dialectical skill' in 'A New Refutation of Time,' and Kierkegaard references it in Fear and Trembling.

Unity of Opposites

One of Heraclitus's central doctrines, the unity of opposites holds that seemingly contradictory attributes share a common essence. This is illustrated by several recurring examples in quiz bowl: the tension in a bow and a lyre, the observation that 'the way up and the way down are one and the same,' and the claim that sea water is 'the purest and most polluted water' (good for fish but poisonous to humans). Other illustrations include 'the same thing in us are living and dead, waking and sleeping, young and old' and the line 'the name of the bow is life; its work is death.' Heraclitus also championed the unity of strife and justice, stating that 'war is common' and 'strife is justice.' The Heidegger-Fink seminar at the University of Freiburg discussed this concept through the bow and lyre example, and Octavio Paz's book The Bow and the Lyre takes its title from this analogy.

Fire as Fundamental Element

Heraclitus believed that fire was the fundamental element or arche of the universe. Clues frequently state that he believed the other elements derived from fire, or that 'all things are composed of fire, and into fire they are again resolved,' as recorded by Diogenes Laertius. His belief in fire connects to his concept of ekpyrosis, or cycles of conflagration. His 'downward path' describes fire transforming to water transforming to earth. He also stated that 'for souls it is death to become water,' often contrasting water with fire. His advice to have a 'dry' soul follows from this belief. The statement that 'lightning steers the universe' also relates to this emphasis on fire.

Logos

Heraclitus is considered the first thinker to use the word logos for a general organizing principle governing the universe. He stated that 'all things are in accordance with logos' but that humans cannot understand it, and that logos is 'common, though most people live as if they had their own private understanding' of it. Martin Heidegger delivered a lecture on Heraclitus's principle of logos. The concept later influenced Stoicism, where logos spermatikos became the supreme creative force, and was taken up by Philo of Alexandria and Gnostic thought to refer to a divine being.

Reception and Legacy

Heraclitus has been the subject of extensive philosophical reception. Hegel, in his Lectures on the History of Philosophy, claimed that 'there is no proposition of Heraclitus which I have not adopted in my Logic' and considered him the teacher of both Hippocrates and Plato. Nietzsche wrote in Twilight of the Idols that Heraclitus will remain 'eternally right' for 'declaring that Being was an empty illusion' and embracing 'becoming,' and in Ecce Homo stated that he feels 'warmest' near Heraclitus because he affirms the need for destruction. Heidegger and Eugen Fink held a 1966 seminar at the University of Freiburg on Heraclitus, and Heidegger also glossed Heraclitus's term aletheia as 'disclosure' of truth. Georges Bataille wrote a 'Heraclitean Meditation' beginning 'I myself am War,' published in Acephale. Val Plumwood adopted a 'Heraclitean' view of the food chain after a near-fatal crocodile attack. In art, Michelangelo served as the model for Raphael's depiction of Heraclitus in the School of Athens.

General / Biographical

Heraclitus was a native of Ephesus and is commonly referred to as 'the weeping philosopher,' in contrast to Democritus. He was also given the epithet 'the obscure' for his self-contradictory statements. According to Diogenes Laertius, whose Lives of the Eminent Philosophers begins Book 9 with Heraclitus's biography, he deposited his writings at a temple of Artemis hoping that familiarity would 'breed contempt,' and he once fled society to live alone in the mountains. Most known anecdotes about him are preserved by Diogenes Laertius, including that he renounced kingship to allow his brother to rule. His works survive only in fragments organized by the Diels-Kranz numbering system (chapter 22). Hermann Diels developed an influential system for organizing his works based upon the author citing him. He described a drunk man as being 'led along, stumbling, by a beardless boy' while his soul is moist, and stated that 'the most beautiful kosmos is a pile of things poured out at random.' Fragment B119, 'ethos anthropoi daimon,' is variously translated as 'a man's character is his fate' or 'destiny.' He also said that people who observe but do not understand have 'barbarian souls,' that Homer should have been 'beaten with a staff,' and that 'nature loves to hide.'

Summary of Facts

Heraclitus of Ephesus is one of the most frequently clued Pre-Socratic philosophers in quiz bowl, with a highly predictable structure to tossups. The overwhelming giveaway is his doctrine of flux, expressed through the aphorism that one cannot step into the same river twice (panta rhei, 'everything flows'). This appears in virtually every tossup and many bonuses. Closely linked is the identification of fire as the fundamental element, which serves as a secondary giveaway or late-mid clue.

The unity of opposites is the next most common concept, appearing in about fifteen clues with several standard illustrations: The Bow and the Lyre (often the subject of an Octavio Paz title), the road up being the same as the road down, and sea water being simultaneously purest and most polluted. His concept of logos as a universal governing principle appears in about eleven clues, typically in the mid-range of tossups.

Cratylus, his radical disciple who appears in the namesake Platonic dialogue and who extended Heraclitus's river claim to the nonsensical position that one cannot step in the river even once, is a very common power-level clue appearing about nine times. The finger-wagging detail from Cratylus is particularly distinctive.

Reception clues feature heavily at the power mark. Hegel's claim that he adopted all of Heraclitus's thought into his Logic appears four times. The Heidegger-Fink seminar at Freiburg and Heidegger's gloss of aletheia as 'disclosure' appear five times combined. Nietzsche's praise in Twilight of the Idols and Ecce Homo appears twice. Georges Bataille's 'Heraclitean Meditation' is a hard power clue.

Biographical details from Diogenes Laertius are common mid-range clues: the 'weeping philosopher' epithet (twelve mentions, usually giveaway), depositing writings at the temple of Artemis, fleeing to the mountains, and renouncing kingship. His fragments are organized as chapter 22 of the Diels-Kranz system. Notable fragment quotations that appear as power clues include 'nature loves to hide,' 'eternity is a child playing at the table' (from the Derveni Papyrus), 'a man's character is his fate' (B119), 'lightning steers the universe,' and 'the most beautiful kosmos is a pile of things poured out at random.'
