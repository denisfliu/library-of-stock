Summary of Facts

Enrico Caruso (1873–1921) was an Italian dramatic tenor from Naples, widely considered among the greatest operatic voices of all time and one of the first superstars of the recording era. His 1902 recording of 'Vesti la giubba' from Leoncavallo's I Pagliacci was the first phonograph record in history to sell over a million copies. He sold over a million copies of this recording in total. He is most closely associated with the dramatic tenor (spinto) Fach.

Caruso shot to fame through his performance as Loris in Umberto Giordano's verismo opera Fedora, singing the aria 'Amor ti vieta.' Puccini was so impressed by Caruso's voice — reportedly exclaiming 'Who sent you to me — God?' after hearing him sing 'Che gelida manina' from La bohème — that he considered casting him as Cavaradossi for the premiere of Tosca in 1900. Puccini ultimately chose the more established tenor Emilio De Marchi instead, a decision he later regretted. Caruso is also associated with Leoncavallo's Canio in I Pagliacci and played Don José (in Bizet's Carmen) the night before the 1906 San Francisco earthquake.

Caruso was a native Neapolitan who popularized Neapolitan songs including 'O Sole Mio'; his recording of that song became iconic. A pasta sauce called 'Caruso sauce' (cream, onions, ham, and cheese) is named after him. He was extorted by two Black Hand gangsters, whom he later helped law enforcement apprehend.

Caruso's records appear as a pivotal prop in Clifford Odets's Depression-era play Awake and Sing! — the matriarch Bessie Berger smashes her father Jacob's prized Caruso record ('O Paradiso'), provoking Jacob to commit suicide by jumping off their Bronx apartment rooftop.
