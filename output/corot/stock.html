French landscape painter of the Barbizon school, considered a bridge between Neoclassicism and Impressionism. Known for plein air sketches, silvery landscapes at Ville-d'Avray, and "souvenir" paintings drawn from memory. His Bridge at Narni is his most frequently asked-about work. Teacher of Berthe Morisot and Camille Pissarro. One of the most forged artists in history.
The Bridge at Narni (1826)

Corot's most iconic work, depicting the ruins of the Ponte d'Augusto, a Roman arch bridge in the Umbrian city of Narni. Painted during an 1825 trip to Italy. Kenneth Clark praised the preliminary plein air sketch as 'as free as the most vigorous Constable,' though he later criticized the finished studio painting. The preparatory sketch and the final landscape (View at Narni) are distinct works; the sketch hangs in the Louvre. Turner also sketched the same bridge years earlier. Corot's teacher Achille Etna Michallon had previously painted the same view. Wikipedia

Views of Ville-d'Avray

Corot inherited a property at Ville-d'Avray, a commune west of Paris, and painted its pond and surrounding trees many times throughout his career. These silvery, shimmering landscapes of spindly trees before a lake became some of his most characteristic and recognizable images. Ville-d'Avray is virtually synonymous with Corot in quiz bowl. Souvenir de Mortefontaine / Recollection of Mortefontaine (1864)

One of Corot's most celebrated 'souvenir' landscapes, painted from memory rather than on-site. Depicts a mother and her daughters picking foliage from a thin tree beside a larger tree that casts a huge shadow on a tranquil lake. Part of his mature practice of painting dreamy, idealized recollections of places he had visited. Wikipedia

Hagar in the Wilderness (1835)

A biblical scene depicting Hagar and Ishmael in the desert after being cast out by Abraham. In Corot's version, a child in white lies prone on a rock next to a kneeling woman in black who clutches her head in despair, while an angel flies toward them in the distance. Corot re-used a study of oak trees at Bas-Breau for this painting, blending his landscape studies with historical subject matter. Wikipedia

Figure Paintings (Young Woman with a Mandolin, Interrupted Reading, etc.)

In his later career, Corot painted many candid-style portraits of lone women, often depicted reading or holding instruments. Young Woman with a Mandolin shows a melancholy woman sitting next to an easel holding the title instrument. His Interrupted Reading shows a young woman who has paused from reading, holding a book in her lap. These figure studies represent a lesser-known but still quizzed side of his output.

Summary of Facts

Jean-Baptiste-Camille Corot (1796-1875) is the leading landscape painter of the Barbizon school and a pivotal figure linking Neoclassicism to Impressionism. In quiz bowl, he appears almost exclusively in Fine Arts Visual tossups and bonuses at difficulty 7-10, with The Bridge at Narni serving as his signature giveaway clue -- this painting of the ruined Ponte d'Augusto in the Umbrian city of Narni is referenced in nearly every question about him. His plein air sketch for the painting is often distinguished from the finished studio version, and Kenneth Clark's praise of the sketch is a notable power clue. His views of Ville-d'Avray, where he inherited property and repeatedly painted the silvery pond and spindly trees, are the second most common identifier. Souvenir de Mortefontaine (also called Recollection of Mortefontaine) is his most celebrated 'souvenir' landscape, depicting a mother and children picking from a tree beside a tranquil lake, and questions often note that his mature practice involved painting landscapes from memory. Hagar in the Wilderness, a biblical scene with a kneeling woman and a child on a rock in a desert, is another regularly asked work, sometimes clued through his re-use of a Bas-Breau oak tree study. Biographical identifiers include his role as teacher of Berthe Morisot and Camille Pissarro, his study under Victor Bertin, his formative 1825 trip to Italy, and his status as one of the most forged artists in history (Rene Huyghe famously quipped he 'painted 3,000 canvases, 10,000 of which have been sold in America'). Less common but still tested clues include his cliche-verres, his figure paintings of solitary women, Le Concert Champetre, Diana and Actaeon, and Homer and the Shepherds.
