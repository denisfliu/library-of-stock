Summary of Facts

Francisco de Zurbarán (1598–1664) was a Spanish Baroque painter from Seville, known as the 'Spanish Caravaggio' for his heavy use of chiaroscuro and tenebrism. His paintings can be divided into 'cool,' 'warm,' and 'vaporous' styles depending on the degree of tenebrism used.

His most frequently clued work is The Martyrdom of Saint Serapion, depicting a white-robed Mercedarian saint with arms bound to a tree by ropes, head slumping to the right. A red and yellow (or orange) Mercedarian badge is pinned to the stark white scapular, which contrasts with the dark background. A small scroll reading 'B. Serapius' appears at right. Agnus Dei depicts a pure white lamb lying with its feet tied together on a dark field — a painting that doubles as both a bodegon and a religious painting.

His Still Life with Lemons, Oranges, and Rose is his best-known genre painting, showing a plate of lemons, a basket of oranges, and a cup of water with a rose in its saucer, spaced at equal intervals — possibly intended as a homage to the Virgin Mary. His still-lifes were influenced by Juan Sanchez Cotan's bodegon genre (Spanish paintings set in kitchens/taverns or featuring food).

For the Hall of Realms in the Buen Retiro Palace, Zurbarán painted The Defense of Cadiz against the English (showing the gout-afflicted Duke of Fernandian in an armchair as a sea battle rages) and a cycle of ten panels on the Labors of Hercules. These hung alongside Velazquez's The Surrender of Breda in the Salon de Reinos.

Other notable works include St. Hugh in the Refectory (seven men clad in white saying grace, unaware of St. Hugh entering) and Bruno of Cologne witnessing meat turning to ash (painted for the Carthusians), Christ Crucified (unusually with four nails rather than three), paintings of Saint Francis 'in meditation' (hooded monk clutching an upside-down skull), the Apotheosis of Saint Thomas Aquinas (a 1631 altarpiece showing Charles V kneeling below Thomas, unusually not using tenebrism), the Immaculate Conception (stairway, mirror, and single star in gaps of cloud cover; moved from tenebrism to brilliantly golden versions), The Death of St. Bonaventure (clergy around deceased saint on rich orange cloth), and the Veil of Veronica (early versions with a pin at the bottom, later with blotchy reddish-ochre image). He also led a 'cabal' with Battistello Caracciolo and Belisario Corenzio that monopolized commissions in Italy. He was a contemporary of Diego Velazquez and influenced the early career of Bartolome Esteban Murillo.
