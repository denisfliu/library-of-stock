Spanish Baroque painter from Seville, known as the 'Spanish Caravaggio' for his use of chiaroscuro/tenebrism. Known for religious paintings including The Martyrdom of Saint Serapion, Agnus Dei (a bound lamb), and Still Life with Lemons, Oranges, and Rose. Painted many works for the Carthusians and the Hall of Realms (Buen Retiro Palace). Contemporary of Diego Velazquez.

Summary of Facts

Francisco de Zurbarán was a Spanish Baroque painter from Seville, known as the 'Spanish Caravaggio' for his heavy use of chiaroscuro and tenebrism. His paintings can be divided into 'cool,' 'warm,' and 'vaporous' styles depending on the degree of tenebrism used.

His most frequently clued work is The Martyrdom of Saint Serapion, depicting a white-robed Mercedarian saint with arms bound to a tree by ropes, head slumping to the right. The massive white scapular contrasts starkly with the dark background, and a small scroll reading 'B. Serapius' appears at right. Agnus Dei depicts a pure white lamb lying with its feet tied together on a dark field, and is another signature work.

His Still Life with Lemons, Oranges, and Rose is his best-known genre painting, showing a plate of lemons, a basket of oranges, and a cup of water with a rose in its saucer, spaced at equal intervals — possibly intended as a homage to the Virgin Mary. His still-lives were influenced by Juan Sanchez Cotan's bodegon genre.

For the Hall of Realms in the Buen Retiro Palace, Zurbarán painted The Defense of Cadiz against the English (showing the gout-afflicted Duke of Fernandian in an armchair as a sea battle rages) and a cycle of ten panels on the Labors of Hercules. These hung alongside Velazquez's The Surrender of Breda in the Salon de Reinos.

Other notable works include St. Hugh in the Refectory and Bruno of Cologne (painted for the Carthusians), Christ Crucified (unusually with four nails rather than three), paintings of Saint Francis 'in meditation' (hooded monk clutching an upside-down skull), the Apotheosis of Saint Thomas Aquinas (a 1631 altarpiece showing Charles V kneeling below Thomas, unusually not using tenebrism), and the Immaculate Conception (Mary above a galleon and castle). He was a contemporary of Diego Velazquez and influenced the early career of Bartolome Esteban Murillo.
