18th-century English poet, associated with the Graveyard poets. Most commonly identified by "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard" ('The curfew tolls the knell of parting day,' 'some mute inglorious Milton'), "Ode on a Distant Prospect of Eton College" ('where ignorance is bliss, / 'Tis folly to be wise'), and "Ode on the Death of a Favourite Cat" (Horace Walpole's cat Selima). Also wrote "The Bard" and "Ode on the Spring." Wordsworth singled out Gray in the Preface to Lyrical Ballads.

Summary of Facts

Thomas Gray was an 18th-century English poet associated with the Graveyard poets. He was an alumnus of Eton College, and his poems frequently meditate on mortality, lost innocence, and unrealized potential.

His most famous work is "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard," which opens with "The curfew tolls the knell of parting day" and is set in the churchyard of Stoke Poges. The speaker meditates on the possibility of "Some mute inglorious Milton" and "Some Cromwell guiltless of his country's blood" resting among the common dead — people whose "Hands, that the rod of empire might have sway'd" and hearts "once pregnant with celestial fire" went unrealized. The poem declares "The paths of glory lead but to the grave" and notes that "full many a flower is born to blush unseen." The phrase "Far from the Madding Crowd's ignoble strife" inspired Thomas Hardy's novel title. The poem's epitaph reads "Here rests his head upon the lap of earth, / A youth to Fortune and to Fame unknown." A "hoary-headed swain" kept "the noiseless tenor" of life "along the cool sequester'd vale." Samuel Johnson wrote that the poem "abounds with images that find a mirror in every mind."

"Ode on a Distant Prospect of Eton College" is a Horatian ode opening with "Ye distant spires, ye antique towers" seen across the "hoary Thames." The speaker addresses "fields beloved in vain, where once my careless childhood strayed, a stranger yet to pain!" and watches young men who "hear a voice in ev'ry wind / and snatch a fearful joy." Frolicking children are called "little victims" surrounded by "ministers of human fate" and "black Misfortune's baleful train," facing eventual encounters with "Disdainful Anger, pallid Fear, and Shame that skulks behind." The poem closes with the famous maxim "where ignorance is bliss, / 'Tis folly to be wise."

"Ode on the Death of a Favourite Cat, Drowned in a Tub of Goldfishes" is a mock elegy for Horace Walpole's cat Selima. The "pensive" Selima gazes at goldfish ("What female heart can gold despise?") before "the slippery verge her feet beguil'd" and she falls in. "Malignant Fate sat by, and smil'd" as "two angel forms were seen to glide." Despite mewing "to every watery God," "No Dolphin came, no Nereid stirred / Nor cruel Tom, nor Susan heard." The poem's moral: "Not all that tempts your wandering eyes / And heedless hearts is lawful prize" and "nor all that glisters gold." Gray posits that "a favourite has no friend." "China's gayest art had dyed / The azure flowers that blow" describes the tub. William Blake illustrated the poem with two fish depicted as winged, angelic beings.

"The Bard: A Pindaric Ode," inspired by Welsh harp music and considered an early work of the Celtic Revival, is set during Edward I's march through Snowdonia. A Welsh bard curses the invading king, chanting "Weave the warp, and weave the woof, / The winding-sheet of Edward's race" before leaping "headlong from the mountain's height, deep in the roaring tide he plunged to endless night."

The "Sonnet on the Death of Richard West" contains the line "I fruitless mourn to him that cannot hear, / And weep the more because I weep in vain." Wordsworth quoted this sonnet in full in the Preface to Lyrical Ballads, italicizing only five lines as worth keeping, and singled out Gray for attempting to widen the space between prose and metrical composition. "Ode on the Spring" imagines "beside some water's rushy brink / With me the Muse shall sit" and concludes "Thy sun is set, thy spring is gone — / We frolic, while 'tis May." Gray also wrote an Essay on Norman Architecture, in which he noted that the designers of Peterborough Cathedral "seem to have thought there was a beauty in this confusion." Samuel Johnson wrote Gray's biography in his Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets.
