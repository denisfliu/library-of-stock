Wisława Szymborska

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Polish poet who won the 1996 Nobel Prize in Literature. Known for her 'ironic precision.' Most frequently clued poems are 'The End and the Beginning,' 'Love at First Sight,' and 'Notes from a Nonexistent Himalayan Expedition.' Major collections include Calling Out to Yeti, View with a Grain of Sand, The End and the Beginning, and Nothing Twice. Translated into English by Clare Cavanagh and into German by Karl Dedecius. Friend of Czesław Miłosz.
"The End and the Beginning"

Szymborska's most frequently clued poem. Opens with 'After every war / someone has to clean up.' Describes those who 'push the rubble to the side of the road,' get 'mired in scum and ashes,' and notes 'someone must be stretched out' in 'grass that has overgrown / causes and effects.' Also mentions that 'all the cameras have left for another war.' Contained in the 1993 collection The End and the Beginning, and also appears in View with a Grain of Sand. Eavan Boland's anthology After Every War was inspired by its title. Wikipedia

"Notes from a Nonexistent Himalayan Expedition" / Calling Out to Yeti

Summary of Facts

Wisława Szymborska is a Polish poet who won the 1996 Nobel Prize in Literature, consistently identified in quizbowl as a Polish Nobel laureate known for her 'ironic precision.'

'The End and the Beginning' is her most frequently clued poem, appearing in nearly every tossup and bonus. It opens with 'After every war / someone has to clean up' and describes the aftermath of conflict — those who 'push the rubble to the side of the road,' 'get mired in scum and ashes,' and the image of someone stretched out 'in the grass that has overgrown / causes and effects.' It also mentions that 'all the cameras have left for another war.' The poem was contained in the 1993 collection The End and the Beginning. Eavan Boland titled her anthology After Every War in homage to this poem.

'Love at First Sight' is the second most frequently clued poem. It describes two lovers 'convinced that a sudden passion joined them' who would be 'amazed to hear that Chance has been toying with them for years.' The poem catalogs possible near-encounters: 'a sorry muttered in a crowd,' 'a curt wrong number caught in the receiver,' 'a moment face to face in some revolving door.' It concludes with the iconic lines 'Every beginning / is only a sequel, after all, / and the book of events / is always open halfway through.' This poem inspired the Red entry in Krzysztof Kieślowski's Three Colors film trilogy.

'Notes from a Nonexistent Himalayan Expedition' is from the collection Calling Out to Yeti (1957). It is an allegory for Stalin: a 'semi-moonman' is told 'we've got Shakespeare there,' 'we play solitaire and violin,' and 'we've inherited hope — the gift of forgetting.' It also declares 'down there, we've got Wednesday, bread and alphabets' and ends with the speaker 'stomping my feet for warmth on the everlasting snow.' Calling Out to Yeti also contains 'Brueghel's Two Monkeys' (inspired by a Pieter Bruegel painting of two chained monkeys, in which the poet takes 'an exam on the history of mankind') and 'Still' (about Jews traveling in boxcars to concentration camps).

Other frequently clued poems include: 'Photograph from September 11' (about people 'halted in life,' 'above the earth toward the earth'); 'Soliloquy for Cassandra' ('look down on yourselves from the stars'); 'Cat in an Empty Apartment' ('die — you can't do that to a cat'); 'A Word on Statistics' ('one hundred out of one hundred' are mortal); 'The Joy of Writing' ('Revenge of a mortal hand'); 'On Death, without Exaggeration' (death 'can't take a joke / find a star, make a bridge'); 'Consolation' (Darwin 'read novels to relax'); 'Nothing Twice' ('today is always gone tomorrow'); 'Possibilities' ('I prefer movies'); 'The Three Oddest Words'; 'Utopia' ('an island where all becomes clear'); 'Lot's Wife'; and 'Map.'

The title poem of View with a Grain of Sand (1995) states that 'the view doesn't view itself' and an object 'does just fine, without a name.' Other collections include The People on the Bridge, Salt, Nothing Twice, Non-Required Reading, That's Why We're Alive, and Miracle Fair.

Biographically, Szymborska was translated into English primarily by Clare Cavanagh and into German by Karl Dedecius. She published pseudonymously in the magazine Kultura. She was a friend of Czesław Miłosz. Her Nobel speech argued that admitting 'I don't know' drives discoveries, and that writers' 'consecutive results of their self-dissatisfaction are clipped together with a giant paperclip by literary historians.' Helen Vendler wrote about Szymborska and Thomas Tranströmer in an article titled 'Staring Through the Stitches.'
