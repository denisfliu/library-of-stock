18th-century Venetian Baroque painter known for grand ceiling frescoes, especially at the Wurzburg Residence (Apollo and the Four Continents, world's largest ceiling fresco) and the Royal Palace of Madrid (Apotheosis of the Spanish Monarchy). Collaborated with quadratura specialist Girolamo Mengozzi-Colonna at the Palazzo Labia. Son Giandomenico also a painter.
Wurzburg Residence Frescoes (Apollo and the Four Continents)

Grand ceiling fresco at the Wurzburg Residence, designed by Balthasar Neumann. The world's largest ceiling fresco. Depicts Apollo touring the universe alongside personifications of Africa, America, Asia, and Europe. Includes Frederick Barbarossa's marriage in the Kaisersaal.

      Summary of Facts

      Giovanni Battista Tiepolo (1696-1770) was an 18th-century Venetian Baroque painter, one of the last great Italian Old Master painters. He was known for his grand ceiling frescoes using di sotto in su and quadratura illusionistic techniques, and worked extensively in Venice, Germany (Wurzburg), and Spain (Madrid). His son Giandomenico (Gianbattista) also worked as a painter and often assisted him.

      Tiepolo's most famous work is the ceiling fresco cycle at the Wurzburg Residence in Bavaria, a palace designed by Balthasar Neumann. The main fresco, Apollo and the Four Continents (also called Allegory of the Planets and Continents), is the world's largest ceiling fresco, depicting Apollo touring the universe alongside personifications of Africa, America (a woman with a feathered headdress riding a crocodile), Asia, and Europe (seated on a pedestal beside a reclining portrait of Neumann). The fresco is gradually revealed as the viewer walks up the Treppenhaus (main staircase). The Kaisersaal in the same building contains a fresco of Frederick Barbarossa's marriage.

      At the Royal Palace of Madrid, Tiepolo worked for Charles III, painting The Apotheosis of the Spanish Monarchy (featuring the Pillars of Hercules symbolizing Gibraltar, Mercury crowning the allegory of royalty, and Ancient Castile personified as a tower on a cliffside), the Immaculate Conception (Virgin trampling a snake, dove and crown of stars), and a ceiling fresco showing Neptune guiding a treasure-laden galleon. His son Giandomenico assisted him there.

      In Venice, Tiepolo collaborated with the quadratura specialist Girolamo Mengozzi-Colonna at the Palazzo Labia, painting The Banquet of Cleopatra (the queen drops a massive pearl into vinegar to win a wager about the most lavish feast, with a black dwarf in particolored outfit), the Meeting of Marc Antony and Cleopatra, and Bellerophon on Pegasus (with a gray obelisk symbolizing eternity).

      Other works include the Capricci and Scherzi print series (reimagining Castiglione and Salvator Rosa), Death Giving an Audience (skeletal dog), Punchinello drawings, Scipio Africanus Freeing Massiva, and the Memento Mori. Marcel Proust named a shade of pink after Tiepolo, referencing Odette Swann.
