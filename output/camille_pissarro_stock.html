Summary of Facts

Camille Pissarro was a Danish-born (St. Thomas) Impressionist painter, the oldest Impressionist and the only artist to exhibit at all eight Impressionist exhibitions. He is often called the 'father' of the Impressionists and Neo-Impressionists, and a younger painter (Cezanne) referred to him as 'le bon dieu' after they lived together in the countryside. He studied under Camille Corot and Anton Melbye.

His most frequently clued work is the Boulevard Montmartre series, comprising 14 paintings showing the boulevard from above at different times and seasons, including day and night versions. He painted many landscapes of Pontoise and Louveciennes, including Jalais Hill (two women walking around a bend). His pointillist works include Apple Harvest at Eragny, one of four pointillist paintings he displayed separately from his other work at the Eighth Impressionist Exhibition.

Pissarro created Social Turpitudes (Turpitudes sociales), a series of 28 drawings with anti-Semitic content. He circulated political drawings including one where an old man watches the word 'anarchy' rise with the sun. A self-portrait shows his infected eye drooping, painted while hiding in a hotel room from anti-Dreyfusard rioters. He painted two peasants and a hay cart directly onto a wooden palette, and painted Rouen cathedral (the same subject as Monet's famous series). While in England he painted views of Sydenham. Mary Cassatt introduced the American artist Lucy Bacon to Pissarro. His son Lucien befriended Walter Sickert and helped found the Camden Town Group.
