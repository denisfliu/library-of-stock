Summary of Facts

Edwin Arlington Robinson is an American poet known for psychologically penetrating character studies set in the fictional Tilbury Town, modeled after Gardiner, Maine. His most famous poem, 'Richard Cory,' describes a wealthy 'gentleman from sole to crown' who shockingly 'one calm summer night, went home and put a bullet through his head.'

'Miniver Cheevy' portrays a nostalgic malcontent who 'loved the Medici / Albeit he had never seen one,' dreamed of Thebes and Camelot, mourned 'Romance, now on the town, and Art, a vagrant,' and ultimately 'kept on drinking.' 'Mr. Flood's Party' describes an elderly man who climbs a hill to drink alone, compared to 'Roland's ghost winding a silent horn,' looking down on a town whose doors have closed to him.

'Luke Havergal' commands its title figure to 'Go to the western gate' where 'the vines cling crimson on the wall' and declares 'hell is more than half of paradise.' 'How Annandale Went Out' is a sonnet about euthanasia where a physician recalls using 'a slight kind of engine.' 'The House on the Hill' is a villanelle alternating 'They are all gone away' and 'There is nothing more to say.' 'Eros Turannos' features a woman who stays with her husband because 'all her doubts of what he says are dimmed with what she knows of days.'

Robinson won three Pulitzer Prizes and his collections include The Children of the Night and The Man Against the Sky. Louise Bogan wrote a critical study titled Tilbury Town and Beyond.
