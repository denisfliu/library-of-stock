Canadian-American architect (b. 1929) associated with Deconstructivism. Best known for the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao (titanium sheets, atrium 'the flower,' sits on Nervion River) and Walt Disney Concert Hall (Los Angeles). Other major works: Dancing House (Prague), Museum of Pop Culture / Experience Music Project (Seattle), Pritzker Pavilion (Chicago). Recurring fish motif; uses undulating curved metal facades.
Guggenheim Museum Bilbao

Gehry's most celebrated building, situated on the Nervion River in Bilbao, Spain (capital of Biscay, in Basque Country). The exterior is clad in undulating titanium sheets designed to resemble fish scales. A central atrium nicknamed 'the flower' is a defining interior feature. The building is described as a 'metaphoric city.' Jeff Koons's topiary sculpture Puppy (a 40-foot-tall dog covered in flowers) stands outside the entrance. Louise Bourgeois's Maman (a large spider) is also outside. It counterparts the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum on 5th Avenue in New York. Used computer software (CATIA) to design the complex curvilinear forms. Gehry's design was part of the 'Bilbao Effect' — using iconic architecture to revitalize an industrial city. The Guggenheim Abu Dhabi, designed by Gehry, is planned for Saadiyat Island.

Walt Disney Concert Hall (Los Angeles)

Home of the Los Angeles Philharmonic, designed with concave wave-like metallic exteriors. Gehry originally wanted to build it of stone, but commissioners refused after seeing the Guggenheim Bilbao. The stainless-steel panels on the exterior had to be dulled/resurfaced in 2005 because they reflected 140-degree heat onto neighboring condos. The interior was designed as a 'living room for the city.' Esa-Pekka Salonen inaugurated it as music director. A glass complex at 8150 Sunset Boulevard was also designed by Gehry for the same city.

Dancing House (Prague)

Collaborative building designed with Czech architect Vlado Milunic in Prague. Two distinct towers evoke the movement of feet — nicknamed 'Fred and Ginger' (Fred Astaire and Ginger Rogers). After Jean Nouvel turned down the job, Gehry took on the commission. A twisted metal sculpture on top is nicknamed 'Medusa.'

Museum of Pop Culture / Experience Music Project (Seattle)

Built for Microsoft co-founder Paul Allen in Seattle. Red, black, and white sections inspired by a smashed guitar (heavily criticized). A monorail passes through the building. Summary of Facts

Frank Owen Gehry (born Frank Owen Goldberg, 1929, Toronto) is a Canadian-American architect widely regarded as one of the most important architects of the late 20th century, associated with Deconstructivism and celebrated for his undulating, curved metallic facades. He won the Pritzker Architecture Prize in 1989.

Gehry's most celebrated work is the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao (1997), situated on the Nervion River in the Basque Country capital of Biscay, Spain. The exterior is clad in undulating titanium sheets designed to resemble fish scales. Its central atrium is nicknamed 'the flower,' and the building has been described as a 'metaphoric city.' Jeff Koons's topiary sculpture Puppy and Louise Bourgeois's Maman (a giant spider) stand outside the entrance. The building used CATIA computer software to realize its complex curvilinear forms and became the paradigm example of the 'Bilbao Effect' — iconic architecture used to regenerate an industrial city. Gehry is also designing the planned Guggenheim Abu Dhabi on Saadiyat Island.

Gehry's Walt Disney Concert Hall (2003), home of the Los Angeles Philharmonic, is characterized by concave wave-like stainless-steel exterior panels — which had to be dulled in 2005 because they reflected 140-degree Fahrenheit heat onto neighboring buildings. Gehry originally wanted to build it of stone, but commissioners refused after seeing his earlier metallic work. Esa-Pekka Salonen inaugurated it as music director.

In Prague, Gehry collaborated with Czech architect Vlado Milunic on the Dancing House (1996), whose two towers evoke the dance movements of Fred Astaire and Ginger Rogers; a twisted metal sculpture on top is nicknamed 'Medusa.' Jean Nouvel had initially turned down the commission. In Seattle, Gehry designed the Experience Music Project (now the Museum of Pop Culture) for Microsoft co-founder Paul Allen, featuring heavily criticized red, black, and white sections inspired by a smashed guitar; a monorail passes through the building, and a spiral sculpture of hundreds of guitars is a focal point.

A recurring fish motif runs through Gehry's career: El Peix, a golden fish sculpture at Barcelona's Port Olimpic (1992 Olympics); the Glass Standing Fish at the Walker Art Center; and a fish-shaped restaurant in Kobe. He collaborated with Claes Oldenburg and Coosje van Bruggen on the Binoculars Building (Chiat/Day Building, Venice, California), whose garage entrance features giant binoculars. The Pritzker Pavilion in Chicago's Millennium Park uses a trellis of large criss-crossing pipes covered in speakers, classified as a work of art to prevent its removal from Grant Park. Gehry was sued over moisture and water leakage problems in his Stata Center at MIT. His 8 Spruce Street residential tower in Manhattan was the tallest residential building in the Western hemisphere upon completion, with the first five floors tan brick housing a school.

Gehry designed an 'Easy Edges' line of corrugated cardboard furniture and a 1992 bentwood furniture series in which each piece was named after a hockey term. He fixed a full-scale F-104 jet fighter to the entrance of the California Aerospace Museum in Los Angeles. His Santa Monica residence uses corrugated steel and mesh fencing in an 'open-ended' approach. He also led the 2008 redesign of the Art Gallery of Ontario, adding a fir-paneled spiral ramp and a 200-meter glass and laminated wood canopy.
