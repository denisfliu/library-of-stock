Canadian-American architect (b. 1929) associated with Deconstructivism. Best known for the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao (titanium sheets, atrium 'the flower,' sits on Nervion River) and Walt Disney Concert Hall (Los Angeles). Other major works: Dancing House (Prague), Museum of Pop Culture / Experience Music Project (Seattle), Pritzker Pavilion (Chicago). Recurring fish motif; uses undulating curved metal facades.
Guggenheim Museum Bilbao

Gehry's most celebrated building, situated on the Nervion River in Bilbao, Spain (capital of Biscay, in Basque Country). The exterior is clad in undulating titanium sheets designed to resemble fish scales. A central atrium nicknamed 'the flower' is a defining interior feature. The building is described as a 'metaphoric city.' Jeff Koons's topiary sculpture Puppy (a 40-foot-tall dog covered in flowers) stands outside the entrance. Louise Bourgeois's Maman (a large spider) is also outside. It counterparts the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum on 5th Avenue in New York. Used computer software (CATIA) to design the complex curvilinear forms. Gehry's design was part of the 'Bilbao Effect' — using iconic architecture to revitalize an industrial city. The Guggenheim Abu Dhabi, designed by Gehry, is planned for Saadiyat Island.

Walt Disney Concert Hall (Los Angeles)

Home of the Los Angeles Philharmonic, designed with concave wave-like metallic exteriors. Gehry originally wanted to build it of stone, but commissioners refused after seeing the Guggenheim Bilbao. The stainless-steel panels on the exterior had to be dulled/resurfaced in 2005 because they reflected 140-degree heat onto neighboring condos. The interior was designed as a 'living room for the city.' Esa-Pekka Salonen inaugurated it as music director. A glass complex at 8150 Sunset Boulevard was also designed by Gehry for the same city.

Dancing House (Prague)

After Jean Nouvel turned down the job, Gehry took on the commission. A twisted metal sculpture on top is nicknamed 'Medusa.'

General / Biographical

Frank Owen Gehry (born Frank Owen Goldberg, 1929, Toronto). Canadian-American architect associated with Deconstructivism. Known as a 'starchitect.' Won Pritzker Architecture Prize 1989. Recurring fish motif: El Peix (golden fish sculpture, Barcelona 1992 Olympics), Glass Standing Fish at Weisman Art Museum (Minneapolis), fish dance restaurant in Kobe — also said to have inspired the titanium scales of Guggenheim Bilbao. Built the Binoculars Building (Chiat/Day) in Venice, California with Claes Oldenburg and Coosje van Bruggen (giant binoculars at entrance; Google later opened an office there). Santa Monica residence: extension of Dutch Colonial structure using corrugated steel and mesh fencing, 'open-ended' approach. Pritzker Pavilion in Chicago's Millennium Park (named for Jay Pritzker): large criss-crossing pipes covered in speakers used as a trellis, classified as art to prevent removal; located in Grant Park. Stata Center at MIT: sued for moisture and water leakage. Art Gallery of Ontario: 2008 redesign with glass-and-wood facade along Dundas Street. 8 Spruce Street (Manhattan): tallest residential tower in Western hemisphere at completion; first five floors tan brick used as school. Designed 'Easy Edges' corrugated cardboard furniture. Bentwood furniture series (1992), each piece named after a hockey term. California Aerospace Museum: F-104 jet fighter at entrance. Dwight D. Eisenhower Memorial: originally conceived as a 'roofless' temple; mesh tapestry depicting French coast, eight large stone columns. Lou Ruvo Center for Brain Health (Las Vegas): initially refused Las Vegas commissions until this one. New World Center concert hall (Miami). Vitra Design Museum (Weil am Rhein, Germany). Biomuseo natural history museum (Panama City, Causeway Islands). Philadelphia Museum of Art: renovated the Vaulted Walkway, closed for 50 years. UTS Chau Chak Wing Building (Sydney): mocked as resembling a brown paper bag. In 1998, covered the Guggenheim New York's ramp with reflective stainless steel for a BMW-sponsored event organized by Thomas Krens. Critics include Charles, Prince of Wales who attacked modern architecture (Gehry and others criticized him back). Young Gehry designed a company headquarters along man-made Lake Kittamaqundi in Columbia, Maryland.

Summary of Facts

Frank Owen Gehry (born Frank Owen Goldberg, 1929, Toronto) is a Canadian-American architect widely regarded as one of the most important architects of the late 20th century, associated with Deconstructivism and celebrated for his undulating, curved metallic facades. He won the Pritzker Architecture Prize in 1989 and is commonly cited as the paradigmatic 'starchitect' — a portmanteau coined to describe architects famous even among the general public.

Gehry's most celebrated work is the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao (1997), situated on the Nervion River in the Basque Country capital of Biscay, Spain. The exterior is clad in undulating titanium sheets designed to resemble fish scales. Its central atrium is nicknamed 'the flower,' and the building has been described as a 'metaphoric city.' Jeff Koons's topiary sculpture Puppy and Louise Bourgeois's Maman (a giant spider) stand outside the entrance. Richard Serra's sheet metal installation Matter of Time is displayed inside. The building used CATIA computer software to realize its complex curvilinear forms and became the paradigm example of the 'Bilbao Effect' — iconic architecture used to regenerate an industrial city. Gehry is also designing the planned Guggenheim Abu Dhabi on Saadiyat Island.

Gehry's Walt Disney Concert Hall (2003), home of the Los Angeles Philharmonic, is characterized by concave wave-like stainless-steel exterior panels — which had to be dulled in 2005 because they reflected 140-degree Fahrenheit heat onto neighboring buildings. Gehry originally wanted to build it of stone, but commissioners refused after seeing his earlier metallic work. Esa-Pekka Salonen inaugurated it as music director; he also composed Wing on Wing for the opening, scored for two sopranos and orchestra, which includes recordings of a fish and of Gehry speaking. John Adams's The Dharma at Big Sur also premiered at the opening, conducted by Salonen; its two movements are A New Day and Sri Moonstone, and it features a solo six-string electric violin. The interior was designed as a 'living room for the city,' contrasting with the cozier Dorothy Chandler Pavilion.

In Prague, Gehry collaborated with Czech architect Vlado Milunic on the Dancing House (1996), whose two towers evoke the dance movements of Fred Astaire and Ginger Rogers; a twisted metal sculpture on top is nicknamed 'Medusa.' Jean Nouvel had initially turned down the commission. In Seattle, Gehry designed the Experience Music Project (now the Museum of Pop Culture) for Microsoft co-founder Paul Allen, featuring heavily criticized red, black, and white sections inspired by a smashed guitar; a monorail passes through the building, and a spiral sculpture of hundreds of guitars is a focal point.

A recurring fish motif runs through Gehry's career: El Peix, a golden fish sculpture at Barcelona's Port Olimpic (1992 Olympics); the Glass Standing Fish at the Weisman Art Museum (Minneapolis); and a fish dance restaurant in Kobe. Notably, the titanium scales of Guggenheim Bilbao are also said to have been inspired by fish. He collaborated with Claes Oldenburg and Coosje van Bruggen on the Binoculars Building (Chiat/Day Building, Venice, California), whose garage entrance features giant binoculars; Google later opened an office there. The Jay Pritzker Pavilion in Chicago's Millennium Park (located in Grant Park) uses a trellis of large criss-crossing pipes covered in speakers, classified as a work of art to prevent its removal. Gehry was sued over moisture and water leakage problems in his Stata Center at MIT. His 8 Spruce Street residential tower in Manhattan was the tallest residential building in the Western hemisphere upon completion, with the first five floors tan brick housing a school.

Among his lesser-clued works: the Biomuseo in Panama City (natural history museum on the Causeway Islands); the New World Center concert hall in Miami; the Vitra Design Museum on the Vitra campus in Weil am Rhein, Germany; the Lou Ruvo Center for Brain Health in Las Vegas (Gehry initially refused Las Vegas commissions until taking this one); the Dwight D. Eisenhower Memorial (originally conceived as a 'roofless' temple, with a mesh tapestry depicting the French coast framed by eight stone columns); a renovation of the Philadelphia Museum of Art's Vaulted Walkway (closed for 50 years); the UTS Chau Chak Wing Building in Sydney (mocked as resembling a brown paper bag); and a 2008 redesign of the Art Gallery of Ontario with a glass-and-wood facade along Dundas Street.

Gehry designed an 'Easy Edges' line of corrugated cardboard furniture and a 1992 bentwood furniture series in which each piece was named after a hockey term. He fixed a full-scale F-104 jet fighter to the entrance of the California Aerospace Museum. His Santa Monica residence extends a Dutch Colonial structure using corrugated steel and mesh fencing in an 'open-ended' approach. In 1998, he covered the spiral ramp of the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum in New York with reflective stainless steel for a BMW-sponsored event organized by Thomas Krens. Charles, Prince of Wales famously attacked modern architecture, provoking responses from Gehry and others; in a 1984 speech, he denounced a National Gallery extension as a 'monstrous carbuncle.' As a young architect, Gehry designed a flat-roofed company headquarters along man-made Lake Kittamaqundi in Columbia, Maryland.
