African-American author and essayist who lived much of his adult life in France. Known for novels Go Tell It on the Mountain, Giovanni's Room, If Beale Street Could Talk, and Another Country, as well as essay collections Notes of a Native Son and The Fire Next Time. Engaged in a famous 1965 Cambridge debate with William F. Buckley on the question 'Is the American Dream at the Expense of the American Negro?'
Go Tell It on the Mountain

Baldwin's most famous novel, set on the fourteenth birthday of the protagonist John Grimes, the son of a preacher. The novel's third section, 'The Threshing-Floor,' depicts John's spiritual rebirth at the Temple of the Fire Baptized Church. Gabriel is revealed not to be John's true father, and in the backstory Gabriel abandons his illegitimate son Royal. John reflects on having 'sinned his hand' (masturbated) and stares at a picture of his father's first wife Deborah before ascending a hill in Central Park. Baldwin's own preacher father partly inspired the novel.

"Sonny's Blues"

A short story from the collection Going to Meet the Man about a heroin-addicted jazz musician named Sonny who is taken in by his unnamed brother, a math/algebra teacher, after reading about Sonny's arrest in the newspaper. The narrator watches Sonny play jazz at a Greenwich Village nightclub with Creole's band. The story ends with a glass of Scotch and milk that 'glowed and shook' like 'the very cup of trembling,' referencing the Book of Isaiah, as the narrator is reminded of his daughter's death from polio. Sonny suggests 'maybe it was good to smell your own stink' while crushing an empty beer can.

Notes of a Native Son

Baldwin's first major essay collection, partly titled after Richard Wright's novel Native Son. Contains the essays 'Everybody's Protest Novel' (attacking Uncle Tom's Cabin and criticizing Wright's depiction of Bigger Thomas), 'Many Thousands Gone' (continuing the critique of Wright), and the title essay about Baldwin's preacher father's death coinciding with the Detroit race riots and Baldwin's 19th birthday. In the title essay, Baldwin describes throwing a water jug at a waitress who refused to serve him. The collection also criticizes the all-Black cast of Otto Preminger's Carmen Jones for its 'sterile and distressing eroticism.'

The Fire Next Time

Baldwin's second major essay collection, containing 'My Dungeon Shook' (a letter to his nephew about race marking one hundred years of emancipation) and 'Down at the Cross' (also called 'Letter from a Region in My Mind'), which describes Baldwin's dinner with Elijah Muhammad. The title comes from the Negro spiritual 'Mary Don't You Weep.' The structure of Ta-Nehisi Coates's Between the World and Me was inspired by this book. Jesmyn Ward edited an anthology titled The Fire This Time as a nod to this work.

The Devil Finds Work

Baldwin's book-length essay on film criticism. The last chapter analyzes the racial subtext of The Exorcist. Baldwin describes how the same loyal maid archetype appears from The Birth of a Nation to Guess Who's Coming to Dinner. The book opens with a childhood memory of 'Joan Crawford's straight, narrow, and lonely back.' Baldwin also criticizes the I Shall Spit on Your Graves adaptation and discusses The Plainsman's Gary Cooper 'killing off the Indians.'

General / Biographical

Baldwin was a gay Black author who spent most of his adult life in France, with his later years in Saint-Paul-de-Vence. He debated William F. Buckley at Cambridge in 1965 on the question 'Is the American Dream at the Expense of the American Negro?', winning by consensus. He hosted a birthday party for jailed Black Panther leader Huey Newton in 1968. His feud with Richard Wright over Native Son began in a Paris cafe. He abandoned a Malcolm X screenplay that later became Spike Lee's film. He also wrote the play Blues for Mister Charlie about the murder of Richard Henry by the white Lyle Britten, and the novel Just Above My Head about a teenage evangelist and soul singer Hall Montana. In Another Country, the jazz musician Rufus Scott jumps off the George Washington Bridge.

Summary of Facts

James Baldwin was a gay African-American author and essayist who spent most of his adult life in France, especially Saint-Paul-de-Vence. In quizbowl, he is among the most frequently clued American authors, with a rich corpus spanning novels, essays, short stories, and cultural criticism.

Baldwin's most commonly clued novel is Go Tell It on the Mountain, set on the fourteenth birthday of John Grimes, the son of a preacher. The third section, 'The Threshing-Floor,' depicts John's spiritual rebirth at the Temple of the Fire Baptized Church. Gabriel is revealed not to be John's true father. Giovanni's Room, set in Paris, follows David's affair with the Italian bartender Giovanni, who is guillotined for murdering the bar owner Guillaume; David's engagement to Hella and his sense of being 'dirty' after same-sex intimacy are central themes. If Beale Street Could Talk depicts the sculptor Fonny falsely accused of rape while his fiancee Tish is pregnant, and was adapted into a 2019 Barry Jenkins film. In Another Country, the jazz musician Rufus Scott jumps off the George Washington Bridge.

Baldwin's short story 'Sonny's Blues,' from the collection Going to Meet the Man, follows a heroin-addicted jazz musician taken in by his math-teacher brother, ending with a glass of Scotch and milk shaking like 'the very cup of trembling.' The title story of Going to Meet the Man depicts a white sheriff recalling a Black man being castrated and burned alive.

Baldwin's essay collections are equally important. Notes of a Native Son contains 'Everybody's Protest Novel' (attacking Uncle Tom's Cabin and Richard Wright's Native Son for trapping Black characters in stereotypes), 'Many Thousands Gone,' the Carmen Jones essay criticizing the film's 'sterile and distressing eroticism,' and the title essay about his father's death coinciding with the Detroit race riots. The Fire Next Time includes 'My Dungeon Shook' (a letter to his nephew marking one hundred years of emancipation) and 'Down at the Cross' (about dinner with Elijah Muhammad); its title comes from the Negro spiritual 'Mary Don't You Weep.' Ta-Nehisi Coates's Between the World and Me was structurally inspired by this book. The Devil Finds Work is Baldwin's book-length film criticism, analyzing racial subtexts from The Birth of a Nation to The Exorcist.

Baldwin's unfinished manuscript Remember This House, about Malcolm X, MLK, and Medgar Evers, became the 2016 documentary I Am Not Your Negro by Raoul Peck, narrated by Samuel L. Jackson. Baldwin famously debated William F. Buckley at Cambridge in 1965 on 'Is the American Dream at the Expense of the American Negro?', winning by consensus. He feuded with Richard Wright over the depiction of Bigger Thomas. He also hosted a birthday party for jailed Black Panther leader Huey Newton, wrote the play Blues for Mister Charlie, and abandoned a Malcolm X screenplay that Spike Lee later completed.
