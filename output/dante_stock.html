Summary of Facts

Dante Alighieri is a medieval Italian poet best known for The Divine Comedy, an epic poem in three parts (Inferno, Purgatorio, Paradiso) written in terza rima, a verse form he invented consisting of stanzas with 11-syllable lines where the first and third lines rhyme and the second line rhymes with the first and last lines of the next stanza.

The Divine Comedy opens 'Midway through life's journey' with the narrator finding himself in 'a forest dark,' where he encounters a lion, a leopard, and a she-wolf. The inscription 'Abandon all hope, ye who enter here' marks the entrance to Hell. Virgil guides Dante through the Inferno and Purgatorio, while Beatrice guides him through Paradiso. Gustave Dore created famous illustrations of the poem, including one of Phlegyas rowing Dante and Virgil across the river Styx in Canto VIII. The poem was illustrated by Domenico di Michelino in Florence Cathedral. Henry Wadsworth Longfellow made an important English translation.

In the Inferno, Canto V features Francesca da Rimini, who shares an adulterous kiss while reading about 'Lancelot, how Love did him enthrall.' Dante asks her to relate how 'Love conceded that you should know your dubious desires.' The 'Canto of Ulysses' (Canto XXVI) features Ulysses declaring 'you were made men / To follow after knowledge and excellence' -- Primo Levi famously describes reciting this canto from memory in Auschwitz. Ugolino bites down on his own fingers in the lower circles. The first circle contains Limbo, populated with virtuous pagans. The city of Dis features a burning background. Lars von Trier based The House that Jack Built on the poem, and Pasolini's Salo takes its section titles from it.

In the Paradiso, Dante meets his great-great-grandfather Cacciaguida on Mars (the fifth sphere), who identifies souls forming the shape of a Greek cross. Beatrice explains the spots of the Moon to Dante in the second canto. The work's cosmology adapted the concept of the Empyrean.

In the Purgatorio, the troubadour Sordello appears. Thomas Merton's The Seven Storey Mountain is titled in reference to Dante's Purgatory.

La Vita Nuova is Dante's early collection of mixed prose and poetry (a prosimetrum) written in the 'sweet new style' (dolce stil novo). It describes his first sight of Beatrice Portinari. The character Love confronts the narrator while clothed in a fiery cloud, saying 'Ego dominus tuus' ('I am your master'). In a vision, Love feeds Dante's heart to Beatrice. Dante writes 'Love hath so long possessed me for his own.' The work begins by noting the 'incipit' in 'that part of the book of my memory.' Dante Gabriel Rossetti made an 1861 translation and painted Elizabeth Siddal as Beatrice.

The Convivio ('The Banquet') is an unfinished work featuring two sections themed on a Boethius-inspired praise for Lady Philosophy, referring to the traditional account that Eros is the son of Venus.

Dante was a close friend of Guido Cavalcanti, a fellow dolce stil novo poet. T.S. Eliot was obsessed with Dante: 'The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock' opens with a six-line quote from the Inferno; 'The Waste Land' calls Ezra Pound 'the better craftsman' referencing Dante; 'Little Gidding' employs terza rima; and Eliot wrote essays titled 'What Dante Means to Me' and simply 'Dante.' Samuel Beckett wrote an essay titled 'Dante... Bruno. Vico.. Joyce' and the story 'Dante and the Lobster.' Auguste Rodin's Gates of Hell depicts scenes from the Inferno including The Kiss, Ugolino and His Children, and The Thinker (originally based on Dante). Delacroix's Barque of Dante shows Dante and Virgil crossing the Styx. Bouguereau submitted a painting of a nude man biting another man's throat (from the Inferno) for the Prix de Rome. Liszt composed both a Dante Sonata and a Dante Symphony. Graham Harman wrote about the 'broken hammer' of Dante. Mary Jo Bang made a translation alluding to Elton John. Christina Rossetti's sister Maria Francesca wrote a study titled The Shadow of Dante.
