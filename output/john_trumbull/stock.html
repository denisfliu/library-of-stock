American painter (1756–1843) known as the 'Painter of the Revolution.' His Declaration of Independence (1818) hangs in the U.S. Capitol Rotunda and appears on the reverse of the two-dollar bill; it depicts 47 men, with five presenters handing the document to a seated John Hancock. Also in the Rotunda: his Surrender of Lord Cornwallis and Surrender of General Burgoyne (Saratoga), painted 49 years after the fact. He painted Washington Resigning His Commission (paralleling Cincinnatus), the Battle of Bunker Hill, and a portrait of Hamilton that is the basis for the ten-dollar bill. Summary of Facts

John Trumbull (1756–1843) was an American painter known as the 'Painter of the Revolution.' His most famous work is The Declaration of Independence (1818), which shows 47 men — only Stephen Hopkins wears a hat — with five men (Benjamin Franklin at the rightmost position) presenting the document to a seated John Hancock. Two sets of red curtains flank the room, and four captured military flags wrapped around spears hang on the back wall alongside a golden drum. Trumbull excluded 14 men he couldn't find reliable pictures of. Asher Durand built his reputation as an engraver by copying this painting. It hangs in the U.S. Capitol Rotunda and appears on the reverse of the two-dollar bill. Apocryphally, Trumbull was paid to depict one participant stepping on another's foot.

Also in the Capitol Rotunda are his Surrender of Lord Cornwallis at Yorktown — featuring Charles O'Hara and a mounted Benjamin Lincoln reaching out his hand — and Surrender of General Burgoyne, painted 49 years after the Saratoga campaign. The Burgoyne surrender painting shows a man extending his arms to Daniel Morgan and a lieutenant in red, and features a cannon and a potentially ahistorical Stars and Stripes. In fact, two different Trumbull paintings feature rectangular arrangements of stars on flags.

Other Revolutionary War works include the Battle of Bunker Hill, which depicts the death of Joseph Warren (Pitcairn Island is named for a midshipman whose father's death appears in this painting), and Death of General Montgomery at Quebec, depicting the failed invasion of Canada. He also painted Washington Resigning His Commission, paralleling the Roman Cincinnatus, and Washington at Verplanck's Point (leaning on his horse), which he presented to Martha Washington.

His portrait of Alexander Hamilton served as the basis for the front of the ten-dollar bill. He was mentored by Benjamin West, who encouraged him to paint Revolutionary scenes. After criticism in London, he painted The Sortie made by the Garrison of Gibraltar. He designed the Art Gallery at Yale, where he and his wife were originally buried before being moved to Street Hall.

Note: a different John Trumbull (1750–1831) was the Hartford/Connecticut Wits poet who wrote The Progress of Dulness and co-authored The Anarchiad.
