Summary of Facts

John Trumbull was an American painter known as the 'Painter of the Revolution.' His most famous work is The Declaration of Independence (1818), which shows five men (including Benjamin Franklin at the right) presenting the document to a seated John Hancock. It hangs in the U.S. Capitol Rotunda and appears on the reverse of the two-dollar bill. He excluded 14 men for whom he could not find reliable pictures; the painting shows 47 men, of whom only Stephen Hopkins wears a hat. Captured British flags appear on the back wall. Asher Durand built his reputation as an engraver by copying this painting.

Trumbull also painted the Surrender of Lord Cornwallis at Yorktown (also in the Capitol Rotunda), and battle scenes including the Battle of Bunker Hill and the Death of General Montgomery at Quebec. His portrait of Alexander Hamilton is the basis for the front of the ten-dollar bill. He painted Washington at Verplanck's Point and presented it to Martha Washington.

He was mentored by Benjamin West, who encouraged him to paint Revolutionary scenes. After criticism in London, he painted The Sortie made by the Garrison of Gibraltar. He designed the Art Gallery at Yale, where he and his wife were originally buried before being moved to Street Hall.
