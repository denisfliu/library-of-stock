Duration and Time

Bergson's central philosophical concept: duration (duree) is the subjective, qualitative experience of time, distinct from the mechanistic 'clock time' of science. Duration is a qualitative rather than quantitative multiplicity, understood as non-linear continuum that cannot be broken into instances. He illustrated it with several images: two spools with a tape running between them, a color spectrum, and the stretching of an infinitely small elastic band. Duration was introduced in his doctoral thesis Time and Free Will, subtitled 'An Essay on the Immediate Data of Consciousness.' He famously debated Albert Einstein in 1922, arguing against removing the human experience aspect from time and publishing Duration and Simultaneity in response to Einstein's claim that 'the time of the philosophers does not exist.' Bergson described relativity as 'a metaphysics grafted upon science.'

He distinguished between sensori-motor 'habit-memory' and unconscious 'pure-memory.' He argued that memory is not localized in a specific part of the brain, written as a response to Theodule Ribot.

General / Biographical

He also wrote The Two Sources of Morality and Religion (dividing morality into 'open' and 'closed' forms corresponding to 'dynamic' and 'static' religion). Summary of Facts

Henri Bergson is a French intuitionist philosopher best known for his concept of duration (duree), the elan vital, and his works Time and Free Will, Creative Evolution, and Matter and Memory. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature.

Bergson's central concept is duration — the subjective, qualitative experience of time, distinct from the mechanistic clock time of science. Duration is understood as a qualitative rather than quantitative multiplicity, a non-linear continuum that cannot be broken into discrete instances. He illustrated it with several vivid images: two spools with a tape running between them, a color spectrum, and the stretching of an infinitely small elastic band. Duration was introduced in his doctoral thesis Time and Free Will, subtitled 'An Essay on the Immediate Data of Consciousness.' In 1922, Bergson famously debated Albert Einstein, publishing Duration and Simultaneity to rebut Einstein's claim that 'the time of the philosophers does not exist.' Bergson described Einstein's theory of relativity as 'a metaphysics grafted upon science.' A 2015 book by Jimena Canales explores this debate, and a chapter about Bergson and Einstein was omitted from the American edition of Sokal and Bricmont's Fashionable Nonsense.

Creative Evolution (1907) argues that a force called elan vital (vital impulse) fuels the development of organisms, proposed as a creative impulse that drives life forward. Leopold Senghor likened the Bantu concept of 'Vital Force' to Bergson's elan vital. Gilles Deleuze wrote Bergsonism to explain the relationships between duration, memory, and elan vital, and also discussed Riemann's theory of discrete and continuous multiplicities in relation to Bergson and Einstein.

Matter and Memory examines the relationship between mind and body, using the image of an inverted cone inserted into a plane (with the summit representing present perception) to describe how the past affects perception of the present to varying degrees. Bergson distinguished between sensori-motor 'habit-memory' and unconscious 'pure-memory,' and argued that memory is not localized in a specific part of the brain, written in response to Theodule Ribot.

Laughter (1900) is the first notable philosophical work on humor, arguing that the comic is a strictly human phenomenon and that when we make fun of animals, we recognize human behavior in them. The methods of comedy are identified as repetition, inversion, and reciprocal interference.

Bergson's final book, The Two Sources of Morality and Religion, divides morality into 'open' and 'closed' forms corresponding to 'dynamic' and 'static' religion. Broadway's first traffic jam was reportedly caused by one of his lectures. Keiji Nishitani wrote a dissertation on 'the Real and the Ideal' in the thought of Schelling and Bergson. Bergson criticized Plato's theory of changeless 'Ideas' in a discussion of the dialogue Parmenides.
