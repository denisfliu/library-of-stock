Jerome Robbins

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
American choreographer and director, known for his work on Broadway musicals and ballet. Most famous for choreographing West Side Story (with its finger-snapping Jets), Fiddler on the Roof (with the Bottle Dance), and On the Town. His first ballet, Fancy Free, was a collaboration with Leonard Bernstein about three sailors on shore leave, inspired by Paul Cadmus's painting The Fleet's In!. Also choreographed The Concert, The Cage, Facsimile, and ballets for The King and I. First ballet master of the New York City Ballet under Balanchine. Won Best Director Oscar for West Side Story alongside Robert Wise.
West Side Story (1957)

Other Works and Biographical

Robbins choreographed the ballet sequences for The King and I, including 'The Small House of Uncle Thomas' (inspired by khon and piphat music). He choreographed for Gypsy (a Styne/Sondheim collaboration). He was the first ballet master of the New York City Ballet under George Balanchine. He created Opus 19: The Dreamer for Mikhail Baryshnikov. He collaborated with Twyla Tharp on Brahms/Handel. He was fond of Chopin's music, setting four ballets to it (including In the Night based on Nocturnes). He fell into the orchestra pit while yelling at the cast during rehearsals for Billion Dollar Baby. His 1953 duet Afternoon of a Faun was inspired by the Nijinsky ballet. He joined the NYCB in the late 1940s where he created plotless works like Dances at a Gathering. Alan Johnson won an award for restaging his works in Gap commercials (2000).

Summary of Facts

Jerome Robbins (born Jerome Wilson Rabinowitz) was an American choreographer and director who defined Broadway dance in the mid-20th century. He is best known for his work on West Side Story, Fiddler on the Roof, and the ballet Fancy Free.

His first ballet, Fancy Free (1944), was a collaboration with Leonard Bernstein about three sailors on shore leave who compete for the attention of two women and end up in a fistfight. It features a galop, waltz, and danzon by three solo dancers, and was inspired by Paul Cadmus's painting The Fleet's In!. Fancy Free was adapted into the musical On the Town, with book and lyrics by Betty Comden and Adolph Green, following the sailors Ozzie, Chip, and Gabey on 24 hours of leave in New York.

For West Side Story (1957), Robbins choreographed and co-directed the original production (with music by Bernstein and lyrics by Stephen Sondheim). His choreography is famous for the finger-snapping that distinguishes the Jets from the Sharks. The prologue features boys doing ballet leaps after rising from bleachers where they had been snapping their fingers. In one number, dancers hunch over and then suddenly spread their limbs wide to emulate a shaken soda bottle. The Jets dress predominantly in blue and the Sharks in red. Robbins choreographed the songs 'Cool' and 'America,' and the dream ballet 'Somewhere' includes two characters who died in a rumble. He won his second Tony for choreography and, alongside Robert Wise, won the Best Director Oscar for the 1961 film. Carol Lawrence premiered the role of Maria; Robbins employed method acting techniques to foment animosity among the cast. Urban legend holds that actors burned their knee pads outside his office after a taxing scene.

For Fiddler on the Roof, Robbins visited Orthodox Jewish weddings for inspiration for the famous Bottle Dance, in which men dance while balancing bottles on their heads at a wedding.

Robbins' ballet The Concert (1956) is a comedic work depicting the daydreams of audience members at an all-Chopin recital, featuring a 'mistake waltz.' The Cage (1951) uses Stravinsky's Concerto in D and depicts female insects killing and devouring a male insect after sex. Facsimile (1946) ends with 'The Woman' character yelling 'Stop!', a role premiered by Nora Kaye; it was banned for portraying a neurotic love triangle.

Robbins was the first ballet master of the New York City Ballet under George Balanchine. He joined NYCB in the late 1940s, creating plotless works like Dances at a Gathering. He was fond of Chopin's music, setting four ballets to it including In the Night (based on Nocturnes). He choreographed 'The Small House of Uncle Thomas' for The King and I, inspired by khon and piphat music. He also choreographed for Gypsy (a Styne and Sondheim collaboration). He created Opus 19: The Dreamer for Mikhail Baryshnikov. His 1953 duet Afternoon of a Faun, set in a ballet studio, was inspired by Nijinsky's original. He collaborated with Twyla Tharp on Brahms/Handel. He fell into the orchestra pit while yelling at the cast during rehearsals for the Comden and Green musical Billion Dollar Baby. Alan Johnson won an American Choreography award for restaging Robbins' works in a 2000 series of Gap commercials.
