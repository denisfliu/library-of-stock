The Dream of the Rood

The most-clued text in the Vercelli Book. The cross is depicted as covered in gems and gold, and as bleeding on its right side. Variously attributed to Caedmon or Cynewulf. Summary of Facts

The Vercelli Book (also called the Vercelli Codex) is a 10th-century manuscript of Old English literature, named for the Italian city of Vercelli where it is held. It is one of the four major surviving codices of Old English writing, alongside the Exeter Book, the Nowell Codex (which contains Beowulf), and the Junius Manuscript.

The codex's most-clued contents are The Dream of the Rood, alliterative poems by Cynewulf, and a collection of prose homilies. The Dream of the Rood is considered one of the oldest surviving Old English poems and is thought to be one of the finest examples of Old English alliterative verse. In this poem, the narrator recounts a 'best of dreams' vision of the Cross (the rood), which then narrates the crucifixion of Christ from its own perspective — describing how it was forced to participate, how it bled on the right side, and how it was adorned with gems and gold. The poem is in two main sections: the narrator's vision, then the cross's first-person account. Authorship is attributed variously to Caedmon or Cynewulf; the attribution to Caedmon rests on circumstantial evidence in Bede's Ecclesiastical History of the English People. An early version of the poem — predating the Vercelli Book manuscript — is inscribed in runic characters on the Ruthwell Cross, an 8th-century stone monument in a Scottish border town. The complete text was discovered in 1822.

Cynewulf is one of the only named Old English poets, having signed his works using runic letters woven into the text. His poems in the Vercelli Book include Elene, in which Helena's discovery of the True Cross is dramatized and a character is renamed Cyriacus after finding three crosses underground, and The Fates of the Apostles. His other known works (in the Exeter Book) include Juliana and Christ II.
