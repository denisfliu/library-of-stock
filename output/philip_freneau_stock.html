The Wild Honeysuckle

Poem addressed to a "fair flower, that dost so comely grow," "by nature's self in white arrayed." The speaker tells the flower "No roving foot shall crush you here" but admits "I grieve to see your future doom." Contains the famous lines "The space between is but an hour, / The frail duration of a flower" and "From morning suns and evening dews." States "if nothing once, you nothing lose, / for when you die you are the same."

The Indian Burying Ground

Poem opening "In spite of all the learned have said, I still my old opinion keep." Describes Native American burial practice of sitting corpses upright, so a dead man seems "seated with his friends" sharing a feast. Contains the line "the hunter still the deer pursues" and "By midnight moons." The site shows "the fancies of a ruder race" depicting "Activity, that knows no rest." Ends "And Reason's self shall bow the knee / To shadows and delusions here." Questions death as "soul's eternal sleep." Inspired by the practice of burying corpses sitting down.

The British Prison Ship

A poem in multiple cantos chronicling the abuse of Americans aboard a British prison ship. Inspired by Freneau's own mistreatment aboard The Scorpion/HMS Jersey. A man says "If that was purgatory, this is hell" about the location described as a "slaughter-house, yet hospital in name."

Summary of Facts

Philip Freneau is known as the "Poet of the American Revolution" and is a key figure in early American literature. His most frequently clued poem is "The Wild Honeysuckle," addressed to a "fair flower, that dost so comely grow" and containing the lines "The space between is but an hour, / The frail duration of a flower." The speaker tells the flower "No roving foot shall crush you here" but admits "I grieve to see your future doom."

"The Indian Burying Ground" opens "In spite of all the learned have said, I still my old opinion keep" and describes Native American burial practices where corpses are seated upright, so a dead man appears "seated with his friends." It contains the line "the hunter still the deer pursues" and ends "And Reason's self shall bow the knee / To shadows and delusions here."

"The British Prison Ship" is a multi-canto poem inspired by Freneau's own imprisonment aboard the HMS Jersey, described as a "slaughter-house, yet hospital in name."

Biographically, Freneau was hired by Thomas Jefferson as an anti-Federalist journalist. He also wrote "The American Soldier" about a veteran who "sits musing o'er his scars, and wooden leg," the anti-slavery poem "To Sir Toby," and "Pictures of Columbus."
