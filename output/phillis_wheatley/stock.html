First African-American woman to publish a book of poetry, Poems on Various Subjects, Religious and Moral. Enslaved poet from colonial America. Best known for "On Being Brought from Africa to America" ("'Twas mercy brought me from my Pagan land") and "To His Excellency, General Washington" ("Celestial choir! enthron'd in realms of light"). Also wrote an elegy for George Whitefield.
On Being Brought from Africa to America

Wheatley's most famous short poem about her enslavement and conversion to Christianity. Opens "'Twas mercy brought me from my Pagan land" and taught her soul "that there's a God, that there's a Saviour too." Warns those who view "colour" as a "diabolic die" to "remember" that those "black as Cain, may be refin'd, and join th' angelic train." Henry Louis Gates's essay about Wheatley "on Trial" quotes the lines about the "diabolic die."

To His Excellency, General Washington

Poem addressed to George Washington, opening "Celestial choir! enthron'd in realms of light." Declares "Columbia's scenes of glorious toils I write" and commands "Proceed, great chief, with virtue on thy side / Thy every action let the Goddess guide." Lists "A crown, a mansion, and a throne that shine" as rewards. Washington later wrote to Wheatley praising her work and invited her to visit his headquarters in Cambridge.

On the Death of the Rev. Mr. George Whitefield

An elegy for the preacher George Whitefield that opens "Hail, happy saint, on thine immortal throne." Describes Whitefield's eloquence: his "eloquence refin'd" is said to "inflame the heart, and captivate the mind." States he "long'd to see America excel" and "charg'd its youth to let the grace divine arise." Dedicated to the Countess of Huntingdon, Whitefield's patron.

General / Biographical

First African-American woman to publish a book of poetry: Poems on Various Subjects, Religious and Moral. The collection was dedicated to the Countess of Huntingdon and published after Wheatley met the Lord Mayor of London. Jupiter Hammon wrote "An Address to Miss Phillis Wheatley" calling her a "pious youth." Robert Hayden wrote "A Letter from Phillis Wheatley" in which a chimney sweep boy asks her if she also sweeps chimneys. Yusef Komunyakaa's "Lament and Praise Song" envisions Wheatley "on the streets of Cambridge, in her heroic couplets." Alice Walker used Wheatley as an example to refute Virginia Woolf's argument in A Room of One's Own. June Jordan wrote "Something Like a Sonnet for" Wheatley. Honoree Fanonne Jeffers wrote The Age of Phillis. Terrance Hayes references her in "American Sonnet for My Past and Future Assassin." Wrote "An Address to the Atheist," poem on Scipio Moorhead's painting, and a letter to Samson Occom about freedom. Thomas Jefferson dismissed her in Notes on the State of Virginia. Sent letters to Obour Tanner preserved by the Beecher family. Wheatley also wrote "To the King's Most Excellent Majesty" about George III's repeal of the Stamp Acts, and "A Hymn to the Morning."

Summary of Facts

Phillis Wheatley is the first African-American woman to publish a book of poetry, Poems on Various Subjects, Religious and Moral, dedicated to the Countess of Huntingdon. Her most frequently clued poem is "On Being Brought from Africa to America," which opens "'Twas mercy brought me from my Pagan land" and warns those who see "colour" as a "diabolic die" that those "black as Cain, may be refin'd, and join th' angelic train."

"To His Excellency, General Washington" opens "Celestial choir! enthron'd in realms of light" and invokes Columbia, declaring "Proceed, great chief, with virtue on thy side" and listing "a crown, a mansion, and a throne that shine." Washington wrote back praising her work and invited her to his Cambridge headquarters.

Her elegy "On the Death of the Rev. Mr. George Whitefield" opens "Hail, happy saint, on thine immortal throne" and praises his "eloquence refin'd" and his desire to see "America excel." It was dedicated to the Countess of Huntingdon.

Wheatley figures prominently in literary and cultural history. Jupiter Hammon wrote "An Address to Miss Phillis Wheatley" calling her a "pious youth." Robert Hayden wrote "A Letter from Phillis Wheatley" in which a chimney sweep asks if she sweeps chimneys. Henry Louis Gates wrote about her "Trials." Alice Walker used her as a counterexample to Virginia Woolf's argument about needing money and a room. Thomas Jefferson dismissed her in Notes on the State of Virginia. Honoree Fanonne Jeffers wrote The Age of Phillis about her life. She also wrote letters to Obour Tanner and to the Mohegan minister Samson Occom defending freedom.
