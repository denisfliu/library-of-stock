Danish film director, best known for The Passion of Joan of Arc (1928) — a silent film starring Maria Falconetti, shot almost entirely in close-up. Other major films: Ordet (1955), Vampyr (1932), Day of Wrath (1943), Gertrud (1964). Paul Schrader analyzed Dreyer in Transcendental Style in Film. His style contrasts expressionism and transcendentalism; characterized by long takes, rotating/circling camera, sparse sets.
The Passion of Joan of Arc (1928)

Silent film depicting Joan of Arc's trial and execution. Stars Maria (Renée) Falconetti in her only notable film role; Pauline Kael called it the greatest acting performance ever. Almost entirely shot in extreme close-up with no establishing shots. Inquisitors filmed at low angles with harsh lighting (grotesque); Joan with soft lighting. Antoine Artaud played a sympathetic monk. Score by Richard Einhorn ('Voices of Light'). Rediscovered in a psychiatric hospital in 1981. Wikipedia

Ordet (1955)

A farmer's family film: son Johannes believes he is Jesus Christ (having gone mad studying Kierkegaard) and can raise the dead. A 360-degree rotating camera shot follows a girl and her uncle talking about her mother's afterlife. At the climax, shots of birds alighting on a church tower intercut with the protagonist's face. Based on a play by Kaj Munk. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Carl Theodor Dreyer is a Danish film director, most famous for The Passion of Joan of Arc (1928), Ordet (1955), Vampyr (1932), Day of Wrath (1943), and Gertrud (1964). Paul Schrader analyzed Dreyer (alongside Ozu and Bresson) in Transcendental Style in Film.

The Passion of Joan of Arc (1928) is a silent film depicting Joan's trial and execution. It stars Maria (Renée) Falconetti — Pauline Kael called her performance the greatest ever. The film is almost entirely shot in extreme close-up with no establishing shots; David Bordwell analyzed all 1500 cuts and found fewer than 30 maintain visual continuity. Inquisitors are filmed at low angles with harsh lighting, making them appear grotesque; Joan is filmed with soft lighting as she weeps and explains her divine messages. Antoine Artaud plays a sympathetic monk. A grating casts a cross-shaped shadow on the ground; Joan wears a straw crown while crying. Joan's already closely-cropped hair is shorn as penance. The film was based on actual 1431 trial transcripts. It was lost and rediscovered in a psychiatric hospital in 1981. Richard Einhorn's composition 'Voices of Light' was inspired by it. Robert Bresson criticized the film's 'grotesque buffooneries' and preferred his own version of Joan's trial.

Ordet (1955) features Johannes, a farmer's son who has gone mad studying Kierkegaard and believes himself to be Jesus Christ, capable of raising the dead. The film's most famous shot is an almost 3-minute 360-degree rotating camera shot around a girl and her uncle discussing her mother's afterlife. The climax intercuts shots of birds alighting on a church tower with close-ups of the protagonist.

Vampyr (1932) — Dreyer's only 1930s film — is based on Sheridan Le Fanu's Carmilla. Its most famous scenes: a protagonist dreams of seeing his own dead body in a coffin, then experiences a corpse-eye view of its funeral procession; a shadow walks independently of the person casting it; a sinister doctor is finally suffocated by flour at a mill. Starred socialite Nicolas de Gunzburg.

Day of Wrath (1943) was made during Nazi occupation and allegorizes that occupation through a 17th-century witch hunt. A woman reads the Song of Solomon to her husband's son; she eventually falsely confesses to killing her husband with witchcraft.

Dreyer's early film Michael (1924) depicts the homosexual relationship between painter Claude Zoret and his model Michael; it is considered an early gay film.
