American philosopher at the University of Chicago. Author of The Fragility of Goodness, Sex and Social Justice, Hiding from Humanity, and Cultivating Humanity. Co-developed the capability approach with Amartya Sen. Known for work on virtue ethics, emotions, disgust in law, objectification, and the ethical significance of literature.
The Fragility of Goodness

Nussbaum's first and most famous book, examining ancient Greek ethics and tragedy. The work argues that Plato's early dialogues break from earlier Greek tragedy by presenting life as free from non-rational factors and fragility, while the book itself argues for the primacy of contingency and luck in ethics. A key chapter, 'The Speech of Alcibiades,' argues that Alcibiades's reappearance at the end of the Symposium highlights human vulnerability, and that Plato undermines Socrates's 'ladder' approach to love. The book contrasts Diotima's theories (which entail a loss of beauty) with Alcibiades's theories (which entail a loss of rational planning). It includes an interlude characterizing Plato's literary style as 'anti-tragic theater' and reads Euripides's Hecuba, noting the significance of the idea that 'nothing human is trustworthy.' The work ends by comparing how Hecuba's burial place serves as a guide for sailors to the way that tragedy serves as a guide to ethical deliberation.

The Capability Approach (with Amartya Sen) →

Nussbaum co-developed the capability approach with Amartya Sen, redefining poverty and human welfare in terms of 'capabilities' — the opportunities and things people can do — rather than income or rights. In Frontiers of Justice, she lists ten 'basic capabilities' including life, bodily health, and control over one's environment, arguing that a life is not worthy of human dignity without them. The approach centers on both capabilities and 'functionings' (defined by Sen as 'beings and doings'). Nussbaum also applied the capability approach to animal rights, borrowing from Aristotle's On the Motion of Animals.

Disgust, Shame, and the Law (Hiding from Humanity / From Disgust to Humanity)

Nussbaum wrote extensively about the role that disgust and shame play in lawmaking and social policy. In Hiding from Humanity: Disgust, Shame, and the Law, she argued against the 'magical ideas of contamination' underlying the use of shame or disgust to justify laws. In From Disgust to Humanity, she analyzed the role that disgust plays in anti-queer legislation. She identified a 'politics of disgust' as a major driver of social policy and argued that disgust should be replaced by Mill's harm principle in determining just restrictions of liberty.

Sex and Social Justice / Objectification

Sex and Social Justice collects essays including 'Equity and Mercy,' 'The Feminist Critique of Liberalism,' and an essay on objectification that defines seven criteria for objectification: fungibility, violability, denial of autonomy, instrumentality, and others. Nussbaum gives a non-negative example of instrumentalizing objectification — using a lover's stomach as a pillow. She condemned female genital mutilation in this book. 'The Professor of Parody,' a famous essay, attacked Judith Butler for her 'teasing, exasperating' writing style and for not offering a positive political program.

Literature, Ethics, and Emotions

Nussbaum is deeply invested in the ethical significance of literature. She analyzed Henry James's The Golden Bowl in the essay 'Flawed Crystals,' highlighting Maggie Verver's realization of humanity's imperfections and debating Richard Posner about the legitimacy of ethical criticism. In 'Perceptive Equilibrium,' she developed ethical theory from James's novels. She compared a sophisticated virtuous agent to a talented jazz improvisationist and defended virtue ethics against charges of relativism. In Upheavals of Thought, she argued for emotions as a form of judgments. In Cultivating Humanity, she defended multiculturalism and liberal arts education by drawing on Diogenes's desire to become a citizen of the world and comparing the efforts of Rabindranath Tagore and John Dewey in combating rote-memorization.

The Therapy of Desire

Nussbaum's book imagines a fictional career for a female student of Epicurus named Nikidion as a way of comparing the therapeutic arguments of different Hellenistic schools of philosophy. This reflects her broader interest in ancient philosophy's practical, therapeutic dimension.

Summary of Facts

Martha Nussbaum is an American philosopher and law professor at the University of Chicago, consistently identified as a feminist philosopher. Her work spans ancient Greek ethics, political philosophy, emotions, law, and the ethical significance of literature.

Her most famous book, The Fragility of Goodness, examines virtue ethics and tragedy in ancient Greece. The work argues for the primacy of contingency and luck in ethics, contending that Plato's early dialogues broke from earlier Greek tragedy by presenting life as free from non-rational factors and fragility. A key chapter, 'The Speech of Alcibiades,' argues that Alcibiades's reappearance at the end of Plato's Symposium highlights human vulnerability, and that Plato undermines Socrates's 'ladder' approach to love. Nussbaum contrasts Diotima's theories (entailing a loss of beauty) with Alcibiades's theories (entailing a loss of rational planning). The book reads Euripides's Hecuba, noting the significance of the idea that 'nothing human is trustworthy,' and ends by comparing Hecuba's burial place as a guide for sailors to the way tragedy guides ethical deliberation.

Nussbaum co-developed the capability approach with Amartya Sen, redefining poverty and human welfare in terms of capabilities rather than income or rights. In Frontiers of Justice, she lists ten 'basic capabilities' including life, bodily health, and control over one's environment. The approach also addresses the disabled and animal rights, the latter borrowing from Aristotle's On the Motion of Animals. 'Functionings,' defined by Sen as 'beings and doings,' complement capabilities in the framework.

Nussbaum wrote extensively about the role of disgust and shame in law. Hiding from Humanity argues against the 'magical ideas of contamination' underlying the use of shame or disgust to justify laws. From Disgust to Humanity analyzes disgust's role in anti-queer legislation. She identified a 'politics of disgust' as a driver of social policy and argued it should be replaced by Mill's harm principle.

In Sex and Social Justice, Nussbaum defined seven criteria for objectification including fungibility, violability, and denial of autonomy, with a non-negative example of instrumentality being using a lover's stomach as a pillow. She condemned female genital mutilation. Her essay 'The Professor of Parody' famously attacked Judith Butler for her 'teasing, exasperating' style and lack of a positive political program.

Nussbaum is deeply engaged with literature and ethics. She analyzed Henry James's The Golden Bowl in 'Flawed Crystals' and 'Perceptive Equilibrium,' debating Richard Posner about ethical criticism. She compared a virtuous agent to a jazz improvisationist and defended virtue ethics against relativism. In Upheavals of Thought, she argued for emotions as a form of judgments. In Cultivating Humanity, she defended liberal arts education by invoking Diogenes's cosmopolitanism and comparing Tagore and Dewey's approaches to rote-memorization. Another book imagines a fictional career for Nikidion, a female student of Epicurus, to compare therapeutic arguments of different Hellenistic schools.
