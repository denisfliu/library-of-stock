19th-century American Romantic/Fireside poet and longtime editor of the New York Evening Post. Best known for "Thanatopsis" (meditation on death; "innumerable caravan"; "go not like the quarry-slave at night") and "To a Waterfowl" ("dost thou pursue / Thy solitary way?"). Called a "smooth, silent iceberg" in Lowell's A Fable for Critics.
Thanatopsis

Bryant's most famous poem, a meditation on death, mostly written when he was 17. Opens by addressing anyone "who in the love of Nature holds / Communion" with her visible forms. Describes "hills / Rock-ribbed and ancient as the sun." Lists "patriarchs of the infant world," "kings," and "the powerful of the earth" as part of an "innumerable caravan" approaching the grave. Advises the reader to "go not like the quarry-slave at night" but with "pleasant dreams." Richard Henry Dana initially thought it was a hoax, exclaiming "That was never written on this side of the water!" States "The golden sun, / The planets, all the infinite host of heaven, / Are shining."

To a Waterfowl

Poem addressing a bird pursuing its "solitary way" through the sky. Asks "whither, midst falling dew [...] dost thou pursue thy solitary way?" Describes how "the abyss of heaven / Hath swallowed up" the bird's form. Meditates on a "Power whose care / teaches thy way along that pathless coast" and the "long way that I must tread alone." The bird seeks a "summer home" after wandering through "illimitable air."

General / Biographical

Longtime editor of the New York Evening Post (succeeding William Coleman in 1829). Introduced Abraham Lincoln at the Cooper Union speech. Called a "smooth, silent iceberg" in "supreme ice-olation" in Lowell's A Fable for Critics. Also wrote "The Battle-Field" ("Truth, crushed to earth, shall rise again" — quoted by MLK in "Give Us the Ballot"), "June" (praised by Poe in "The Poetic Principle"), "To the Fringed Gentian" (addressed to a "blossom bright with autumn dew" — "hope, blossoming within my heart, may look to heaven as I depart"), "The Prairies" ("gardens of the desert" — imagines mound builders destroyed by "warlike" Native Americans), "The Embargo" (early anti-Jeffersonian political satire), "The Ages" (Phi Beta Kappa address in Spenserian stanzas), "The Death of Lincoln" ("perished in the cause of Right"), "Mutation" ("pain dies as quickly" as "short-lived pleasure"), and "The Strange Lady" (youth Albert meets figure who shoots arrow into his hand). Translated Homer late in life. Friend of Thomas Cole; depicted together in Asher Durand's Kindred Spirits. Friend of Hiram Powers. Attacked rival editor William Stone with a whip in 1831.

Summary of Facts

William Cullen Bryant was a leading 19th-century American Romantic poet and one of the Fireside Poets. His most famous poem, "Thanatopsis" (meaning "meditation on death"), was mostly written when he was 17 and describes the "innumerable caravan" of humanity approaching the grave, advising the reader to "go not like the quarry-slave at night." Richard Henry Dana initially thought it was a hoax, exclaiming "That was never written on this side of the water!"

"To a Waterfowl" addresses a bird pursuing its "solitary way" through the sky, whose form is "swallowed up" by "the abyss of heaven." The speaker meditates on a "Power whose care / teaches thy way along that pathless coast" and draws lessons for "the long way that I must tread alone."

Bryant served as longtime editor of the New York Evening Post and introduced Abraham Lincoln at his Cooper Union speech. James Russell Lowell called him a "smooth, silent iceberg" in "supreme ice-olation" in A Fable for Critics. His poem "The Battle-Field" contains the line "Truth, crushed to earth, shall rise again," which MLK quoted in "Give Us the Ballot." Other poems include "The Prairies" ("gardens of the desert"), "To the Fringed Gentian," and "June" (praised by Poe). He translated Homer late in life and was depicted with Thomas Cole in Asher Durand's Kindred Spirits.
