Giotto

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Proto-Renaissance / early Renaissance Italian painter, student of Cimabue. Best known for his fresco cycles at the Arena (Scrovegni) Chapel in Padua, commissioned by the usurer Enrico Scrovegni. Also painted the Ognissanti Madonna, the Stefaneschi Triptych, frescoes at Assisi, and designed the campanile for Florence Cathedral. Famous for drawing a perfect circle freehand for the Pope and painting a fly so realistic Cimabue tried to brush it off.

            Summary of Facts

            Giotto di Bondone (c. 1267-1337) was an Italian proto-Renaissance painter, generally considered the first artist of the Renaissance. He was a student of Cimabue and broke from the Byzantine style (maniera greca) to introduce naturalism, expressiveness, and three-dimensionality into painting. Dante, in the Purgatorio, noted that Giotto had overshadowed his teacher Cimabue. Vasari called him the ugliest man in Florence, and when Dante teased him about his ugly children, Giotto replied that he 'makes his pictures by day, and his babies by night.'

            Giotto's most famous work is his fresco cycle at the Arena (Scrovegni) Chapel in Padua, a series of 37 frescoes commissioned by the penitent usurer Enrico Scrovegni to atone for his family's sins. The chapel has a rich cobalt/lapis lazuli blue ceiling decorated with stars and circular busts of Jesus and the Four Apostles. The frescoes depict the lives of Christ, the Virgin Mary, and Joachim, including the Lamentation (set on a barren hill with a bare tree, with ten small anguished angels darting above), the Kiss of Judas (Judas in golden robes kissing Jesus in profile, with Peter cutting off a man's ear), the Last Judgment (Christ surrounded by a rainbow/gold ring, with a blue devil, and donor Scrovegni holding a model of the chapel), and the Adoration of the Magi (where the Star of Bethlehem is depicted as Halley's Comet). The lower register features grisaille panels of seven virtues and seven opposing vices, including Despair (a demon flies toward a hanged woman). The vices may omit avarice because of the patron's reputation for usury.

            Other major works include the Ognissanti Madonna, displayed in the Uffizi alongside Cimabue's Santa Trinita Maesta and Duccio's Rucellai Madonna (attributed through Lorenzo Ghiberti's autobiography); the Stefaneschi Triptych (donor offering the altarpiece itself to St. Peter, an early Droste effect); frescoes at the Upper Church of the Basilica of St. Francis at Assisi (28 scenes including the first known stigmatization, trial by fire before a Sultan, and Sermon to the Birds — attribution debated per Bruno Zanardi); and frescoes in the Peruzzi and Bardi Chapels at Santa Croce in Florence (St. John the Evangelist raising Drusiana, scenes of John the Baptist).

            Giotto designed the campanile (bell tower) for Florence Cathedral, decorated with polychrome marble, though only the lower level with hexagonal sculptural panels was completed during his lifetime. He is allegedly buried beside it. He also created the Navicella, a mosaic of Christ walking on water now in fragmentary form at St. Peter's Basilica, and a painted crucifix for Santa Maria Novella praised by Petrarch.

            Famous anecdotes from Vasari include Giotto drawing a perfect circle freehand for Pope Benedict XI without a compass ('Giotto's O'), and painting a fly so lifelike onto one of Cimabue's paintings that Cimabue tried to brush it off.
