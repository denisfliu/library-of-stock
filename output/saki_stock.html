General / Biographical

Pen name comes from the Rubaiyat's word for 'cupbearer.' Real name Hector Hugh Munro. Edwardian British author. Early career included reworking Alice in Wonderland into political satires for the Westminster Gazette (with Francis Carruthers Gould). Parodied Shaw's Man and Superman with his collection Beasts and Super-Beasts. Recurring characters include Clovis Sangrail (from The Chronicles of Clovis) and Reginald. Sandie Byrne coined the 'feral ephebe' trope for his early protagonists. Other stories include 'Esme' (a hyena eats a child), 'Laura' (reincarnation as an otter), 'The Storyteller' (bachelor tells children about a 'horribly good' girl eaten by a wolf), 'Gabriel-Ernest' (werewolf), 'The Wolves of Cernogratz,' 'The East Wing,' 'The Toys of Peace,' 'The She-Wolf,' and 'The Occasional Garden.'

Summary of Facts

Saki (1870-1916), the pen name of Hector Hugh Munro, was an Edwardian British short story writer renowned for dark humor, sardonic wit, and twist endings. His pen name derives from the Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam's word for 'cupbearer.'

'The Open Window' is his most frequently clued story. In it, the young Vera lies to the nervous visitor Framton Nuttel about the deaths of three hunters and a dog who supposedly disappeared into a bog. When they return through the open window, Nuttel flees in terror, and the story concludes with the iconic line 'Romance at short notice was her specialty.' Vera then smoothly explains Nuttel's departure as due to a 'horror of dogs.'

'Sredni Vashtar' features the boy Conradin who forms a cult around a polecat-ferret he names Sredni Vashtar. He steals powdered nutmeg as a reward for causing his guardian's toothache. The ferret kills his cruel guardian Mrs. De Ropp, and the story ends with Conradin calmly making toast while hearing the maid's screams.

'The Interlopers' depicts the feud between Georg Znaeym and Ulrich von Gradwitz, who get trapped under a fallen tree in the Carpathians. They reconcile and share a wine flask, only for the story to end with the devastating one-word sentence 'Wolves.' 'Tobermory' features Cornelius Appin teaching a cat to speak, who then reveals everyone's secrets at Mrs. Blemley's party.

'The Lumber Room' follows Nicholas, punished for putting a frog in his breakfast, who discovers a tapestry depicting a stag hunt and refuses to rescue his aunt from a rainwater tank, pretending she is the Devil. 'The Schartz-Metterklume Method' features a woman pretending to be a governess who makes children reenact the Rape of the Sabine Women. 'The Unrest-Cure' sees the recurring character Clovis Sangrail pretend to plot a massacre to cure J. P. Huddle's complacency.

Saki's recurring characters include the witty Clovis Sangrail (from The Chronicles of Clovis) and the earlier Reginald. His collection Beasts and Super-Beasts parodied Shaw's Man and Superman. His early career included reworking Alice in Wonderland into political satires for the Westminster Gazette. Other notable stories include 'Esme' (a hyena eats a child), 'Laura' (reincarnation as an otter), 'The Storyteller' (a bachelor tells children about a 'horribly good' girl eaten by a wolf), 'Gabriel-Ernest' (a werewolf story), 'The Wolves of Cernogratz,' 'The East Wing,' 'The Toys of Peace,' and 'The Occasional Garden.'
