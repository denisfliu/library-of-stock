General / Biographical

Maria Callas (born Maria Anna Cecilia Sophia Kalogeropoulos; also accepted as Sophie Cecilia Kalos) was a Greek-American soprano nicknamed 'La Divina.' She was known for bel canto roles, a wide vocal range, and dramatic intensity. Despite having a voice criticized by some as 'great and ugly,' her technical skill prompted a famous 'debate' comparing her to the classically aesthetic voice of rival Renata Tebaldi. She lost nearly eighty pounds in the early 1950s. She had a well-publicized affair with shipping magnate Aristotle Onassis. Her mentor and 'discoverer' was conductor Tullio Serafin, whom she impressed by sight-reading Act II of Tristan und Isolde. She was the first to sing Elvira in I puritani just days after singing Brünnhilde in Die Walküre (1949), marking her turn towards bel canto roles. She was fired by Met General Manager Rudolf Bing via telegram. Her only film role was as Medea in Pier Paolo Pasolini's 1969 film. She is the subject of Terrence McNally's play Master Class (in which two sopranos, Sophie and Sharon, are coached by Callas).

Norma was Callas's signature role, performed 89 times in concert during the postwar bel canto revival. The title Druid priestess sings the aria 'Casta Diva' to the moon, originally subtitled 'The Infanticide' — the opera shows Norma conflicted over her love for the Roman proconsul Pollione. Norma ultimately throws herself into a burning temple with Pollione. Roles and Repertoire

Beyond Norma, Callas was celebrated in a range of bel canto and verismo roles. She revived Cherubini's Medea at La Scala (1953) and sang the role in Italian for the 1958 Dallas Opera. She played Violetta in La Traviata (arguing that Act 2, Scene 1 should end after 'Amami, Alfredo'); Tosca (where her wig caught fire, and she apocryphally spent an hour perfecting one line); Amina in La sonnambula (1955 La Scala production conducted by Bernstein); and Imogene in Bellini's Il pirata. She interpolated a high E-flat in Aida's Act II finale in Mexico City. She also performed in Nabucco (the notoriously difficult role of Abigaille), and sang as Fiorilla in Rossini's Il Turco in Italia.

Summary of Facts

Maria Callas (born Maria Anna Cecilia Sophia Kalogeropoulos; also accepted as Sophie Cecilia Kalos) was a Greek-American soprano nicknamed 'La Divina' who transformed mid-20th-century opera through her bel canto revival, wide vocal range, and theatrical intensity. Although her voice was criticized by some as 'great and ugly,' her technical mastery prompted a famous critical 'debate' contrasting her with the more aesthetically pleasing but less dramatically powerful voice of her chief rival Renata Tebaldi. Callas lost nearly eighty pounds in the early 1950s and had a well-publicized affair with shipping magnate Aristotle Onassis.

Callas's most significant role was Norma in Bellini's opera of the same name, which she performed 89 times in concert during the postwar bel canto revival. Her recording of the role under conductor Tullio Serafin is considered definitive. Callas made her American debut singing Norma at the 1954 opening of the Lyric Opera of Chicago. In 1958 she infamously cancelled the Rome Opera's season-opening Norma after only the first act, claiming illness — with the President of Italy in attendance. When singing 'Casta Diva,' she often employed the bel canto technique of messa di voce: sustaining a single pitch while crescendoing then decrescendoing.

Tullio Serafin was Callas's mentor and 'discoverer,' whom she impressed by sight-reading Act II of Tristan und Isolde. Her pivot to bel canto roles began dramatically in 1949 when she shocked the opera world by singing Elvira in I puritani just days after singing Brünnhilde in Die Walküre, replacing an ill Margherita Carosio on short notice. She opened her first recital by singing Isolde's Liebestod in Italian.

Beyond Norma, Callas revived Cherubini's Medea at La Scala's opening week in 1953 and sang the role in Italian for the 1958 Dallas Opera (after which Met General Manager Rudolf Bing fired her via telegram). In La Traviata she argued that Act 2, Scene 1 should end directly after Violetta's 'Amami, Alfredo.' In Tosca, her wig caught fire during a performance and co-star Tito Gobbi leaped on her to extinguish it; apocryphally she spent an hour perfecting the line 'E avanti a lui tremava tutta Roma.' A 1955 La Scala production of La sonnambula conducted by Leonard Bernstein and directed by Luchino Visconti — with Callas in the lead — was credited with eliminating excessively flashy soprano antics then common in that opera. Her signature Bellini roles also included Amina (La sonnambula) and Imogene (Il pirata).

Callas's only film role was as Medea in Pier Paolo Pasolini's 1969 film. Her last public performance was in Sapporo (Hokkaido, Japan) in 1974. She is the subject of Terrence McNally's play Master Class, in which two sopranos (Sophie and Sharon) are coached by Callas near the end of her life. Callas led the mid-20th-century bel canto revival alongside Joan Sutherland and Beverly Sills.
