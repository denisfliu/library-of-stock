1980 horror film directed by Stanley Kubrick, adapted from Stephen King's novel. Jack Torrance (Jack Nicholson) serves as winter caretaker of the isolated Overlook Hotel in Colorado, where he goes mad and pursues his family — wife Wendy (Shelley Duvall) and son Danny — with an axe. Famous for 'Here's Johnny!', the tricycle, and the twins scene. Score assembled from Penderecki, Wendy Carlos/Rachel Elkind, Bartok, and Ligeti.
Score and Music

The Shining's score is a collage of pre-existing classical works. The main theme was created by Wendy Carlos and Rachel Elkind, adapting the 'Dream of a Witches' Sabbath' from Berlioz's Symphonie Fantastique. Penderecki's tone cluster-heavy pieces are used extensively: Utrejna, Polymorphia, The Awakening of Jacob, and two pieces titled De Natura Sonoris. Bartok's 'night music' excerpts play during a crane shot. Ligeti's Lontano was used in the film. A Penderecki piece plays when Danny turns the corner on his tricycle and sees the identical twins.

Key scenes: the tricycle sequence (floor-level tracking shots follow Danny on a blue-seated tricycle through the hotel; he sees a torrent of blood from an elevator and the identical twins — inspired by a Diane Arbus photograph). The Gold Room scene ('Midnight, the Stars and You' plays as Jack anachronistically appears in a 1921 party photo). The bathroom scene (red walls, white ceiling/floor; a man wipes Jack's jacket and mentions 'correcting' his daughters). The axe scene (Jack breaks through a door; 'Here's Johnny!'). The axe scene was influenced by Victor Sjostrom's Swedish silent film The Phantom Carriage (1921).

Production / Novel / Legacy

Stanley Kubrick film (1980), adapted from Stephen King's novel. The Stanley Hotel in Colorado inspired the novel. In the novel, Dick Halloran gets a distress call and rushes to Colorado but is attacked by topiaries (in the film he is killed by Jack). Shelley Duvall played Wendy; she denied being professionally abused by Kubrick. The film is set at the Overlook Hotel. Kubrick is credited with having influenced subsequent horror film music through this film. The film is analyzed in Mark Fisher's book The Weird and the Eerie.

Summary of Facts

The Shining (1980) is a horror film directed by Stanley Kubrick, adapted from Stephen King's novel (which was inspired by the Stanley Hotel in Colorado). Jack Torrance (Jack Nicholson) becomes the winter caretaker of the Overlook Hotel, where he goes mad. His wife Wendy (Shelley Duvall) and son Danny (who rides a blue-seated tricycle through the hotel in floor-level tracking shots) face his increasing violence. Danny sees a torrent of blood gushing from an elevator and the famous identical ghost twins — the twins scene was inspired by a Diane Arbus photograph of two girls in matching outfits. Jack pursues his family with an axe, breaking through a door with 'Here's Johnny!'; he ends up frozen to death in a hedge maze. The axe scene drew on Victor Sjostrom's 1921 Swedish silent film The Phantom Carriage. The Gold Room scene features 'Midnight, the Stars and You' playing as the camera slowly zooms to reveal Jack anachronistically in a 1921 party photograph. A bathroom scene features red walls with white ceiling and floor, where a man wipes Jack's jacket while mentioning how he 'corrected' his daughters. In the novel, chef Dick Halloran receives a distress call and rushes to Colorado where he is attacked by topiaries (in the film he is simply killed). Shelley Duvall denied being professionally abused by Kubrick during filming.

The film's score is a collage of classical works. Wendy Carlos and Rachel Elkind created the main theme adapting the 'Dream of a Witches' Sabbath' from Berlioz's Symphonie Fantastique. Penderecki's tone cluster-heavy works are used extensively: Utrejna, Polymorphia, The Awakening of Jacob, and two pieces titled De Natura Sonoris. A Penderecki piece plays when Danny turns the corner on his tricycle to see the twins. Bartok's 'night music' plays during a crane shot transitioning between a model and what it represents. Ligeti's Lontano also appears. Wendy Carlos also scored A Clockwork Orange for Kubrick.
