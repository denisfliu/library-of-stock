Early Netherlandish painter known for fantastical, nightmarish imagery in religious triptychs. Best known for The Garden of Earthly Delights, The Haywain, and The Temptation of St. Anthony. Works are housed primarily in the Prado. Often identified as a proto-Surrealist.
The Garden of Earthly Delights

Bosch's most famous work, a large triptych housed in the Prado. The left panel depicts Eden with God presenting Eve to Adam, featuring a pink fountain and exotic animals including an elephant and giraffe. The central panel shows masses of nude figures frolicking in the title setting, sharing oversized strawberries and floating in bubbles. The right panel depicts Hell, featuring the iconic "Tree-Man" (a pale figure with a cracked eggshell torso impaled by a tree, often considered a self-portrait), a knife between two giant ears, musical instruments used as torture devices (people strung on harp strings, oversized bagpipes and lutes), a birdlike creature defecating souls, and a pig in a nun's habit. When closed, the exterior shows a grisaille globe depicting the Earth on the third day of creation, with the inscription "For he spoke, and it came to be." The work inspired Joan Miró's The Tilled Field, Czesław Miłosz's ekphrastic poetry, and Carla Gannis's emoji-headed animation. Wikipedia

The Haywain Triptych

Triptych whose central panel depicts Christ in the clouds looking down on a large hay wagon being pulled by demons toward Hell, while a pope and king ride behind it and crowds scramble to grab handfuls of hay. The left panel shows the Fall of the Rebel Angels (angels transforming into insects) and the expulsion of Adam and Eve. The right panel depicts a burning Hell. When closed, the exterior shows The Wayfarer — a man in gray carrying a basket and wooden spoon, warding off a dog with a walking stick. The wagon is pulled by an anthropomorphic fish demon. Also known as The Hay Wain. Wikipedia

The Temptation of St. Anthony

Triptych depicting the temptation of St. Anthony in the desert, housed in Lisbon's Museu Nacional de Arte Antiga. The central panel shows Anthony kneeling amid ruins with a crucifix, surrounded by demons. The left panel depicts Anthony being whisked into the sky above sick people crossing a bridge. Fish imagery appears throughout — a fish wears armor and a sheathed sword, and a woman rides on a fish. The painting is connected to ergotism: a skating figure carries a cartouche with the word 'Fat,' a burning village appears in the background, and the subject matter references Anthony's role as protector against ergotism (Saint Anthony's fire). Bosch may have depicted himself as an insect-winged demon. Wikipedia

The Ship of Fools

Painting that was once part of a larger triptych (with Allegory of Gluttony). Depicts a boat with a tree serving as its mast, containing a nun and a monk playing a lute among other revelers. Illustrates an allegory by Sebastian Brant. A man attempts to cut down a chicken/roast hanging from the tree. Was part of the Wayfarer triptych before being separated. Stefan Jonsson connected it to James Ensor's Christ's Entry into Brussels, arguing they share a 'red thread of madness.' Wikipedia

The Last Judgment

Bosch painted multiple versions of The Last Judgment as triptychs. A pink-heavy version in Bruges features pink-clad Christ and is more hellish than versions in Vienna and Munich. The Vienna version shows scenes of torture including a glutton forced to drink from an enormous cauldron. All versions place Jesus at the top of the central panel, surrounded by a heavenly blue sphere with trumpeting angels. The fall of the rebel angels appears at the left. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Bosch is identified in clues as an early Dutch / Early Netherlandish painter. His hometown burning may have inspired the blackened buildings in his paintings. He is frequently cited as a proto-Surrealist alongside Arcimboldo — André Breton called Arcimboldo 'the grandfather of Surrealism' while Bosch is similarly cited. Hieronymus Cock initially credited Bruegel's Big Fish Eat Little Fish drawing to Bosch to popularize the print. His works are primarily housed in the Prado. Czesław Miłosz wrote an ekphrastic poem about the Garden, and Henry Miller titled a memoir Big Sur and the Oranges of Hieronymus Bosch.

Summary of Facts

Hieronymus Bosch (also known as Jheronimus van Aken) was an Early Netherlandish/Dutch painter whose fantastical, nightmarish imagery has made him one of the most clued visual artists in quizbowl. His works are primarily housed in the Prado in Madrid.

Bosch's most famous work, The Garden of Earthly Delights, is an enormous triptych that dominates his clue profile. Its left panel depicts the Garden of Eden with God presenting Eve to Adam, a pink fountain with an owl, and exotic animals including an elephant and giraffe. The center panel shows masses of nude figures frolicking among oversized strawberries, floating in bubbles, and engaging in sinful activity. The right panel, depicting Hell, contains some of quizbowl's most recognizable visual clues: the "Tree-Man" with a cracked eggshell torso (widely considered a self-portrait), a giant knife between two oversized ears, musical instruments used as torture devices (people strung on harp strings, a large bagpipe balanced on the Tree-Man's head), a birdlike creature defecating souls, and a pig dressed in a nun's habit. When closed, the exterior shows a grisaille globe depicting Earth on the third day of creation. The painting inspired Joan Miró's The Tilled Field, an ekphrastic poem by Czesław Miłosz, Carla Gannis's emoji-headed animation, and was referenced in M.C. Escher's Belvedere.

The Haywain (or Hay Wain) Triptych is Bosch's second most frequently clued work. Its central panel shows a massive hay wagon being pulled by demons toward Hell while a pope (possibly Alexander VI) and a king ride behind it, and crowds scramble to grab handfuls. The left panel depicts the Fall of the Rebel Angels — angels transforming into insects — and the expulsion of Adam and Eve. An anthropomorphic fish demon pulls the wagon. When closed, the exterior panels display The Wayfarer, showing a man in gray with a basket and wooden spoon warding off a dog with his walking stick. The Wayfarer triptych was later separated into individual panels including The Ship of Fools.

The Temptation of St. Anthony is a triptych in Lisbon depicting demons tormenting the desert saint. The left panel shows Anthony whisked into the sky above sick people crossing a bridge. Ergotism (Saint Anthony's fire) is a major theme: a skating figure carries a cartouche bearing the word "Fat," a burning village appears in the background, and Anthony was traditionally the protector against ergotism. Fish imagery recurs throughout — an armored fish with a sheathed sword, figures riding fish. This work predates famous versions of the same subject by Salvador Dalí and Max Ernst.

The Ship of Fools depicts a boat with a tree for a mast, containing a nun and a monk playing a lute among revelers. It illustrates an allegory by Sebastian Brant and was once part of the larger Wayfarer triptych alongside the Allegory of Gluttony.

Death and the Miser shows a bedridden figure with a skeleton poking through a door holding a silver arrow, while the miser reaches for coins offered by a demon rather than a crucifix visible in the window. It hangs opposite The Ship of Fools in the National Gallery of Art.

The Seven Deadly Sins and the Four Last Things is a circular tabletop painting with the Eye of God at its center bearing the inscription "Cave cave dominus videt" ("Beware, beware, the Lord sees"). Four exterior roundels depict Death, Judgement, Heaven, and Hell.

Extraction of the Stone of Madness (also called Cutting the Stone) depicts a doctor wearing a funnel hat removing a flower (not a stone) from a patient's skull, with a woman balancing a book on her head. The inscription reads "my name is Lubbert Das."

Bosch painted multiple versions of The Last Judgment, with versions in Vienna, Munich, and Bruges. All place Jesus at the top of the central panel within a heavenly blue sphere with trumpeting angels. Other works include The Conjurer (a cup-and-ball trick), Ecce Homo, and St. Christopher Carrying the Christ Child.

Bosch is frequently identified as a proto-Surrealist. The burning of his hometown ('s-Hertogenbosch) may have inspired the blackened buildings recurring in his paintings. Hieronymus Cock, the Antwerp printmaker, once attributed Bruegel's drawing Big Fish Eat Little Fish to Bosch to boost sales. Henry Miller's memoir Big Sur and the Oranges of Hieronymus Bosch references the artist in its title.
