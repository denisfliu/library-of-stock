Australian Nobel laureate novelist (1973 Nobel Prize in Literature). Known for novels set in the Australian outback and suburbs, often featuring artists, eccentrics, and explorers. Major works include Voss (based on explorer Ludwig Leichhardt), Riders in the Chariot (four visionaries in the suburb of Sarsaparilla), The Vivisector (painter Hurtle Duffield), The Tree of Man, and The Twyborn Affair. Won the Nobel Prize in 1973 and subsequently set up a namesake literary award in Australia.
Voss

Summary of Facts

Patrick White (1912-1990) was an Australian novelist who won the 1973 Nobel Prize in Literature, the only Australian to have done so. He subsequently set up a namesake literary award in Australia, the first recipient of which was Christina Stead.

His best-known work is Voss, based on the real naturalist and explorer Ludwig Leichhardt. The novel follows the German explorer Voss on an expedition across Australia, backed by Edmund Bonner, with companions including Frank Le Mesurier and Palfreyman. Voss has a deep spiritual and romantic connection with Laura Trevelyan, an orphan from New South Wales who arrives and meets Voss at her uncle's home. Laura later adopts an orphan named Mercy. Voss ultimately dies in the Outback. At the end, Laura's cousin Belle Radclyffe hosts a garden party for the unveiling of a statue of Voss, attended by Mr. Judd. The novel also describes the flora of the outback.

Riders in the Chariot is set in the fictional suburb of Sarsaparilla and concerns four people who share a vision from the Book of Ezekiel. The four visionaries are: Mary Hare (an eccentric spinster heiress living on the decaying estate of Xanadu), Mordecai (a Holocaust survivor who is subjected to a mock crucifixion outside a bicycle lamp factory and dies), Alf Dubbo (an Aboriginal artist), and Ruth Godbold (a devout mother). The title was partially inspired by an Odilon Redon painting that White saw as a child. Wine merchant Norbert poses a question to his young daughter that references the title.

The Vivisector follows the painter Hurtle Duffield, who grew up poor in Sydney. He smears excrement onto a painting showing the eye of God, an image that has haunted him since childhood. He is inspired by characters including Kathy Volkov, Hero Pavloussi, and the hunchbacked Rhoda Courtney. He seeks to complete his magnum opus after suffering a stroke, and the novel closes with him suffering another stroke while writing the word 'indigo,' an anagram for 'god in I.' The protagonist is theorized by some critics to be based on the Australian painter Sidney Nolan.

The Tree of Man chronicles the Parker family adapting their traditional way of life to the frontier. Stan Parker marries Amy, whom he meets at a dance. A kitchen maid touches a gum tree for the first time during a bushfire.

The Twyborn Affair was White's last published novel. It features a non-gender conforming protagonist who inhabits three different identities (two female, one male) using the names Eudoxia, Eddie, and Eadith. Reminiscent of Virginia Woolf's Orlando, the novel was purported to be edited from the papers of Alex Xenophon Demirjian Gray. An unfinished novel by White was published in 2012.

Other biographical details: White praised Janet Frame's autobiography as 'amongst the wonders of the world.' One of his novels features shipping merchant Angelos leaving a Paris hotel after Joan Golson meets his mistress.
