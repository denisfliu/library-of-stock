Summary of Facts

Joan Tower (b. 1938) is a contemporary American composer best known for her Fanfares for the Uncommon Woman, a series of six brass-and-percussion fanfares written between 1986 and 2016. The series was a direct response to Aaron Copland's Fanfare for the Common Man — which opens with timpani, bass drum, and tam-tam before trumpets play the ascending F, B-flat, F figure — using the same orchestration. The series was dedicated to conductor Marin Alsop of the Baltimore Symphony Orchestra, who became the first woman to direct the Last Night of the Proms in 2013 and who created the Taki Concordia (later Taki Alsop) Fellowship to increase mentorship for female conductors. The fanfares honor 'risk-takers and adventurers,' and Tower's 1991 Concerto for Orchestra was written between their installments. Tower co-founded the Da Capo Chamber Players, an ensemble that uses Pierrot instrumentation (flute, clarinet, violin, cello, and piano), and several of her works were written for or commissioned by the group. Among these is Petroushskates, a chamber piece inspired by figure skating that pays homage to Stravinsky's Petrushka via repeating sixteenth notes derived from the 'Shrovetide Fair.' Her first orchestral work, Sequoia (1981), described as Beethoven-inspired, opens with rapid percussion supporting a pedal point G that slowly 'branches out,' inspired by the sequoia tree. Tower also wrote a group of so-called 'mineral works,' including Black Topaz (for piano and six instruments, featuring two percussionists each playing vibraphone, tom-toms, marimba, and temple block — inspired by the artist's own drawing of colored rays emanating from a piano) and Silver Ladders. Her string quartet In Memory was dedicated to victims of 9/11, though originally intended as a memorial for her friend Margaret Shafer. Her only piano quintet is titled Dumbarton Oaks and opens with the sostenuto notes D, E, F, then E-flat, F-sharp, E-natural, F; it was recorded alongside her third through fifth string quartets by Blair McMillen and the Daedalus Quartet. A one-movement flute concerto was dedicated to Juilliard professor Carol Wincenc. Tower won three Grammy Awards for Made in America (2008).
