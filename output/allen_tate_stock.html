Summary of Facts

Allen Tate is an American Southern Agrarian poet and critic, best known for "Ode to the Confederate Dead" (1928), which opens "Row after row with strict impunity / The headstones yield their names to the element" and features a recurring image of flying and swirling leaves in a Confederate cemetery. The poem commands "Leave now / The shut gate and the decomposing wall" and describes "inexhaustible bodies that are not / Dead, but feed the grass," which Tate explained in his critical essay Narcissus as Narcissus. The poem's title was later echoed by Robert Lowell's "For the Union Dead," and Natasha Trethewey wrote "Elegy for the Native Guards" as a response to it.

Tate was a Southern Agrarian who co-authored the manifesto I'll Take My Stand. He wrote biographies of Stonewall Jackson and Jefferson Davis. He served as mentor to Robert Lowell and defended the role of traditional religion in literature in his essay "The Fallacy of Humanism." He controversially called Melvin Tolson the first black author to assimilate "the language of the Anglo-American tradition" and once canceled a reception for Langston Hughes to avoid socializing with him.
