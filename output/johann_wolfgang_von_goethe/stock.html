Summary of Facts

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe is a German polymath and one of the most prominent figures in quizbowl literature, clued across an enormous range of works spanning poetry, drama, novels, and scientific treatises. His two-part play Faust is his most iconic work: in it, the title scholar makes a bet with Mephistopheles that he will not tell the passing moment 'tarry awhile, thou art so fair.' Faust dances with witches on Walpurgis Night, encounters a homunculus, has a child with Helen of Troy, and the play ends by invoking the 'eternal feminine' that 'draws us on high.' Gretchen, the female lead, sings 'The King in Thule' and laments 'My peace is gone, my heart is sore' at her spinning wheel, the latter inspiring Schubert's famous lied 'Gretchen am Spinnrade.' The Prologue includes scenes set both in a theater and in Heaven. Faust Part II features Simon Magus appearing on paper money. Delacroix created lithographs illustrating Faust in 1828, Harry Clarke made Art Nouveau illustrations in 1925, and Charles Gounod composed an opera based on it.

Goethe's epistolary novel The Sorrows of Young Werther is his other most-clued work and a quintessential Sturm und Drang text. The protagonist Werther falls in love with Charlotte (Lotte) and cries when she says the name 'Klopstock.' After Lotte marries Albert, Werther borrows Albert's pistols and commits suicide; he is buried under a linden tree. A contemporary (Friedrich Nicolai) parodied the novel by adding chicken's blood to the suicide scene. Thomas Mann's novel Lotte in Weimar depicts Goethe's childhood lover Charlotte confronting him for fictionalizing her.

Goethe's ballad 'Der Erlkonig' is one of the most frequently clued individual poems. It opens 'Who rides so late through The Night and wind?' and depicts a father riding on horseback with his son, who is tempted by the Erlking's promises that 'my daughters shall tend thee with sisterly care.' The Father dismisses the apparitions as willows and fog, but the child dies in his father's arms. Schubert's famous setting opens with G octaves in the piano's right hand.

Goethe's Italian sojourn (1786-88) produced multiple works: the Roman Elegies (originally titled Erotica, dedicated to Christiane Vulpius), Venetian Epigrams, and the travelogue Italian Journey with the epigraph 'I, Too in Arcadia.' The first Roman Elegy commands 'Tell me you stones, O speak, you towering palaces!' The fourth Roman Elegy imagines people questioning him about Werther. J.H.W. Tischbein painted Goethe reclining on a rock slab 'in the Roman Campagna' next to a relief of Iphigenia's sacrifice during this trip. Angelica Kauffman also painted a portrait of Goethe in Rome.

The West-Eastern Divan was inspired by Joseph Hammer-Purgstall's translation of Hafez and an affair with Marianne von Willemer. It uses ghazals and features conversations between the lovers Hatem and Suleika. Each of its twelve books has a Persian and German title. The Book of the Singer opens the collection.

Elective Affinities is a novel in which Eduard and his wife Charlotte invite Captain Otto and Ottilie to their Weimar home, and the resulting romantic attractions are explained through the metaphor of chemical reactions (double-displacement). A woman loses her footing while pushing a rowboat into a lake and drops her baby cousin, causing him to drown. Walter Benjamin wrote an essay on it.

The Wilhelm Meister novels are Goethe's major bildungsroman series. In Wilhelm Meister's Apprenticeship, the protagonist joins an acting troupe, plays Hamlet, is rejected by Mariane, and eventually joins the Tower Society. The character Mignon sings 'Do you know the land where the lemon trees bloom?' and 'None but the Lonely Heart.' Goethe's last novel details Wilhelm's 'Journeyman Years.' Friedrich Schlegel adopted the concept of 'Bildung' partly from this work.

Goethe wrote two poems titled 'Wanderer's Nightsong.' One declares 'Over all hilltops is peace' and notes that 'in all the treetops / hearest thou / hardly a breath,' ending with 'soon thou too shalt rest.' He inscribed the lines of the second on a cabin wall. 'The Sorcerer's Apprentice' features a magic broom instructed to fetch pails of water that floods a workshop; Paul Dukas set it to music. Goethe also wrote 'Welcome and Farewell,' 'Blessed Longing' (about a moth consumed by a flame), 'The God and the Bayadere,' 'Anacreon's Grave,' and a poem harshly criticizing Christianity titled 'Prometheus.'

The 'Marienbad Elegy' was written after the 73-year-old Goethe proposed to 17-year-old Ulrike von Levetzow and was rejected. It includes the lines 'They urged me to those lips, with rapture crowned / Deserted me, and hurled me to the ground.' It forms the second part of a 'Trilogy of Passion.'

Goethe's scientific works include Theory of Colors, an anti-Newtonian treatise that influenced a triangle of nine segments with symbolic labels like 'unnecessary.' Turner referenced it in a painting about the morning after the biblical flood. Ludwig Wittgenstein's Remarks on Colour expands on ideas from it. The Metamorphosis of Plants drew on Goethe's theory of a primordial 'Urpflanze' from which all plant life developed.

Biographically, Goethe was a major exponent of the Sturm und Drang movement, was a childhood friend of Friedrich Klinger, worked for the Duke of Saxe-Weimar who ennobled him, was regarded as the second-most famous European behind Napoleon in his lifetime, maintained skepticism against German nationalism, met Napoleon (who called his translation of Voltaire's Mahomet 'an awful play'), called out 'more light! more light!' on his deathbed, and was a pioneer of the Innerlichkeit style. Hugo Wolf wrote 51 art songs based on his texts. Thomas Mann was deeply influenced by Goethe, writing about him in the essay 'Goethe as a Representative of the Bourgeois Age,' the novel Lotte in Weimar, and modeling Doktor Faustus on Faust. Goethe coined the phrase 'the epoch of world literature is at hand' and defined a novella as telling of a 'single remarkable event.' His table talk was recorded by his secretary Eckermann.
