Indian-Canadian Parsi novelist. Best known for A Fine Balance, set during Indira Gandhi's Emergency, about the Parsi seamstress Dina Dalal, untouchable tailors Ishvar and Omprakash, and student boarder Maneck Kohlah. Ishvar loses his legs and Om is forcibly castrated; Maneck later commits suicide. Also wrote Such a Long Journey (Gustad Noble in 1971 Bombay during the Bangladesh war, money laundering with Jimmy Bilimoria) and the story collection Tales from Firozsha Baag (including 'Squatter' about a man unable to use Canadian toilets). His works are set in Mumbai ('the city by the sea') and engage with caste, the Emergency, and the Indian diaspora in Canada.

Summary of Facts

Rohinton Mistry is an Indian-Canadian Parsi novelist, part of the Indian diaspora in Canada alongside Bharati Mukherjee and Michael Ondaatje.

His best-known work is A Fine Balance, set during Indira Gandhi's Emergency in Mumbai (described as 'the city by the sea'). The Parsi seamstress Dina Dalal hires untouchable tailors Ishvar and Omprakash (Om) to sew patterns for Au Revoir Exports and takes on student Maneck Kohlah as a paying boarder. In the novel's climactic scene, Ishvar and Om are taken to a Family Planning Center, where Ishvar loses his legs to gangrene during a procedure ordered by the gangster Thakur, and Om is forcibly castrated after undergoing compulsory vasectomies. A Beggarmaster manages a troupe of people with diverse deformities. A helicopter causes a giant cutout of a leader to topple on civilians, and a billboard for Modern Bread is replaced by the prime minister's picture. In the epilogue, Maneck returns to India, sees the widespread killing of Sikhs after Indira Gandhi's assassination, and commits suicide by letting himself get run over by a train. Another character weeps upon recognizing three girls who hanged themselves because their father could not afford their dowries. The novel chronologically begins at India's Independence.

Such a Long Journey is set in 1971 Bombay during the Bangladesh war. Gustad Noble becomes involved in a money laundering scheme with his friend Jimmy Bilimoria. Gustad is repeatedly told to 'hug the bear,' referring to a dancing bear named Kano that holds a secret message. The novel was withdrawn from the University of Mumbai's English syllabus after the grandson of nationalist politician Bal Thackeray complained, and it is critical of the right-wing Shiv Sena party.

Tales from Firozsha Baag is a short story collection set in a Parsi apartment complex. 'Squatter' features a man who returns home because he cannot use Canadian toilets (Dr. No-Ilaaz cannot help him). 'Auspicious Occasion' involves a Behram Roje celebration where a man's clean outfit is stained by tobacco spit. 'Swimming Lessons' follows a writer who sends his parents letters from Canada, leading his mother to wonder why his stories are not set there.
