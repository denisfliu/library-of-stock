Gwendolyn Brooks

African-American poet from Chicago, the first Black person to win the Pulitzer Prize (for Annie Allen). Known for poems about Black urban life including 'We Real Cool,' 'The Bean Eaters,' 'kitchenette building,' and 'the mother.' Her collections include A Street in Bronzeville and The Bean Eaters.
We Real Cool

Brooks's most famous poem, depicting seven pool players at the Golden Shovel who 'lurk late,' 'strike straight,' 'sing sin,' 'thin gin,' 'Jazz June,' and 'Die soon.' The poem uses single-syllable words and places 'We' at the end of each line, creating hesitations. Terrance Hayes created the 'Golden Shovel' poetic form based on this poem, and Joy Harjo's 'An American Sunrise' references its final line.

The Bean Eaters

the mother

A poem that opens 'Abortions will not let you forget' and describes how 'You remember the children you got that you did not get.' The speaker stole 'your births and your names' from 'damp small pulps' and concludes 'Believe me, I loved you all.' The poem asks 'Why should I whine, / Whine that the crime was other than mine?'

kitchenette building

A poem that opens 'We are things of dry hours and the involuntary plan' and contrasts the word 'dream' (a 'giddy sound') with 'rent,' 'feeding a wife,' 'satisfying a man.' Dreams cannot 'flutter, or sing an aria down these rooms.' The reverie is interrupted because 'Number Five is out of the bathroom now.' Richard Wright said only someone who lived in a kitchenette could render its 'lonely frustration.'

Annie Allen

The collection that won Brooks the Pulitzer Prize, the first won by a Black writer. It details a Black girl's coming of age, divided into 'Notes from the Childhood and Girlhood,' 'The Anniad' (a 43-stanza mock-epic in rhyme royal adapting Chaucer), and 'The Womanhood.' Annie's parents are Maxie and Andrew. The poem describes 'sweet and chocolate' and an imagined lover who leaves and returns.

A Street in Bronzeville

Brooks's 1945 debut collection set in the Bronzeville neighborhood of Chicago's South Side. It includes 'kitchenette building,' 'the mother,' the sonnet sequence 'Gay Chaps at the Bar,' and poems about soldiers and urban Black life. Brooks also wrote 'A Bronzeville Mother Loiters in Mississippi' about Emmett Till.

General / Biographical

Brooks was from Chicago and set most of her poetry there. She served as U.S. Poet Laureate (1985-1986). She wrote one novel, Maud Martha, about a Bronzeville native. She wrote 'Primer for Blacks' and 'Boy Breaking Glass.' Sonia Sanchez wrote a poem for her calling her a 'sister called life.' She was involved with the Black Arts Movement. She wrote about Emmett Till in 'A Bronzeville Mother Loiters in Mississippi' and 'The Last Quatrain of the Ballad of Emmett Till.'

Summary of Facts

Gwendolyn Brooks is the most frequently clued African-American female poet in quizbowl, known for her poems about Black urban life in Chicago. Her most famous poem, 'We Real Cool,' depicts seven pool players at the Golden Shovel who 'lurk late,' 'strike straight,' 'Jazz June,' and 'Die soon,' with the word 'We' placed at the end of each line.

'The Bean Eaters' describes an 'old yellow pair' who are 'Mostly Good' eating dinner as 'a casual affair' in a 'rented back room.' 'the mother' opens 'Abortions will not let you forget' and ends 'Believe me, I loved you all.' 'kitchenette building' contrasts 'dream' (a 'giddy sound') with 'rent, feeding a wife, satisfying a man,' interrupted by 'Number Five is out of the bathroom now.'

Annie Allen won the Pulitzer Prize, making Brooks the first Black writer to win it. The collection includes 'The Anniad,' a 43-stanza mock-epic in Chaucerian rhyme royal. A Street in Bronzeville (1945) is her debut collection set in Chicago's South Side.

Brooks served as U.S. Poet Laureate (1985-1986) and wrote about Emmett Till in 'A Bronzeville Mother Loiters in Mississippi' and 'The Last Quatrain of the Ballad of Emmett Till.' Her only novel is Maud Martha. Terrance Hayes created the Golden Shovel poetic form inspired by 'We Real Cool.'
