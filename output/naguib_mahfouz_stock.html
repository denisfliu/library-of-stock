Egyptian novelist (1911-2006), winner of the 1988 Nobel Prize in Literature -- the only Egyptian and only Arabic-language author to win the Nobel. Best known for the Cairo Trilogy (Palace Walk, Palace of Desire, Sugar Street) about the al-Jawad family. Also wrote Children of Gebelawi (allegorizing the Abrahamic religions, for which he was stabbed in 1994), The Thief and the Dogs, Midaq Alley, Miramar, and Adrift on the Nile. Supported Salman Rushdie after the fatwa. Wrote the sequel Arabian Nights and Days.
Cairo Trilogy (Palace Walk, Palace of Desire, Sugar Street)

Mahfouz's most famous work, three novels about several generations of the al-Jawad (also written al-Gawad) family in Cairo. The patriarch al-Sayyid Ahmad Abd al-Jawad is a philanderer who cheats on his conservative wife Amina with women like the wedding singer Jalila. Amina disobeys her husband by leaving the house and is hit by a car, fracturing her collarbone. Their son Fahmy is an activist who agitates against British occupation and is shot dead by British soldiers at a protest. Kamal (a possibly autobiographical character for Mahfouz) is told by his father to 'show him a single statue of a teacher,' but goes to Teachers College anyway; he pines after Aida, gets a degree in philosophy, becomes a teacher, and carries his dying father home from an air raid shelter. The eldest son Yasin discovers he shares his father's debauched habits. The 1001 Nights inspires Kamal. Jackie Kennedy Onassis oversaw the first English translation at Doubleday by William M. Hutchins. Kadijah is al-Sayyid Ahmad's daughter whose residence is Sugar Street. The Nile serves as a backdrop. Wikipedia

Children of Gebelawi

A controversial allegorical novel in which the patriarch Gebelawi's descendants live in an alley near his mansion. Five characters represent Adam (Adham), Moses (Gabal), Jesus (Rifa'a), Muhammad (Qasim), and modern science (Arafa the magician). Adham is exiled for trying to see his father's forbidden book/will; Idris tempts Adham. Idris gets his brother drunk and convinces him to steal the father's will. Arafa attempts to overthrow the Chief Strongman. The scientist Arafa causes the death of the title character. Mahfouz was stabbed in the neck in 1994 at age 82 by Islamic extremists for this novel, partly due to his defense of Salman Rushdie's Satanic Verses. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Naguib Mahfouz (1911-2006) was an Egyptian novelist who won the 1988 Nobel Prize in Literature, the only Egyptian and only Arabic-language author to have done so. He co-sponsors the Caine Prize for African Writing alongside fellow Nobel laureates Wole Soyinka, Nadine Gordimer, and J. M. Coetzee.

His most famous work is the Cairo Trilogy, consisting of Palace Walk, Palace of Desire, and Sugar Street, each named after streets in Cairo. The trilogy follows several generations of the al-Jawad family. The patriarch al-Sayyid Ahmad Abd al-Jawad is a philanderer who cheats on his conservative wife Amina with women like the wedding singer Jalila. In the most iconic scene, Amina disobeys her husband by leaving the house and is hit by a car, fracturing her collarbone. Their son Fahmy is a law student and activist who agitates against British occupation; he is shot dead by British soldiers at a protest. Kamal, a possibly autobiographical character for Mahfouz, wants to attend Teachers College despite his father telling him to 'show him a single statue of a teacher.' Kamal pines after Aida, gets a philosophy degree, and becomes a schoolteacher. He writes about Darwin, outraging his father. Kamal carries his dying father home from an air raid shelter. The eldest son Yasin discovers he shares his father's debauched habits. Fahmy sees his crush Maryam waving at a British soldier named Julian. The kneading of bread serves as the alarm clock for the family. Amina washes her husband every night for 25 years. Sugar Street is titled after the residence of Kadijah, Ahmad's daughter. Aisha's wedding is a central event. A preacher 'believes in two things: God in heaven and adolescent boys on earth.' The maid Umm Tanafi outlives the patriarch. Jackie Kennedy Onassis oversaw the first English translation at Doubleday by William M. Hutchins. The 1001 Nights inspires Kamal to study at Teachers College.

Children of Gebelawi (also Children of the Alley) is a controversial allegorical novel. The patriarch Gebelawi's descendants live in an alley near his mansion. Five characters represent Adam (Adham), Moses (Gabal), Jesus (Rifa'a), Muhammad (Qasim), and modern science (the magician Arafa). Adham is exiled for trying to see his father's forbidden book. Idris tempts Adham and gets his brother drunk to steal the will. Arafa attempts to overthrow the Chief Strongman and causes the death of Gebelawi. One character imagines turning into a goat or grain of sand after killing his brother. Hearing his grandfather's voice in the desert inspires a man to save souls before he is murdered. Mahfouz was stabbed twice in the neck in 1994 at age 82 by Islamic extremists for this novel, partly due to his defense of Salman Rushdie's Satanic Verses.

Midaq Alley features residents of the titular Cairo street, including the young woman Hamida who is prostituted by a pimp and takes the nickname Titi to please English punters. She refuses to marry the barber Abbas. When Abbas discovers her with British troops and throws a beer glass at her face, he is beaten to death by soldiers. A cafe run by Mr. Kirsha replaces its poet with a radio.

The Thief and the Dogs follows Said Mahran, recently released from prison, seeking revenge on his ex-wife and two former friends including Rauf Ilwan, a former revolutionary who abandoned his principles. He mistakenly kills a doorman and a lodger. He is chased and shot by police in a cemetery while thinking of his daughter Sana. A cafe owner named Tarzan appears.

Miramar is set at the title boarding house in Alexandria with multiple narrators representing different Egyptian political philosophies. Various characters are entranced by the beautiful peasant/servant girl Zohra.

Other works include: Adrift on the Nile (1966; drug addict Anis Zani and friends on a houseboat; journalist Samara investigates); Arabian Nights and Days (1979 sequel to 1001 Nights where Scheherazade distrusts Shahryar); The Day the Leader Was Killed (Sadat's assassination; Randa and Elwan; 'abyss of the infitah'); The Harafish (character meets wife as donkey-boy, becomes chief); Respected Sir (bureaucrat: 'the State is an exhalation of the spirit of God'); The Beggar (Omar's existential crisis); Mirrors (autobiographical stories with Seif Waly portraits); short stories including 'Half a Day' (boy emerges from school old), 'Zaabalawi' (man seeks healer), 'At the Bus Stop' (commuters think they're filmed), 'Under the Shelter,' conjoined twins 'My Fate' and 'My Destiny,' and Voices from the Other World (adapts Sinuhe). His plays include Project for Discussion and Death and Resurrection. He worked at the Ministry of Religious Endowments and was a youthful member of the Wafd Party.
