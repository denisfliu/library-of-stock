Japanese ukiyo-e artist, best known for the woodblock print series Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji, which includes The Great Wave off Kanagawa and Fine Wind, Clear Morning. Also known for the Hokusai Manga (15-volume sketch collection), One Hundred Ghost Stories, Dream of the Fisherman's Wife (erotic shunga), Oceans of Wisdom series, and late-life paintings including Tiger in the Snow. Called himself 'old man mad about painting.'
Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji (General)

Series of woodblock prints depicting Mount Fuji from different locations and conditions. Made using the 'brocade' technique allowing many distinct colors. Incorporated newly imported Prussian blue pigment. Over 100 depictions of Fuji in an associated series of books. Ten additional paintings later added depicting a timberyard and pleasure district.

Summary of Facts

Katsushika Hokusai (1760-1849) was a Japanese ukiyo-e artist (woodblock printmaker specializing in 'pictures of the floating world'), one of the most influential artists in Japanese history. His father was a mirror-maker, and Hokusai spent his adolescence polishing mirrors before beginning his artistic career, changing his name 30 times over his life. He supposedly lived in over 100 houses since he never cleaned them. His daughter Oi was also a painter.

Hokusai's most famous work is the series Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji, which extensively uses the newly imported pigment Prussian blue and was made using the 'brocade' (nishiki-e) technique allowing many distinct colors. The series includes The Great Wave off Kanagawa (the first print: a giant wave with claw-like foam threatening three fishing boats, with tiny Mount Fuji in the background; it inspired Roy Lichtenstein's Drowning Girl), Fine Wind, Clear Morning (also called 'Red Fuji,' showing Mount Fuji in bright red/orange from sunrise against a Prussian blue sky with rippling clouds), a view of Fuji through an unfinished barrel being assembled by a cooper, A Sudden Gust of Wind at Ejiri (travelers clutching hats and papers; inspired Jeff Wall's photograph), and Thunderstorm Beneath the Summit (five yellow lines depicting lightning). Other prints in the series show a boy leading a turtle on a leash near a waterwheel, and the Mannen Bridge.

Hokusai legendarily dipped a chicken's feet in red paint and chased it across canvas to create a painting of maple leaves in a river. He called himself 'old man mad about painting' and predicted his art would achieve a divine state at age 110. In late life, he painted a tiger (or lion) every day as 'daily exorcisms,' including the hanging scroll Tiger in the Snow.

Other major works include the Hokusai Manga (15 volumes of sketches begun during a stay in Nagoya, containing images of everything he saw), Quick Lessons in Simplified Drawing (a three-part technique manual on breaking figures into elementary shapes), One Hundred Ghost Stories (including The Plate Mansion with a spirit whose neck is made of stacked blue plates, The Ghost of Oiwa as a paper lantern with a gaping mouth, and Memorial Anniversary contrasting a peaceful leaf with an obsessive snake), the Oceans of Wisdom series (fishing by torchlight, whaling), Dream of the Fisherman's Wife (notorious erotic shunga print of a woman with two octopi from the Young Pines series), the Great Picture Book of Everything (103 drawings rediscovered in 2019), and various bridge and waterfall prints.

Hokusai influenced the Japonisme movement and the Impressionists. Van Gogh included Hokusai's subjects in the background of his Portrait of Pere Tanguy. The bokashi technique (applying two colors of ink to one block) was one of his printing methods.
