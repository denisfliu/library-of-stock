Fanfare for the Common Man

A brief fanfare for brass and percussion composed in 1942. The piece is scored exclusively for brass (four French horns, three trumpets, three trombones) and percussion. It was commissioned by conductor Eugene Goossens for the Cincinnati Symphony Orchestra as one of 18 wartime fanfares, and premiered on Tax Day 1943. Copland drew inspiration from Vice President Henry Wallace's speech 'The Price of Free World Victory,' which proclaimed the 'Century of the Common Man.' The culminating chord is in D major. Copland later reused its melody in the finale of his Third Symphony (1946). Emerson, Lake and Palmer covered the piece; Copland preferred his own version.

Aaron Copland – General / Biographical

American modernist composer (1900–1990), known for his 'Americana' style blending folk and classical elements. Studied with Nadia Boulanger in Fontainebleau. Major works include the ballets Appalachian Spring (uses Shaker hymn 'Simple Gifts,' choreographed by Martha Graham), Rodeo (includes 'Hoe-Down'), and Billy the Kid; A Lincoln Portrait (narrated, quotes 'Camptown Races' and Gettysburg Address, commissioned by Andre Kostelanetz); El Salon Mexico; Clarinet Concerto (commissioned by Benny Goodman); and the 'Short' Symphony No. 2 (dedicated to Carlos Chavez). Also wrote Quiet City (for trumpet, English horn, and strings) and Piano Fantasy. Late in his career embraced twelve-tone composition in Connotations and Piano Fantasy. Studied with Rubin Goldmark early on and composed The Cat and the Mouse as an early piano piece. Described three 'planes' of listening in his book What to Listen for in Music.

Joan Tower – Fanfare for the Uncommon Woman

A series of six fanfares (composed over 30 years) by American composer Joan Tower, written in direct response to Copland's piece and using the same instrumentation. The set is dedicated to conductor Marin Alsop (music director of the Baltimore Symphony Orchestra, first woman to direct the Last Night of the Proms in 2013). Tower co-founded the Da Capo Chamber Players and won three Grammys for her 2008 piece Made in America.

Summary of Facts

Fanfare for the Common Man is a short work for brass and percussion composed in 1942 by Aaron Copland. It was one of 18 fanfares commissioned by conductor Eugene Goossens for the Cincinnati Symphony Orchestra during World War II, and it premiered on Tax Day, April 12, 1943. The piece was inspired by Vice President Henry Wallace's famous wartime speech 'The Price of Free World Victory,' which proclaimed the dawning of the 'Century of the Common Man.' Copland's title directly echoes Wallace's rhetoric.

The fanfare opens with a solemn percussion introduction — gong (tam-tam), bass drum, and timpani — lasting approximately five bars, with silences between the strikes. The brass then enter with the heroic main theme: three trumpets playing in unison the ascending notes F, B-flat, F. The full scoring calls for four French horns, three trumpets, three trombones (plus tuba), and percussion only; no strings or woodwinds are present. The piece culminates in a D major chord. Copland later incorporated the fanfare's melody into the finale of his Third Symphony (1946), his last symphony, which was published in 1946. That symphony also draws attention for Leonard Bernstein cutting 12 bars from its fourth movement. Emerson, Lake and Palmer recorded a famous rock arrangement of the fanfare; Copland reportedly preferred his own version.

Joan Tower composed a series of fanfares titled Fanfare for the Uncommon Woman as a direct response to Copland's piece, using the same brass-and-percussion instrumentation. The series, comprising six pieces composed over roughly 30 years, is dedicated to conductor Marin Alsop — music director of the Baltimore Symphony Orchestra and the first woman to direct the Last Night of the Proms (2013). Tower also co-founded the Da Capo Chamber Players and won three Grammy Awards for her 2008 piece Made in America.

Aaron Copland (1900–1990), the composer, is closely associated with an 'Americana' style. He studied with Nadia Boulanger at Fontainebleau. His major works include three ballets — Appalachian Spring (incorporating the Shaker hymn 'Simple Gifts,' choreographed by Martha Graham), Rodeo (featuring 'Hoe-Down' and 'Saturday Night Waltz'), and Billy the Kid — as well as A Lincoln Portrait (a narrated work quoting 'Camptown Races' and the Gettysburg Address, commissioned by Andre Kostelanetz), El Salon Mexico (inspired by a Depression-era trip to Mexico), and a Clarinet Concerto commissioned by Benny Goodman (two movements linked by a clarinet cadenza). Late in his career, Copland turned to twelve-tone composition in works like Piano Fantasy and Connotations. He described three 'planes' of musical listening — sensuous, expressive, and sheerly musical — in his book What to Listen for in Music. He also wrote Quiet City for trumpet, English horn, and strings, based on incidental music for an Irwin Shaw play.
