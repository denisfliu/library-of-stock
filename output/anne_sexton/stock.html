American Confessional poet who committed suicide by carbon monoxide poisoning in 1974. Known for the collections To Bedlam and Part Way Back, Live or Die, and The Awful Rowing Toward God. Close friend of Sylvia Plath; mentored by W.D. Snodgrass and Robert Lowell.
Her Kind

One of Sexton's most recognizable poems, from the collection To Bedlam and Part Way Back. The poem opens 'I have gone out, a possessed witch, haunting the black air.' It is divided into three stanzas comparing the speaker to a witch, a crazy woman in a cave, and a woman persecuted for religion. Each stanza ends with the refrain 'I have been her kind.' The poem notes 'a woman like that is not a woman, quite.' Sexton began writing poetry on the advice of her therapist.

All My Pretty Ones

An elegy addressed to Sexton's deceased father. The poem catalogs possessions left behind, including 'boxes of pictures of people I do not know' and 'a five-year diary that my mother kept for three years.' The speaker mentions 'an English Ford, the love and legal verbiage of another will' and says 'each Christmas Day with your blood, will I drink down your glass of wine?' The title comes from Macduff's reaction in Macbeth to losing his 'chickens.' The poem concludes 'Whether you are pretty or not, I outlive you, / bend down my strange face to yours and forgive you.' Sexton tells of forgiving her alcoholic father after discovering her mother's diary.

Wanting to Die

A poem that declares 'suicides have a special language. Like carpenters they want to know which tools. They never ask why build.' The poem describes death leaving 'the page of the book carelessly open,' 'the phone off the hook,' and 'the love, whatever it was, an infection.' The last stanza mentions 'something unsaid, a phone off the hook.'

The Awful Rowing Toward God

A posthumously published collection that serves as a spiritual journey toward God. The first poem observes 'God was there like an island' and repeats 'I grew, I grew' and 'I am rowing, I am rowing.' The speaker describes being 'stamped out like a Plymouth fender into this world' and the story ending 'with me still rowing.' The final poem, 'The Rowing Endeth,' describes the speaker arriving at 'the island called God' and playing poker, getting a royal straight flush but surprisingly losing to God who holds five aces. The collection describes 'mooring my rowboat at the dock of the island.' Kumin helped Sexton revise the manuscript on the day of Sexton's suicide. Adrienne Rich called Sexton's poetry 'a guide to the ruins, from which we learn what women have lived.'

Live or Die

A Pulitzer Prize-winning poetry collection that includes poems such as 'Flee On Your Donkey' and 'Sylvia's Death.' The collection was titled after a quote from Saul Bellow's Herzog about doing the title actions 'but don't poison everything.' It includes a poem about menstruating at the age of forty. 'Sylvia's Death' is an elegy for Sylvia Plath that asks 'Sylvia, Sylvia, where did you go after you wrote me from Devonshire about raising potatoes and keeping bees?' and ends calling Plath 'O tiny mother, you too! O funny duchess! O blonde thing!'

Transformations

A collection of 17 retellings of Grimms' fairy tales. The poem 'Rapunzel' opens 'a woman / who loves a woman / is forever young' and tells the story of Mother Gothel with the refrain 'hold me, my young dear, hold me.' The world 'is made up of couples' and 'a rose must have a stem.' The collection came two years before The Book of Folly.

Other Works and General

Sexton was a Confessional poet who committed suicide by carbon monoxide poisoning in 1974 at age 46. She began writing on the advice of her therapist. She was mentored by W.D. Snodgrass and studied with Robert Lowell. With Sylvia Plath and George Starbuck, she drank at the Ritz-Carlton after Lowell's writing seminars. Her poem 'My Friend, My Friend' partly inspired Plath's 'Daddy.' The memoir Searching for Mercy Street by another person included accounts of abuse by Sexton. Her poem 'Consorting with Angels' features a speaker who dreams of 'a city made of chains' and declares 'I'm no more a woman than Christ was a man.' 'The Truth the Dead Know' opens 'the wind falls in like stones / from the whitehearted water.' 'To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Triumph' is about Icarus, ending as 'his sensible daddy goes straight to town.' 'O Ye Tongues' is a sequence based on Christopher Smart's 'Jubilate Agno' in which 'birth was a disease and Christopher and I invented the cure.' 'Little Girl, My String Bean, My Lovely Woman' asks her daughter 'how do you grow?' 'Rats Live On No Evil Star' was inspired by a palindrome on an Irish barn. A poem ends 'the lowest of us all / deserve to smile in eternity / like a watermelon.' Erica Jong recalled Sexton writing 'Yes! Yes! We'll live!' Maxine Kumin was deeply shaken by Sexton's death. Adrienne Rich eulogized Sexton's poetry as 'a guide to the ruins.'

Summary of Facts

Anne Sexton is an American Confessional poet who lived from 1928 to 1974, when she committed suicide by carbon monoxide poisoning at age 46. She is consistently identified in clues through her collections To Bedlam and Part Way Back, Live or Die, and The Awful Rowing Toward God, as well as her friendship with Sylvia Plath and her status as a Confessional poet.

'Her Kind,' from To Bedlam and Part Way Back, is Sexton's most frequently clued individual poem. It opens with the iconic line 'I have gone out, a possessed witch, haunting the black air' and features three stanzas each ending with the refrain 'I have been her kind.' The poem compares the speaker to a witch, a crazy woman, and a persecuted woman.

'All My Pretty Ones' is an elegy for Sexton's father, cataloging his possessions -- boxes of pictures, her mother's diary, an English Ford. The title comes from Macduff's reaction in Macbeth. The poem concludes with the powerful lines 'Whether you are pretty or not, I outlive you, / bend down my strange face to yours and forgive you.' Sexton forgives her alcoholic father after discovering her mother's diary.

'Wanting to Die' is a poem about suicide that declares 'suicides have a special language. Like carpenters they want to know which tools. They never ask why build.' Death is described as leaving 'the page of the book carelessly open' and 'the phone off the hook.'

The Awful Rowing Toward God is a posthumously published collection representing a spiritual journey. The first poem observes 'God was there like an island' and repeats 'I am rowing, I am rowing.' In the final poem 'The Rowing Endeth,' the speaker arrives at 'the island called God' and plays poker with a royal flush, but loses to God's five aces. Maxine Kumin helped Sexton revise the manuscript on the very day of her suicide.

Live or Die won the Pulitzer Prize and includes the elegy 'Sylvia's Death,' which asks 'Sylvia, Sylvia, where did you go after you wrote me from Devonshire about raising potatoes and keeping bees?' The collection was titled from a quote in Saul Bellow's Herzog.

Transformations is a collection of 17 retellings of Grimms' fairy tales, including 'Rapunzel' which opens 'a woman who loves a woman is forever young.'

Sexton began writing on the advice of her therapist. She was mentored by W.D. Snodgrass and studied with Robert Lowell. She, Sylvia Plath, and George Starbuck would drink at the Ritz-Carlton after Lowell's seminars. Her poem 'My Friend, My Friend' partly inspired Plath's 'Daddy.' Other notable poems include 'Consorting with Angels' (the speaker dreams of 'a city made of chains' and declares 'I'm no more a woman than Christ was a man'), 'The Truth the Dead Know,' 'To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Triumph' (about Icarus), and 'O Ye Tongues' (based on Christopher Smart's 'Jubilate Agno'). Adrienne Rich eulogized Sexton's poetry as 'a guide to the ruins, from which we learn what women have lived.'
