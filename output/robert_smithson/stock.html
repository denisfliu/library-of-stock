American pioneer of land art / earthworks. Best known for Spiral Jetty (1970), a 1500-foot-long coiled basalt formation in Utah's Great Salt Lake. Died in a plane crash in Texas while surveying sites for Amarillo Ramp. Also created Partially Buried Woodshed at Kent State. Associated with entropy and natural disintegration. Married to artist Nancy Holt.

Summary of Facts

Robert Smithson (1938-1973) was an American artist who pioneered the land art / earthworks movement. His most iconic work is Spiral Jetty (1970), a massive 1500-foot-long coil of black basalt rocks extending into Utah's Great Salt Lake at Rozel Point. This work, now managed by the Dia Foundation, has been submerged and re-emerged multiple times as lake levels fluctuate, and the originally black basalt has turned pink from bacterial and algal growth. Smithson documented the construction in a 30-minute film with his wife Nancy Holt, and famously quipped 'et in Utah ego' upon viewing the completed piece. Questions frequently reference the work's location, its spiral shape, and its connection to entropy.

Smithson's second most-asked-about work is Partially Buried Woodshed (1970) at Kent State University, where he had a backhoe dump earth onto a shed until a beam cracked, illustrating his ideas about entropy. The work was destroyed by arsonists in 1975, and only a plaque remains. His third major work, Amarillo Ramp, was never completed by Smithson himself — he died in a plane crash in Texas while surveying sites for it, and Nancy Holt finished it after his death.

Other works that appear in questions include Floating Island to Travel Around Manhattan Island (a tree-covered barge concept), the Mirror Displacement series, Hypothetical Continents, Broken Circle (at Emmen, Holland), A Nonsite: Franklin, New Jersey, and St. John in the Desert. Smithson is also referenced in questions about the broader land art movement alongside artists like Walter De Maria, Nancy Holt, James Turrell, Michael Heizer, Ana Mendieta, and Andy Goldsworthy. His theories of entropy influenced Gordon Matta-Clark's concept of 'anarchitecture,' and he associated with Minimalist artists like Donald Judd and Robert Morris.
