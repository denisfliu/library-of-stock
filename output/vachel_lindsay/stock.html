The Congo

Lindsay's most famous poem, subtitled "A Study of the Negro Race." Contains the refrain "boomlay, boomlay, boomlay, BOOM" and the warning "Mumbo-Jumbo will hoo-doo you." Describes a river "cutting through the jungle with a golden track" and includes sections like "Their Irrepressible High Spirits" and "Their Basic Savagery." Contains performance directions like "with a philosophic pause," "slow philosophic calm," and "imitation of camp-meeting racket." Declares "Death is an Elephant, / Torch-eyed and horrible." Describes "cake-walk royalty" and "a minstrel river / Where dreams come true." Features Leopold II's ghost and the word "BLOOD" screamed by warriors. Attacks Leopold II.

Abraham Lincoln Walks at Midnight

A poem in which a "bronzed, lank man" in a "suit of ancient black" paces near the old court-house in Springfield, Illinois, unable to sleep. Lincoln "cannot sleep upon his hillside now" because "It breaks his heart that kings must murder still" and "too many peasants fight." He "sees the dreadnaughts scouring every main" and "lingers where his children used to play." Ends asking "who will bring white peace / That he may sleep upon his hill again?"

General / Biographical

Lindsay was known as the "Prairie Troubadour" and pioneer of "singing poetry" / "Higher Vaudeville." He wandered across America reciting poems and encouraged audiences to recite along. He mentored Langston Hughes. He had a relationship with Sara Teasdale, attempting to court her in 1914, but she married Ernst Filsinger instead. Other poems include "The Eagle That Is Forgotten" (about John P. Altgeld: "Sleep on, O brave-hearted"), "Bryan, Bryan, Bryan, Bryan" (about William Jennings Bryan as a "gigantic troubadour"), "The Flower-Fed Buffaloes" (buffaloes "gore no more" and are "lying low"), "The Santa Fe Trail" (lists car horns; bird called the Rachel-Jane), "The Chinese Nightingale" ("a king of beauty and tempest and thunder"), and "The Spider and the Ghost of a Fly."

Summary of Facts

Vachel Lindsay is remembered as a pioneer of "singing poetry" or "Higher Vaudeville," earning the nickname "Prairie Troubadour" for his practice of wandering across America performing his poems. His most famous poem, "The Congo," repeats the refrain "boomlay, boomlay, boomlay, BOOM" and warns "Mumbo-Jumbo will hoo-doo you." It includes performance directions like "with a philosophic pause" and "imitation of camp-meeting racket," describes "Death is an Elephant, / Torch-eyed and horrible," and features a river "cutting through the jungle with a golden track."

"Abraham Lincoln Walks at Midnight" depicts a "bronzed, lank man" in a "suit of ancient black" pacing near the court-house in Springfield, unable to sleep because "too many peasants fight." It ends by asking "who will bring white peace / That he may sleep upon his hill again?"

"General William Booth Enters into Heaven" celebrates the Salvation Army's founder and repeats "Are you washed in the blood of the Lamb?" Lindsay also wrote "The Eagle That Is Forgotten" (about John P. Altgeld), "Bryan, Bryan, Bryan, Bryan" (about William Jennings Bryan as a "gigantic troubadour"), "The Flower-Fed Buffaloes," "The Santa Fe Trail," and "The Chinese Nightingale."

Lindsay mentored Langston Hughes and had a romantic relationship with Sara Teasdale, attempting to court her in 1914 before she married Ernst Filsinger.
