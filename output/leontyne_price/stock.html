Summary of Facts

Leontyne Price (born 1927) is an African-American soprano widely considered the first Black prima donna at the Metropolitan Opera, where she made her debut in 1961 as Leonora in Verdi's Il Trovatore. She is particularly known for her Verdi roles, most especially Aida, which she recorded twice — once in 1962 conducted by Georg Solti and once in 1970 conducted by Erich Leinsdorf. In 1960, she was the first African-American lead at La Scala in the role of Aida. She was also the first Black singer to appear in a televised opera, performing the role of Floria Tosca.

In the 1960s, Price made recordings of Tosca, Carmen, and Verdi's Requiem with conductor Herbert von Karajan, who also guided her early appearances in Austria. Her 1955 Carnegie Hall debut (later released as an album called 'Rediscovered') opened with Handel arias and included Brahms's Zigeunerlieder; her longtime collaborator and accompanist was David Garvey. Her opera stage debut was as Madame Lidoine in the US premiere of Poulenc's Dialogues of the Carmelites.

Price was a particularly close collaborator of the American composer Samuel Barber. She premiered his Hermit Songs (a ten-work song cycle for voice and piano setting anonymous poems by Irish monks) at the Library of Congress in 1953, with Barber himself at the piano. Barber also wrote the song cycle Despite and Still for her. She premiered the title female role (Cleopatra) in Barber's opera Antony and Cleopatra at the new Metropolitan Opera House at Lincoln Center in 1966 — a premiere that was considered a disaster. Price also starred in a touring production of Gershwin's Porgy and Bess with her then-husband William Warfield; this production was chronicled by Truman Capote in The Muses Are Heard. In a master class, Price reportedly said that Serena's aria 'My Man's Gone Now' from Porgy and Bess requires a distinctive sighing quality 'like moaning in church.'
