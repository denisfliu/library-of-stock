Scottish novelist and poet (1771–1832), credited with inventing the historical novel and inspiring a wave of historical fiction across Europe. Best known for Ivanhoe, Waverley, Rob Roy, The Heart of Midlothian, The Bride of Lammermoor, and the narrative poems Marmion and The Lady of the Lake. Mark Twain blamed Scott's romanticized chivalry for causing the American Civil War. His home was Abbotsford; his publisher Ballantyne & Co. collapsed, ruining him. Scott organized George IV's 1822 tartan pageant visit to Scotland.
Ivanhoe

Scott's most famous novel, set in 12th-century England. The protagonist disguises himself as 'Desdichado' (the Disinherited Knight, the Spanish word written on his shield) after returning from the Crusades. He defeats the Templar Brian de Bois-Guilbert at a tournament at Ashby-de-la-Zouche and bestows the honor of 'Queen of Love and Beauty' (or 'Queen of the Tournament') upon Lady Rowena. The Templar Lucas de Beaumanoir later forces Bois-Guilbert to fight for the Knights Templar in a trial by combat to defend Rebecca; Bois-Guilbert dies of 'contending passions.' The sage Miriam taught Rebecca a mysterious healing balsam she uses on Ivanhoe. The villainess Ulrica burns down Torquilstone Castle, killing Reginald Front-de-Boeuf. Athelstane apparently rises from the grave. Cedric's name was accidentally invented by switching consonants in a Saxon name. The swineherd Gurth wears a brass collar with a Saxon inscription; Thomas Carlyle praises it in Past and Present. Scott introduced tedious background via the fictional antiquarian 'Reverend Jonas Dryasdust.' The novel inspired Southern chivalric culture; Rena Walden in Chesnutt's The House Behind the Cedars is named for Rowena. Thomas Dixon's The Clansman has a cross-burning scene inspired by Scott. Readers wished Ivanhoe had married Rebecca rather than Rowena. Thackeray wrote a sequel novella Rebecca and Rowena.

Waverley

Scott's first novel (subtitled 'Tis Sixty Years Since), often regarded as the first historical novel. The protagonist Edward Waverley visits the outlaw Donald Bean Lean's hideout (whose daughter Alice is beloved by Edward) and the hillside castle of Flora MacIvor, inspiring him to desert the British army and join the Jacobite Rising of 1745 under 'Bonnie' Prince Charlie (also called the '45 rising'). Highland chieftain Fergus Mac-Ivor sings 'Lillibullero.' Edward sprains his ankle during a stag hunt after Flora performs a war ballad on the harp. Scott's subsequent novels were published 'by the author of Waverley' because he was popular as a poet. A wrecked steamboat in Huckleberry Finn is named after Scott. Castle Rackrent by Maria Edgeworth served as a model. The full Waverley series was published in a 'Magnum Opus' edition by Robert Cadell; Benedetto Croce said the series had 'too many' entries.

Rob Roy

Frank Osbaldistone, whose amateurish poems keep falling out of his books, travels to Scotland to track down his cousin Rashleigh, who has embezzled funds from his father's firm and absconded with assets to ruin the Scottish highlands during the Jacobite Uprising of 1715 (the 'Fifteen'). Frank attends a church service in catacombs and is warned of danger. The comic relief is provided by the magistrate Baillie Nicol Jarvie (Bailie Jarvie) and the superstitious gardener Andrew Fairservice. Frank falls in love with and marries his cousin Diana ('Die') Vernon. The title character is saved by his ruthless wife Helen; Rob Roy kills Rashleigh after altering Sir Hildebrand's will so Frank inherits the estate. Rob Roy's real name is Rob Roy MacGregor; he disguises himself as the cattle dealer Campbell. Set during the Highland Rising of 1715.

The Lady of the Lake

A narrative poem in which Ellen Douglas sings an Ave Maria in a celebrated scene. Roderick Dhu lights a burning cross, which inspired the Ku Klux Klan's practice of doing the same. The poem helped spark the 19th-century Highland Revival. Schubert composed a song cycle based on it; Ellen's third song ('Ellens dritter Gesang') is known as the Schubert Ave Maria. Rossini based his opera La donna del lago on it, in which King James V disguises himself as 'Uberto' to seduce Elena (Elena = Ellen). Also called The Lay of the Last Minstrel is a separate poem.

General / Biographical

Sir Walter Scott (1771–1832) was a Scottish novelist and poet, often credited with inventing the historical novel. He published novels anonymously as 'by the author of Waverley' because his fame as a poet was eclipsed by Lord Byron. His home was Abbotsford. His publisher Ballantyne & Co. collapsed, ruining him financially; his acclaimed posthumously-published journal recounts this. Scott organized George IV's 1822 tartan-heavy visit to Scotland. His characteristic historical notes were attributed to the fictional antiquarian Reverend Jonas Dryasdust. Gyorgy Lukacs gave a Marxist reading of Scott, praising his use of mediocre heroes. E.M. Forster called him a mediocre novelist but good storyteller. Hazlitt praised Rob Roy's ability to transport readers to an age of feuds. Hugh Trevor-Roper accused Scott of fabricating Scottish nationalism. Scott compiled the Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border. He wrote a biography of Napoleon with unique access to archives. James Hogg (his friend and father-in-law's subject) wrote an unauthorized biography after Scott's death. John Gibson Lockhart was Scott's son-in-law. Maria Edgeworth influenced Scott. Frederick Douglass took his surname from a Scott poem character. Scott wrote 'William and Helen,' an early adaptation of Burger's 'Lenore.'

Summary of Facts

Sir Walter Scott (1771–1832) was a Scottish novelist and poet often credited with inventing the historical novel. He published his novels anonymously—as 'by the author of Waverley'—because his popularity as a poet had been eclipsed by Lord Byron. His home was Abbotsford; his publisher Ballantyne & Co. collapsed, ruining him financially, and his posthumously published journal recounts this disaster. He organized George IV's 1822 tartan-heavy visit to Scotland (the first royal visit since Charles II), helping establish the modern Scottish national identity. In Life on the Mississippi, Mark Twain accused Scott of doing 'more real and lasting harm, perhaps, than any other individual that ever wrote,' blaming Scott's romanticized medieval chivalry for causing the American Civil War and naming the South's backwardness the 'Walter Scott disease.'

Ivanhoe is Scott's most famous novel, set in 12th-century England. The Saxon protagonist disguises himself as 'Desdichado' (the Spanish word on his shield for 'disinherited') at the tournament at Ashby-de-la-Zouche, defeats the Templar Brian de Bois-Guilbert, and crowns Lady Rowena 'Queen of Love and Beauty.' The sage Miriam taught Rebecca a mysterious healing balsam she uses on Ivanhoe. Ulrica burns Torquilstone Castle, killing Reginald Front-de-Boeuf. Lucas de Beaumanoir later forces Bois-Guilbert into a trial by combat to defend Rebecca; Bois-Guilbert dies of 'contending passions.' Athelstane 'apparently rises from the grave' before ultimately allowing Ivanhoe to marry Rowena while Rebecca leaves for Granada. The swineherd Gurth's brass collar with a Saxon inscription is praised by Thomas Carlyle in Past and Present. Many readers wished Ivanhoe had married Rebecca; Thackeray wrote a sequel novella, Rebecca and Rowena. The novel inspired Southern chivalric culture: Rena Walden in Charles Chesnutt's The House Behind the Cedars is named for Rowena, and Thomas Dixon's The Clansman (adapted as Birth of a Nation) features a cross-burning scene inspired by Scott. Jem Finch reads Mrs. Dubose passages from Ivanhoe in To Kill a Mockingbird.

Waverley (subtitled 'Tis Sixty Years Since) is often regarded as the first historical novel. The officer Edward Waverley visits the hideout of outlaw Donald Bean Lean and the castle of Flora MacIvor, becoming infatuated with Flora; her brother Chieftain Fergus Mac-Ivor sings 'Lillibullero.' Edward deserts the British army and joins the Jacobite Rising of 1745 under Bonnie Prince Charlie. Maria Edgeworth's Castle Rackrent served as a model. The full Waverley series was published in a 'Magnum Opus' edition by Robert Cadell; Benedetto Croce complained it had 'too many' entries. The Tales of My Landlord sub-series was published under the pseudonym Jedediah Cleishbotham.

Rob Roy is set during the Jacobite Uprising of 1715 (the 'Fifteen'). Frank Osbaldistone, whose amateurish poems keep falling from his books (a running gag), travels to Scotland to track down his cousin Rashleigh, who embezzled the family firm's assets. Frank attends a church service held in catacombs and is warned of danger. Comic relief comes from the magistrate Baillie Nicol Jarvie and the superstitious gardener Andrew Fairservice. Frank falls in love with and marries his cousin Diana ('Die') Vernon. The title outlaw—Rob Roy MacGregor, disguised as cattle dealer Campbell—is saved by his ruthless wife Helen and kills Rashleigh after altering Sir Hildebrand's will.

The Heart of Midlothian opens with the Porteous Riots in Edinburgh. Effie Deans is wrongly imprisoned; her sister Jeanie Deans walks barefoot to London to seek a pardon from Queen Caroline. Effie's illegitimate son 'the Whistler' (sold to Donacha Dhu) later murders her husband.

The Bride of Lammermoor features Edgar of Ravenswood, who saves a woman from a charging bull and falls in love with her. Her brother Enrico engineers her marriage to Arturo; she stabs Arturo on their wedding night and goes insane; Edgar walks into quicksand in despair. This novel inspired Donizetti's opera Lucia di Lammermoor, featuring the sextet 'Chi mi frena in tal momento' and the mad scene aria 'Il dolce suono.'

The Lady of the Lake is a narrative poem in which Ellen Douglas sings an Ave Maria (the basis for Schubert's 'Ellens dritter Gesang,' known as the Schubert Ave Maria). Roderick Dhu lights a burning cross—inspiring the KKK's practice—and the poem sparked the 19th-century Highland Revival. Rossini's opera La donna del lago is based on it.

Marmion is a narrative poem published with copious antiquarian notes, containing the famous couplet 'What a tangled web we weave, when first we practice to deceive!' It ends with Ralph De Wilton marrying Clara de Clare.

Kenilworth features Amy Robsart being tricked into falling to her death by Richard Varney. Additional works include The Antiquary, Guy Mannering (featuring Romani 'Bohemians'), Quentin Durward, Redgauntlet (with the ghost story 'Wandering Willie's Tale'), Castle Dangerous, and Old Mortality. Scott also compiled the Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border, wrote a biography of Napoleon with unique access to St. Helena archives, and introduced the fictional antiquarian 'Reverend Jonas Dryasdust' for historical background. Gyorgy Lukacs's Marxist reading credited Scott's use of mediocre heroes with inspiring Balzac and Tolstoy. John Gibson Lockhart was Scott's son-in-law. Frederick Douglass took his surname from a Scott poem character.
