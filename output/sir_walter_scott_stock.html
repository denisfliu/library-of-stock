Sir Walter Scott

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Scottish novelist and poet, often considered the inventor of the historical novel. Known for Ivanhoe, Waverley, Rob Roy, The Heart of Midlothian, and The Bride of Lammermoor. Mark Twain blamed Scott's influence for causing the American Civil War.
Ivanhoe

Scott's most famous novel, in which the protagonist disguises himself as the knight 'Desdichado' (the Disinherited Knight) after returning from the Crusades. He defeats the Templar Brian de Bois-Guilbert at a joust and bestows 'Queen of the Tournament' honor upon Lady Rowena. The Jewish moneylender Isaac of York is ordered arrested by Bois-Guilbert. Ivanhoe defends the Jewish Rebecca from being burned as a witch; Bois-Guilbert dies in a trial by combat. Ulrica helps save the title character. Ivanhoe is disinherited by his father Cedric 'the Saxon.' The swineherd Gurth wears a brass collar with a Saxon inscription. Thomas Carlyle praises Gurth's collar in Past and Present. The novel inspired Southern chivalric culture, jousting tournaments in Chesnutt's The House Behind the Cedars, and a cross-burning scene in Thomas Dixon's The Clansman.

Waverley

Scott's first novel, often regarded as the first historical novel. The protagonist Edward visits the outlaw Donald Bean Lean's hideout and Flora MacIvor's castle, inspiring him to take up the Jacobite cause. Scott's subsequent novels were published 'by the author of Waverley.' A wrecked steamboat in Huckleberry Finn is named after Scott.

Other Novels

The Heart of Midlothian: Jeanie Deans walks barefoot to London to seek a pardon for her sister Effie from Queen Caroline. Effie's son 'the Whistler' murders her husband. Part of Tales of My Landlord, purportedly edited by Jedediah Cleishbotham. Rob Roy: Frank Osbaldistone tracks down the disinherited Rashleigh in Scotland; the title Highlander disguises himself as the cattle dealer Campbell. The Bride of Lammermoor: Edgar of Ravenswood falls in love with a woman he saves from a bull; she stabs her husband on their wedding night and goes insane. Inspired Donizetti's Lucia di Lammermoor (with sextet 'Chi mi frena in tal momento'). Kenilworth: Amy Robsart is tricked into falling to her death by Richard Varney. Marmion: contains 'What a tangled web we weave, when first we practice to deceive!' The Lady of the Lake features Ellen Douglas singing Ave Maria; Roderick Dhu lights a burning cross, inspiring the KKK. Scott organized George IV's 1822 visit to Scotland. He cited the fictional antiquarian Dryasdust. Gyorgy Lukacs's Marxist reading praised Scott's mediocre heroes. E.M. Forster deemed him a mediocre novelist but good storyteller.

Summary of Facts

Sir Walter Scott was a Scottish novelist and poet often credited with inventing the historical novel. His works profoundly influenced 19th-century culture, particularly in the American South, where Mark Twain blamed Scott's romanticized chivalry for causing the Civil War.

Ivanhoe is his most frequently clued novel. The protagonist disguises himself as Desdichado at a tournament, defeats the Templar Brian de Bois-Guilbert, and wins Lady Rowena. He defends the Jewish Rebecca from being burned as a witch. The novel inspired Southern jousting culture, Charles Chesnutt's The House Behind the Cedars, and Thomas Dixon's cross-burning scenes in The Clansman.

Waverley is his first novel and often regarded as the first historical novel. Edward joins the Jacobite cause after visiting Donald Bean Lean and Flora MacIvor. Scott's later novels were published 'by the author of Waverley.' A steamboat in Huckleberry Finn is named after him.

The Heart of Midlothian follows Jeanie Deans walking barefoot to London to seek a pardon for her sister Effie. Rob Roy features Frank Osbaldistone tracking the disinherited Rashleigh. The Bride of Lammermoor inspired Donizetti's opera Lucia di Lammermoor. Marmion contains the famous line 'What a tangled web we weave.' The Lady of the Lake inspired the KKK's cross-burning through Roderick Dhu's scene. Scott organized George IV's 1822 tartan-heavy visit to Scotland and cited the fictional antiquarian Dryasdust.
