American photographer (born in Luxembourg) and a founding figure of Pictorialist and modernist photography. Co-founded the Photo-Secession movement and Gallery 291 with Alfred Stieglitz. Summary of Facts

Edward Steichen (1879–1973), born Eduard Jean Steichen in Luxembourg, became one of the most influential photographers in American art history. He was a central figure in the Photo-Secession movement, which he co-founded with Alfred Stieglitz, and both men ran the Little Galleries of the Photo-Secession — popularly known as Gallery 291 — which helped introduce avant-garde European art by Matisse, Picasso, and others to American audiences. Steichen was the most frequently featured artist in Stieglitz's quarterly journal Camera Work, and also designed the journal's covers.

As a Pictorialist photographer, Steichen pioneered the use of gum bichromate printing to produce color effects in photographs. His most iconic photograph in this vein, The Pond-Moonlight (c. 1904), was taken at a location in Mamaroneck, New York, and involved manually layering light-sensitive gums over the negative to approximate color. When it sold at auction for $2.9 million in 2006, it briefly held the record as the world's most expensive photograph. Steichen also photographed the Flatiron Building, producing a famous blue-toned image influenced by both Whistler's Nocturnes and an earlier Stieglitz photograph of the same subject. Another body of work includes photographs of his friend Auguste Rodin, most notably Towards the Light, Midnight, which depicts Rodin's monument to Balzac.

Steichen shifted dramatically from fine-art photography to commercial work after World War I. He became chief photographer for Condé Nast publications Vogue and Vanity Fair from the 1920s through the 1930s, pioneering fashion and celebrity photography. His 1911 photographs of Paul Poiret's gowns for the French magazine Art et Decoration are considered the first modern fashion photographs. One of his most famous fashion photographs depicted actress Gloria Swanson with black lace in front of her face, with Steichen likening her presence to a leopardess. This commercial turn was reportedly lamented by Stieglitz.

During World War II, Steichen served as director of the U.S. Navy's photography program. He directed the documentary film The Fighting Lady (1944), which won the Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature, with narrator Robert Taylor soberly informing viewers that all servicemen depicted had since been killed. After the war, Steichen became head of the Department of Photography at the Museum of Modern Art in New York, where he curated the 1955 exhibition The Family of Man — one of the most ambitious photography exhibitions in history, featuring 503 photographs selected from 68 countries. The exhibition included works such as Dorothea Lange's Migrant Mother. Steichen was also an avid horticulturist who took the photograph Heavy Roses and cultivated new breeds of delphinium. Stephen Shore, the noted American photographer, famously cold-called Steichen at age 14 and sold three photographs, launching Shore's career.
