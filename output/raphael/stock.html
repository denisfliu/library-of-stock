Summary of Facts

Raphael (Raffaello Sanzio da Urbino) was a High Renaissance Italian painter, student of Perugino. His most famous work is The School of Athens, a fresco in the Vatican's Stanza della Segnatura showing Plato pointing upward and Aristotle gesturing outward among philosophers, with Bramante modeled as a figure using a compass at the bottom right. The same room holds La Disputa (depicting theologians debating transubstantiation with four putti holding the gospels) and The Parnassus (Apollo playing violin among poets and Muses).

His final painting, The Transfiguration (1520), shows Moses and Elijah appearing above while apostles attempt to cure a possessed boy below. A pope kept it instead of sending it to Narbonne Cathedral. He painted numerous Madonnas: Madonna of the Goldfinch (infants Jesus and John the Baptist play with the bird), Marriage of the Virgin (adapts Perugino's version with Dome of the Rock-inspired building), and Madonna of the Pinks. He designed tapestries depicting the lives of Peter and Paul known as the 'Raphael Cartoons.'

The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood was named in opposition to his influence; Millais and Rossetti believed he 'ruined art.' Joshua Reynolds's praise of Raphael in his Discourses led to the nickname 'Sir Sloshua.' Ingres painted Raphael and his mistress La Fornarina five times. Marcantonio Raimondo's engraving of Raphael's Judgement of Paris inspired Manet's Luncheon on the Grass. He inspired the German Nazarene movement; Benjamin West was called the 'American Raphael.'
