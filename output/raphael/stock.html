High Renaissance Italian painter from Urbino. Most commonly identified by The School of Athens, La Disputa, and The Transfiguration. Student of Perugino. Painted numerous Madonnas (Goldfinch, Pinks, Sistine). The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood was named in opposition to his influence. Ingres painted him with La Fornarina. Died at 37.
The School of Athens (1509–1511)

Fresco in the Stanza della Segnatura, Vatican. Plato (modeled on Leonardo da Vinci) points upward while Aristotle gestures outward. Bramante modeled as Euclid using a compass at bottom right. Self-portrait of Raphael appears at right, depicted as Apelles. A six-pointed star is drawn on a small chalkboard by a figure analyzed by Guerino Mazzola. The tondo above reads 'causarum cognitio.' Anton Raphael Mengs made a copy titled Philosophy for the Duke of Northumberland. Wikipedia

Sistine Madonna (Madonna di San Sisto, c. 1512)

Raphael painting moved from Piacenza to Dresden in 1754 by King Augustus III, who moved his throne to better view it. The Virgin and Child stand on clouds flanked by Pope Sixtus II (modeled on Julius II) and Saint Barbara. Two famous bored putti rest at the bottom parapet. Dozens of cherub faces emerge from the clouds in the background. A tiara with a Della Rovere acorn emblem rests on the parapet. Fresco naming the Stanza dell'Incendio di Borgo (one of the Raphael Rooms). Depicts Leo IV extinguishing a fire from a loggia with his benediction. Madonnas (Goldfinch, Pinks, Marriage of the Virgin)

Raphael painted numerous Madonna scenes. Madonna of the Goldfinch shows infants Jesus and John the Baptist playing with the title bird while Mary holds a book in a pastoral landscape. Marriage of the Virgin adapts Perugino's version with a Dome of the Rock-inspired building. Madonna of the Pinks (on fruitwood) shows a corner window looking onto a ruin. Other versions include Madonna of the Meadow, Cowper Madonnas, Madonna of the Ansidei, and Alba Madonna.

Summary of Facts

Raphael (Raffaello Sanzio da Urbino, 1483–1520) was a High Renaissance Italian painter and architect, born in Urbino and trained under Pietro Perugino. He died at 37, having produced an enormous body of work in a short life.

His most famous work is The School of Athens (1509–1511), a fresco in the Vatican's Stanza della Segnatura. Plato, modeled on Leonardo da Vinci, points upward while Aristotle gestures outward among ancient philosophers. Bramante was modeled as Euclid using a compass in the bottom right. Raphael inserted a self-portrait as the painter Apelles at the right edge (standing beside a man modeled on Protogenes). Guerino Mazzola analyzed a six-pointed star drawn on a small chalkboard within the painting. The tondo above reads 'causarum cognitio.' Anton Raphael Mengs made a copy for the Duke of Northumberland titled Philosophy. The same room (Stanza della Segnatura) holds La Disputa (theologians including Aquinas and Jerome debating transubstantiation, with four putti holding the gospels below Christ's feet) and The Parnassus (Apollo playing a precursor to the violin, surrounded by Muses and poets, which inspired Mengs's own Parnassus fresco).

The Sistine Madonna (c. 1512) was moved from a Benedictine monastery in Piacenza to Dresden in 1754 by King Augustus III, who reportedly moved his throne to better view it. The Virgin and Child stand on clouds, flanked by Pope Sixtus II (modeled on Julius II) and Saint Barbara. Two famous bored putti rest on the parapet at the bottom; dozens of ghostly cherub faces emerge from the clouds. A Della Rovere tiara with acorn emblem lies on the parapet. Dostoyevsky kept a reproduction above his desk and would climb a chair to stare at a version for hours. Hans Belting analyzed this painting in Invisible Masterpiece.

The Fire in the Borgo (c. 1514–1517) is a fresco naming the Stanza dell'Incendio di Borgo; it shows Pope Leo IV giving a benediction from a loggia while crowds flee flames. At the left, a muscular nude dangles from a wall and a man carries an old man on his back in a pose echoing Aeneas bearing Anchises. Sydney Freedberg attributed much of the execution to Giulio Romano.

His final painting, The Transfiguration (1520), shows Moses and Elijah appearing above while apostles attempt to cure a possessed boy below. He painted numerous Madonnas: the Madonna of the Goldfinch (infants Jesus and John the Baptist playing with the bird while Mary holds a book), the Marriage of the Virgin (adapting Perugino's version with a Dome of the Rock-inspired building), and the Madonna of the Pinks (on fruitwood; central figures hold carnations; a corner window looks onto a ruin). The early Mond Crucifixion is Perugino-esque, with the sun and moon flanking I.N.R.I. and angels collecting Christ's blood in chalices. He also designed the Raphael Cartoons — tapestry designs depicting the lives of Peter and Paul.

The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood was named in opposition to his influence; Millais and Rossetti believed he 'ruined art.' Joshua Reynolds's praise of Raphael in his Discourses led to the nickname 'Sir Sloshua.' Ingres painted Raphael and his mistress La Fornarina five times over 35 years, with the third version including The Transfiguration in the background. Marcantonio Raimondi's engraving of Raphael's Judgement of Paris inspired Manet's Luncheon on the Grass (the trio in the bottom right). He inspired the German Nazarene movement; Benjamin West was called the 'American Raphael.' Baldassare Castiglione wrote a sonnet on Raphael's death and was closely associated with him at the court of Urbino. Federico Zuccaro complained in the margins of his copy of Vasari's Lives that Raphael was underpraise compared to Michelangelo.
