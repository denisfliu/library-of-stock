Summary of Facts

Titian (Tiziano Vecellio, c. 1488-1576) was the leading Venetian Renaissance painter. His most commonly clued work is Venus of Urbino (1538), depicting a reclining nude on a bed with a sleeping dog while a maid rummages through a chest, inspired by his teacher Giorgione's Sleeping Venus.

His 'poesie' paintings for King Philip II of Spain include Rape of Europa (a cherub rides a fish as Europa clings to the horn of a white bull), Danaë (a maid watches a gold cloud approach the reclining nude), and Diana and Actaeon. The Equestrian Portrait of Charles V (1548) unrealistically depicts the gouty emperor in full armor on horseback at the Battle of Mühlberg. The Flaying of Marsyas is a late masterpiece featuring a self-portrait as Midas watching Apollo flay the upside-down Marsyas, with a dog lapping blood.

His portrait of a man with a blue quilted sleeve (Man with a Quilted Sleeve) was incorrectly attributed by Vasari as depicting Ariosto and was quoted by Rembrandt. He portrayed Isabella d'Este at age 62 as a baby-faced young woman. His Allegory of Prudence shows heads of a dog, lion, and wolf beneath heads of three men (thought to be Titian, his son Orazio, and cousin Marco). He was a student of Giorgione and collaborated on Pastoral Concert.
