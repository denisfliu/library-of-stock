American photorealist painter known for large-scale portraits. Became quadriplegic after a spinal artery collapse in 1988 (which he called "The Event") and continued painting by strapping a brush to his arm. Summary of Facts

Chuck Close (1940-2021) was an American painter and photographer who became one of the most prominent figures in the photorealism movement. He is best known for his monumental, large-scale portraits that replicate the detail of photographs. His early masterpiece Big Self-Portrait (1967-68) is a monochrome work depicting himself with a cigarette in his mouth, and is housed in the Walker Art Gallery.

In 1988, Close suffered a spinal artery collapse that left him paralyzed from the neck down, an event he referred to simply as "The Event." He continued to paint from a wheelchair by strapping a brush to his wrist, developing his signature late style of dividing portraits into grid patterns filled with concentric circles and abstract shapes that, when viewed from a distance, resolve into recognizable faces. This technique is his most frequently clued characteristic in quiz bowl.

Notable works include his 2006 portrait of Bill Clinton using his grid-based technique, the painting Mark (which hangs in MoMA depicting a bespectacled man in a plaid shirt), and his mosaic portraits at New York's 86th Street subway station featuring Kara Walker, Lou Reed, and Philip Glass. He also collaborated with daguerreotypist Jerry Spagnoli on works like Untitled Torso.

Close became the subject of a Guerrilla Girls protest artwork in 2018, which offered three alternative wall labels for his Clinton portrait, referencing sexual harassment accusations against him. He is frequently associated with fellow photorealists Richard Estes, Audrey Flack, and Ralph Goings, and is sometimes also grouped with the Minimalist movement.
