[10] Name this figure, who “mew’d to every watery God” after she “stretch’d, in vain, to reach the prize” and plunged into a bowl of water.
ANSWER: Horace Walpole’s cat [accept Selima; accept, but do not reveal, Thomas Gray’s favorite cat]
[10] This English poet wrote “Ode on the Death of a Favorite Cat,” as well as “Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard.”
ANSWER: Thomas Gray
[10] Gray wrote how “My lonely anguish melts no heart but mine” in a sonnet on the death of this man, which appears in the preface to another poet’s Lyrical Ballads.
ANSWER: Richard West
B11 2014 NASAT (2014) Diff 8 · Literature
William Wordsworth's preface to Lyrical Ballads criticized a sonnet by this poet for using imagery of the god Phoebus. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this alumnus of Eton College, who reminded readers that "all that glisters [is not] gold" in an ode for a cat that drowned in a tub of goldfish.
ANSWER: Thomas Gray
[10] Gray wrote that "the paths of glory lead but to the grave" in this poem, which opens as "the curfew tolls the knell of parting day" near the title gloomy locale.
ANSWER: "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard"
[10] After noting that "Some mute inglorious Milton here may rest," Gray writes that someone like this leader, "guiltless of his country's blood," might rest in the churchyard.
ANSWER: Oliver Cromwell
B12 2012 Penn Bowl (2012) Diff 8 · Literature
This poet claimed to “weep the more, because I weep in vain” at the end of his “Sonnet on the Death of Mr. Richard West.” For 10 points each:
[10] Name this 18th century British poet, who also mused on whether “some mute inglorious Milton” lay dead in the title location of his “Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard.”
ANSWER: Thomas Gray
[10] In this poem, Gray describes how young men will eventually have to face “Disdainful Anger, pallid Fear, and Shame that skulks behind,” but decides not to tell them, since “ignorance is bliss.”
ANSWER: “Ode on a Distant Prospect of Eton College”
[10] This contemporary of Gray claimed that “God moves in a mysterious way” in one of his Olney Hymns, which he authored with John Newton. He wrote a long epic poem called The Task on the subject of a sofa in his parlor.
ANSWER: William Cowper
B13 2012 Oxford Open (2012) Diff 8 · Literature
For 10 points each, identify the following poets who wrote poems about poets who did not write enough poems before dying.
[10] This poet imagined that somebody in the graveyard Stoke Poges might have been “some mute inglorious Milton” who died before he could write great poems in his “Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard.”
ANSWER: Thomas Gray
[10] This poet wrote of “fears that I may cease to be / before my pen has glean’d my teeming brain” in one poem. He also wrote “To Autumn.”
ANSWER: John Keats
[10] In 1965, this poet eulogized his friend Louis MacNeice by angrily declaring that MacNeice had wasted his talent on boozing and profligate living and charging that, on Judgment Day, God will “recit[e] by heart / the poems you would have written / had your life been good.”
ANSWER: W.H. Auden
B14 2012 ACF Nationals (2012) Diff 9 · Literature
The speaker of this poem calls frolicking children “little victims” surrounded by “the ministers of human fate” and “black Misfortune’s baleful train.” For 10 points each:
[10] Name this poem, which begins by addressing the “distant spires” and “antique towers” of the title location. It proclaims “where ignorance is bliss, ‘tis folly to be wise.”
ANSWER: “Ode on a Distant Prospect of Eton College”
[10] “Ode on a Distant Prospect of Eton College” was written by this Graveyard poet, who imagined a “mute inglorious Milton” in his “Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard.”
ANSWER: Thomas Gray
[10] Gray wrote a mock elegy about this author’s cat Selima. He designed the villa Strawberry Hill and wrote the essay On Modern Gardening, as well as the epigram “The world is a comedy to those who think, a tragedy to those who feel.”
ANSWER: Horace Walpole
