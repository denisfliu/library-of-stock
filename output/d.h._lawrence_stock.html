English novelist, poet, and critic (1885–1930). Best known for Lady Chatterley's Lover (subject of a landmark 1960 obscenity trial), Sons and Lovers, and Women in Love. Also wrote The Rainbow (sequel to Women in Love), following the Brangwen family. Poetry includes 'Snake,' 'Bavarian Gentians,' and 'Piano.' Short stories include 'Odour of Chrysanthemums' and 'The Rocking-Horse Winner.' His critical study Studies in Classic American Literature was praised by Auden. Lived for a period in Taos, New Mexico with patron Mabel Dodge Luhan.

Summary of Facts

D.H. Lawrence (David Herbert Lawrence, 1885–1930) was an English novelist, poet, short story writer, and literary critic. His most famous novel, Lady Chatterley's Lover, tells the story of Constance (Connie) Chatterley's affair with gamekeeper Oliver Mellors at the Wragby estate while her husband Sir Clifford — paralyzed from the waist down after World War I — is tended to by the caregiver Mrs. Bolton. The novel opens with the line 'Ours is essentially a tragic age, so we refuse to take it tragically.' It was banned for decades and the 1960 R v Penguin Books Ltd trial, in which defense counsel Gerald Gardiner defeated prosecution by Reginald Manningham-Buller, became a landmark moment for British censorship law. The prosecution notoriously catalogued every instance of 'cock,' 'shit,' 'cunt,' and 'fuck.' Philip Larkin's 'Annus Mirabilis' famously opens with the novel. Earlier plot details include the Irish playwright Michaelis (whom Connie briefly has an affair with), Mellors's estranged wife Bertha Coutts, and Connie's lie that her child's father is painter Duncan Forbes.

Sons and Lovers (1913) is Lawrence's autobiographical novel about Paul Morel, son of a coal miner, growing up in a mining community called The Bottoms in Nottinghamshire. Paul is torn between his domineering, cancer-stricken mother Gertrude (née Coppard) and his loves: the farmer's daughter Miriam Leivers at Wiley Farm, and Clara Dawes, a suffragette whose estranged metalworker husband Baxter attacks Paul. Paul's older brother William dies early in the novel. Paul works at Thomas Jordan's warehouse as a painter; his coworker Fanny organizes birthday paints for him. Clara is nicknamed the 'Queen of Sheba' by her coworkers. The novel ends with Paul mercy-killing his dying mother with morphine. Editor Edward Garnett cut roughly a tenth of the manuscript to make it publishable. Mabel Dodge Luhan traded a ranch to Lawrence for the original manuscript.

The Rainbow (published 1915, two years after Sons and Lovers) follows three generations of the Brangwen family from patriarch Tom to Ursula Brangwen, set in a Nottingham rural community. Tom's wife is Polish. Ursula falls for Anton Skrebensky, who leaves for the Boer War; during his absence she has a sapphic romance with schoolteacher Winifred Inger, who later marries Ursula's coal baron uncle Tom. The novel ends with Ursula seeing a rainbow in the sky after Anton abandons her — 'the old, brittle corruption of houses and factories' swept away. Women in Love (1920) is the direct sequel, following Ursula and her sister Gudrun Brangwen (based on Katherine Mansfield) as they pair with Rupert Birkin and industrialist Gerald Crich respectively. Key scenes include Birkin forcing a terrified mare to stand at a railway crossing (David Lodge cited this as a model of literary symbolism), Gudrun tutoring a girl who has a pekinese named Looloo while being shocked by the ferocity of rabbit Bismarck, and the lisping model Pussum's friends reading Birkin's letter about the 'Flux of Corruption' at a London cafe. The novel ends with a climactic trip to the Alps where sculptor Loerke is attacked by Gerald Crich.

Lawrence's poetry is associated with the Georgian movement; his 1923 collection Birds, Beasts and Flowers includes 'Snake' — in which the speaker hurls a 'clumsy log' at a snake drinking at his water-trough and later regrets 'a pettiness,' calling the snake 'a king in exile, uncrowned in the underworld' — 'Bavarian Gentians' (the title flowers possess 'the smoking blueness of Pluto's gloom' and serve as torches to the underworld), and 'Piano' ('manhood is cast / Down in the flood of remembrance'). A poem compares the crucifixion to two tortoises mating. His 1929 collection Pansies had thirteen pages confiscated by customs.

His short stories include 'Odour of Chrysanthemums' — in which Elizabeth Bates (née Coppard) tracks down her husband Walter only to learn he has died in a coal mine collapse, while chrysanthemums symbolize her changing feelings about marriage — and 'The Rocking-Horse Winner,' in which a boy named Paul develops clairvoyant powers over horse races by riding his toy horse into a frenzy; a voice in the house whispers 'there must be more money'; Paul wins 80,000 pounds and screams 'It's Malabar!' before dying; his mother's name is Hester, uncle is Oscar Cresswell, and he has a financial partnership with gardener Bassett. Both stories appear in a collection titled The Prussian Officer, whose title story has an officer torment his orderly until the orderly kills him.

Among his critical and travel writings, Studies in Classic American Literature (1923) covered Benjamin Franklin, James Fenimore Cooper, Herman Melville, and others; W.H. Auden praised it in his essay 'Making, Knowing, and Judging.' Its penultimate chapter on Moby-Dick helped revive interest in Melville. Lawrence also wrote Twilight in Italy and Sea and Sardinia (travel writing with wife Frieda), Etruscan Places (travelogue about Etruscan civilization), and Kangaroo (novel set in Australia where protagonist Richard Somers meets Ben Cooley, a former soldier planning to overthrow the government; it inspired the pro-Aboriginal Jindyworobak literary movement). He preferred the title Quetzalcoatl for The Plumed Serpent, a novel about a Quetzalcoatl cult in Mexico that he finished while living in Taos, New Mexico at the ranch of art patron Mabel Dodge Luhan (who also traded a ranch for the Sons and Lovers manuscript). Lawrence's ashes are interred at his Taos ranch; the La Fonda hotel in Taos holds a collection of his 'forbidden' paintings. Mark Rampion in Aldous Huxley's Point Counter Point was based on Lawrence.
