De Musica and Music Theory

Boethius's De institutione musica was the most influential source for Greek-derived musical thought in the medieval era. He divided music into three kinds: musica mundana (celestial harmony), musica humana (bodily harmony), and instrumental music. The work popularized Pythagorean ratio-based derivations of consonances.

Summary of Facts

Boethius (Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius) is a 6th-century Roman philosopher most famous for The Consolation of Philosophy, written while he was imprisoned and awaiting execution by Theodoric the Great. The work is structured as an alternation between prose and poetry, and Joel Relihan has argued it follows the Menippean satire form. It centers on a dialogue between a fictional Boethius and the allegorical Lady Philosophy, who appears wearing a garment embroidered with the Greek letters pi at the bottom and theta at the top, connected by a staircase. Lady Philosophy consoles Boethius by discussing providence, fate, and the nature of happiness.

The Consolation popularized the image of the Wheel of Fortune, with Lady Philosophy describing her 'unchanging sport' of turning the highest to the bottom and the lowest to the top. The work compares God to a ship's 'rudder and helm' and draws on Plato's Gorgias to argue that evildoers punish themselves by ceasing to exist as truly human. Book V addresses the compatibility of divine prescience with free will, comparing God's timeless perspective to a point at the center of a circle equidistant from the entire circumference. The work was later translated into English by Geoffrey Chaucer. Fortuna from the Consolation also plays a major role in the medieval manuscript Carmina Burana, famously set by Carl Orff.

Beyond the Consolation, Boethius was immensely important as a transmitter of Greek thought to the Latin West. His translations of Aristotle were virtually the only Aristotelian works available to medieval Europeans until the 12th century. His translation of Porphyry's Isagoge became the standard medieval logic textbook and codified the 'problem of universals,' which would dominate medieval philosophy. Lorenzo Valla dubbed him 'the first of the Scholastics.'

Boethius also made significant contributions to music theory through his De institutione musica, which divided music into three categories: musica mundana (the harmony of the celestial spheres), musica humana (the harmony of the human body), and instrumental music. This was the most influential source for Greek-derived musical thought throughout the medieval era. His logical works include De Topicis Differentiis, which compares the approaches of Cicero and Themistius to topics, and De Divisione. A Christian legend held that he married the hymnist Helpes, though Edward Gibbon disputed this.
