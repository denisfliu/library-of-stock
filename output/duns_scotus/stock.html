13th-century Scottish/British Scholastic philosopher known as Doctor Subtilis, author of the Ordinatio. Famous for the concepts of haecceity ('thisness') and the univocity of being, his defense of the Immaculate Conception, and his influence on Gerard Manley Hopkins's ideas of inscape and instress.
Haecceity ('Thisness')

Haecceity is the concept coined by Scotus's followers (and developed from his philosophy) denoting the irreducible properties of a thing that make it a particular individual rather than a member of a class. Often translated as 'thisness,' it contrasts with Aquinas's quiddity ('whatness'). C.S. Peirce later used the term, and The Social Construction of Reality by Berger and Luckmann borrows it.

Ordinatio and Scholastic Works

The Ordinatio is Scotus's major work, compiled from his Oxford lectures. It serves as a commentary on Peter Lombard's Four Books of Sentences and introduces both haecceity and univocity of being. Scotus also wrote a Treatise on God as the First Principle and commentaries on Aristotle's categories and Porphyry's Isagoge.

General / Biographical

Summary of Facts

John Duns Scotus is a 13th-century British/Scottish Scholastic philosopher known as Doctor Subtilis ('the Subtle Doctor'). He is primarily associated with two philosophical concepts: haecceity and the univocity of being.

Haecceity (from the Latin for 'thisness') denotes the irreducible properties that make a thing a particular individual rather than merely a member of a class. The term was coined by Scotus's followers based on his philosophy and contrasts with Aquinas's concept of quiddity ('whatness'). C.S. Peirce later used the term to outline how we perceive 'secondness,' and Berger and Luckmann's The Social Construction of Reality borrows it from the Ordinatio.

The univocity of being holds that properties like 'goodness' mean the same thing when applied to God as to creatures - the ultimate difference is one of degree, not of meaning. This directly opposes Thomas Aquinas's analogical predication. The Radical Orthodox School (Catherine Pickstock, John Milbank) blames Scotus for ushering in modernity through this concept, and Gilles Deleuze wrote of an 'ontological' version in his commentary on Spinoza.

Scotus's major work, the Ordinatio, was compiled from his Oxford lectures and serves as a commentary on Peter Lombard's Four Books of Sentences. He also wrote A Treatise on God as the First Principle and developed an argument for God's existence based on first efficient cause. He rejected the existence-essence distinction posited by Aquinas and attacked Henry of Ghent's divine illumination theory.

The Victorian poet Gerard Manley Hopkins drew heavily on Scotus's haecceity when formulating his ideas of inscape and instress. Hopkins's poem 'Duns Scotus's Oxford' describes the city as a 'Towery city' that is 'cuckoo-echoing, bell-swarmed, lark charmed, rook-racked, river-rounded,' calls Scotus 'he who of all men most sways my spirits to peace,' and concludes 'he fired France for Mary without spot,' referencing Scotus's famous defense of the Immaculate Conception, which he championed six centuries before the Church made it official doctrine.

Scotus's British place of birth gave rise to the word 'dunce,' naming a conical cap. His Scotist school opposed the Ockhamists on the problem of universals. Martin Heidegger wrote his Habilitation thesis thinking it was about Scotus, but the text De modis significandi was actually by Thomas of Erfurt. Scotus argued that angels have individual, incomplete knowledge as a sign of their haecceity, contra Aquinas who claimed angels already know everything.
