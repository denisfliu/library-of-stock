            Summary of Facts

            Gerhard Richter (born 1932) is a German artist and one of the most prominent postwar painters, known for his signature 'photo-paintings' created by copying photographs in paint and then blurring them. He is associated with the capitalist realism movement, which developed in 1960s Germany in response to pop art. He was once a member of the Fluxus movement and is linked to the Dusseldorf art scene.

            His photo-paintings include Ema (Nude on a Staircase), a blurred portrait of his wife descending stairs that pays homage to Duchamp's Nude Descending a Staircase, No. 2. He also painted his daughter Betty looking away from the viewer (1988) and created the Baader-Meinhof / Red Army Faction series of blurred photorealistic portraits, as well as Uncle Rudi (a Nazi-themed work). His Atlas project comprises nearly 4000 photographs forming a cultural history from 1962 onwards, including source images for the RAF portraits.

            Richter designed pixelated stained-glass windows (Symphony of Light) for the Cologne Cathedral, the largest Gothic church in northern Europe, using chance to determine color placement. He also created abstract works using a homemade squeegee technique, including a series named for John Cage (six paintings at the Tate Modern). His color chart paintings, including the 4900 Colors grid series, involve colors selected at random. The Birkenau series of four abstract works was featured in his COVID-shortened retrospective Painting After All at the Met Breuer.

            Other notable works include his Annunciation after Titian series (which grows increasingly abstract), candle and skull paintings from the 1980s (one appears on Sonic Youth's Daydream Nation cover), Eight Grey, and Abstraktes Bild (auctioned for a record-breaking 21 million dollars in 2010). A fictionalized version of Richter was the subject of the film Never Look Away by Florian Henckel von Donnersmarck. Photographer Thomas Struth included Richter in his Family Portraits series.
