The Phenomenology of Perception

Merleau-Ponty's magnum opus (1945), which argues that the body is not merely an object but a subject — the means by which we interact with and experience the world. It rejects both empiricism and idealism for their 'unquestioned belief in the world,' instead favoring Husserl's phenomenological method. The introduction argues 'the greatest lesson of the reduction is the impossibility of a complete reduction.' It closes with Saint-Exupery's quote that 'Man is but a network of relationships.' The last section concerns freedom. It rejects the Cartesian cogito and dualism, instead arguing perception is prior to consciousness. Merleau-Ponty examined the phantom limb feeling in amputees to develop his concept of 'body image.' It inspired Iris Marion Young's 'Throwing Like a Girl,' which argues women have 'inhibited intentionality' in physical tasks.

The Visible and the Invisible

Merleau-Ponty's unfinished, posthumously published work (1964), developing his 'ontology of flesh.' It distinguishes between the 'flesh of the body' and 'flesh of the world' as dual aspects of one substance. The last chapter, 'The Intertwining — The Chiasm,' was read by Luce Irigaray in An Ethics of Sexual Difference. The work argues that raw perception occurs 'anonymously,' prior to individuation of the self. It cautions against method and advocates introspection into 'perceptual faith.'

Cezanne's Doubt and Eye and Mind

Two essays on art. 'Cezanne's Doubt' examines the artist's shyness and solitary devotion to painting, influenced by Merleau-Ponty's time as Chair of Child Learning at the Sorbonne, which also produced The Structure of Behavior. 'Eye and Mind' argues that a painter 'offers himself or herself' to the world to produce a painting, and criticizes 'operational thinking.' It describes a black line in a portrait that dislocates as it passes behind the subject in analysis of Cezanne's 'lived perspective.'

General / Biographical

French phenomenologist, member of Husserl's school. Also wrote The Structure of Behavior and Adventures of the Dialectic (criticizing Sartre's 'Ultrabolshevism' of The Communists and Peace, and condemning Kojeve's 'end of history'). Wrote Humanism and Terror examining the 'Communist Problem.' Co-edited Les Temps Modernes with Sartre; quit after the Korean War. Sartre wrote a eulogy titled 'Merleau-Ponty Vivant.' Merleau-Ponty argued that 'there is no world without an Existence that bears its structure,' anticipating a 1951 debate with A.J. Ayer over whether the sun existed before human beings. Argued humans experience space and time as 'unlimited milieus' due to symbolic behavior. Concept of 'being-toward-the-world.'

Summary of Facts

Maurice Merleau-Ponty is a French phenomenologist best known as the author of The Phenomenology of Perception (1945) and the posthumous, unfinished The Visible and the Invisible (1964).

The Phenomenology of Perception is his magnum opus, which argues that the body is not merely an object but a subject — the means by which we interact with and experience the world. It rejects both empiricism and idealism for their 'unquestioned belief in the world,' favoring Husserl's phenomenological method. The introduction states that 'the greatest lesson of the reduction is the impossibility of a complete reduction.' The work rejects the Cartesian cogito, arguing that perception is prior to consciousness, and compares the body in a world to a heart in an organism. Merleau-Ponty examined phantom limb feelings in amputees to develop his concept of 'body image.' The work closes with Saint-Exupery's quote that 'Man is but a network of relationships.' It inspired Iris Marion Young's influential essay 'Throwing Like a Girl,' which argues that women are conditioned to have 'inhibited intentionality' in physical tasks.

The Visible and the Invisible develops the 'ontology of flesh,' distinguishing between the 'flesh of the body' and 'flesh of the world' as dual aspects of one substance. The last chapter, 'The Intertwining — The Chiasm,' was read by Luce Irigaray in An Ethics of Sexual Difference. The work argues that raw perception occurs 'anonymously,' prior to individuation of self, and cautions against method while advocating introspection into 'perceptual faith.'

Merleau-Ponty's essays on art include 'Cezanne's Doubt' (examining the artist's shyness and solitary devotion) and 'Eye and Mind' (arguing a painter 'offers himself or herself' to the world and criticizing 'operational thinking'). He wrote The Structure of Behavior and Adventures of the Dialectic (criticizing Sartre's 'Ultrabolshevism' and Kojeve's 'end of history'). He co-edited Les Temps Modernes with Sartre but quit after the Korean War. Sartre wrote a eulogy titled 'Merleau-Ponty Vivant.' He wrote Humanism and Terror examining the 'Communist Problem.' He argued humans experience space and time as 'unlimited milieus' due to symbolic behavior.
