Indian-born Canadian-American novelist and short story writer. Graduate of the Iowa Writers' Workshop. Married to Clark Blaise. Best known for the novel Jasmine (Indian woman widowed by a bombing kills ship captain Half-Face, immigrates to America, settles in Iowa with wheelchair-bound banker Bud Ripplemayer) and the short story 'The Management of Grief' (inspired by Air India Flight 182 bombing; Shaila Bhave helped by social worker Judith Templeton). Other notable works include 'A Wife's Story' (Panna Bhatt offended by Glengarry Glen Ross), The Holder of the World (a retelling of The Scarlet Letter using VR technology to travel to Mughal-era India), and The Tiger's Daughter. Themes of displacement, immigration, and identity transformation.

Summary of Facts

Bharati Mukherjee (1940-2017) is an Indian-born Canadian-American novelist and short story writer. A graduate of the Iowa Writers' Workshop, she was married to writer Clark Blaise. Her themes center on displacement, immigration, and identity transformation.

Her best-known novel, Jasmine (1989), follows an Indian woman originally named Jyoti whose husband Prakash is killed by the radical terrorist Sukhwinder in a bombing while they are shopping for saris. She immigrates to Florida, kills the ship captain Half-Face who raped her during the voyage, and eventually settles in Iowa with the wheelchair-bound banker Bud Ripplemayer. She changes names throughout (Jyoti, Jasmine, Jane, Jazzy). Bud repeatedly refuses loan applications from the farmer Darrel Lutz, who eventually hangs himself. The novel features the adopted child Du. It was expanded from a story in The Middleman and Other Stories.

Her most frequently clued short story is 'The Management of Grief,' inspired by the Air India Flight 182 bombing by Sikh terrorists. The narrator Shaila Bhave loses her husband Vikram and two sons. Social worker Judith Templeton asks Shaila to help bereaved Indian-Canadian community members sign government forms. Shaila imagines her husband telling her to 'finish what we started together' in a Himalayan temple. The narrator thinks 'I think I can only envy her' about Kusum, who searches for photos of her husband and daughter. Boys mutter 'Sikh bomb.' The protagonist eventually drops a package and walks away after hearing the voices of her dead family, and at one point wades into a bay in Ireland.

Other notable works include 'A Wife's Story,' in which Panna Bhatt is offended by a production of David Mamet's Glengarry Glen Ross. The Holder of the World loosely retells The Scarlet Letter, with Beigh Masters using a VR program to travel to Mughal-era India, where Hannah Easton falls in love with a prince. The Tiger's Daughter follows Calcutta native Tara, who writes her dissertation on Katherine Mansfield at Vassar and returns to India. In the novel Wife, the protagonist stabs her husband seven times. Mukherjee co-wrote The Sorrow and the Terror with Clark Blaise about the Air India bombing. 'The World According to Hsu' features the Claytons vacationing in an African country rocked by civil war.
