Summary of Facts

The Overture in the French Style (BWV 831) is a harpsichord suite in B minor by J. S. Bach, published in the Clavier-Übung II (1735) alongside the Italian Concerto in F major. The pairing was deliberate, contrasting French and Italian musical styles. The work was originally composed in C minor but transposed to B minor to complete the cycle of tonalities in the collection. It concludes with an 'Echo' movement that emphasizes dynamic contrasts.

The work exemplifies the French overture form: a slow introduction characterized by dotted rhythms, followed by a fast fugal or imitative section. This form was developed by Jean-Baptiste Lully and first appeared appended to early operas such as Robert Cambert's Pomone and Lully's Atys; Lully used it in his intermèdes, tragédies lyriques, and comédies-ballets. Telemann wrote over a hundred suites in overture form. Handel's Water Music (composed for a Thames barge trip planned by King George I) also opens with a French overture, as does Les Indes galantes by Rameau. The 16th variation of Bach's Goldberg Variations is also in French overture form.
