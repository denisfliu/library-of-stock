French existentialist philosopher, novelist, and playwright. Author of Being and Nothingness, the novel Nausea, and the play No Exit. Known for the concepts of bad faith, 'existence precedes essence,' and 'hell is other people.' Partner of Simone de Beauvoir. Declined the 1964 Nobel Prize in Literature.
Being and Nothingness

Sartre's major philosophical work distinguishing between being-in-itself (objects of consciousness) and being-for-itself (experience/consciousness). It introduces the concept of 'bad faith' (mauvaise foi), illustrated by a cafe waiter whose movements are 'a little too precise, a little too rapid' because he is playing at being a waiter. The work discusses 'the look' as an objectifying phenomenon, uses the example of searching for Pierre in a cafe to illustrate nothingness, defines 'facticity' as limits on human freedom, argues that bad faith may be surpassed when facticity is in harmony with transcendence, and contains a late passage on slime (le visqueux) as the 'revenge of the in-itself.' The first part discusses the 'origin of negation.'

Nausea

Sartre's existentialist novel in which Antoine Roquentin, living in Bouville, suffers from a 'sweetish sickness' and tries to write a biography of the Marquis de Rollebon. He fails to convey his philosophy to the pedophilic 'Self-Taught Man' Ogier P, who reads library books alphabetically. Roquentin is left at a loss for words by a chestnut tree and insists that he and his surroundings are 'superfluous.' He loved Anny and makes love to Francoise. A bad trip on mescaline caused Sartre to hallucinate lobster-like creatures, possibly influencing Roquentin's references to crabs.

No Exit

Sartre's most famous play, in which Garcin (a Brazilian deserter), Inez (a lesbian postal clerk), and Estelle are trapped in a room with Second Empire furnishings and a bronze ornament on the mantlepiece, attended by a valet with no eyelids. Garcin exclaims the famous line 'hell is other people.' Estelle stabs Inez with a paper-knife/letter-opener to show it has no effect. Inez died by gas, Estelle's lover Florence turned on gas while she slept. Garcin ends the play saying 'Well, well, let's get on with it.' E.M. Cioran often stalked Sartre to the Cafe de Flore and wrote 'On an Entrepreneur of Ideas' about him.

"The Wall" and Short Stories

Sartre's short story collection, titled after 'The Wall,' set during the Spanish Civil War. In the title story, political prisoner Pablo Ibbieta gives what he thinks is false information (that Ramon Gris is hiding in a cemetery), but it turns out to be true, and Gris is found and executed. Other stories include 'Erostratus' (Paul Hilbert plans to kill five random people with a gun and commit suicide with the sixth bullet, inspired by Herostratus's burning of the Temple of Artemis), 'The Room' (Eve refuses to send her insane husband Pierre to a clinic), 'Intimacy' (Lulu questions social roles), and 'The Childhood of a Leader' (Lucien refuses to shake hands with a Jewish person).

Colonialism, Negritude, and Frantz Fanon

Sartre wrote the preface/introduction to Frantz Fanon's The Wretched of the Earth (where he explained how 'violence, like Achilles' lance, can heal the wounds that it has inflicted' and wrote 'the status of native is a nervous condition,' a quote that became the title of Tsitsi Dangarembga's novel). His essay 'Black Orpheus' described the Negritude movement as 'anti-racist racism,' asked 'when you removed the gag that was keeping these black mouths shut, what were you hoping for?' and opens Leopold Senghor's poetry anthology. He wrote Colonialism and Neocolonialism (the fifth volume of his Situations series). Fanon critiques Sartre's 'Black Orpheus' in 'The Lived Experience of the Black Man' in Black Skin, White Masks. Ideas from Anti-Semite and Jew were applied to African colonialism by Fanon.

Anti-Semite and Jew

Sartre's essay examining antisemitism through the lens of bad faith. It divides Jews into 'authentic' and 'inauthentic' forms, examines prejudice, and argues that antisemites avoid responsibility through bad faith. The essay opens with a discussion of a classmate failing an exam while a Jewish classmate passed. Sartre notes that 'the Jewishness of the Jew can go unnoticed.' Fanon found a flaw in Sartre's conception of race in this work.

General / Biographical and Other Works

Sartre declined the 1964 Nobel Prize in Literature due to Eurocentrism of the Swedish Academy. He was partner of Simone de Beauvoir, who developed an ethical system from his work in The Ethics of Ambiguity. With Beauvoir, he cofounded the literary magazine Les Temps Modernes (Modern Times). He wrote a 2,800-page study of Flaubert titled The Family Idiot and analyzed Flaubert via 'regressive-progressive method' in Search for a Method. He wrote Saint Genet (about Jean Genet, subtitled 'Actor and Martyr'). He coined the term 'anti-novel' in his preface to Nathalie Sarraute's Portrait of a Man Unknown. What is Literature? advocates socially 'committed' or 'engaged' writing. The Roads to Freedom is an incomplete tetralogy (including Troubled Sleep, The Reprieve, The Last Chance) about Mathieu Delarue. Critique of Dialectical Reason calls humanity a 'fused group' and discusses the 'practico-inert.' Pierre Bourdieu criticized him for writing as a 'transcendent consciousness.' Fictionalized as Robert Dubreuilh in Beauvoir's The Mandarins. In Boris Vian's Froth on the Daydream, a character is named after Sartre. Merleau-Ponty quit Les Temps Modernes after the Korean War and criticized Sartre's 'Ultrabolshevism' in Adventures of the Dialectic. He experimented with mescaline and hallucinated lobster-like creatures following him. His essay on The Sound and the Fury accused Faulkner of 'abolishing the future.' He defined freedom as 'the nihilation of a given' and argued 'there is freedom only in situations.' Sarah Bakewell's 2016 history of Existentialism is subtitled for 'Apricot Cocktails.'

Summary of Facts

Jean-Paul Sartre is a French existentialist philosopher, novelist, and playwright, identified primarily by Being and Nothingness, Nausea, and No Exit. He was the partner of Simone de Beauvoir and declined the 1964 Nobel Prize in Literature.

Being and Nothingness is Sartre's major philosophical work, distinguishing between being-in-itself and being-for-itself. It introduces the concept of bad faith (mauvaise foi), most famously illustrated by a cafe waiter whose movements are 'a little too precise, a little too rapid' because he is playing at being a waiter. Other illustrations include a woman on a date who ignores sexual undertones. The work discusses 'the look' as an objectifying phenomenon, uses the example of searching for friend Pierre in a cafe to illustrate nothingness, defines facticity as limits on human freedom, and contains a passage on slime (le visqueux) as the 'revenge of the in-itself.' Sartre argued that human situations arise from the interaction between facticity and transcendence.

Nausea is Sartre's existentialist novel about Antoine Roquentin, living in the town of Bouville, who suffers from a 'sweetish sickness' while trying to write a biography of the Marquis de Rollebon. He fails to convey his philosophy to the pedophilic 'Self-Taught Man' Ogier P, who reads library books alphabetically. Roquentin is overwhelmed by a chestnut tree and concludes that he and his surroundings are 'superfluous.' Sartre's own mescaline hallucinations of lobster-like creatures may have influenced Roquentin's references to crabs.

No Exit is Sartre's most famous play, in which Garcin (a Brazilian deserter), Inez (a lesbian postal clerk who died by gas), and Estelle are trapped in a room with Second Empire furnishings and a bronze ornament, attended by a valet with no eyelids. The play concludes with Garcin's famous line 'hell is other people.' Estelle stabs Inez with a paper-knife to show it has no effect.

'The Wall' is the title story of Sartre's only short story collection, set during the Spanish Civil War. Political prisoner Pablo Ibbieta gives what he believes is false information (that Ramon Gris is hiding in a cemetery), but it turns out to be true and Gris is executed. Other stories include 'Erostratus' (Paul Hilbert plans to kill five random people with six bullets), 'The Room' (Eve stays with her insane husband Pierre), and 'The Childhood of a Leader' (Lucien refuses to shake hands with a Jewish person).

Sartre's engagement with colonialism was extensive. He wrote the preface to Frantz Fanon's The Wretched of the Earth, where he stated 'the status of native is a nervous condition' (inspiring Tsitsi Dangarembga's novel title). His essay 'Black Orpheus' described the Negritude movement as 'anti-racist racism' and opens Leopold Senghor's poetry anthology. Anti-Semite and Jew examines antisemitism through bad faith, dividing Jews into 'authentic' and 'inauthentic' forms. Fanon applied ideas from this essay to African colonialism.

Dirty Hands features Hugo Barine taking the code-name Raskolnikov and murdering the Illyrian socialist Hoederer. The Respectful Prostitute has Senator Clarke convincing sex worker Lizzie to falsely accuse a black man. The Flies retells the Oresteia with Orestes refusing guilt for killing Clytemnestra.

Other significant works include What is Literature? (advocating 'committed' writing), The Family Idiot (2,800-page Flaubert study), Saint Genet (on Jean Genet), Critique of Dialectical Reason (calling humanity a 'fused group'), Search for a Method (the 'regressive-progressive method'), and the incomplete Roads to Freedom tetralogy. He cofounded Les Temps Modernes with Beauvoir. He was fictionalized as Robert Dubreuilh in Beauvoir's The Mandarins. Merleau-Ponty quit Les Temps Modernes after the Korean War and criticized Sartre's 'Ultrabolshevism.' He coined 'anti-novel' in his preface to Sarraute's Portrait of a Man Unknown.
