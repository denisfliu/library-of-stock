Irish playwright and novelist, key figure of the Theatre of the Absurd. Best known for Waiting for Godot, Endgame, and his trilogy of novels (Molloy, Malone Dies, The Unnamable). Wrote many works in French, then translated them into English. Also wrote Happy Days, Krapp's Last Tape, Not I, and the novel Murphy.
Waiting for Godot

Beckett's most famous play, about Vladimir and Estragon waiting by a tree on a country road for the mysterious Godot, who never arrives. Characters often say 'nothing to be done.' They discuss hanging themselves from the lone tree. A few leaves appear on the tree between acts despite only one day passing. Vivian Mercer described it as a play in which 'Nothing happens, twice.' Lucky delivers a rambling monologue stopped when his bowler hat is removed. Pozzo says Lucky will be 'chucked away like a banana skin.' A 'gravedigger puts on the forceps' and 'habit is the great deadener.' Beckett told a friend that a Caspar David Friedrich painting of two men contemplating the moon 'was the source of Waiting for Godot.' Originally written in French.

Endgame

A one-act play set in a room shaped like a human skull. The blind Hamm sits in an armchair and abuses his servant Clov, who cannot sit down. Hamm's legless parents Nagg and Nell live in ashbins (dustbins/trash cans). They lost their legs in a bicycling accident in the Ardennes. Nell dies, saying 'nothing is funnier than unhappiness.' Clov opens curtains via stepladder at the start. An alarm clock is placed on Nagg's ashbin. The title is a chess allusion. Beckett drew on his mother's death from Parkinson's. Premiered at the Royal Court Theatre.

Molloy

First novel of the trilogy (with Malone Dies and The Unnamable). Written in French. The title character travels between unnamed towns on a bicycle searching for his possibly-dead mother and is pursued by the detective Jacques Moran. Moran's son goes to buy a bicycle and takes months to return. The protagonist spends an eighty-page paragraph on a bicycle. Molloy murders a 'charcoal-burner' with the aid of his crutches. A woman saves a man who ran over her dog with a bicycle, sparing him from a mob.

The Unnamable

Contains the line 'if I am Mahood, I am Worm too,' suggesting shifting narrators. A story about a legless, armless, and speechless character named Mahood is told within. Murphy

Beckett's first novel. Opens with 'The sun shone, having no alternative, on the nothing new.' The protagonist enjoys rocking in a chair while tied to it naked and his mind is described as 'a large hollow sphere, hermetically closed to the universe without.' Murphy begins a relationship with the prostitute Celia. He plays chess with the schizophrenic Mr. Endon at a mental hospital. Murphy burns to death when someone turns on the gas instead of flushing the toilet. His friend Cooper loses his ashes in a bar fight after Murphy wished for them to be flushed down the toilet at the Abbey Theater. Mentored by Neary, a practitioner of self-induced cardiac arrests.

General / Biographical

Irish author who switched to writing in French mid-career in order to write 'without style.' Won an unspecified prize and agreed to a Swedish Television interview on the condition no questions be asked. Stabbed by a pimp named Prudent; James Joyce paid for his hospital room despite Beckett once being the lover of Lucia Joyce. Drove young Andre the Giant to school. His first published work was a Schopenhauer-influenced monograph on Proust. Was interested in the philosopher Arnold Geulincx. Michel Foucault's 'What is an Author?' quotes his Texts for Nothing. Collaborated with Morton Feldman on the opera Neither. The 35-second play Breath is his shortest work. Worstward Ho contains the advice 'Fail again. Fail better.' Wrote the novel Watt while hiding from the Gestapo. The short film Film starred Buster Keaton.

Summary of Facts

Samuel Beckett (1906-1989) was an Irish playwright and novelist, one of the most important figures in twentieth-century literature and a central figure of the Theatre of the Absurd. In quizbowl, he is among the most heavily clued literary figures, with a vast body of work appearing across difficulty levels.

Waiting for Godot is Beckett's most recognizable work, depicting Vladimir and Estragon waiting by a tree on a country road for the mysterious Godot, who never arrives. The play opens with 'Nothing to be done' as Estragon fails to remove his boot. A few leaves appear on the tree between acts despite only one day passing. Lucky delivers a rambling monologue that stops only when his bowler hat is removed. Vivian Mercer famously described it as a play in which 'nothing happens, twice.' Beckett once told a friend that Caspar David Friedrich's painting of two men contemplating the moon 'was the source of Waiting for Godot.' Originally written in French.

Endgame is a one-act play set in a room shaped like a human skull. The blind Hamm sits in an armchair abusing his servant Clov, who cannot sit down. Hamm's legless parents Nagg and Nell live in ashbins (they lost their legs in a bicycling accident in the Ardennes). Clov opens curtains via a stepladder at the start. Before dying, Nell says 'nothing is funnier than unhappiness.' The title is a chess allusion.

Beckett's prose trilogy consists of Molloy, Malone Dies, and The Unnamable. In Molloy, the title character travels on a bicycle searching for his possibly-dead mother while the detective Jacques Moran pursues him. The Unnamable ends with the famous lines 'You must go on. I can't go on. I'll go on' and features shifting narrators ('if I am Mahood, I am Worm too'). Malone Dies features a narrator immobile in a hospital bed who writes a story about a boy called Sapo, later renamed Macmann. Murphy is Beckett's first novel, opening with 'The sun shone, having no alternative, on the nothing new.' The protagonist befriends the prostitute Celia, plays chess with the schizophrenic Mr. Endon, and burns to death when someone turns on the gas instead of flushing the toilet.

Happy Days features Winnie buried progressively deeper in a mound of earth while talking to her husband Willie. Her parasol catches fire, and she plays a music box from The Merry Widow. Krapp's Last Tape is a one-man play in which the title character listens to recordings of his past self on his birthday, eating bananas and ending by covering his face with a handkerchief called an 'old stancher.' Not I features only a woman's mouth, spotlit eight feet above the stage in pitch darkness, while a black-robed Auditor makes 'gestures of helpless compassion.'

Biographically, Beckett switched to writing in French to write 'without style.' He was influenced by the philosopher Arnold Geulincx. He was stabbed by a pimp named Prudent, and James Joyce paid for his hospital room despite Beckett having been the lover of Joyce's daughter Lucia. He drove young Andre the Giant to school. His first published work was a Schopenhauer-influenced monograph on Proust. He wrote Watt while hiding from the Gestapo. Worstward Ho contains the advice 'Fail again. Fail better.' The short film Film starred Buster Keaton. He collaborated with Morton Feldman on the opera Neither and wrote the 35-second play Breath.
