Hiroshima

A book-length essay published in the New Journalism style that occupied an entire 1946 issue of the New Yorker. It follows the lives of six people who survived the atomic bombing of Hiroshima, including the German Jesuit father Kleinsorge (found wandering with glass shards in his back) and Reverend Kiyoshi Tanimoto. Sections include 'A Noiseless Flash' and 'The Fire.' The work profiles a seamstress and a priest among its six subjects.

Summary of Facts

John Hersey (John Richard Hersey) was an American journalist and novelist best known for two works that appear prominently in quizbowl clues. His most famous work, Hiroshima, is a book-length essay in the New Journalism style that occupied an entire 1946 issue of the New Yorker. It follows six survivors of the atomic bombing, including the German Jesuit father Kleinsorge (found wandering a mission with glass embedded in his back) and Reverend Kiyoshi Tanimoto, who was later surprised on the TV show This is Your Life. The essay's sections include 'A Noiseless Flash' and 'The Fire,' and it profiles subjects including a seamstress and a priest.

Hersey also wrote the novel A Bell for Adano, which won the 1945 Pulitzer Prize for Fiction. It follows Major Joppolo's efforts to acquire a replacement bell for a Sicilian town after the original 700-year-old bell was stolen by Fascists. A short-tempered general in the novel orders a cart and its wine-drunk driver thrown into a ravine after being blocked in the road.
