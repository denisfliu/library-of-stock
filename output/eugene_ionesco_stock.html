Summary of Facts

Eugene Ionesco (Eugen Ionescu) is a Romanian-French absurdist playwright best known for The Bald Soprano, Rhinoceros, The Chairs, The Lesson, and Exit the King. He is a central figure in the Theatre of the Absurd and created the recurring character Berenger, who appears across multiple plays.

Rhinoceros is his most famous play. In it, Berenger is the only character who refuses to transform into the title animal. His friend Jean's skin turns green as he transforms. The typist Daisy and Dudard also transform, and Daisy leaves Berenger. A logician uses a syllogism to prove a dog is a cat. A cat is trampled. Berenger shouts 'I'm not capitulating!' at the end. The play satirizes French behavior under German occupation; the green skin represents the green uniforms of the Iron Guard and Orpo. The Israeli word for getting swayed by nationalistic fervor was coined from the play's title.

The Bald Soprano is Ionesco's first play, an 'anti-play' set in an 'English interior, with English armchairs.' Everything in the opening is described as 'English' seventeen times. The Smiths and the Martins engage in surreal conversation after the Fire Chief mentions the title character. Mrs. Smith responds that the bald soprano 'always wears her hair the same way.' The maid Mary claims her real name is Sherlock Holmes and recites a poem about objects catching fire. Bobby Watson's death is discussed -- every member of his family is named Bobby Watson. The Fire Chief settles the 'doorbell debate' and tells an experimental fable. Ionesco was inspired to write it after reading an English language primer (Assimil), whose textbook opens with 'My tailor is rich.' Originally directed by Nicholas Bataille. The Theatre de la Huchette has permanently staged it and The Lesson for 70 years.

The Chairs is a 'tragic farce' in which the Old Man and Old Woman arrange chairs for invisible guests on their island. The Old Man introduces his wife as Semiramis and greets the Lady, the Photo-engraver, and the Belle. The deaf-mute Orator writes 'ANGELFOOD' on a chalkboard. An invisible Emperor arrives. The Old Man and Old Woman throw themselves out of a window. The Old Man imitates the month of February and addresses 'all that is left from humanity' as 'leftovers.'

The Lesson is a one-act play in which 'the Pupil' impresses her teacher by naming all four seasons and can add and multiply but not subtract. The maid warns 'Philology leads to calamity!' The Professor ignores her toothache and stabs her to death, restarting the cycle with the next pupil. The Professor wears an armband with a swastika.

Exit the King features Berenger as a 400-year-old king who confronts his impending death. His two wives, Marie and Marguerite, react differently -- Marguerite tells him 'You're going to die at the end of the show' while Marie tries to prevent him from learning. He is credited with inventing the tractor, setting the Thames on fire, and writing all of Shakespeare's plays.

The Killer features Berenger in a 'radiant city' where a murderer drowns his victims. The killer uses a 'picture of a colonel' to lure Dany to her death. The killer does almost nothing except chuckle and shrug.

Other works include Amedee, or How to Get Rid of It (about an ever-growing corpse), The New Tenant (a man trapped under ever-increasing furniture), A Stroll in the Air (Berenger can fly; inspired by Ionesco's childhood Experience of floating in a whitewashed village), Jack, or Obedience and The Future is in Eggs (Jack rejects a girl for having two noses but marries Roberta; ends with egg-hatching), Macbett (inspired by Macbeth, featuring a lemonade seller and butterfly catcher), Improvisation, or The Shepherd's Chameleon (playwright Ionesco is bullied by three versions of Bartholomeus), and the critical essays Nu. In 1958, Kenneth Tynan touched off the 'London Controversy' with a negative review. Ionesco was a member of the College de Pataphysique alongside Duchamp and the Marx Brothers.
