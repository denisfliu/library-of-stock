Learning from Las Vegas (1972)

Co-written with husband Robert Venturi and Steven Izenour (1972). Influenced by a Yale studio studying A&P parking lots and Las Vegas commercial strips. Introduces the typology of 'duck' (building whose form is symbolic of its purpose — named for the Big Duck on Long Island) versus 'decorated shed' (functionally built structure using vivid signage). Contrasts these with reference to billboards along the Strip. Celebrates roadside decor and vernacular architecture against modernist orthodoxy. Led to the emergence of two architectural factions: the 'Grays' (Venturi/Scott Brown postmodernists) and the 'Whites' (pure modernists like Meier and Eisenman). The book was critical of the phrase 'less is more' (Mies van der Rohe); Venturi responded with 'less is a bore.'

'Room at the Top?' / Pritzker Prize controversy

Scott Brown published the essay 'Room at the Top? Sexism and the Star System in Architecture,' which examines sexism in the profession and blames the 'starchitect' phenomenon for obscuring female collaborators' contributions. She accused the system of crediting her husband Robert Venturi for coining 'duck building' even though she was equally responsible. The essay noted that 'QP' (a colleague) invited only male friends to the Century Club. In 1991, when Venturi alone was awarded the Pritzker Architecture Prize, Scott Brown boycotted the ceremony in protest; she never received the award. Frederic Jameson's essay on postmodernism compared her role to Howard Roark / 'Star Quality.'

Summary of Facts

Denise Scott Brown (b. 1931, South Africa) is an American architect and urban theorist who has worked closely with her husband Robert Venturi as part of the firm Venturi, Scott Brown and Associates. She is best known for co-authoring Learning from Las Vegas (1972) with Venturi and Steven Izenour, one of the defining texts of postmodern architecture. The book grew out of a Yale studio that studied A&P parking lots and the commercial strip of Las Vegas. It introduced the typology of 'duck' (a building whose form represents its purpose — named for the Big Duck on Long Island) versus 'decorated shed' (a functionally built structure that uses vivid signage), and celebrated roadside vernacular architecture against modernist orthodoxy. Billboards along the Strip were examined as a means of attracting consumer attention, and the La Tourette Monastery was used as a comparative example in a section called 'Space as God.' The book's publication triggered the 'Whites vs. Grays' debate in architecture: the Grays (led by Venturi and Scott Brown) championed postmodern ornamentation, while the Whites (Peter Eisenman, Richard Meier) maintained pure modernism. Venturi's quip that 'less is a bore' — directed at Mies van der Rohe's 'less is more' — was central to the book's polemic.

Scott Brown is also known for her essay 'Room at the Top? Sexism and the Star System in Architecture,' which examines how the 'starchitect' phenomenon obscures the contributions of female collaborators. The essay notes that a colleague referred to as 'QP' invited only male friends to the Century Club, and critiques how Venturi was credited for coining the term 'duck building.' The controversy reached its peak in 1991 when the Pritzker Architecture Prize — the highest honor in architecture — was awarded solely to Venturi; Scott Brown boycotted the ceremony in protest and has never received the prize.

Scott Brown's built work includes the Sainsbury Wing of the National Gallery in London and the Seattle Art Museum, both realized in collaboration with Venturi. She designed campus plans for Williams College, Bryn Mawr College, and the University of Michigan. Both she and Venturi were students of Louis Kahn in Philadelphia, and helped revive the reputation of Victorian architect Frank Furness.
