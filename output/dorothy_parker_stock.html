Summary of Facts

Dorothy Parker is an American poet, short story writer, and satirist best known as a founding member of the Algonquin Round Table, the group of 1920s New York wits (also called "the Vicious Circle") that included Robert Benchley, Robert Sherwood, George S. Kaufman, and Alexander Woollcott.

Her most frequently clued poem is "Resume," an eight-line piece that opens "Razors pain you; / Rivers are damp" and catalogs the drawbacks of various suicide methods ("Acids stain you," "Drugs cause cramp," "Guns aren't lawful; / Nooses give," "Gas smells awful") before concluding "You might as well live." Her two-line poem "News Item" is equally iconic: "Men seldom make passes / At girls who wear glasses."

"Big Blonde" is her most important short story, centering on Hazel Morse, a woman called a "good sport" by men who struggles with alcoholism and depression. Hazel's husband Herbie gives her a black eye and leaves for a job in Detroit. She spends nights at Jimmy's bar with boyfriends, toasting "Here's mud in your eye." Her behavior at the theater is a "standing joke" because she cries at everything. She attempts suicide with twenty tablets of veronal but fails, waking two days later to share a drink with her maid Nettie.

Other notable poems include "General Review of the Sex Situation" ("Woman wants monogamy; Man delights in novelty... Count to ten, and man is bored"), "Love Song" (each stanza ends with lines like "I wish somebody'd shoot him"), "Inventory" from the collection Enough Rope ("Love, curiosity, freckles, and doubt"), and "One Perfect Rose" (wishing someone would send "one perfect limousine" instead). Her story "The Standard of Living" follows stenographers Annabel and Midge playing a million-dollar fantasy game. "A Telephone Call" consists entirely of a woman's thoughts while waiting for a man to call.

Parker wrote the "Constant Reader" book review column for The New Yorker, most famously savaging A.A. Milne with the line "Tonstant Weader Fwowed up" in response to his use of the word "hummy." She was on the magazine's first board of editors. She got her start replacing P.G. Wodehouse at Vanity Fair and co-wrote the screenplay for A Star is Born with her husband Alan Campbell. She parodied Housman, imagining "how gay 'twould be / to hang me from a flowering tree," and wrote a response to Marlowe's "Passionate Shepherd" containing "we'll always be Jung together." She was fictionalized as Gora Dwight in Gertrude Atherton's Black Oxen, and her short story "Lolita" about widow Mrs. Ewing was published three weeks before Nabokov's novel of the same name.
