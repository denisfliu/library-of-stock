German-born Jewish composer (1791–1864), the dominant figure in French grand opera. Born Jakob Liebmann Beer, he took an Italian first name when he achieved success in Italy. Major operas include Les Huguenots, Robert le Diable, Le Prophète, and L'Africaine (his final opera, about Vasco da Gama). Frequently collaborated with librettist Eugène Scribe. General / Biographical

Les Huguenots

Grand opera in five acts (1836) set during the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572). Raoul (Protestant tenor) falls in love with Valentine (Catholic soprano); her father, Count de Saint-Bris, kills them both in the massacre. Other characters: Marcel (old Huguenot servant, bass), Queen Marguerite de Valois (who tries to broker peace via Raoul and Valentine's marriage). Overture quotes Martin Luther's chorale 'Ein feste Burg.' Marcel sings the battle song 'Piff, paff, piff, paff.' Act II features a swearing of eternal peace in C minor octaves ('Par l'honneur'). Act IV features the 'Blessing of the Daggers' (Berlioz praised this scene in three articles) and an immortal love duet 'Tu l'as dit.' The opera calls for a viola d'amore in the aria 'Plus blanc que la blanche hermine.' Often performed only through Act IV. Wikipedia

Robert le Diable (Robert the Devil)

Grand opera (1831). Historically significant for its 'Ballet of the Nuns' ('Resurrection of the Nuns'): in Act III, the villain Bertram summons dead nuns from their tombs; the Abbess Helena (played by Marie Taglioni) orders them to dance. The scene made use of the Paris Opéra's newly installed gas lighting and established Taglioni's fame. Berlioz praised its use of gongs and bassoon in this scene. Widely considered the beginning of Romantic ballet and credited with introducing the ballet tutu. Opens with a short prelude. Ends with the cello accompanying a character's hallucination of her lover in a swan-drawn chariot. Wikipedia

Le Prophète (The Prophet)

Grand opera (1849) about John of Leyden (Jean de Leyde), who is proclaimed a prophet by scheming Anabaptists to depose Count Oberthal and take Münster. Act III contains a famous 'skating' ballet in a forest near Westphalia. Contains the chorale 'Ad nos ad salutarem undam.' Ends with an explosion that destroys a palace where all major characters are attending a party; Jean's lover Berthe sets fire to the palace and kills herself. Act II features soldiers singing 'God wants blood!' and a dramatic bell tolling. Another scene features a band of Huguenots singing the 'rataplan' that interrupts a duel. Wikipedia

L'Africaine (The African Woman)

Meyerbeer's final opera (premiered posthumously, 1865), originally titled Vasco da Gama. About the Portuguese explorer Vasco da Gama, his love for Ines, and the enslaved African queen Selika. Explorer sings 'O Paradis!' on Selika's island. Selika ends the opera by inhaling the poisonous blossoms of the manchineel tree. The slave Nelusko sings a 'Ballad of Adamastor' (about the sea giant Adamastor). A 2018 critical edition revealed that François-Joseph Fétis moved the central setting to Madagascar for the Paris premiere. At one point, workers below the stage turned a wheel to simulate a shipwreck by shaking the stage. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Giacomo Meyerbeer (1791–1864), born Jakob Liebmann Beer in Berlin, was the dominant figure in French grand opera for much of the nineteenth century. He took the Italian name Giacomo after early successes in Italy, where his final Italian opera, Il crociato in Egitto (The Crusader in Egypt, 1824), is likely the last opera ever to feature a castrato and the last to call for keyboard accompaniment in recitatives. He then conquered Paris, collaborating extensively with the librettist Eugène Scribe (the inventor of the 'well-made play') on his most celebrated works. Meyerbeer fused German orchestration, Italian melody, and French theatrical spectacle. Berlioz admired his orchestration, particularly praising his use of gongs and the bassoon's middle register.

His four grand operas are the core of his quizbowl presence. Robert le Diable (1831) is historically significant for its Act III 'Ballet of the Nuns' (also called the 'Resurrection of the Nuns'), in which the villain Bertram summons dead nuns from their tombs to dance; the Abbess Helena, played by Marie Taglioni, leads them. The scene was the first to exploit the Paris Opéra's newly installed gas lighting and is widely credited with introducing the Romantic ballet tutu. Berlioz praised Meyerbeer's use of gongs and bassoon in this scene. The opera ends with the cello accompanying a character's hallucination of her lover arriving in a swan-drawn chariot. Les Huguenots (1836), his most celebrated work, is set against the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572). The Protestant tenor Raoul and the Catholic soprano Valentine are killed by her father, the Count de Saint-Bris. The overture quotes Martin Luther's chorale 'Ein feste Burg ist unser Gott,' which the old Huguenot servant Marcel also sings as a prayer and mimics cannon fire in his battle song 'Piff, paff, piff, paff.' Act II ends with a solemn oath of eternal peace ('Par l'honneur') in C minor, after which Raoul rejects Valentine by mistake. Act IV features the 'Blessing of the Daggers,' which Berlioz analyzed in three detailed articles. The opera also calls for a viola d'amore in the aria 'Plus blanc que la blanche hermine.' Le Prophète (1849) depicts John of Leyden being proclaimed a prophet by scheming Anabaptists to seize Münster. Act III features a famous skating ballet in a Westphalia forest; it also contains the chorale 'Ad nos ad salutarem undam.' The opera ends with Jean's lover Berthe setting fire to a palace, causing an explosion that kills all the characters gathered there. Liszt wrote a set of piano transcriptions based on its music. L'Africaine (premiered posthumously in 1865) was originally titled Vasco da Gama; it tells the story of the Portuguese explorer and the enslaved African queen Selika. Vasco sings the famous 'O Paradis!' on Selika's island; Selika ends the opera by inhaling the poisonous blossoms of the manchineel tree. The slave Nelusko sings the 'Ballad of Adamastor.' A 2018 critical edition revealed that the musicologist François-Joseph Fétis made major changes for the Paris premiere, including relocating the central setting to Madagascar. At the original premiere, workers turned a wheel beneath the stage to simulate an Act III shipwreck.

Meyerbeer was famously targeted by Richard Wagner, who believed him to be the principal target of his 1850 anti-Semitic polemic Das Judenthum in der Musik (On Jewishness in Music) and later attacked him by name in Opera and Drama. Ironically, Meyerbeer was an early supporter of Wagner; Wagner's own grand opera Rienzi was so indebted to Meyerbeer's style that critics called it 'Meyerbeer's best opera.'
