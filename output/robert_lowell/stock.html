American Confessional poet. Best known for "Skunk Hour" ("I myself am hell"), "For the Union Dead" (Colonel Shaw memorial, South Boston Aquarium), and the collection Life Studies (whose review by M.L. Rosenthal coined the term "confessional poetry"). Also wrote "The Quaker Graveyard in Nantucket" and Homage to Mistress Bradstreet-era work in Lord Weary's Castle. Close friend of Elizabeth Bishop; mentor was Allen Tate.

Summary of Facts

Robert Lowell is an American Confessional poet whose collection Life Studies (1959) inspired M.L. Rosenthal to coin the term "confessional poetry." The collection contains "Skunk Hour" (concluding it), the prose memoir "91 Revere Street" (childhood vignettes; mother calls him "man of the house"), "Beyond the Alps" (opening poem, a train from Rome to Paris symbolizing loss of Catholic faith), "Memories of West Street and Lepke" (the "tranquillized Fifties" and encountering the head of Murder Inc. in jail), and poems for Delmore Schwartz and Ford Madox Ford.

"Skunk Hour," dedicated to Elizabeth Bishop in response to her "Armadillo," is his most frequently clued poem. Set on Nautilus Island, the speaker hears a car radio bleat "Love, O careless Love" and declares "I myself am hell" before observing a skunk that "jabs her wedge-head in a cup of sour cream" and "drops her ostrich tail, and will not scare." The poem mentions the island's "hermit heiress" and a lost "summer millionaire, who seemed to leap from an L.L. Bean catalogue."

"For the Union Dead" opens at "the old South Boston Aquarium" standing "in a Sahara of snow" and meditates on the Robert Gould Shaw Memorial, which "sticks like a fishbone / in the city's throat." It describes "yellow dinosaur steamshovels," a photograph of Hiroshima, "wasp-waisted" statues, and ends "a savage servility / slides by on grease." Its title echoes his mentor Allen Tate's "Ode to the Confederate Dead."

"The Quaker Graveyard in Nantucket" from Lord Weary's Castle (1946) is dedicated to "Warren Winslow, dead at sea" and alludes to Moby-Dick, concluding "The Lord survives the rainbow of his will." Lord Weary's Castle also contains "Mr. Edwards and the Spider," based on Jonathan Edwards (from whom Lowell descended).

The Dolphin (1973) is a sonnet sequence about leaving wife Elizabeth Hardwick for Lady Caroline Blackwood, controversially using Hardwick's private letters; Elizabeth Bishop rebuked him, writing "art just isn't worth that much." "Mills of the Kavanaughs" features Anne at her dead husband Harry's grave.

Lowell was part of the New England Lowell family (descended from James Russell Lowell, first editor of The Atlantic Monthly). He went to jail as a conscientious objector during WWII. His close friendship with Elizabeth Bishop lasted 30 years (correspondence in Words in Air, adapted into a play by Sarah Ruhl). They were introduced by Randall Jarrell. He translated Montale's "Arsenio" and adapted Hawthorne stories into plays.
