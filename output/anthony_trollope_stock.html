Summary of Facts

Anthony Trollope was a prolific Victorian novelist who wrote 47 novels. He is best known for the Chronicles of Barsetshire and the Palliser parliamentary novels.

The Warden, the first Barsetshire novel, follows Septimus Harding, warden of Hiram's Hospital, who is challenged by the young reformer John Bold over his 800-pound income. Bold enlists the help of The Jupiter newspaper's editor Tom Towers, and the Attorney General Sir Abraham Haphazard becomes involved. Harding, who plays an imaginary violoncello when stressed, ultimately resigns. The novel includes parodies of Thomas Carlyle (Dr. Pessimist Anticant) and Charles Dickens (Mr. Popular Sentiment).

Barchester Towers, the second and most famous Barsetshire novel, begins after Bishop Grantly's death when the Low Church Dr. Proudie is controversially appointed bishop. His domineering wife Mrs. Proudie and the slimy chaplain Obadiah Slope, who delivers an unpopular sermon against chanting, become central antagonists. Eleanor Bold (the Warden's daughter) is wooed by three men — Bertie Stanhope, Mr. Slope, and Mr. Arabin. She slaps Slope when he proposes, and ultimately marries Arabin, who is appointed dean. La Signora Madeline Vesey Neroni, crippled yet seductive, flirts with men while lying on her couch; her father Vesey Stanhope is summoned from Italy where he spent twelve years collecting butterflies on the pretense of a sore throat. At a party at Ullathorne Court, two men propose to Eleanor. Mr. Quiverful, who lives in dilapidated Puddingdale with 14 children, becomes the new warden. The Grantly family lives at Plumstead Episcopi. Near the end, the narrator quotes 'all the world's a stage' while bemoaning the difficulty of writing endings.

Other Barsetshire novels include The Small House at Allington, Framley Parsonage, Doctor Thorne, and The Last Chronicle of Barset (in which Rev. Josiah Crawley is blamed for stealing a check worth 20 pounds).

The Way We Live Now, his longest novel, is a sweeping satire of London corruption. The financier Augustus Melmotte partners with Hamilton Fisker to sell shares in a fake railroad from Salt Lake City to Veracruz. Felix Carbury pursues Melmotte's daughter Marie to pay off his mother Lady Carbury's debts. Lady Carbury, inspired by the author's mother Fanny Trollope's Domestic Manners of the Americans, writes letters begging editors to publish. Melmotte donates to both Catholic and Protestant causes to avoid splitting votes, is attacked by the Evening Pulpit newspaper, and hosts an awkward dinner for the Emperor of China. The upstart lawyer Squercum is hated by the firm Slow and Bideawhile. John Crumb beats Felix at the Bear-Garden and becomes engaged to Ruby Ruggles. Paul Montague ends up with Hetta. Melmotte eventually kills himself by drinking prussic acid. Trollope reflected: 'dishonesty, if it can become splendid, will cease to be abominable.'

The Palliser parliamentary novels feature Plantagenet Palliser, who becomes Prime Minister. The Eustace Diamonds (third in the series) follows Lizzie Greystock, who pursues Lord Fawn and Lord George Carruthers after her husband Sir Florian's death; Mr. Camperdown tries to retrieve the family heirloom diamonds, and Lizzie is disgraced after their theft. Phineas Redux features the discovery that Emilius possessed a duplicate key, allowing the protagonist to be acquitted of Mr. Bonteen's murder.

Trollope worked at the General Post Office for over 30 years and is credited with introducing freestanding mailboxes to England; he frequently feuded with Rowland Hill. He resigned to stand as the Liberal candidate for Beverley in Yorkshire. He befriended William Makepeace Thackeray and later wrote a biography of him. His mother Frances (Fanny) Trollope wrote the travel book Domestic Manners of the Americans. Other works include He Knew He Was Right (about Louis Trevelyan's suspicions regarding his wife Emily Rowley) and The American Senator (featuring the incredulously named Elias Gotobed).
