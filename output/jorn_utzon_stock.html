Danish architect (1918–2008), best known for the Sydney Opera House. Practitioner of 'additive architecture' — buildings as the sum of repeating constitutive elements inspired by nature. Other major works: Kuwait National Assembly Building (designed with son Jan; inspired by Isfahan; set on fire by retreating Iraqi troops) and Bagsvaerd Church (all-white interior, vaulted ceiling inspired by Chinese pagoda roof). Won the Pritzker Prize in 2003.
Sydney Opera House

Located on Bennelong Point (also called Bennelong Point or Bellegong Point) on Sydney Harbor, Australia. Roof consists of fourteen interlocking sail-like white shells (vaulted concrete ribs), which were cast as sections of a sphere — the 'spherical solution,' apocryphally inspired by peeling an orange. Engineered by Ove Arup and Co. (14 years to complete). Massive cost overruns: ran 1300% over budget. Utzon resigned in 1966 before completion; Peter Hall oversaw completion. At the 1973 opening, Utzon was neither invited nor mentioned. Contains the Joan Sutherland Theater (formerly the Opera Theater), a concert hall, drama theater, playhouse, and a 40-foot woolen tapestry by Le Corbusier (originally commissioned by Utzon, later scrapped by Hall but eventually acquired). A room containing the tapestry was later renamed for Utzon after the government resolved their dispute. The design was famously pulled from a pile of rejected submissions by juror Eero Saarinen. Contains more than a million white tiles on the outer shells.

Kuwait National Assembly Building

Designed by Utzon in collaboration with his son Jan. Combines a flat-roofed mosque with a public square topped by an inclined roof rising toward the Persian Gulf. Inspired by a visit to Isfahan (the bazaars). Set on fire by retreating Iraqi troops during the Gulf War. Central hall flanked by rooftops made of curving semi-cylindrical columns. Described as having a flowing roof like the motion of fabric.

General / Biographical

Danish architect Jorn Utzon (1918–2008). Devised 'additive architecture': buildings as the sum total of their constitutive repeating elements, inspired by nature's growth principles. Designed the L-shaped Kingo Houses (courtyard houses along cul-de-sacs in Denmark) — which inspired Bjarke Ingels's Mountain Dwellings. In 1982 received an award from the Finnish Association of Architects named for Alvar Aalto. Won the Pritzker Prize in 2003. Designed the Svaneke Water Tower (a spiral staircase ascends to the body of a water tank). An unrealized museum meant to house works of a COBRA founding member was explored in the 2016 exhibition FATAMORGANA.

Summary of Facts

Jorn Utzon (1918–2008) was a Danish architect best known for the Sydney Opera House and a proponent of 'additive architecture' — the idea that buildings are the sum total of their constitutive repeating elements, modeled on nature's growth principles.

The Sydney Opera House is Utzon's defining work. Situated on Bennelong Point on Sydney Harbor, its roof consists of fourteen interlocking sail-like white shells (vaulted concrete ribs) cast as sections of a sphere — a 'spherical solution' apocryphally inspired by peeling an orange. The project was engineered by Ove Arup and Co. over 14 years, ran 1300% over budget, and involved massive cost overruns. Utzon's design was famously rescued from a pile of rejected submissions by juror Eero Saarinen, who insisted the other judges accept it. Utzon resigned from the project in 1966 due to conflicts with the New South Wales government; Peter Hall oversaw completion. At the 1973 opening, Utzon was neither invited nor mentioned. The exterior shells are covered with more than a million white tiles; 100-meter-wide stairs lead to the main entrance. The building contains the Joan Sutherland Theater (formerly the Opera Theater), a concert hall, drama theater, and playhouse. Le Corbusier was commissioned to create a tapestry for the building; one room was eventually renamed for Utzon after the government and architect resolved their dispute.

Utzon also designed the Kuwait National Assembly Building in collaboration with his son Jan Utzon. The building combines a flat-roofed mosque with a public square topped by an inclined roof rising toward the Persian Gulf, inspired by Utzon's visit to the bazaars of Isfahan. The building was set on fire by retreating Iraqi troops during the Gulf War. His Bagsvaerd Church in Denmark features an all-white, naturally lit interior with a vaulted ceiling of reinforced concrete shells buttressed by double columns — its interior form is said to be inspired by a Chinese pagoda roof or a line of clouds. Architecture critic Kenneth Frampton cited the Bagsvaerd Church to introduce the concept of 'Critical Regionalism.' Utzon's L-shaped Kingo Houses — courtyard dwellings along cul-de-sacs in Denmark — later inspired Bjarke Ingels's Mountain Dwellings project. In 1982, Utzon received an award from the Finnish Association of Architects named for Alvar Aalto. He won the Pritzker Architecture Prize in 2003.
