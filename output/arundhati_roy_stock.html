Indian author and political activist. Won the Booker Prize for her debut novel The God of Small Things (1997), about the twins Estha and Rahel in Kerala. Her second novel, The Ministry of Utmost Happiness (2017), features the hijra Anjum. Also known for political essays including 'The End of Imagination' (against nuclear testing), 'The Algebra of Infinite Justice' (against the Iraq War), and 'Walking with the Comrades' (about Maoist insurgents).
The God of Small Things

Roy's debut novel (1997), winner of the Booker Prize. Set primarily in Ayemenem, Kerala. Follows the fraternal twins Estha and Rahel, who commit incest at the end of the novel. Their mother Ammu has an affair with Velutha, a Dalit (Untouchable) Communist worker at the family's pickle factory, Paradise Pickles and Preserves, owned by Mammachi. Baby Kochamma, whose unrequited love for Father Mulligan caused her to convert to Catholicism, blames Sophie Mol's drowning on Velutha, who is beaten to death by police led by Thomas Mathew. Estha is molested by the Orangedrink Lemondrink Man during a screening of The Sound of Music. Estha becomes mute after being 'returned' to his father in Kolkata. The factory illegally manufactures banana jam. An Englishman's abandoned home is called the History House. The novel uses the epigraph 'Never again will a single story be told as though it's the only one' from John Berger. Wikipedia

The Ministry of Utmost Happiness

Roy's second novel, published in 2017, twenty years after her debut. Features the hijra (transgender/intersex woman) Anjum, who witnesses a massacre of Hindu pilgrims in Gujarat. Anjum follows Bombay Silk to Khwabgah and later constructs the Jannat Guest House (named after the Urdu word for Paradise) in a graveyard in Delhi. Also features the architecture student Tilottama (Tilo) and the character nicknamed 'Commander Gulrez,' who fathers one of two girls named Miss Jebeen. The ideas 'It's Best to be Prepared' and 'Anything can Happen to Anyone' are called one character's Two Thoughts, with capital T's. A fashion designer marries a guest named Saddam Hussein. A major murders a human rights activist. Musa meets his wife in a stationary shop where there is a grenade explosion. Addresses the Kashmiri insurgency and anti-Muslim riots in Gujarat. Wikipedia

Essays and Nonfiction

Roy is known for her political essays and activism. 'The End of Imagination' is a broadside against India's nuclear testing (Pokharan test). 'The Algebra of Infinite Justice' is a collection; the title essay criticized the American response to 9/11 and the invasion of Afghanistan. She attacked Gandhi as a 'Saint of the Status-Quo' for not sufficiently renouncing the caste system. She wrote 'Walking with the Comrades' about marching with Maoist insurgents. She donated her Booker Prize money to fight dam construction by funding Narmada Bachao Andolan. She declared 'another world is not only possible, she is on her way' at the 2003 World Social Forum.

Summary of Facts

Arundhati Roy is an Indian author and political activist from Kerala. She is best known for her debut novel The God of Small Things (1997), which won the Booker Prize. The novel is set primarily in Ayemenem, Kerala, and follows the fraternal twins Estha and Rahel, who commit incest at the end of the novel. Their mother Ammu transgresses the 'Love Laws' by having an affair with Velutha, a Dalit (Untouchable) Communist worker at the family's pickle factory, Paradise Pickles and Preserves, which is owned by Mammachi and illegally manufactures banana jam. Baby Kochamma, whose unrequited love for Father Mulligan caused her to convert to Catholicism, blames the drowning of Sophie Mol on Velutha, who is beaten to death by police led by Thomas Mathew. Estha is molested by the Orangedrink Lemondrink Man during a screening of The Sound of Music and later becomes mute after being 'returned' to his father in Kolkata. Estha calls Rahel a 'Refugee Stick Insect' while she calls him 'Elvis the Pelvis.' A planned college performance of Norman, Is That You? is cancelled due to a political assassination. An Englishman's abandoned home is called the History House. Baby Kochamma is forced to wave a Communist flag by a protestor. The novel uses the epigraph 'Never again will a single story be told as though it's the only one' from collaborator John Berger.

Roy's second novel, The Ministry of Utmost Happiness (2017), was published twenty years after her debut. It features the hijra (transgender/intersex woman) Anjum, who follows Bombay Silk to Khwabgah and later constructs the Jannat Guest House, named after the Urdu word for Paradise, in a graveyard in Delhi. Anjum witnesses a massacre of Hindu pilgrims in Gujarat. The novel also features the architecture student Tilottama (Tilo). A man nicknamed 'Commander Gulrez' fathers one of two girls named Miss Jebeen. The ideas 'It's Best to be Prepared' and 'Anything can Happen to Anyone' are called one character's Two Thoughts. A fashion designer marries a guest named Saddam Hussein. Musa meets his wife in a stationary shop where there is a grenade explosion. A major murders a human rights activist and flees to the United States claiming to be a victim of torture. Attackers are described as 'saffron parakeets.' The novel addresses the Kashmiri insurgency and the anti-Muslim riots in Gujarat in 2002.

Roy is also known for her political essays. 'The End of Imagination' is a broadside against India's nuclear testing at Pokharan, opening with one government saying 'The desert shook' and another replying 'The whole mountain turned white.' The Algebra of Infinite Justice is a collection whose title essay criticized the Bush administration's response to 9/11; Roy correctly forecast that the invasion of Afghanistan would spread 'more terror across the world' in a September 2001 Guardian piece. She attacked Gandhi as a 'Saint of the Status-Quo' who did not sufficiently renounce the caste system. She wrote 'Walking with the Comrades' about marching through the forest with Maoist insurgents. She donated her Booker Prize money to fight dam construction by funding the group Narmada Bachao Andolan. She declared 'another world is not only possible, she is on her way' at the 2003 World Social Forum on 'Confronting Empire.'

Biographically, Roy calls herself the 'twin' of Uruguayan author Eduardo Galeano, and she once turned down an offer to model khakis for The Gap. She recalled passing a friend a napkin inscribed with 'the only dream worth having,' which is 'to love. To be loved. To never forget your own insignificance.' Her two novels and twenty years of political advocacy between them are frequently noted.
