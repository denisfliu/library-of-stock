Summary of Facts

Annibale Carracci was an Italian Baroque painter from Bologna who led the Bolognese school alongside his cousin Ludovico and brother Agostino. His most frequently clued work is the Loves of the Gods, a fresco cycle on the ceiling of the Farnese Gallery in the Palazzo Farnese, depicting scenes from Ovid's Metamorphoses. This cycle is notable as an early example of the illusionistic quadri riportati technique, and its center panel is The Triumph of Bacchus and Ariadne. He also painted The Choice of Hercules for the camerino of the same building.

Carracci's early genre paintings are also heavily clued. The Butcher's Shop depicts men posed in front of slabs of meat and is grouped with The Beaneater as early examples of genre painting in Italy. C. D. Dickerson examined The Butcher's Shop in his book Raw Painting. His 1580 painting A Boy Drinking was stolen from Christ Church Picture Gallery in 2020.

His Landscape with the Flight into Egypt is a semicircular lunette painting in the Doria Pamphilj gallery, commissioned by Pietro Aldobrandini, showing a ferryman crossing a river next to the small figures of Joseph and Mary. A red cloak is draped over the back of a donkey in this work.

Carracci is also credited with inventing the caricature, reportedly saying that the task of creating caricatures was exactly the same as the task of the classical artist. A member of the Carracci family famously called Giorgio Vasari a 'shitface' in the margins of his copy of Vasari's Lives. Carracci's painting of Luxuria (Vice) with her back to the viewer was borrowed as a pose by Caravaggio for the violin-playing angel in a painting for the Palazzo Doria Pamphilj.
