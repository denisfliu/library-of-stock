Summary of Facts

Jacques Rivette (1928–2016) was a French New Wave director and film critic associated with Cahiers du Cinéma. As a critic, he is famous for a 1961 essay condemning Gillo Pontecorvo's film Kapo, in which he wrote that he had the 'most profound contempt' for any filmmaker who would tilt the camera up to linger on Emmanuelle Riva's hand as her character dies against a barbed wire fence — a critique of the aestheticization of atrocity. A quote linking camera movements to morality is often misattributed to Godard but was actually by Luc Moullet, cited by Rivette. He also led a putsch against Eric Rohmer to become head editor of Cahiers du Cinéma, subsequently taking it in a Marxist direction, and wrote 'The Genius of Howard Hawks.' He infamously called Showgirls 'one of the great American films of the last few years.'

Rivette's most celebrated film is Celine and Julie Go Boating (Céline et Julie vont en bateau, 1974). Two women — Celine (Juliet Berto) and Julie (Dominique Labourier) — repeatedly visit a mysterious haunted house at 7 bis, rue du Nadir-aux-Pommes, each time leaving with no memory of what happened inside and a hard candy mysteriously in their mouths. They suck on the candies to recapture their memories of what occurred. The film opens with Julie dropping her scarf in a Parisian park, like the White Rabbit from Alice in Wonderland, and Celine chasing her through Paris (including onto a tram) for 15 minutes. The two actresses moved in together during production and recalled their dreams to each other to prepare for their roles. The film alludes to a theater called 'The House of Fiction.'

Out 1 (1971, full title: Out 1: Noli Me Tangere) is a 13-hour film inspired by Balzac's Human Comedy, whose first episode is titled 'From Lili to Thomas.' It used 16mm film for the TV crew footage of a theater company and 35mm film for the crew themselves — Rivette's first film with extensive improvisation. It was first publicly shown in the U.S. at the Museum of the Moving Image in 2006, 35 years after its premiere. L'amour fou (1969) depicts the breakdown of the relationship between Sébastien, who directs rehearsals of Racine's Andromaque, and his wife Claire (playing Hermione), who is overwhelmed by the presence of a TV crew filming the rehearsals. La Belle Noiseuse (1991) is a four-hour film based on Balzac's 'The Unknown Masterpiece' from the Human Comedy — the story of the master painter Frenhofer who destroys his unfinished masterpiece. Le Pont du Nord (1982) features a dragon sculpture in Paris's Parc de la Villette.
