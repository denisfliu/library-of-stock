A 14th-century Ming Dynasty novel attributed to Luo Guanzhong, one of the Four Great Classical Novels of China. It details the conflict between the states of Wei, Shu, and Wu after the fall of the Han Dynasty during the Three Kingdoms period. The novel's protagonist is Liu Bei, founder of Shu, with Cao Cao serving as the primary antagonist. Major events include the Oath of the Peach Garden, the Battle of Red Cliffs, and the stratagems of Zhuge Liang (the 'Sleeping Dragon' or 'Crouching Dragon'). The most common version was compiled in 1679 with an opening line about the cyclical nature of empires. A proverb states that the old should not read this book.
Oath of the Peach Garden

The foundational scene of the novel in which Liu Bei, Guan Yu, and Zhang Fei swear brotherhood. They meet at a tavern, bring a black bull and white horse to the peach garden, and vow to die 'on the same day, in the same month, in the same year.' This occurs during the Yellow Turban Rebellion at the novel's opening. The oath is the most frequently referenced single event from the novel in quizbowl. Wikipedia

Zhuge Liang's Stratagems

Zhuge Liang, nicknamed 'Sleeping Dragon' or 'Crouching Dragon,' is the most prominent strategist in the novel. He is recruited after Liu Bei visits his thatched cottage three times. His exploits include the empty fort strategy (playing a guqin at Xicheng with only 100 soldiers), the seven captures of Meng Huo, the Stone Sentinel Maze, praying to the Big Dipper at the Wuzhang Plains, and having a wooden statue of himself made to scare his rival after death. Cao Cao's Deeds

Cao Cao serves as the primary antagonist. He is depicted as cunning and ruthless. He claims to enjoy killing in his sleep (and kills a servant to prove it), sets 'chicken ribs' as a password, slaughters a household after a misunderstanding about cooking a pig, and tells Liu Bei they are the only two real heroes (causing Liu Bei to pretend to be scared by lightning). He nearly succeeds in assassinating the tyrant Dong Zhuo early in the novel. Wikipedia

Liu Bei's Character

Liu Bei is the novel's protagonist, founder of Shu. He is known for benevolent leadership — but also for extreme gestures such as throwing his infant son to the ground after Zhao Yun risks his life to save the baby. He claims to enjoy his new life too much to think of home (the 'lezhong bu si shu' incident). A hunter once serves him the flesh of his own wife, claiming it was wolf meat.

Summary of Facts

Romance of the Three Kingdoms is a 14th-century Ming Dynasty novel attributed to Luo Guanzhong, and one of the Four Great Classical Novels of China. It chronicles the conflict between the states of Wei, Shu, and Wu following the collapse of the Han Dynasty. The most commonly read version was compiled in 1679, which added the famous opening line about the cyclical nature of empires: 'what is long divided must unite; what is long united must divide.' The novel begins with the Yellow Turban Rebellion led by Zhang Jue and ends with Sima Yan establishing the Jin Dynasty.

The protagonist is Liu Bei, founder of Shu, whose sworn brotherhood with Guan Yu and Zhang Fei in the Oath of the Peach Garden is the novel's most iconic scene. They meet at a tavern, bring a black bull and white horse to the peach garden, and vow to die on the same day. The primary antagonist is Cao Cao of Wei, characterized as ruthless and cunning — he claims to enjoy killing in his sleep and proves it by killing a servant, sets 'chicken ribs' as a password, slaughters a household over a misunderstanding about pig-cooking, and tells Liu Bei they are the only two real heroes (causing Liu Bei to feign fright from lightning). Cao Cao nearly assassinates the tyrant Dong Zhuo early in the novel.

The novel's strategic centerpiece is the Battle of Red Cliffs, where Zhuge Liang borrows arrows using straw boats on a foggy night, summons the southeast wind for a fire attack, and the Young Phoenix (Pang Tong) tricks Cao Cao into chaining his ships. Guan Yu later allows Cao Cao to escape. Zhuge Liang, nicknamed Sleeping Dragon or Crouching Dragon, is recruited after Liu Bei visits his thatched cottage three times. His other exploits include the empty fort strategy at Xicheng (bluffing Sima Yi by playing a guqin with only 100 soldiers), the seven captures and releases of the barbarian leader Meng Huo, the Stone Sentinel Maze, praying to the Big Dipper at the Wuzhang Plains, and having a wooden statue made of himself to frighten his rival after death.

Notable episodes include Liu Bei throwing his infant son to the ground after Zhao Yun risked his life to save the baby, Liu Bei claiming to enjoy his new life too much to think of home, a hunter serving Liu Bei the flesh of his own wife while claiming it was wolf meat, Guan Yu defeating six generals to pass through five passes, and Guan Yu playing Go while being treated for a poison arrow wound. Pang Tong is killed at the Valley of the Fallen Phoenix after switching horses with his master. The Red Hare horse is given to Lu Bu by Dong Zhuo, and Dong Zhuo is later killed by Lu Bu over the beauty Diao Chan.

A Chinese proverb states that the young should not read Water Margin and the old should not read this book. Luo Guanzhong is also credited with editing or co-authoring Water Margin. Lu Xun was inspired by this novel; the protagonist of his story 'Storm in a Teacup' obsessively rereads it. In Akutagawa's 'The Nose,' Father Zenchi compares himself to Liu Bei. The novel is also referenced extensively in the Magic the Gathering Portal: Three Kingdoms card set.
