[B1] 2024 COOT (2024) | Diff: 8 | Fine Arts
     Leadin: One version of this scene was stolen and relocated to Danzig, causing a lawsuit to be placed against the Hanseatic ("han-zee-AT-ick") League. For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: The Last Judgement (The versions are by Memling and Michelangelo.)):
    Name this scene. In the aforementioned version, the donor sits on the right-hand scale held by Saint Michael in the foreground. Another version of this scene represents the critic Biagio de Cesena ("B'YA-joh de cheh-ZAY-nah") as a donkey-eared King Minos and the artist as St. Bartholomew.
  Part 2 (ans: Petrus Christus ("KRIS-tus") [or Pieter Christus]):
    Hans Memling's time as the leading painter of Bruges ("broozh") was shared with this other artist, who pioneered the use of perspective in Early Netherlandish painting and produced Portrait of a Carthusian ("car-THOO-zee-uhn").
  Part 3 (ans: Jan van Eyck ("van IKE")):
    Memling and Christus rose to prominence after the death of this possible inventor of oil painting, whose own works include the Ghent Altarpiece and the Arnolfini ("ar-noll-FEE-nee") Wedding.

[B2] 2022 ARCADIA (2022) | Diff: 8 | Fine Arts
     Leadin: One of these animals appears to sit on the frame of a Portrait of a Carthusian in an example of an early Renaissance trompe l'oeil ("tromp LOY") trick of depicting them. For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: flies [accept houseflies; accept musca depicta]):
    Name these animals that represented sin in paintings. According to Vasari, Giotto ("JO-toh") would fool his teacher Cimabue ("chee-mah-BOO-ay") by painting these animals hyper-realistically onto portraits by him.
  Part 2 (ans: Petrus Christus):
    The realistic fly in the Portrait of a Carthusian was painted by this artist, who may have inherited Van Eyck's workshop and introduced geometric perspective to Flemish painting. He also painted A Goldsmith in His Shop.
  Part 3 (ans: still life):
    A frequent interpretation of the fly is as a moralizing reminder of death, like the vanitas skull paintings in this genre. Dutch artists painted flies and other insects in works in this genre that depict fruits and flowers.

  Part 1 (ans: Petrus Christus):
    Identify this Netherlandish artist who brought geometric perspective and the vanishing point to Northern Europe with his Virgin Enthroned with Saints Jerome and Francis. This artist of Portrait of a Carthusian was Bruges' leading painter, along with Memling, in the generation after van Eyck.
  Part 2 (ans: Gerard David):
    The medieval craze for the Veil of Veronica, or Holy Face, led many other artists to paint small devotional portraits of Christ, such as this Flemish artist whose Judgment of Cambyses was one of the first Northern Renaissance paintings to include Italian-style putti.
  Part 3 (ans: Albrecht Durer):
    This later artist's Munich Self-Portrait is a riff on the Holy Face motif. This German artist of Praying Hands and The Four Apostles holds a thistle in an early self-portrait.

[B4] 2011 ACF Nationals (2011) | Diff: 9 | Fine Arts
     Leadin: This artist painted portraits of Edward Grimston and a young girl wearing a blue headdress with a strap of cloth under her chin. For 10 points each:
  Part 1 (ans: Petrus Christus):
    Name this Early Netherlandish artist who possibly depicted St. Eligius in his painting A Goldsmith in His Shop. He is often considered a pupil of Jan van Eyck, and dominated painting in Bruges after van Eyck's death.
  Part 2 (ans: trompe l'oeil):
    The fly Christus painted at the bottom of his Portrait of a Carthusian is an early example of this technique, in which an object is depicted so realistically that it appears to be three-dimensional. Its name is French for "fool the eye."
  Part 3 (ans: Rogier van der Weyden):
    The swooning position of Mary in Christus's Lamentation echoes this earlier painter's Deposition, which was commissioned by a guild of crossbowmen. His panels depicting the Justice of Herkenbald and the Justice of Trajan for the town hall of Brussels were much admired until they were destroyed in 1695.
