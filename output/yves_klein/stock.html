French experimental artist and judo instructor, co-founder of Nouveau Realisme with critic Pierre Restany. Known for International Klein Blue (IKB), Anthropometries (nude models as 'living brushes'), The Void exhibition at the Iris Clert Gallery, Leap into the Void photograph, monochrome and monogold paintings, fire paintings, and the Zone of Immaterial Pictorial Sensibility. Also a Rosicrucian. Died of a heart attack after seeing Mondo Cane.

Summary of Facts

Yves Klein was a French experimental artist, judo instructor, and co-founder of Nouveau Realisme (New Realism) alongside the art critic Pierre Restany, who coined the term and edited the art magazine Domus. Restany referred to Nouveau Realisme as an answer to Fluxism and Pop Art. The movement's manifesto was signed in Klein's apartment, written in white cursive on blue canvas, and advocated a return to reality and departure from abstract themes. Members included Christo and Jeanne-Claude, Jean Tinguely, Niki de Saint-Phalle, and Arman. Klein was an avid judo practitioner and had a fascination with Rosicrucianism that led to his obsession with emptiness and the void.

His most frequently clued works are the Anthropometries, in which nude female models ('living brushes') covered themselves in his signature International Klein Blue (IKB) paint and rolled or pressed on canvases, leaving imprints on the wall. A 20-minute making-of film is accompanied by the 'Monotone Silence Symphony,' a single D-flat chord followed by silence. The Anthropometries were controversially described as 'performance art.' Rachel Lachowicz later appropriated the Anthropometries using lipstick instead of paint. Klein's The Void (Le Vide) exhibition at the Iris Clert Gallery in 1958 consisted of an empty white gallery with only an empty display cabinet; 3,000 visitors were served blue cocktails, and 1,001 balloons were released. Brian O'Doherty's influential 'white cube' essays discuss The Void as an example of presenting the gallery itself as art. Arman later filled the same gallery with trash in his response piece The Full (Le Plein).

Klein's Leap into the Void is a doctored photograph (also called a photomontage) by Harry Shunk and Jean Kender showing him in a black suit apparently jumping from a second-story rooftop, with a bicyclist visible in the background. It was collected in his artbook Dimanche. His painting People Begin to Fly, depicting silhouettes in blue, echoes the Leap photograph.

International Klein Blue is his namesake shade of deep synthetic ultramarine, used across hundreds of monochrome paintings and spongy canvases. The Proposte monocrome exhibition at the Galleria Apollinaire in Milan featured eleven identical, differently priced blue canvases, which inspired Piero Manzoni's white Achromes. Derek Jarman's experimental 1993 AIDS film Blue approximated IKB; the film's narrators include Nigel Terry and Tilda Swinton. Klein also applied the pigment to headless plaster casts of the Venus de Milo, which appears 28 times in Dali's The Hallucinogenic Toreador.

In his Zone of Immaterial Pictorial Sensibility, he sold certificates of ownership of empty space for gold, then threw the gold dust into the Seine and burned the receipt. His Monogold series consisted of panels using gold with the 'mono-' prefix. Other works include fire paintings (asbestos cardboard burned with a blowtorch/flamethrower) and the Cosmogonies series (canvases fastened to his Citroen car and exposed to the elements). The Monotone Symphony — a single long chord followed by silence — served as background music for both IKB exhibitions and Anthropometries performances. He theorized the void in his Chelsea Hotel Manifesto, which states that 'birds must be eliminated.' Klein died of a heart attack the night he watched Mondo Cane, which featured a parodic recreation of his Anthropometries.
