E. T. A. Hoffmann

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
German Romantic author best known for the short story 'The Sandman,' in which Nathanael falls in love with the automaton Olympia created by Coppelius. Freud's essay 'The Uncanny' analyzes 'The Sandman.' Also wrote 'The Nutcracker and the Mouse King' (adapted by Tchaikovsky via Dumas's translation), 'The Golden Pot,' 'Mademoiselle de Scuderi,' The Life and Opinions of Tomcat Murr (featuring alter ego Johannes Kreisler), and The Devil's Elixirs. His 'Tales' were adapted into the opera Les contes d'Hoffmann by Jacques Offenbach. Changed his middle name to 'Amadeus' in honor of Mozart. Also a music critic who praised Beethoven as 'purely romantic.'
"The Sandman"

Hoffmann's most frequently clued short story. The protagonist Nathanael associates the lawyer Coppelius/Coppola with the mythical Sandman who steals children's eyes. A telescope/barometer salesman (Coppola) advertises 'pretty eyes, pretty eyes.' Nathanael falls in love with Olympia, the automaton daughter of Professor Spalanzani, who can only say 'Ah, ah!' He strangles Spalanzani after discovering Olympia is an automaton. Nathanael's fiancee Clara asks him to burn a 'frightful' poem, and he calls her 'accursed, inanimate.' At the climax, Nathanael shrieks 'Circle of fire! Spin round! Spin round!' while holding Clara over a ledge, before jumping off a bell-tower to his death. As a child, Coppelius nearly blinded Nathanael with grains of fire. Clara is saved by her brother Lothair. Included in the collection Night Pieces. Analyzed in Freud's 'The Uncanny' and Ernst Jentsch's essay on 'intellectual uncertainty.' Inspired Delibes's ballet Coppelia. Wikipedia

The Tales of Hoffmann (Les contes d'Hoffmann)

An opera by Jacques Offenbach based on Hoffmann's stories, in which Hoffmann appears as a tenor character recounting his three great loves: Olympia, Antonia, and Giulietta, who are all facets of the prima donna Stella. The frame story takes place during a performance of Don Giovanni in a Nuremberg tavern. Four villains are sung by one bass-baritone (including Dapertutto and Dr. Miracle). The famous barcarolle 'Belle nuit, o nuit d'amour' opens the Venetian act with Giulietta and Nicklausse. The 'Doll Song' ('Les oiseaux dans la charmille') is a coloratura aria sung by Olympia, who must be periodically rewound. Dr. Miracle makes a portrait of Antonia's dead mother urge her to sing until she dies. Dapertutto offers Giulietta a diamond to steal Hoffmann's reflection. Nicklausse reveals himself to be the Muse at the end. The 'Legend of Kleinzach' describes a hunchbacked dwarf. The servant Frantz sings about his lack of talent. The 1951 Powell-Pressburger film adaptation changed a character from soprano to ballerina. Wikipedia

The Life and Opinions of Tomcat Murr

A novel in which a highly intelligent tomcat named Murr writes his memoirs, but a printer's error causes pages of a biography of the composer/Kapellmeister Johannes Kreisler (Hoffmann's alter ego) to be spliced into the text. Murr lives with the alchemist Master Abraham. Introduces Kreisler, whom Hoffmann also used in his music criticism (collected as Kreisleriana). Wikipedia

"The Golden Pot"

A fairy tale in twelve 'vigils.' A student named Anselmus crashes into an applemonger who punishes him by making him spill ink on Archivist Lindhorst's Arabic manuscripts; he is then imprisoned in a crystal bottle. In the last vigil, Anselmus is overjoyed to see a lily grow out of the title object. The protagonist stops believing in the supernatural and hears the speech of birds as mere chatter. Parallels Goethe's 'Marchen' (snake and lily motif). Influenced Gogol. Wikipedia

"Mademoiselle de Scuderi"

A novella often cited (along with Poe) as one of the first modern detective stories. The protagonist, based on the real French writer Madeleine de Scudery, solves the mystery surrounding the death of the goldsmith Rene Cardillac. Olivier is accused of murdering a goldsmith who hates parting with his jewelry. The protagonist receives a jewelry box with the couplet 'a lover who is afraid of thieves / is not worthy of love.' Other Works and Stories

Several other works appear in clues. 'Ritter Gluck' features a strange man from the Tiergarten who sings from blank pages and claims to be a dead composer; Hoffmann changed his name to 'Amadeus' for its pseudonym. 'The Mines of Falun' depicts the discovery of Fet-Mats in a mine (compared to Hebel's 'Unexpected Reunion'). Die Serapionsbruder is a Decameron-esque collection incorporating friends like Theodor von Hippel and David Koreff, outlining the 'Serapion principle.' 'A New Year's Eve Adventure' features Erasmus Spikher bartering away his reflection, alluding to Chamisso's Peter Schlemihl. 'The Talking Turk' centers on a prophetic machine. Councillor Krespel forbids his daughter Antonia from singing due to lung risk; inspired Act II of The Tales of Hoffmann. Undine is a German-language opera based on Fouque's fairy tale.

General / Biographical / Music Criticism

Hoffmann is identified as a German Romantic author. He was also a composer and music critic. His music criticism, collected as Kreisleriana, declared 'Music is the most romantic of all the arts.' He praised Beethoven as a 'purely romantic composer,' especially enthusiastic about the Fifth Symphony's development from its simple opening. His reviews appeared in the Allgemeine musikalische Zeitung in Leipzig. He changed his middle name from Wilhelm to Amadeus. He influenced Russian literature, including Gogol and the Soviet 'Serapion Brothers' circle. Tarkovsky wrote a never-filmed screenplay based on his work.

Summary of Facts

E. T. A. Hoffmann (Ernst Theodor Amadeus Hoffmann) is a German Romantic author whose works straddle literature, music, and opera. He is most frequently clued for 'The Sandman' and for The Tales of Hoffmann, the opera by Jacques Offenbach based on his stories.

'The Sandman' is Hoffmann's single most important literary work for quiz purposes. The protagonist Nathanael associates the lawyer Coppelius (who also appears as the barometer/telescope salesman Coppola, advertising 'pretty eyes, pretty eyes') with the mythical Sandman who steals children's eyes. Nathanael falls in love with Olympia, the automaton daughter of Professor Spalanzani, who can only say 'Ah, ah!' He strangles Spalanzani upon discovering Olympia is an automaton. Nathanael's fiancee Clara asks him to burn a 'frightful' poem, and he calls her 'accursed, inanimate.' At the climax, Nathanael shrieks 'Circle of fire! Spin round! Spin round!' while holding Clara over a ledge, then jumps off a bell-tower to his death; Clara is saved by her brother Lothair. Sigmund Freud's essay 'The Uncanny' (Das Unheimliche) analyzes 'The Sandman' extensively, arguing that castration anxiety underlies the eye-stealing motif. Ernst Jentsch earlier analyzed the story's 'intellectual uncertainty.' The story is included in Hoffmann's collection Night Pieces and inspired Leo Delibes's ballet Coppelia.

The Tales of Hoffmann (Les contes d'Hoffmann) by Offenbach is equally prominent but from the Fine Arts side. The opera's frame story places Hoffmann in a Nuremberg tavern during a performance of Don Giovanni, recounting his three great loves: Olympia (automaton; 'Doll Song' / 'Les oiseaux dans la charmille'), Antonia (whose dead mother's portrait makes her sing to death, caused by Dr. Miracle), and Giulietta (Venetian courtesan; Dapertutto offers her a diamond to steal Hoffmann's reflection). These three are facets of the prima donna Stella, who leaves with Councillor Lindorf at the end. Four villains are traditionally sung by one bass-baritone. The famous barcarolle 'Belle nuit, o nuit d'amour' opens the Venetian act. Nicklausse reveals himself to be the Muse at the end. The 'Legend of Kleinzach' describes a hunchbacked dwarf. The 1951 Powell-Pressburger film adaptation changed a soprano to a ballerina (Moira Shearer).

'The Nutcracker and the Mouse King' is another major work: the clockmaker Drosselmeier creates the title toy that defends Marie (Clara) from the seven-headed Mouse King. Alexandre Dumas pere's French translation was adapted by Tchaikovsky into the famous ballet.

The Life and Opinions of Tomcat Murr is a novel in which a printer's error splices the intelligent cat Murr's autobiography with pages from a biography of Kapellmeister Johannes Kreisler, Hoffmann's alter ego. Murr lives with the alchemist Master Abraham.

'The Golden Pot' is a fairy tale in twelve 'vigils' following the student Anselmus, who spills ink on Archivist Lindhorst's manuscripts and is imprisoned in a crystal bottle. In the last vigil, a lily grows from the title object. It parallels Goethe's 'Marchen' and influenced Gogol.

'Mademoiselle de Scuderi' is a novella often cited alongside Poe as one of the first detective stories. The title character (based on the real French writer) solves the mystery of goldsmith Rene Cardillac's death; Olivier is accused of murder.

The Devil's Elixirs follows the monk Medardus, driven mad by the title potion, who murders the family of Princess Aurelie and stabs her on their wedding day. Inspired by Matthew Lewis's The Monk.

Other notable works include 'Ritter Gluck' (a strange man sings from blank pages and claims to be a dead composer; for this story Hoffmann changed his name to 'Amadeus'), 'A New Year's Eve Adventure' (Erasmus Spikher barters away his reflection, alluding to Chamisso's Peter Schlemihl), 'The Mines of Falun' (discovery of Fet-Mats in a mine), Die Serapionsbruder (a collection outlining the 'Serapion principle'), 'Councillor Krespel' (who forbids his daughter Antonia from singing), and the opera Undine.

Biographically, Hoffmann was also a composer and music critic. His collected music criticism, Kreisleriana, includes his declaration that 'Music is the most romantic of all the arts.' He praised Beethoven as 'purely romantic,' especially admiring the Fifth Symphony. His reviews appeared in the Allgemeine musikalische Zeitung in Leipzig. He influenced Russian literature (Gogol, the Soviet 'Serapion Brothers'), and Andrei Tarkovsky wrote a never-filmed screenplay based on his work.
