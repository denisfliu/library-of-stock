The Capability Approach

Sen's most influential theoretical contribution, developed in the Tanner Lecture 'Equality of What?' and elaborated in collaboration with Martha Nussbaum. The capability approach measures human development not by income or goods but by what people are able to do — their 'capabilities.' Sen identified personal, social, and environmental conversion factors as limits to capability. The approach extends Rawls's concern for primary goods to 'what goods do to human beings,' and seeks to equate distributive justice with establishing a level of 'midfare' for all members of society. The capability approach underpins the UN Human Development Index (HDI), developed with Mahbub ul Haq. Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics also draws on this framework.

The Liberal Paradox (Collective Choice and Social Welfare)

Sen's liberal paradox, introduced in Collective Choice and Social Welfare, demonstrates that it is impossible to simultaneously satisfy the conditions of minimal liberalism (individual rights) and Pareto optimality. Drawing on Arrow's Impossibility Theorem, Sen showed that markets cannot be both efficient and respect freedom of choice. The paradox is illustrated with an example involving a 'lewd' and 'prude' agent making choices. This result is a foundational contribution to social choice theory and welfare economics.

Poverty and Famines / Bengal Famine Analysis

Sen's most famous work, Poverty and Famines, argued that famines are caused not by insufficient food supply but by failures in distribution — hoarding, panic buying, price gouging, and preexisting economic inequalities. His key case study was the 1943 Bengal famine, which killed 3 million people in his native West Bengal. Sen concluded that a rise in food prices, rather than a shortage of supply, caused the catastrophe. This work established the 'entitlement approach' to famine analysis. Development as Freedom extends these themes.

More Than 100 Million Women Are Missing

Sen's influential 1990 NYRB (New York Review of Books) article 'More Than 100 Million Women Are Missing' drew attention to the male-biased sex ratio in Asia, highlighting female infanticide and sex-selective practices. Emily Oster later disputed his figure of 100 million, though the article remains a landmark in gender-based demographic analysis.

The Idea of Justice

In The Idea of Justice, Sen defended his teacher John Rawls's 'justice as fairness' concept while arguing that Adam Smith's impartial spectator is a better alternative to Rawls's veil of ignorance. Sen also wrote an introduction to Smith's A Theory of Moral Sentiments, emphasizing that Smith's economic theories must be read in the context of his moral philosophy.

Summary of Facts

Amartya Sen is an Indian (Bengali) welfare and development economist who won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1998. The clues consistently identify him through three major contributions: the capability approach, the liberal paradox, and his work on famine analysis.

Sen's capability approach, introduced in the Tanner Lecture 'Equality of What?' and developed in collaboration with Martha Nussbaum, measures human development not by income or commodities but by what people are actually able to do — their capabilities and functionings. He identified personal, social, and environmental conversion factors as limits to this framework. The approach extends John Rawls's concern for primary goods to 'what goods do to human beings' and seeks to equate distributive justice with establishing a level of 'midfare.' The capability approach underpins the United Nations Human Development Index (HDI), developed with Mahbub ul Haq, and has influenced subsequent thinkers like Kate Raworth, whose Doughnut Economics draws on Sen's framework.

The liberal paradox, introduced in Collective Choice and Social Welfare, is Sen's demonstration that it is impossible to simultaneously have both minimal liberalism (individual rights) and Pareto optimality. Drawing on Arrow's Impossibility Theorem, the paradox uses the example of a 'lewd' and 'prude' agent to show that markets cannot be both efficient and respect freedom of choice. This result is a foundational contribution to social choice theory.

Sen's most famous book, Poverty and Famines, argued that famines are caused not by insufficient food supply but by failures in distribution — hoarding, panic buying, price gouging, and preexisting economic inequalities that lead to misdistribution. His key case study was the 1943 Bengal famine, which killed 3 million people in his native West Bengal. He showed that a rise in food prices, rather than a shortage of supply, caused the catastrophe. Development as Freedom extends these themes.

Sen also wrote the influential NYRB article 'More Than 100 Million Women Are Missing,' which drew attention to the male-biased sex ratio in Asia and female infanticide, though Emily Oster later disputed his figure. In The Idea of Justice, Sen defended his teacher John Rawls's 'justice as fairness' concept while arguing that Adam Smith's impartial spectator is a better alternative to the veil of ignorance. He also wrote an introduction to Smith's A Theory of Moral Sentiments.

Biographically, Sen worked with the Marxist supervisor Maurice Dobb, and together they formulated the Dobb-Sen principle regarding optimal investment strategies. He co-authored a report with Joseph Stiglitz criticizing GDP as a development measure. His 2022 memoir Home in the World discusses childhood influences including reflecting on prices at a local fish market. Other works mentioned include Rationality and Freedom and Collective Choice and Social Welfare.
