La Traviata

Opera in three acts by Giuseppe Verdi, with a libretto by Francesco Maria Piave. Based on Alexandre Dumas fils's novel La Dame aux Camélias (which has a camellia in its title). The opera was originally set 'in the era of Richelieu' at Teatro La Fenice's insistence, despite protests from Verdi, who wanted it set in the contemporary era. The premiere at La Fenice in 1853 was unsuccessful; Verdi reportedly asked afterward, 'Was the fault mine or the singers?' The title translates as 'The Fallen Woman.' The plot concerns Violetta Valéry, a Paris courtesan dying of tuberculosis, and her love affair with Alfredo Germont. His father Giorgio Germont convinces Violetta to leave Alfredo for the sake of Giorgio's daughter's reputation; Alfredo publicly humiliates Violetta at Flora's party by throwing his casino winnings at her feet; eventually they are reconciled, but Violetta dies of tuberculosis. Doctor Grenvil treats Violetta. Violetta is generally considered to be based on Marie Duplessis, who was a lover of Franz Liszt in her last year. A 2012 French documentary followed soprano Natalie Dessay as she prepared for the role. Carlos Kleiber's only studio recording of a non-German-language opera was La Traviata with Ileana Cotrubaș. Willy Decker's sparse 2005 staging featured a massive clock symbolizing the ticking down of Violetta's life. Maria Callas sang Violetta in multiple productions including one in London opposite Cesare Valletti. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Giuseppe Verdi (1813–1901), Italian opera composer. La Traviata (1853) is one of his middle-period works, alongside Il Trovatore (premiered in the same year). Verdi's opera heroines include Violetta (La Traviata), Leonora (Il Trovatore), and Lady Macbeth (Macbeth). His opera Nabucco features the famous chorus 'Va, pensiero.'

Summary of Facts

La Traviata ('The Fallen Woman') is a three-act opera by Giuseppe Verdi, with a libretto by Francesco Maria Piave based on Alexandre Dumas fils's novel La Dame aux Camélias (which takes its title from a camellia flower). It premiered at Teatro La Fenice in Venice in 1853, initially to a mixed reception; Verdi reportedly asked afterward, 'Was the fault mine or the singers?' At La Fenice's insistence — against Verdi's wishes — the original production was set in the historical era of Richelieu rather than the contemporary Paris setting the composer intended. The title character, Violetta Valéry, is widely considered to be modeled on the real-life courtesan Marie Duplessis, who was a lover of Franz Liszt in her last year.

The opera opens at a lavish Parisian party hosted by Violetta, where the young nobleman Alfredo Germont sings the brindisi (drinking song) 'Libiamo ne' lieti calici' with her after Baron Douphol refuses to give a toast. Alfredo declares his love in the Act 1 duet 'Un dì, felice, eterea' ('Love is the pulse of the universe'); Violetta demurs, offering only friendship, and gives him a camellia flower telling him to return it when it has wilted. After the guests leave, Violetta sings the introspective 'Ah, fors' è lui' (pondering if Alfredo might be 'the one') followed by the cabaletta 'Sempre libera' ('Free forever'), in which she resolves to live freely and frolic from joy to joy — while Alfredo's voice floats in offstage singing of love. A piano theme in the high strings beginning with a B minor chord represents Violetta and opens both the prelude and the third act.

In Act 2, Violetta and Alfredo have retreated to a country house. Alfredo's father Giorgio Germont arrives and sings 'Pura siccome un angelo' ('Pure as an angel'), demanding Violetta abandon Alfredo for the sake of Giorgio's daughter, whose engagement has been threatened by the scandal. Violetta reluctantly agrees; as she writes her farewell letter, a solo clarinet plays a repeated eighth-note, quarter-note, eighth-note, eighth-note-rest figure. Giorgio comforts the devastated Alfredo by singing 'Di Provenza il mar, il suol' ('The sea and soil of Provence'), invoking their ancestral home. At Flora's party, gypsy entertainers sing 'Noi siamo zingarelle' and matador entertainers perform 'E Piquillo un bel gagliardo,' about a bold matador from Spain/Biscay. Alfredo discovers Violetta professing love for Baron Douphol and throws his casino winnings at her feet in humiliation; Baron Douphol and Alfredo fight an offstage duel.

In Act 3, the dying Violetta reads a letter from Giorgio Germont — the only non-sung passage in the opera — and cries 'E tardi!' ('Too late!') as a carnival procession passes by outside. She sings the farewell aria 'Addio, del passato.' Alfredo arrives; they are reconciled. Violetta experiences a brief false recovery, saying her spasms have ceased and she feels she returns to life — and then dies. Before dying, she gives Alfredo a portrait and asks him to imagine her as his guardian angel.

The opera has a rich performance history. Carlos Kleiber's only studio recording of a non-German opera was La Traviata with Ileana Cotrubaș. A 2012 French documentary followed soprano Natalie Dessay preparing for the role. Willy Decker's 2005 staging at the Salzburg Festival used a massive clock as a central symbol of Violetta's mortality. Maria Callas sang Violetta in numerous celebrated productions. 'Sempre libera' is a classic cabaletta example in Italian opera, as is 'Di quella pira' from Verdi's Il Trovatore.
