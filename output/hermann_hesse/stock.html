German-Swiss author of philosophical and spiritual novels. Best known for Siddhartha, Steppenwolf, The Glass Bead Game, Demian, and Narcissus and Goldmund. His works explore themes of self-discovery, spiritual enlightenment, dualities between intellect and sensuality, and coming of age. Won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1946.
Steppenwolf

A novel about Harry Haller, who believes himself to be part man and part wolf. Subtitled 'For Madmen Only,' it features the Magic Theater, the musician Pablo, and the enticing Hermine. Haller reads a treatise about himself, learns he is a 'suicide' who may become an 'immortal,' and enters a hall of mirrors with doors marked 'ALL GIRLS ARE YOURS' and 'HOW ONE KILLS FOR LOVE.' Summary of Facts

Hermann Hesse was a German-Swiss author known for philosophical and spiritual novels exploring self-discovery, duality, and enlightenment. He is considered Swiss despite being born in Germany.

His most frequently clued novel is Steppenwolf, about Harry Haller, who believes himself to be part man and part wolf. The work is framed as a manuscript subtitled 'For Madmen Only.' Haller reads a treatise that calls him a 'suicide' who may become an 'immortal.' He meets the enticing Hermine and the saxophonist Pablo, who take him to the Magic Theater. In the Magic Theater, Haller steps through doors marked 'ALL GIRLS ARE YOURS' and 'HOW ONE KILLS FOR LOVE' in a hall of mirrors, witnesses Brahms and Wagner 'striving for redemption' while speaking with Mozart, and ultimately kills Hermine. He offends a right-wing nationalist professor by insulting a portrait of Goethe, and Mozart hands him a radio playing Handel. A potted araucaria standing outside his apartment contrasts with his turbulent lifestyle.

Siddhartha traces the spiritual journey of a man seeking enlightenment contemporaneous with Gautama Buddha. He abandons his home with his friend Govinda, works with the merchant Kamaswami, and loves Kamala, who later dies of a snakebite. He dreams of a dead songbird in a gold cage belonging to Kamala. He learns wisdom from the ferryman Vasudeva by listening to the river, and at the end envisions a river of faces after kissing the forehead of Govinda, who wears a much-admired secret smile.

The Glass Bead Game is set in the futuristic province of Castalia, where Joseph Knecht rises to the title 'Magister Ludi' — master of the glass bead game, an esoteric activity combining math and music whose symbolic language was developed by Joculator Basiliensis. Knecht debates Plinio Designori, who criticizes the game's mental life without real-world action. He learns from the Music Master, visits the monastery Mariafels to meet the historian Father Jacobus (representing Jakob Burckhardt), and travels to a bamboo grove to learn from Elder Brother. Fritz Tegularius represents Nietzsche. Boarding schools are set up to overthrow the 'Age of the Feuilleton.' Knecht gains a title previously held by Thomas van der Trave. The novel includes 'posthumous writings' such as 'The Indian Life' (about Dasa killing his half-brother Nala) and 'The Rainmaker' (featuring Ada, daughter of Master Turu). Knecht drowns while following Designori's son Tito on a swim in a mountain lake.

Demian is a Bildungsroman narrated by Emil Sinclair, originally published under the pseudonym Emil Sinclair. The title character Max Demian bears 'the mark of Cain' and introduces Sinclair to the Gnostic deity Abraxas. Demian stops the bully Franz Kromer from blackmailing Sinclair over a lie about stealing apples. Sinclair is mentored by an organist named Pistorius and raised in a 'Scheinwelt.' The novel uses Biblical imagery, including chapters like 'Lady Eve.' A boy appears to stop breathing during class, then passes a note: 'The bird is struggling out of the egg... whoever wants to be born must first destroy a world.' Sinclair paints portraits of a beautiful girl that also depict Demian. The novel ends with Demian giving Sinclair a kiss in an infirmary on Eva's behalf.

Narcissus and Goldmund follows two friends who meet at the Mariabronn monastery. Narcissus becomes abbot; Goldmund leaves after being sexually awakened by a gypsy woman. Goldmund apprentices under the woodcarver Master Niklaus after seeing a beautiful wooden Madonna, witnesses the Black Death (to which he appears immune), has a relationship with a countess named Agnes, and carves his mother before dying.

Journey to the East is narrated by 'H.H.' and follows a pilgrimage with 'The League,' a secret society whose members include Mozart, Plato, Puss in Boots, Tristram Shandy, Paul Klee, and characters from Hesse's other works (Vasudeva, Klingsor). The journey falls apart when the League's president Leo disappears at the gorge Morbio Inferiore. André Gide wrote the introduction for the French edition.

Klingsor's Last Summer is a novella about a painter experiencing a final burst of creativity in Italy, who paints a self-portrait he locks in an unused kitchen. He is visited by an Armenian astrologer who believes sadness can be overcome in one hour of concentration, and his friends include artists called Louis the Cruel and Tu Fu.

Other works include Beneath the Wheel (Hans Giebenrath crushed by the German educational system), Peter Camenzind (a man leaves his mountain village, falls in love with painter Erminia, contrasts Nietzsche's ethics with Saint Francis of Assisi), and Gertrud (singer Heinrich Muoth's marriage inspires an opera). Hugo Ball wrote a 1927 biography of Hesse.
