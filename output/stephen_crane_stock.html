American author and poet of the late 19th century, known for the Civil War novel The Red Badge of Courage, the naturalist novella Maggie: A Girl of the Streets, the poetry collection The Black Riders, short stories 'The Open Boat,' 'The Blue Hotel,' and 'The Bride Comes to Yellow Sky,' and the novella The Monster.
The Red Badge of Courage

Crane's most famous work, a Civil War novel following the soldier Henry Fleming of the 304th New York Infantry Regiment. Henry hopes to receive the title wound to make up for fleeing from the Battle of Chancellorsville. Jim Conklin ('the tall soldier') and 'the tattered man' are among Henry's companions. The critic George Wyndham praised the novel for viewing events through the protagonist's 'morbidly sensitive' temperament. Paul Auster's 2021 biography Burning Boy discusses Crane's life, and in an NPR interview Auster described this novel as being about 'the interior life of an adolescent boy.'

The Black Riders and Other Lines

Crane's first poetry collection, notable for its short, parable-like verses. The collection opens with 'Black riders came from the sea' featuring 'clang and clang of spear and shield' and 'clash and clash of hoof and heel.' The most famous poem, 'In the Desert,' describes a 'creature, naked, bestial' who eats his own heart 'because it is bitter / And because it is my heart.' Other poems include one where a spirit declares 'Ah, there is no God!' and is smote dead by 'a sword from the sky'; one where a man says 'Sir, I exist!' but the universe replies 'The fact has not created within me a sense of obligation'; and 'I saw a man pursuing the horizon.' The poet called many of his short verses 'lines' rather than poems. Crane intended the collection to be published in all capital letters.

War Is Kind

Crane's second poetry collection, whose title poem repeats the ironic instruction 'Do not weep, maiden, for war is kind' addressed to a maiden, a baby, and a mother. The poem sarcastically describes the warmth of conflict and how 'these men were born to drill and die.' The sixth poem of the collection begins 'I explain the silvered passing of a ship at night,' which Daniel Hoffman traced to Longfellow.

Maggie: A Girl of the Streets

An 1893 novella subtitled 'A Girl of the Streets,' described as a 'Bowery Tale.' The protagonist Maggie is a shirt factory worker who grows up in a Manhattan tenement with her brother Jimmie Johnson. Jimmie fights a gang of boys from Devil's Row in the opening. Maggie's boyfriend, the bartender Pete, is seduced by Nellie (a 'woman of brilliance and audacity') in the 'hilarious hall,' and after being dumped by Pete, Maggie becomes a prostitute. Her mother scolds her for going 'to deh devil,' and the novella ends with the mother screaming 'I'll fergive her!' at Maggie's funeral. George's Mother is a companion piece to the novella.

The Open Boat

A short story inspired by Crane's real-life shipwreck aboard the SS Commodore. The story opens with the famous line 'None of them knew the color of the sky.' Four characters -- the correspondent (a fictionalized Crane), the oiler (named Billie, based on a real person who died on the Commodore), the cook, and the captain -- are cast adrift in a dinghy. They repeatedly murmur 'Funny they don't see us' as they try to reach shore. Two characters argue about whether 'houses of refuge' are staffed by people. The correspondent recites a poem beginning 'A soldier of the Legion lay dying in Algiers.' The oiler dies trying to swim to shore, and the others discover his body.

The Bride Comes to Yellow Sky

When Wilson discovers Potter is newly married (and unarmed -- 'I ain't got a gun on me'), he lowers his revolvers in disbelief and decides not to shoot. Scratchy sticks paper to a doorframe with a knife and shoots at it from across the street, missing by half an inch. He also shoots at a dog sleeping outside a boarded-up saloon, whose owner boards it up when Scratchy comes by. Other Works and General

Crane wrote several other works that appear in clues. 'An Experiment in Misery' follows a young man who drinks free soup at a bar and lodges with an assassin after disguising himself as a homeless man. 'A Dark Brown Dog' ends with a child's drunk father fatally throwing a dog out a window as an allegory for Jim Crow. 'A Self-Made Man' parodies Horatio Alger's rags-to-riches stories. The Third Violet is about the New York painter Hawker, called by Paul Auster 'probably the world's first postmodern novel.' Other Sullivan County Sketches include 'An Explosion of Seven Babies,' a surreal story about a giant tigress watching over crying infants. 'In the Tenderloin' and 'Flowers of Asphalt' (possibly non-existent) are also mentioned. John Berryman published a study of Crane in 1950. Joyce Carol Oates titled her novel Because It Is Bitter, and Because It Is My Heart after Crane's poem.

Summary of Facts

Stephen Crane is an American author and poet of the late 19th century, primarily known for his Civil War novel The Red Badge of Courage and his naturalist fiction. He occupies a unique position in American literature as both a significant prose writer and poet.

The Red Badge of Courage (1895) is Crane's most frequently clued work, serving as the standard giveaway identifier. The novel follows Henry Fleming, a soldier in the 304th New York Infantry Regiment during the Civil War, who flees from the Battle of Chancellorsville and hopes to receive the title wound to redeem himself. Jim Conklin and 'the tattered man' are his key companions. The critic George Wyndham praised the novel for depicting events through Henry's 'morbidly sensitive' temperament.

Crane's poetry, collected primarily in The Black Riders and Other Lines (1895) and War Is Kind (1899), features short, parabolic verses. The Black Riders is his most heavily clued poetry work. Its opening poem describes black riders who 'came from the sea' with 'clang and clang of spear and shield.' The collection's most famous poem, 'In the Desert,' describes a 'creature, naked, bestial' who eats his own heart 'because it is bitter / And because it is my heart' -- this is the single most frequently recurring Crane clue. Other notable poems include one where a spirit declares 'Ah, there is no God!' and is smote by a sword from the sky, and one where a man tells the universe 'Sir, I exist!' only to be told the fact creates no 'sense of obligation.' War Is Kind features the ironic title poem that instructs 'Do not weep, maiden, for war is kind.'

Maggie: A Girl of the Streets (1893) is Crane's naturalist novella about the title shirt factory worker who grows up in a Manhattan tenement with her brother Jimmie Johnson. After being abandoned by the bartender Pete (who is seduced by Nellie), Maggie becomes a prostitute. The novella ends with her mother's exclamation 'I'll fergive her!' George's Mother is a companion piece.

'The Open Boat' is one of Crane's most important short stories, inspired by his real shipwreck on the SS Commodore. It opens with the famous line 'None of them knew the color of the sky' and follows four men -- the correspondent, the oiler (Billie), the cook, and the captain -- adrift in a dinghy. The oiler dies trying to swim to shore.

'The Blue Hotel' takes place at Pat Scully's establishment during a blizzard. The Swede accuses Johnnie of cheating at cards, leading to a fight. The story ends with the cowboy asking 'I didn't do anything, did I?' after the Easterner declares 'Every sin is the result of a collaboration.'

'The Bride Comes to Yellow Sky' follows marshal Jack Potter returning with his new bride. The town drunk Scratchy Wilson plans a confrontation but lowers his revolvers in disbelief when he discovers Potter is newly married and unarmed.

The Monster is a novella set in Whilomville about the black coachman Henry Johnson, whose face is disfigured by chemicals during a house fire while saving a boy. The Trescott family faces community ostracism for sheltering the disfigured Henry.

Paul Auster's 2021 biography Burning Boy chronicles Crane's life. John Berryman published an academic study of Crane in 1950. Joyce Carol Oates titled her novel Because It Is Bitter, and Because It Is My Heart after Crane's poem 'In the Desert.'
