The Adventures of Augie March

A picaresque novel whose title character is described as 'an American, Chicago born.' Augie works for Einhorn, catches eagles with Thea Fenchel, survives a shipwreck on a lifeboat with a madman, and finds that his friend Sylvester has become a bodyguard for Trotsky in Mexico. The novel's opening lines discuss knocking on doors, and it is considered part of the picaresque genre alongside The Life of Lazarillo de Tormes.

Humboldt's Gift

A novel based on Bellow's real-life friendship with Delmore Schwartz, who is fictionalized as the character Von Humboldt Fleisher. The protagonist is the writer Charlie Citrine, who experiences a midlife crisis. Humboldt's Gift competed with William Gaddis's JR for the National Book Award, and JR won. Delmore Schwartz was a poet known for 'In Dreams Begin Responsibilities' and was the dedicatee of John Berryman's Dream Songs.

Henderson the Rain King

A novel about the American millionaire Eugene Henderson who travels to Africa. He tries to rid the Arnewi tribe of frogs in their cistern, then accidentally earns the title of Rain King from the Wariri tribe by lifting a statue of the goddess Mummah. King Dahfu hunts a lion he believes is the reincarnation of his dead father. Henderson's guide is Romilayu, who leads him between the villages. The novel was likely inspired by Melville Herskovits's book The Cattle Complex in East Africa.

Ravelstein

Bellow's final novel, a fictionalization of his friend Allan Bloom. The title character is a political philosopher who studied under Alexandre Kojeve and is diagnosed with AIDS, dying at the Hotel Crillon in Paris. Bellow nearly died after eating a fish full of ciguatera toxin, which inspired parts of the novel. His fourth wife, mathematician Alexandra Tulcea, is fictionalized as Vela. The Malaysian character Nikki is Abe Ravelstein's lover. Bellow also wrote the introduction to Bloom's The Closing of the American Mind.

Other Novels and Novellas

General / Biographical

Bellow was a Canadian-American Nobel Prize-winning author. His dying words were reportedly 'Was I a man or was I a jerk?' He was mentored by and friends with several literary figures: Martin Amis considered him a writing mentor (fictionalized in Inside Story), and Christopher Hitchens was a friend. Bellow caused controversy by asking 'Who is the Tolstoy of the Zulus?' and defended himself by citing Thomas Mofolo's Chaka and his study under Melville Herskovits at Northwestern. He translated Isaac Bashevis Singer's 'Gimpel the Fool' from Yiddish, introducing Singer to a wider English-speaking audience.

Summary of Facts

Saul Bellow (born Solomon Bellows) was a Canadian-American Jewish author and Nobel Prize laureate whose works form a central part of the American literary canon in quizbowl. His dying words, 'Was I a man or was I a jerk?', appear in clues alongside biographical details like his controversial remark 'Who is the Tolstoy of the Zulus?' and his defense citing Thomas Mofolo's Chaka and his study under Melville Herskovits at Northwestern University.

Bellow's most frequently clued novel is The Adventures of Augie March, a picaresque tale whose title character is introduced as 'an American, Chicago born.' Augie works for Einhorn, catches eagles with Thea Fenchel, survives a shipwreck, and encounters his friend Sylvester as a bodyguard for Trotsky in Mexico. Seize the Day is a novella about the failed actor Tommy Wilhelm, who loses his money speculating on lard commodities with the sinister Dr. Tamkin while his father Dr. Adler refuses to help him; Tommy ends up weeping at a stranger's funeral. Herzog follows a divorced Jewish professor who writes unsent letters to celebrities while processing his divorce from Madeleine and betrayal by Valentine Gerbasch.

Humboldt's Gift, based on Bellow's friendship with the poet Delmore Schwartz (fictionalized as Von Humboldt Fleisher), centers on the writer Charlie Citrine's midlife crisis. The novel lost the National Book Award to William Gaddis's JR. Henderson the Rain King follows a millionaire who travels to Africa, attempts to remove frogs from the Arnewi cistern, and earns the title of Rain King by lifting the statue of the goddess Mummah among the Wariri. Ravelstein, Bellow's final novel, fictionalizes his friend Allan Bloom as a political philosopher who dies of AIDS at the Hotel Crillon in Paris; Bellow's near-death from ciguatera poisoning inspired parts of the work.

Other notable works include Mr. Sammler's Planet (about a Holocaust survivor), The Dean's December (Albert Corde visits his dying mother-in-law in a totalitarian state), More Die of Heartbreak (featuring a passage about Stalin and 'old death' vs. American 'new death'), and the novellas The Actual and A Theft. Bellow also wrote the introduction to Allan Bloom's The Closing of the American Mind and translated Isaac Bashevis Singer's 'Gimpel the Fool' from Yiddish. His literary friendships included Martin Amis (who considered Bellow his writing mentor) and Christopher Hitchens.
