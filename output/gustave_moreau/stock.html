French Symbolist artist, teacher of Henri Matisse. Known for colorful erotic paintings of Biblical and mythological scenes. Most commonly identified by Oedipus and the Sphinx, The Apparition / Salome paintings, and Jupiter and Semele. House-museum in Paris (curated by student Georges Rouault after his death). Friend of Theodore Chasseriau. Associated with Pierre Puvis de Chavannes, Odilon Redon, and Fernand Khnopff in the Symbolist movement. Protagonist of Huysmans's A rebours collects his paintings.
Oedipus and the Sphinx (1864)

Moreau's breakthrough painting, an immediate success at the 1864 Salon. Depicts the Sphinx clinging to Oedipus's chest in a chasm. Influenced by Ingres's version of the same subject. Moreau obsessively copied Carpaccio's painting and used St. George as the facial model for Oedipus. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Gustave Moreau (1826-1898) was a French Symbolist artist, renowned for his colorful, erotic paintings of Biblical and mythological scenes. Called 'the Prince of Mysterious Dreams,' he belonged to the Symbolist movement (whose manifesto was written by Jean Moreas, declaring opposition to 'matter-of-fact description') alongside Pierre Puvis de Chavannes, Odilon Redon, Arnold Bocklin, and Fernand Khnopff. Symbolism broke from classical iconography by focusing on mythological and dream imagery, a reaction against Realism. Moreau was compared to Pre-Raphaelite Edward Burne-Jones by critic Robin Ironside. He was a friend and student of Theodore Chasseriau, and himself served as a crucial teacher -- Henri Matisse studied under him at the Ecole des Beaux-Arts (Matisse later painted a scene of Moreau's studio), and one of the first Fauvist works was painted by a student of Moreau. Fauvism was coined by Louis Vauxcelles at the 1905 Salon d'Automne. After Moreau's death, his student Georges Rouault -- an Expressionist known for paintings of courts, clowns, and prostitutes, and his magnum opus The Old King -- was nominated as curator of the Gustave Moreau Museum, a house-museum in Paris that contains most of his best-known works. Moreau is closely linked to the Decadent movement: the protagonist of Joris-Karl Huysmans's novel A rebours (the 'yellow book' that poisoned Dorian Gray) decorates his home with Moreau's paintings, alongside a jewel-encrusted tortoise shell and a liquor 'mouth organ,' before eventually abandoning his pursuit of aestheticism and returning to Parisian life.

Moreau's most frequently clued work is Oedipus and the Sphinx (1864), an immediate success at the 1864 Salon. It depicts a winged Sphinx clinging to Oedipus's chest in a chasm, with the two title characters staring at each other. A foot and hand jut from the rocks at the bottom alongside a crown and bones, and Oedipus holds a red spear point-down. The painting was influenced by Ingres's version of the same subject. Moreau obsessively copied Vittore Carpaccio's painting (variously identified as a St. George scene or Ten Thousand Martyrs of Ararat) and used St. George as the facial model for Oedipus.

Nearly as frequently clued are his Salome paintings, centered around The Apparition, in which Salome points at the floating, haloed, decapitated head of John the Baptist -- surrounded by a radiant gold disk above a pool of blood -- with blood pouring from the neck. Moreau drew on Japanese prints for the halo. A solemn king in heavy white robes sits on his throne to the right of a blue column; in another version, an indifferent man in a turban and bright red robe holds a sword. In all versions, the central figure stretches her left arm pointing to the right. Different versions include distinguishing features: one prominently features a statue of Diana of Ephesus towering in the background; a black panther appears in the foreground of one and at the central figure's feet in another; one version depicts a tattooed Salome with outlines in white and black. The floating heads were supposedly inspired by ukiyo-e prints that Moreau copied, and Salome wears a revealing dress made almost entirely of jewels. The Alhambra inspired the background of one version. His Salome Dancing Before Herod is housed at the Hammer Museum in Los Angeles. The painting is described in detail in Huysmans's A rebours and discussed in Cynthia Burlingham's book titled for its 'Strange Magic.'

Jupiter and Semele (1895) depicts Jupiter seated on a massive gold throne with a red halo, garlanded with flowers, depicting the parents of Dionysus. Semele, struck dead, is draped over his arm/thigh, and a woman with an open wound in her thigh recoils at the glory of the divine lover. At the bottom, Pan glowers beneath a huge black eagle, and a green snake eating its own tail sits under Jupiter's right foot. The painting includes female figures representing Death (holding a white lily) and Sorrow. Moreau's own commentary mentions 'colossal aerial buildings, with neither foundations nor roof-tops, covered with teeming, quivering vegetation.'

Other notable works include The Life of Humanity, a polyptych with a 3x3 grid depicting the Golden, Silver, and Iron Ages via Adam, Hesiod and Orpheus, and Cain; Les Licornes (The Unicorns), depicting three unicorns surrounded by princesses, inspired by the rediscovery of The Lady and the Unicorn tapestries and commissioned by Edmond de Rothschild, who continually criticized the work, prompting a furious note from Moreau about 'imbeciles whose understanding of art is scarcely better than the average concierge or hog butcher'; Orpheus, showing a woman holding the head and lyre of the title bard; The Chimeras; Young Man and Death, dedicated to mentor Chasseriau, depicting death as a ghostly woman floating along a diagonal axis; Saint George with a golden halo and pink cape riding a white horse; a ghostly Lady Macbeth standing between two red curtains; and Alexander the Great walking through a path between a mountain and a high throne outside an impossibly fantastic castle. One other painting features a heavily-garlanded Pan sprawling in front of an eagle, flanked by personifications of Death (holding a bloody sword) and Pain (wearing a crown of thorns).
