Summary of Facts

Hart Crane is an American modernist poet best known for The Bridge, an epic poem using the Brooklyn Bridge as its central symbol. The poem ends with the plea "of the curveship lend a myth to God" and contains the section "Powhatan's Daughter" (with the invocation "O Appalachian Spring!" and Rip Van Winkle sweeping a tenement) and a Columbus dramatic monologue. White Buildings (1926), introduced by Allen Tate, contains "Voyages" (six love poems for sailor Emil Opffer describing the ocean as "a great wink of eternity") and "At Melville's Tomb" ("The dice of drowned men's bones he saw bequeath / An embassy"). Crane committed suicide in 1932 by jumping from the ship Orizaba into the Gulf of Mexico after being attacked for making sexual advances toward crew members.
