American minimalist short story writer known as the 'American Chekhov.' Associated with the 'dirty realism' movement. His stories were famously edited by Gordon Lish. Key works include 'Cathedral,' 'What We Talk About When We Talk About Love,' and 'Where I'm Calling From.' He studied under John Gardner at Chico State College.
Cathedral

Carver's most celebrated story, in which the narrator's wife invites her blind friend Robert to visit. The narrator and Robert smoke weed, watch a TV documentary about the Middle Ages, and then share a spiritual experience as the narrator holds the blind man's hand to draw the title building. Robert had lost his wife Beulah to cancer and tugs his beard as the TV plays. Carver claimed that unlike much of his minimalist fiction, this story 'affirms something.' David Foster Wallace attacked the 'catatonic realism' style modeled on Carver's prose.

What We Talk About When We Talk About Love

A story structured after Plato's Symposium, in which two couples — Mel and Terri McGinnis, and Nick and Laura — sit around drinking gin while discussing love. Mel is a cardiologist who tells anecdotes usually involving full-body coverage. Terri's abusive ex Ed shot himself. The story ends with the characters drunkenly sitting and listening to 'the human noise we sat there making.' The narrator hears a heartbeat in the dark. The title collection was famously edited by Gordon Lish; the manuscript version was called Beginners. Two characters argue over 'vassals' vs 'vessels.' Mel says 'It seems to me we're just beginners at love.' The title inspired Murakami's memoir and Nathan Englander's story about Anne Frank.

Where I'm Calling From

A story set at Frank Martin's 'drying-out facility' (alcohol rehab center). The narrator meets J.P., who tells about meeting his chimney sweep wife Roxy when he kissed her for good luck. The narrator compares himself to the protagonist of Jack London's 'To Build a Fire' while contemplating whether to call his wife or his girlfriend. At the end, the narrator promises to answer 'Hello, sugar' to his wife on New Year's. Haruki Murakami was especially fond of this story.

So Much Water So Close to Home

A story in which Claire is disturbed that her husband Stuart may have committed murder after he finds a dead girl on a camping trip. Claire attends the dead girl's funeral. The story explores the tension between the couple. Murakami became enthralled with Carver through this story.

Other Stories

Carver wrote numerous other short stories. 'A Small, Good Thing' features a baker who repeatedly calls a couple after their son's accident and eventually gives them hot cinnamon rolls. 'Gazebo' features motel managers Holly and Duane discussing their failing marriage and Duane's affair with cleaning lady Juanita. 'Fat' has a waitress who serves an obese man and later imagines herself too fat. 'Will You Please Be Quiet, Please?' follows Ralph Wynam struggling with his wife's affair. 'Neighbors' has Bill Miller putting on Harriet Stone's dress. 'Vitamins' ends with the narrator rummaging through a medicine cabinet. 'What Do You Do in San Francisco?' features a mailman trying to get hippie neighbors to work. His poem 'Rain' asks 'Would I live my life over again? Yes. Given half a chance. Yes.' His story 'Errand' depicts Chekhov's death. He also wrote the collection Will You Please Be Quiet, Please?

General / Biographical

Carver was a minimalist and 'dirty realist' from Oregon. His early works were heavily rewritten by his editor Gordon Lish, who told him to use five words in place of fifteen while writing for Esquire. D.T. Max chronicled this editing controversy. John Gardner was an important mentor, teaching Carver at Chico State in 1958. David Foster Wallace attacked the 'catatonic realism' modeled on Carver. In 'On Influence,' Carver claimed Hemingway wasn't an influence on his style. Denis Johnson took Carver's classes at the Iowa Writers' Workshop. Murakami ordered a custom bed for Carver to visit Japan, but Carver died in 1988 before he could visit.

Summary of Facts

Raymond Carver is the most frequently clued American short story writer of the late 20th century, known for his minimalist and 'dirty realist' style. His story 'Cathedral' is by far his most famous: the narrator and a blind man named Robert smoke weed, watch a TV documentary about the Middle Ages, and share a spiritual experience drawing the title building together. Carver said this story 'affirms something' unlike his other work.

'What We Talk About When We Talk About Love,' structured after Plato's Symposium, features two couples (the cardiologist Mel and Terri, and Nick and Laura) drinking gin while discussing love. Terri's abusive ex Ed shot himself. The title collection was famously edited by Gordon Lish, with the manuscript version called Beginners. 'Where I'm Calling From' is set at Frank Martin's rehab center, where the narrator meets J.P. and his chimney sweep wife Roxy, comparing himself to the protagonist of Jack London's 'To Build a Fire.'

Other important stories include 'So Much Water So Close to Home' (Claire disturbed after her husband finds a dead girl), 'A Small, Good Thing' (a baker gives cinnamon rolls), 'Gazebo' (motel managers Holly and Duane), and 'Errand' (depicting Chekhov's death). His editing relationship with Gordon Lish is a major biographical clue: Lish told Carver to 'use five words in place of fifteen,' and D.T. Max chronicled the controversy. John Gardner mentored Carver at Chico State. Murakami was deeply influenced by Carver and titled his memoir What I Talk About When I Talk About Running.
