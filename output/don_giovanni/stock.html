Don Giovanni

Mozart's opera in two acts, libretto by Lorenzo Da Ponte. The title libertine (Don Giovanni) kills the Commendatore in a duel at the opera's opening, and is ultimately dragged to hell by the Commendatore's stone statue when he refuses to repent. Full Italian title: Il dissoluto punito, ossia il Don Giovanni. Premiered in Prague in 1787. Eberhard Wächter played the title role in a famous 1959 recording conducted by Carlo Maria Giulini. Gustav Mahler cut the opera's final sextet when he conducted it. Pauline Viardot bought the original manuscript and built a shrine to it. Wikipedia

The Overture

Begins in D minor with a loud tutti chord, followed by crashing syncopated chords in the violins and violas. The Ball Scene / Three Simultaneous Dances

During the masked ball at the end of Act I, the pit orchestra and two onstage ensembles play three dances simultaneously in different time signatures: a minuet in 3/4, a contradance (or 'follia') in 2/4, and a waltz (or 'teitsch') in 3/8. Wye Jamison Allanbrook's analysis of the dances and social class influenced Peter Sellars's 1988 Trump Tower production.

Don Giovanni – Connections to Other Topics

The Don Juan legend originated in Tirso de Molina's play The Trickster of Seville and the Stone Guest. Later treatments include Molière's Dom Juan, Pushkin's 'little tragedy' The Stone Guest, Zorrilla's Don Juan Tenorio (performed every All Saints' Day), and Max Frisch's Don Juan, or The Love of Geometry. Byron's Don Juan is an unfinished comic epic in ottava rima. Kierkegaard analyzed the character in Either/Or as 'absolutely musical.'

Summary of Facts

Don Giovanni (full title: Il dissoluto punito, ossia il Don Giovanni, 'The Rake Punished, or Don Giovanni') is a two-act opera by Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart with a libretto by Lorenzo Da Ponte, premiered in Prague in 1787. It stands as one of the central operas in the repertoire and a touchstone of Western music.

The opera follows the Spanish libertine Don Giovanni, who murders the Commendatore (Donna Anna's father) in a duel at the opera's opening, then spends the work seducing and deceiving various women. His servant Leporello opens the opera with the complaint 'Notte e giorno faticar' ('Night and day I toil') sung to quarter notes alternating between F and C. In the Catalogue Aria ('Madamina, il catalogo è questo'), Leporello enumerates his master's conquests for Donna Elvira, claiming 1,003 women in Spain alone and listing seductions by social class. The German (Viennese) version of the opera also includes Elvira herself in the list of Spanish conquests; the aria interpolates dotted arpeggiated D major figures between repetitions of 'mille e tre' ('one thousand and three').

The central Act I centerpiece is the duet 'La ci darem la mano,' sung between Don Giovanni and the peasant girl Zerlina as he attempts to seduce her away from her fiancé Masetto. The duet opens with an ascending motif (A, A, B, C-sharp, A, F-sharp, up to B) and shifts abruptly from 2/4 to 6/8 time when the two sing 'Andiam!' together. A teenage Frédéric Chopin composed a set of variations on this duet, prompting Robert Schumann's celebrated exclamation 'Hats off, gentlemen — a genius!' The Act I finale features one of the most complex scenes in opera: a masked ball at which the pit orchestra and two onstage ensembles simultaneously play three dances in different meters — a minuet in 3/4, a contradance in 2/4, and a waltz in 3/8. Musicologist Wye Jamison Allanbrook analyzed these dance rhythms in relation to social class, influencing Peter Sellars's 1988 Trump Tower production.

The overture opens in D minor with a loud tutti chord followed by crashing syncopated chords in the violins and violas — an effect that imitates the opening of Gluck's Alceste. Flutes and violins then play ascending and descending D minor scales, followed by eerily ascending major and melodic minor scales. The overture transitions without pause into Leporello's opening aria. Alfred Einstein posited that two recitatives in the opera were forgeries by Guardasoni. The D minor chords of the overture are reharmonized into diminished seventh chords for the opening of the final scene.

In the Act II feast scene, a diegetic onstage band plays excerpts from other operas — Una cosa rara (Martín y Soler) and Fra i due litiganti (Sarti) — before culminating in 'Non più andrai' from Mozart's own Marriage of Figaro. Don Giovanni remarks 'I know this only too well!', and Leporello is annoyed by the familiar tune. The climactic finale arrives when the stone statue of the murdered Commendatore comes to dinner, chanting 'Pentiti!' (Repent!) and grabbing Don Giovanni's hand. Don Giovanni repeatedly shouts 'No!' — on a high D — and is dragged to hell. At the premiere, this scene was rearranged so that the bass Giuseppe Lolli could change costumes to play a different character in the epilogue sextet. Leporello hides under the table during the confrontation. The final sextet, which Gustav Mahler was known to cut, closes with the moral 'this is how all evildoers end.'

Notable performers and productions: Eberhard Wächter played the title role in a famous 1959 recording conducted by Carlo Maria Giulini. Alfred Roller designed a white urn-embroidered jacket for a Mahler-era production. Pauline Viardot purchased the original manuscript and built a shrine to it; Tchaikovsky saw the score at her home and subsequently composed his Orchestral Suite No. 4 (Mozartiana). Lorenzo Da Ponte produced the Garcia family's 1825 New York tour, which staged this opera with Manuel Garcia Sr. as the title character, Manuel Jr. as Leporello, and Maria (Malibran) as Zerlina.

Philosopher Søren Kierkegaard devoted a long essay ('The Immediate Stages of the Erotic, or the Musical Erotic') in Either/Or to the analysis of Don Giovanni, arguing that the title character is 'not character but essentially life' and 'absolutely musical,' with his passion setting the passions of all others in motion. Don Giovanni is 'heard through' Leporello in the passage describing seasonal preferences among the conquests. The Don Juan legend itself originated in Tirso de Molina's The Trickster of Seville and the Stone Guest; subsequent treatments include Molière's Dom Juan, Pushkin's 'little tragedy' The Stone Guest, Zorrilla's Don Juan Tenorio, and Lord Byron's unfinished epic Don Juan in ottava rima.
