English political philosopher of the 17th century, best known for Leviathan. Argued for absolute sovereignty and the social contract to escape the state of nature, which he described as a 'war of all against all' producing a life that is 'solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.' Also wrote De Cive and Behemoth, debated Robert Boyle on experimental philosophy, and influenced legal positivism.
Leviathan and the State of Nature

Hobbes's masterwork Leviathan (1651) argues that the state of nature is a 'war of all against all' producing a life that is 'solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short,' and that only an absolute sovereign can maintain peace. The famous frontispiece depicts the ideal leader of a Christian Commonwealth as a giant composed of hundreds of smaller people, carrying a sword and scepter. The work's four parts include 'Of Man,' 'Of Commonwealth,' and 'The Kingdom of Darkness' (Part IV, about ignorance from scriptural misinterpretation). Key concepts include the social contract as a 'covenant' or 'mutual transferring of right,' and the Fool who objects that there is 'no such thing as Justice.'

De Cive and Other Political Works

De Cive (On the Citizen, 1642) preceded Leviathan and contains the first use of the phrase 'war of all against all' (bellum omnium contra omnes). It was part of a planned trilogy with De Corpore and De Homine. De Cive argues that a fundamental law of nature is to seek peace, analogizes a 'great family' to a kingdom, and concludes that only faith in Jesus is necessary for salvation. Hobbes also wrote Behemoth, a history of the English Civil War that criticized tyrannicide.

Debate with Robert Boyle and Philosophy of Science

Hobbes's rejection of Robert Boyle's experimental philosophy, particularly his denial of the vacuum, is analyzed in Steven Shapin and Simon Schaffer's influential book Leviathan and the Air-Pump. Hobbes's skepticism about experiments as a source of knowledge is contrasted with Boyle's experimentalism. Margaret Cavendish, who married into the family of Hobbes's patrons, sided with his critique of Boyle but disputed his claim that passions are 'voluntary' motions. The patronage of the Cavendish family also connects Hobbes to French intellectual circles.

Psychology and Philosophy of Mind

Hobbes's mechanistic philosophy classified all mental phenomena as forms of 'motion.' He categorized appetites and aversions as 'endeavors' (motions toward or away from goals) and argued that the will is merely an 'appetite or aversion,' provoking a debate with John Bramhall on liberty and necessity. He believed motion was the universal cause of all things and rejected the notion of a summum bonum, instead identifying avoiding death as the political goal. His theory of language treated names as 'marks' to recall concepts, and Charles Taylor placed him alongside Locke and Condillac in the 'HLC theory' of enframing language.

General / Biographical and Reception

Common identifiers include English philosopher, author of Leviathan, the phrase 'nasty, brutish, and short.' Hobbes was tutor to Charles II and fled to Paris during the English Civil War. He was accused of atheism for arguing the Ten Commandments had no 'obligatory force of laws.' His reception includes Montesquieu's 'fable of the Troglodytes,' Shaftesbury's opposition to his egoism, Isaiah Berlin's definition of negative liberty rooted in his work, legal positivism's debt to him, and d'Holbach translating his De Cive and De Homine into French.

Summary of Facts

Thomas Hobbes (1588-1679) is one of the most heavily clued political philosophers in quizbowl, with virtually every question centering on Leviathan and the state of nature. The overwhelming giveaway is the phrase 'nasty, brutish, and short' describing life in the state of nature, or 'war of all against all,' leading to his argument for an absolute sovereign.

Leviathan's frontispiece, depicting a crowned giant composed of hundreds of smaller people holding a sword and scepter, is a frequent mid-level clue. The work contains the concept of the social contract as a 'covenant' or 'mutual transferring of right.' Its four sections include 'Of Man,' 'Of Commonwealth,' and 'The Kingdom of Darkness' (Part IV, about ignorance from scriptural misinterpretation). The Fool, a character in Chapter 15 who denies justice, appears in bonus questions.

De Cive (1642) preceded Leviathan and contains the first use of 'war of all against all' (bellum omnium contra omnes). It was part of a planned trilogy with De Corpore and De Homine. Behemoth was Hobbes's history of the English Civil War.

Hobbes's debate with Robert Boyle over experimental philosophy is analyzed in Shapin and Schaffer's Leviathan and the Air-Pump. Hobbes denied the vacuum and was skeptical of experiments as a source of knowledge. Margaret Cavendish (of Hobbes's patron family) sided with his critique of Boyle but disputed his mechanistic view of passions.

Hobbes's mechanistic philosophy classified appetites and aversions as 'endeavors' (motions toward or away from goals), and his deterministic view of the will as merely an 'appetite or aversion' provoked John Bramhall's critique. He condemned scholastics for 'insignificant speech,' criticized Peter Lombard and Duns Scotus, and was accused of atheism for arguing the Ten Commandments had no 'obligatory force of laws.'

His reception is wide-ranging: Montesquieu critiqued his human nature with the 'fable of the Troglodytes'; Shaftesbury opposed his egoism; Isaiah Berlin rooted negative liberty in his work; legal positivism (H. L. A. Hart) drew from his writings; and d'Holbach translated his works into French. Quentin Skinner argued Hobbes must be understood through contemporary 'rhetorical culture.' The Ship of Theseus puzzle—what if you used the original planks to build a second ship—is attributed to him.
