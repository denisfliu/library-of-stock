Summary of Facts

Alfred, Lord Tennyson was a Victorian English Poet Laureate, one of the most frequently clued poets in quizbowl.

In Memoriam A.H.H. is a long elegiac poem mourning the death of Tennyson's friend Arthur Henry Hallam. It famously describes nature as 'red in tooth and claw' (often associated with Darwin's theory of evolution, though Tennyson wrote 'love Creation's final law' in the preceding line). The poem declares ''tis better to have loved and lost / Than never to have loved at all.' The opening invokes 'Strong Son of God, immortal love.' It asks 'wild bells' to 'ring out' the old, the false, and the grief. Other notable passages include 'of fifty seeds' often only one sprouts, 'Dragons of the prime, / That tare each other in their slime' (the 'Dinosaur Cantos'), 'hope of answer, or redress' behind 'the veil,' and man working 'out the beast, / and let the ape and tiger die.' It ends with an epithalamium for Tennyson's sister. Canto 118 describes the advent of man, which Amitav Ghosh in The Nutmeg's Curse critiques for 'absolute exceptionalism.' It is quoted in John Fowles's The French Lieutenant's Woman and by Mrs. Spring Fragrance in Sui Sin Far's story.

'Ulysses' is a dramatic monologue by an aging Odysseus who resolves to set out on one more voyage. He announces 'I am become a name' and recalls 'much have I seen and known.' He leaves 'the scepter and the isle' to his son Telemachus, resolves to 'sail beyond the sunset' and to 'follow knowledge like a sinking star,' and closes with the famous line 'to strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield.' Linda K. Hughes suggests this reflects Tennyson's wish to reunite with Hallam.

'The Lady of Shalott' depicts a cursed woman near Camelot who weaves a tapestry and sees the world only through a mirror. She complains 'I am half-sick of shadows' before the mirror cracks and she dies floating in a boat with her name carved on it. The poem inspired major Pre-Raphaelite paintings by Waterhouse and Millais's Mariana. Agatha Christie's novel The Mirror Crack'd from Side to Side takes its title from this poem.

'The Charge of the Light Brigade' immortalizes the disastrous cavalry charge at Balaclava: 'Ours not to reason why / ours but to do and die.' The six hundred rode 'into the valley of Death.'

'The Lotos-Eaters' is based on The Odyssey. Mariners eat the lotus and resolve 'rest ye, brother mariners, we will not wander more,' languishing in a land 'where all things always seem'd the same,' 'propt on beds of amaranth and moly.' The 'mild-eyed melancholy' Lotos-Eaters bear branches 'of that enchanted stem.'

'The Kraken' is a fifteen-line sonnet about a creature sleeping 'far, far beneath in the abysmal sea' in 'dreamless, uninvaded sleep' amid 'huge sponges of millennial growth and height.' It will 'rise and on the surface die.' China Mieville's novel Kraken was inspired by this poem.

'Locksley Hall' features a young soldier recalling his cousin Amy: 'In the Spring a young man's fancy lightly turns to thoughts of love.' It envisions a 'Parliament of man, the federation of the world' and 'nations' airy navies.' The sequel 'Locksley Hall Sixty Years After' rebuts the earlier optimism. Isaac Asimov called it 'the most remarkable example of science fiction poetry.'

'Tithonus' opens 'The woods decay, the woods decay and fall' and notes 'after many a summer dies a swan.' The speaker laments 'The Gods themselves cannot recall their gifts.'

The Princess is a serio-comic narrative poem containing the interpolated lyrics 'Tears, Idle Tears' ('I know not what they mean'; 'the days that are no more'), 'Now Sleeps the Crimson Petal, Now the White' ('slip / into my bosom and be lost in me'), 'Come Down, O Maid' ('moan of doves in immemorial elms, and murmuring of innumerable bees'), and 'Sweet and Low' ('Wind of the western sea').

'Crossing the Bar' asks 'May there be no sadness of farewell, / When I embark' and hopes 'to see my Pilot face to face.' Idylls of the King is a collection of twelve narrative poems retelling Arthurian legend. Other works include 'The Eagle' ('ringed with the azure world'), 'Enoch Arden,' 'Break, Break, Break,' 'The Splendor Falls,' 'The Palace of Art,' and the 'Flower in the crannied wall' poem. Tennyson's impressions of the Liverpool-Manchester railway inspired 'Forward, forward let us range.' Edward Lear illustrated and set his poems to music.
