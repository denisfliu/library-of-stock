# Classical Sociology
> The discipline's 19th- and early-20th-century founders and the core concepts they coined to explain modern social order.
Max Weber | German sociologist of Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism; theorized bureaucracy, rationalization, and three types of authority.
Emile Durkheim | French founder of sociology; wrote The Division of Labor in Society and Suicide; coined anomie and social facts.
Georg Simmel | German formal sociologist of social interaction and money; wrote "The Metropolis and Mental Life" and The Philosophy of Money.
Karl Marx | German philosopher-economist (b. 1818) of historical materialism and class conflict; co-wrote The Communist Manifesto with Engels.
Thorstein Veblen | American economist-sociologist who coined "conspicuous consumption" in The Theory of the Leisure Class.
Marcel Mauss | French sociologist and Durkheim's nephew; wrote The Gift, analyzing reciprocity and exchange in archaic societies.
anomie | Durkheim's concept of normlessness arising from breakdown of social regulation, linked to his study of suicide.
Auguste Comte | French founder who coined the term "sociology" and proposed positivism and the law of three stages.
suicide | Subject of Durkheim's landmark study distinguishing egoistic, altruistic, and anomic types by social integration.
Alexis de Tocqueville | French thinker who wrote Democracy in America, analyzing civic associations and the tyranny of the majority.
crowds | Studied in classical collective-behavior theory examining how individuals act differently in mass gatherings.
democracy | Analyzed by Tocqueville's Democracy in America as sustained by civic associations and equality of conditions.
division of labor | Subject of Durkheim's The Division of Labor in Society, contrasting mechanical and organic solidarity.
Harriet Martineau | British writer often called sociology's first female founder; translated Comte and wrote Society in America.
religion | Analyzed by Durkheim's Elementary Forms of Religious Life and Weber's Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism.
bureaucracy | Weber's model of rational-legal authority, characterized by hierarchy, rules, and impersonal administration.
social facts | Durkheim's term for external, coercive social phenomena like law and morality that constrain individual behavior.
Friedrich Engels | Marx's collaborator who wrote The Condition of the Working Class in England and co-authored the Communist Manifesto.
organic solidarity | Durkheim's term for cohesion in complex societies based on interdependence from specialized division of labor.
Roman Catholics | Compared with Protestants in Durkheim's Suicide, which found lower suicide rates linked to stronger social integration.

# Symbolic Interactionism & Micro-Sociology
> Micro-level approaches treating everyday interaction, role-play, and shared meaning as the basic unit of social life.
Erving Goffman | Canadian-American sociologist of dramaturgical theory; wrote The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life and Stigma.
symbolic interactionism | Micro-sociological tradition, rooted in George Herbert Mead, holding that meaning is created through social interaction.
George Herbert Mead | American philosopher-sociologist who founded symbolic interactionism, theorizing the social self through "I" and "me."
ethnography | Qualitative fieldwork method exemplified by Goffman's asylum studies and Laud Humphreys's Tearoom Trade.
Harold Garfinkel | American sociologist who founded ethnomethodology, studying how people produce shared social order through everyday practices.
roles | Expected behaviors attached to social positions, central to Goffman's dramaturgical theory and role theory.
symbolic | Common-link term spanning symbolic interactionism, Bourdieu's symbolic capital, and symbolic violence.
symbols | Shared meaningful signs that, per symbolic interactionism, are interpreted and negotiated through social interaction.
The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life | Erving Goffman's 1956 book applying dramaturgical theory, comparing social interaction to theatrical performance.
actors | In Goffman's dramaturgical theory, individuals performing social roles before an audience in everyday interaction.
Tearoom Trade: Impersonal Sex in Public Places | Laud Humphreys's controversial ethnography of anonymous encounters between men in public restrooms.

# Structural Functionalism & Middle-Range Theory
> Mid-20th-century American theory explaining social institutions by the functions they perform for the wider system.
Talcott Parsons | American sociologist who imported Weber to the US; wrote The Structure of Social Action; developed structural functionalism.
Robert King Merton | American sociologist who coined "self-fulfilling prophecy," Strain Theory, and middle-range theory.
Strain Theory | Robert Merton's theory that deviance arises from a gap between culturally valued goals and legitimate means.
middle-range theory | Robert Merton's approach bridging abstract grand theory and narrow empirical description.
Robert K. Merton | Same sociologist as Robert King Merton, known for Strain Theory and middle-range theory.
structural functionalism | Parsons-led paradigm analyzing society as an interconnected system of institutions serving functional needs.
Robert Merton | Same sociologist as Robert King Merton, known for Strain Theory and middle-range theory.
The Structure of Social Action | Talcott Parsons's 1937 book synthesizing Weber, Durkheim, and Pareto into an action theory.

# Conflict, Power & Critical Theory
> Approaches centered on power, domination, and ideology, spanning Marxist-inflected conflict theory, the Frankfurt School, and Foucault.
C. Wright Mills | American sociologist who wrote The Power Elite and The Sociological Imagination, critiquing concentrated elite power.
Jurgen Habermas | German Frankfurt School theorist (b. 1929) of the public sphere and communicative action.
the power elite | C. Wright Mills's concept of a unified elite spanning corporate, political, and military leadership.
violence | Analyzed through Weber's monopoly of legitimate violence and Bourdieu's concept of symbolic violence.
Charles Wright Mills | Same sociologist as C. Wright Mills, author of The Power Elite and White Collar.
hegemony | Antonio Gramsci's concept of ruling-class dominance maintained through cultural consent rather than force alone.
Stuart Hall | Jamaican-British cultural theorist who developed encoding/decoding media theory and studied hegemony and race.
Michel Foucault | French post-structuralist (b. 1926) of Discipline and Punish, theorizing power/knowledge and disciplinary institutions like prisons.
neoliberalism | Political-economic ideology favoring markets and deregulation, critiqued by Harvey and other critical theorists.
pluralism | Theory that power is dispersed among competing groups, opposed to Mills's power elite thesis.
power | Central concept analyzed by Weber, Foucault, Mills, and pluralist theorists as domination or dispersed capacity.
prisons | Institutions analyzed in Foucault's Discipline and Punish and criticized by Angela Davis's abolitionist writings.
The Sociological Imagination | C. Wright Mills's 1959 book urging the linkage of personal troubles to broad public issues.
white-collar | C. Wright Mills's term for the salaried middle class, analyzed in his book White Collar.

# Urban Sociology & the Chicago School
> The study of cities, neighborhoods, and urban life, anchored by the Chicago School's ethnographic tradition and its later critics.
cities | Central subject of urban sociology, from the Chicago School's ecological models to Jane Jacobs's street-level critiques.
Jane Jacobs | Urbanist who wrote The Death and Life of Great American Cities, championing sidewalks and mixed-use neighborhoods.
crime | Studied via Strain Theory, Broken Windows theory, and Chicago School ecological approaches to deviance.
Chicago | Home of the Chicago School of urban sociology, centered at the University of Chicago's sociology department.
deviance | Rule-breaking behavior explained by Strain Theory, labeling theory, and Chicago School ecological approaches.
Broken Windows theory | Wilson and Kelling's theory that visible disorder like broken windows invites further crime and decay.
David Harvey | Marxist geographer of the urban process, spatial fixes, and "the right to the city."
Henri Lefebvre | French Marxist theorist of The Production of Space and "the right to the city."
sidewalks | Focus of Jane Jacobs's argument that pedestrian street life produces urban safety and community.
city | Object of study for the Chicago School's ecological models and later critical urban theorists like Harvey and Lefebvre.
immigration | Studied by the Chicago School through assimilation and ethnic-enclave models in American cities.
Lewis Mumford | American urbanist and historian who wrote The City in History, critiquing sprawling suburban development.
Middletown | Robert and Helen Lynd's landmark community study of Muncie, Indiana, as a typical American town.
suburbs | Residential form critiqued by Lewis Mumford and studied as a counterpoint to Jane Jacobs's dense cities.
"The Metropolis and Mental Life" | Georg Simmel's essay on how urban life produces a blasé attitude and mental overstimulation.
New York City | American metropolis studied in urban sociology alongside Chicago as a site of density, inequality, and street life.
Philadelphia, Pennsylvania | City studied in Du Bois's pioneering urban sociological survey, The Philadelphia Negro.
Sir Patrick Geddes | Scottish biologist-urbanist who pioneered regional planning and coined "conurbation."
space | Analyzed by Henri Lefebvre's The Production of Space as socially produced rather than a neutral container.
University of Chicago | Home institution of the Chicago School, whose sociology department pioneered urban ethnography in the early 1900s.

# Race, Class & Gender
> Scholarship on racial, economic, and gender inequality, including foundational Black sociology and second- and third-wave feminist theory.
intersectionality | Concept coined by Kimberle Crenshaw describing overlapping systems of race, gender, and class oppression.
gender | Social category theorized by Judith Butler as performative; central to feminist sociology and studies of masculinity.
W. E. B. Du Bois | Pioneering Black sociologist who wrote The Souls of Black Folk and The Philadelphia Negro; coined "double consciousness."
masculinity | Gendered identity studied through concepts like Raewyn Connell's "hegemonic masculinity."
Patricia Hill Collins | Black feminist sociologist who wrote Black Feminist Thought, developing the matrix of domination framework.
poverty | Studied through structural inequality, William Julius Wilson's urban poverty research, and welfare-state analysis.
Barbara Ehrenreich | American writer-sociologist who wrote Nickel and Dimed, an undercover account of low-wage work in America.
bell hooks | Black feminist scholar (Gloria Watkins) who wrote Ain't I a Woman, theorizing intersecting race, class, and gender oppression.
marriage | Studied as a social institution shaped by gender roles, class, and changing family structures.
race | Social category central to Du Bois's, Wilson's, and intersectionality scholars' analyses of inequality.
Arlie Russell Hochschild | American sociologist who coined "emotional labor" in The Managed Heart, studying feeling in service work.
Judith Butler | Philosopher-theorist of gender performativity, arguing gender is constituted through repeated acts, as in Gender Trouble.
Angela Davis | Black feminist scholar-activist who wrote Women, Race, and Class and championed prison abolition.
meritocracy | Concept of advancement by talent and effort, critiqued by sociologists studying persistent class and racial inequality.
patriarchy | System of male-dominated social structures central to feminist sociological theory.
pornography | Subject of feminist sociological debate over its relation to patriarchy, exploitation, and sexual freedom.
social mobility | Movement between class positions, studied as a measure of equality of opportunity and structural inequality.
The Feminine Mystique | Betty Friedan's 1963 book on suburban housewives' dissatisfaction, sparking second-wave feminism.
third-wave feminism | Feminist movement from the 1990s emphasizing intersectionality, individual identity, and critique of earlier feminist waves.
William Julius Wilson | American sociologist who wrote The Truly Disadvantaged, linking urban poverty to deindustrialization and social isolation.
working class | Social class studied by Marx and Engels, and by later sociologists of labor and inequality.

# Social Capital & Networks
> Theories of social ties, trust, and the convertible resources embedded in networks and communities.
Pierre Bourdieu | French sociologist of habitus and cultural, social, and symbolic capital; wrote Distinction on taste and class.
social networks | Ties linking individuals or groups, studied via network analysis, weak-tie theory, and social capital research.
social capital | Resources embedded in social networks, theorized by Bourdieu and popularized by Robert Putnam's Bowling Alone.
weak ties | Mark Granovetter's concept that loose acquaintances, not close ties, best transmit new information like job leads.
capital | In Bourdieu's framework, includes economic, cultural, social, and symbolic forms convertible into social advantage.
habitus | Bourdieu's concept of internalized dispositions shaping perception and behavior, shaped by one's social position.
Robert Putnam | American political scientist who wrote Bowling Alone, arguing American social capital and civic engagement declined.
networks | Structures of social ties analyzed through network analysis, weak-tie theory, and social capital research.

# Nations, States & Global Systems
> Macro-historical sociology of nation-states, nationalism, revolutions, and the modern global economy.
Immanuel Wallerstein | American sociologist who developed world-systems theory, dividing the global economy into core, periphery, and semi-periphery.
Italy | National context for studies of civil society and social capital, as in Putnam's Making Democracy Work.
nations | Political communities analyzed through Benedict Anderson's "imagined communities" and theories of nationalism.
Benedict Anderson | Political scientist who wrote Imagined Communities, defining nations as socially constructed political communities.
Charles Tilly | American historical sociologist who studied state formation, revolutions, and contentious politics, coining "war made the state."
China | National context for sociological studies of state formation, revolution, and rapid modernization.
England | Nation studied in sociology as an early industrial society, from Engels's Manchester to class and welfare-state analysis.
globalization | Process of increasing worldwide economic and cultural interconnection, analyzed through Wallerstein's world-systems theory.
Imagined Communities | Benedict Anderson's 1983 book arguing nations are constructed through shared print media and imagination.
nationalism | Ideology of national identity and self-determination, theorized through Anderson's imagined communities.
peace | Studied alongside war and revolution in historical and political sociology, as in Charles Tilly's work.
Poland | National context for studies of nationalism, state socialism, and social movements like Solidarity.
revolutions | Studied by historical sociologists like Charles Tilly, explaining state breakdown and collective political upheaval.

# Culture, Media & Modernity
> Theories of mass culture, media, and the changing character of the self and society under modern conditions.
Anthony Giddens | British sociologist of structuration theory and "runaway world" globalization; advised Tony Blair's Third Way.
Norbert Elias | German sociologist who wrote The Civilizing Process, tracing long-term shifts in manners and self-restraint.
culture | Shared beliefs, symbols, and practices studied across sociology, from Bourdieu's cultural capital to cultural studies.
Sigmund Freud | Austrian psychoanalyst whose theories of the unconscious and civilization influenced sociological theories of the self.
television | Mass medium analyzed in studies of media effects, consumer culture, and McLuhan's media theory.
Daniel Bell | American sociologist who wrote The Coming of Post-Industrial Society, forecasting a knowledge- and service-based economy.
education | Social institution studied for its role in socialization, credentialing, and the reproduction of inequality.
George Ritzer | American sociologist who coined "McDonaldization," describing rationalized efficiency spreading through modern society.
knowledge | Studied in the sociology of knowledge, examining how social context shapes what counts as truth.
Marshall McLuhan | Canadian media theorist who coined "the medium is the message" and "global village."
modernity | Condition of modern society theorized by Giddens and others as marked by rationalization and reflexivity.
sociobiology | Field, associated with E. O. Wilson, explaining social behavior through evolutionary biology, controversial among sociologists.
The Lonely Crowd | David Riesman's book distinguishing inner-directed and other-directed character types in modern American society.
