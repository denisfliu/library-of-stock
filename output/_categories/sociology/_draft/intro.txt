Sociology questions are dominated by the classical European canon and by twentieth-century American theory in roughly equal measure. Max Weber, Erving Goffman, and Emile Durkheim are the three most-asked answers, followed closely by Pierre Bourdieu, Talcott Parsons, Georg Simmel, and C. Wright Mills. The classical triumvirate of Durkheim, Weber, and Marx anchors a cluster of foundational concepts — anomie, social facts, division of labor, bureaucracy, and the Protestant work ethic — while Goffman's dramaturgical theory and George Herbert Mead's symbolic interactionism supply the discipline's dominant micro-sociological vocabulary of roles, symbols, and the presentation of self.

A second major cluster concerns cities and urban life, running from Georg Simmel's essay on the metropolis through the Chicago School centered at the University of Chicago to Jane Jacobs's street-level critique of urban planning and the Broken Windows theory of crime and disorder. Later urban theorists such as David Harvey and Henri Lefebvre extend this tradition into a Marxist analysis of space, while community studies like Middletown and The Philadelphia Negro anchor it in specific American places. A parallel cluster addresses race, class, and gender inequality, drawing on W. E. B. Du Bois, Patricia Hill Collins, bell hooks, Judith Butler, and Angela Davis alongside concepts like intersectionality, patriarchy, and meritocracy.

Mid-century American structural functionalism, represented by Talcott Parsons and Robert K. Merton, contributes strain theory and middle-range theory as recurring answers, while a distinct conflict and critical-theory cluster spans C. Wright Mills's power elite, the Frankfurt School's Jurgen Habermas, and Michel Foucault's writing on power, knowledge, and prisons. Bourdieu's concepts of capital, habitus, and social capital connect to a separate network-theory cluster including weak ties, social networks, and Robert Putnam's civic-engagement research.

A smaller but consistent set of questions covers nations and global systems — Benedict Anderson's imagined communities, Immanuel Wallerstein's world-systems theory, and Charles Tilly's historical sociology of state formation and revolution — and the sociology of culture and media, including Marshall McLuhan, Daniel Bell's post-industrial society, and George Ritzer's McDonaldization thesis. Named works recur across these clusters as answers in their own right, among them The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life, The Sociological Imagination, Imagined Communities, and The Feminine Mystique.
