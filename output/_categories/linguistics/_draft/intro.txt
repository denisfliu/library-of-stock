Linguistics questions in this unit cluster heavily around a small set of foundational scholars and the theories named for them. Noam Chomsky is the single most-asked answerline, reflecting his centrality to generative grammar, Universal Grammar, and the innateness debate; he appears both under his own name and as "Avram Noam Chomsky." William Labov follows closely, anchoring the unit's sociolinguistics content through his variationist studies of Martha's Vineyard and New York City department stores. Ferdinand de Saussure, Edward Sapir, Benjamin Lee Whorf, George Lakoff, and Steven Pinker round out the roster of frequently cited theorists, with the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis of linguistic relativity appearing as a distinct high-frequency answerline in its own right.

Below the level of individual scholars, the canon distributes fairly evenly across the traditional core subfields. Phonetics and phonology contribute answerlines such as vowels, phonemes, tones, aspiration, vowel harmony, and click consonants, alongside transcription and theoretical apparatus like the International Phonetic Alphabet and optimality theory. Morphology supplies word classes (nouns, verbs, adjectives, pronouns) and grammatical categories (number, gender, case, evidentiality), while syntax contributes structural concepts including binding, X-bar theory, recursion, ellipsis, and word order. Semantics and pragmatics are represented by deictics, metaphor, politeness theory, and cross-linguistic color terminology.

A substantial share of questions test specific languages and language families rather than abstract theory. Japanese, Arabic, English, Sanskrit, French, Korean, and Spanish are the most frequently asked individual languages, while Celtic, Germanic, Romance, and Bantu languages recur as family-level answerlines alongside language isolates like Basque and Navajo. Historical and comparative linguistics is represented by Proto-Indo-European, the comparative method, and named sound laws such as Grimm's Law and Grassmann's Law, reflecting the canon's continued emphasis on Indo-European reconstruction.

A smaller but consistent cluster covers language contact and variation: code switching, African American Vernacular English, diglossia, and heritage language on the sociolinguistic side, and pidgins, creoles, Tok Pisin, Esperanto, and sign languages on the contact and constructed-language side. First and second language acquisition, Universal Grammar, and poverty-of-the-stimulus arguments round out a recurring psycholinguistic thread tied to the unit's emphasis on Chomskyan theory.
