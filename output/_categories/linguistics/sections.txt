# Key Linguists and Theories
> The scholars whose names anchor major linguistic hypotheses, from generative grammar to linguistic relativity.
Noam Chomsky | American linguist who founded generative grammar and Universal Grammar; wrote Syntactic Structures and critiqued behaviorist theories of language.
William Labov | Sociolinguist who pioneered quantitative variationist studies, including the Martha's Vineyard and New York City department store research.
Ferdinand de Saussure | Swiss linguist and founder of structuralism who distinguished langue from parole and signifier from signified.
Sapir-Whorf hypothesis | Idea that language shapes or constrains thought and perception, associated with Edward Sapir and Benjamin Lee Whorf.
Avram Noam Chomsky | Full name of the linguist behind generative grammar, Universal Grammar, and the minimalist program in syntax.
Benjamin Lee Whorf | American linguist who studied Hopi and argued language influences thought, co-namesake of the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis.
Edward Sapir | American linguist and anthropologist who studied Native American languages and co-developed linguistic relativity with Whorf.
George Lakoff | Cognitive linguist known for conceptual metaphor theory, co-author of Metaphors We Live By with Mark Johnson.
Lakoff | Short-form reference to George Lakoff, cognitive linguist known for conceptual metaphor theory and framing research.
Steven Arthur Pinker | Full name of the cognitive psychologist and linguist known for The Language Instinct and nativist views on acquisition.
Steven Pinker | Cognitive scientist who popularized Chomskyan linguistics in The Language Instinct and argued for an innate language faculty.
William "Bill" Labov | Sociolinguist famous for the Martha's Vineyard and New York City department store studies on sound change and social stratification.

# Phonetics, Phonology, and Writing Systems
> The sounds of speech, how they pattern into systems, and the scripts and frameworks used to describe them.
vowels | Speech sounds produced without obstruction of airflow, classified by tongue height, backness, and lip rounding.
phonemes | Smallest contrastive sound units in a language whose substitution changes meaning, identified via minimal pairs.
phonology | Subfield studying how speech sounds pattern within a language, including processes like assimilation and epenthesis.
optimality theory | Phonological framework in which surface forms emerge from ranked, violable constraints rather than ordered rules.
tones | Pitch patterns that distinguish word meaning in tonal languages such as Mandarin and many African languages.
aspiration | Puff of air accompanying certain consonants, phonemically contrastive in Hindi and Sanskrit but allophonic in English.
vowel harmony | Phonological process requiring vowels within a word to share features like backness, found in Turkish and Finnish.
click consonants | Consonants produced with a suction airstream mechanism, found in Khoisan languages and in Zulu.
r | Common-link answerline on rhotic consonants, whose realization as trill, tap, or approximant varies across languages.
rhoticity | Presence or absence of pronounced r sounds, a key variable distinguishing dialects like American from British English.
syllables | Units of speech organization built around a nucleus, typically a vowel, flanked by onset and coda consonants.
tone | Use of pitch to distinguish lexical or grammatical meaning, as in Mandarin, Vietnamese, and many African languages.
abugida | Writing system in which consonant-vowel sequences are written as units with vowels marked by diacritics, as in Devanagari.
fricatives | Consonants produced by forcing air through a narrow channel, creating turbulence, such as f, s, and sh.
IPA | International Phonetic Alphabet, a standardized system of symbols for transcribing the sounds of any spoken language.
lips | Articulators used to produce labial and bilabial sounds; common-link answerline tying together lip-articulated sounds.
sandhi | Phonological process altering sounds at word or morpheme boundaries, as in Sanskrit's extensive sandhi rules.
schwa | The mid-central unstressed vowel sound, the most common vowel in English, often written as an inverted e.

# Morphology and Grammatical Categories
> How words are built from smaller units and how languages grammatically mark categories like number, gender, and case.
verbs | Word class denoting actions, states, or occurrences, central to predicate structure across languages.
morphemes | Smallest meaningful units of language, combining as roots and affixes to build words.
pronouns | Word class substituting for nouns or noun phrases, including personal, demonstrative, and relative types.
grammatical number | Grammatical category marking quantity distinctions such as singular, plural, and dual across nouns and verbs.
inflection | Morphological process modifying a word to express categories like tense, case, number, or gender.
accusative case | Grammatical case marking the direct object of a transitive verb in many inflected languages.
adjectives | Word class modifying nouns by describing their qualities or properties.
evidentiality | Grammatical category marking the source of information for a statement, obligatory in Quechua and Turkish.
grammatical gender | Noun classification system, often masculine, feminine, or neuter, that triggers agreement on adjectives and verbs.
nouns | Word class denoting people, places, things, or concepts, typically inflecting for number and case.
reduplication | Morphological process repeating all or part of a word to mark categories like plurality or intensity.
agglutination | Morphological type where words form by stringing together clearly segmentable affixes, typical of Turkish and Korean.
dual number | Grammatical number category marking exactly two entities, distinct from singular and plural, found in Sanskrit and Arabic.
gender | Grammatical noun-class system, often masculine, feminine, or neuter, requiring agreement with adjectives and verbs.
grammatical cases | System of noun and pronoun inflection marking grammatical role, as in Latin's nominative, genitive, dative, and accusative.
markedness | Theoretical concept describing how one member of a linguistic opposition is more basic or unmarked than the other.
morphology | Subfield studying the internal structure of words, including roots, stems, and affixes.
numbers | Common-link answerline on numeral systems and how languages express counting and grammatical number.
transitive verbs | Verbs that take a direct object, contrasted with intransitive verbs.
verb | Word class denoting actions or states, central to predicate and tense/aspect marking.

# Syntax
> The rules governing how words combine into phrases and sentences.
questions | Common-link answerline on interrogative sentence formation, including wh-movement and yes/no question structures across languages.
syntax | Subfield studying how words combine into phrases and sentences according to structural rules.
binding | Syntactic theory governing the distribution of pronouns, reflexives, and anaphora within a sentence.
X-bar theory | Chomskyan framework positing a uniform phrase structure template with head, complement, and specifier positions.
ellipsis | Syntactic phenomenon in which understood material is omitted from a sentence, as in "I can, and you can too."
recursion | Property allowing structures to embed within structures of the same type, cited by Chomsky as a hallmark of language.
grammar | Common-link answerline on the system of rules governing a language's structure, spanning syntax and morphology.
head | The word that determines the grammatical category and properties of a phrase, central to X-bar theory.
negation | Syntactic and semantic process of denying or reversing the truth of a proposition, marked variously across languages.
raising | Syntactic construction in which the subject of an embedded clause appears as the subject of a higher verb.
word order | Typological classification of languages by the relative order of subject, verb, and object.

# Semantics and Pragmatics
> How meaning is encoded in language and shaped by context, inference, and figurative thought.
pragmatics | Subfield studying how context, speaker intention, and inference shape meaning beyond literal sentence content.
semantics | Subfield studying the literal meaning of words, phrases, and sentences.
deictics | Words like "here," "now," and "I" whose reference depends entirely on the context of utterance.
metaphors | Figurative mappings between conceptual domains, studied in George Lakoff's conceptual metaphor theory.
colors | Common-link answerline on cross-linguistic color terminology, studied since Berlin and Kay's universal color-term hierarchy.
gestures | Nonverbal communicative movements studied alongside speech in multimodal and pragmatic research on meaning-making.
politeness | Pragmatic theory, notably Brown and Levinson's face-saving framework, describing strategies for softening face-threatening speech.
time | Common-link answerline on how languages encode temporal reference through tense, aspect, and time adverbials.

# Historical and Comparative Linguistics
> Sound change, reconstruction, and the family trees connecting related languages over time.
Proto-Indo-European | Reconstructed ancestor of the Indo-European family, recovered via the comparative method from Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin.
Grimm's Law | Sound-shift pattern describing systematic consonant changes from Proto-Indo-European to Proto-Germanic, formulated by Jacob Grimm.
comparative method | Technique reconstructing proto-languages by systematically comparing sound correspondences among related languages.
Grassmann's law | Sound law describing dissimilation of aspirated consonants in Sanskrit and Ancient Greek, formulated by Hermann Grassmann.

# Sociolinguistics
> Variation in language tied to community, identity, social class, and context of use.
code switching | Alternation between two or more languages or varieties within a single conversation by bilingual or multilingual speakers.
African American Vernacular English | Systematic English variety with distinctive phonological, grammatical, and lexical features, studied extensively by William Labov.
women | Common-link answerline on sociolinguistic findings, notably Labov's, that women often lead change toward prestige forms.
codes | Common-link answerline evoking Basil Bernstein's theory of restricted and elaborated speech codes tied to social class.
diglossia | Situation in which two language varieties, a "high" and a "low" form, are used in complementary social functions.
heritage language | A language learned at home from family that is not the dominant language of the surrounding society.
Martha's Vineyard | Site of William Labov's landmark study linking vowel centralization to local identity and resistance to outside influence.
prestige | Social value attached to a language variety that influences which forms speakers adopt or avoid.

# Language Acquisition and Psycholinguistics
> How children and adults acquire language and the mental processes and debates behind it.
first language acquisition | Process by which children naturally acquire their native language, central to debates over innateness.
language acquisition | General term for how humans acquire language, whether as a first or subsequent language.
second language acquisition | Study of how people learn a language after their first, examining input, age effects, and interference.
poverty of the stimulus | Argument that children acquire grammar despite limited and imperfect input, used to support innate Universal Grammar.
Universal Grammar | Chomsky's proposed innate linguistic structure shared by all humans that constrains the grammars children can acquire.

# Language Contact, Creoles, and Constructed Languages
> Languages that arise from contact between speech communities, plus languages deliberately invented rather than naturally evolved.
calques | Loan translations in which a foreign expression's parts are translated literally into the borrowing language.
creoles | Stable natural languages that develop from the nativization of a pidgin, acquiring full grammatical complexity.
Esperanto | Constructed international auxiliary language created by L. L. Zamenhof in 1887 to facilitate global communication.
sign languages | Manual-visual languages with full grammatical structure, such as American Sign Language, distinct from spoken modality.
Tok Pisin | English-based creole and official language of Papua New Guinea, developed from a Pacific trade pidgin.
creole languages | Natural languages that emerge when a pidgin becomes the native language of a speech community.
loanwords | Words borrowed from one language into another, often adapted to the borrowing language's phonology and morphology.
pidgins | Simplified contact languages that develop for limited communication between groups without a shared language, lacking native speakers.

# Language Families and Individual Languages
> Specific languages and language families that recur as answerlines, spanning every inhabited continent.
Japanese | East Asian language with subject-object-verb order, agglutinative morphology, and multiple honorific speech levels.
Arabic | Semitic language with templatic root-and-pattern morphology, official across the Middle East and North Africa.
English | West Germanic language with limited inflection, relatively fixed word order, and heavy French and Latin borrowing.
Sanskrit | Classical Indo-Aryan language of Hindu scripture, with an elaborate case system and grammar codified by Panini.
French | Romance language descended from Latin, official in France and much of Francophone Africa and Canada.
Australia | Continent home to hundreds of indigenous Aboriginal languages alongside Australian English.
Korean | East Asian language with agglutinative morphology and the Hangul writing system, often classified as a language isolate.
Spanish | Romance language descended from Latin, the most widely spoken Romance language, official across Spain and Latin America.
Ancient Greek | Indo-European language of Homer and classical Greece, with rich case, aspect, and dialectal variation.
Celtic languages | Indo-European branch including Irish, Welsh, and Scottish Gaelic, known for initial consonant mutations.
Romance languages | Languages descended from Vulgar Latin, including Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese, and Romanian.
Bantu languages | Large Niger-Congo language family of sub-Saharan Africa featuring extensive noun-class systems.
Basque | Language isolate spoken in northern Spain and southern France, unrelated to any other known language family.
Commonwealth of Australia | Official name of Australia, whose linguistic landscape includes Australian English and numerous endangered Aboriginal languages.
Germanic languages | Indo-European branch including English, German, and the Scandinavian languages, shaped historically by Grimm's Law.
Latin | Classical Italic language of ancient Rome, ancestor of the Romance languages, with an elaborate case and conjugation system.
Navajo | Athabaskan language of the American Southwest, known for complex verb morphology and use as a WWII code.
Russian | East Slavic language with a six-case system and Cyrillic script, widely spoken across Eastern Europe and Central Asia.
Swedish | North Germanic language with pitch accent, spoken primarily in Sweden and parts of Finland.
Yiddish | Historically Germanic language written in Hebrew script, traditionally spoken by Ashkenazi Jews across Central and Eastern Europe.
