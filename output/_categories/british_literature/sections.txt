# Old English & Anglo-Saxon Literature
> Alliterative verse and prose in the earliest form of English, running from Caedmon through the Norman Conquest of 1066.
Beowulf | Anonymous Old English epic of a Geatish hero who slays Grendel, Grendel's mother, and a dragon.
Old English | Earliest stage of the English language, used in Beowulf and Anglo-Saxon poetry before roughly 1150.
Grendel | Monster slain by the hero in Beowulf, along with Grendel's mother and later a dragon.
riddles | Old English poetic form preserved in the Exeter Book, posing clever double-meaning puzzles.
alliteration | Key structural device of Old English verse, linking stressed syllables across the poetic line as in Beowulf.
Caedmon | Earliest known named English poet, a herdsman said to have received the gift of song in a dream.
The Dream of the Rood | Old English dream-vision poem in which the cross (rood) itself narrates Christ's crucifixion.
Bede | Anglo-Saxon monk-historian; the Ecclesiastical History of the English People, "the father of English history."
Exeter Book | Major manuscript anthology of Old English poetry, preserving riddles, elegies, and religious verse.
Grendel's mother | Vengeful monster slain by Beowulf after he kills her son Grendel.
kennings | Compound metaphorical phrases, such as "whale-road" for the sea, characteristic of Old English poetry.
Taliesin | Legendary early medieval Welsh bard credited with a body of Brythonic praise poetry.
The Venerable Bede | Anglo-Saxon monk-historian; the Ecclesiastical History of the English People, "the father of English history."

# Middle English Literature
> Vernacular poetry and prose written after 1066 under Norman and continental influence, culminating in Chaucer's generation.
Geoffrey Chaucer | Father of English poetry; The Canterbury Tales and Troilus and Criseyde, written in Middle English.
The Canterbury Tales | Chaucer's frame narrative of pilgrims to Canterbury telling tales, including the Wife of Bath and the Pardoner.
Middle English | Stage of the English language, used by Chaucer and the Gawain-poet, between Old English and the modern tongue.
Sir Gawain and the Green Knight | Anonymous alliterative romance in which Gawain accepts a beheading challenge from a mysterious green knight.
Everyman | Anonymous medieval morality play in which the allegorical figure Everyman is summoned by Death.
the Wife of Bath = Wife of Bath | Chaucer's oft-married, outspoken pilgrim in The Canterbury Tales who argues for female authority in marriage.
The Pearl Poet | Anonymous author credited with Sir Gawain and the Green Knight, Pearl, and other alliterative Middle English poems.
John Gower | Middle English poet and contemporary of Chaucer; Confessio Amantis, a collection of moral tales.
morality plays | Medieval allegorical drama genre, exemplified by Everyman, teaching moral lessons through personified virtues and vices.
Sir Gawain | Arthurian knight who accepts the Green Knight's beheading challenge in the alliterative romance bearing his name.
Thomas More | Early Tudor humanist statesman and author of Utopia, executed under Henry VIII.
"The Knight's Tale" | First and longest tale in Chaucer's Canterbury Tales, of two knights' rivalry for the same woman.
the Pardoner | Corrupt seller of indulgences among Chaucer's Canterbury Tales pilgrims, whose tale warns against greed.
Utopia | Thomas More's work describing an ideal imagined island society, coining the term "utopia."
William Caxton | Introduced the printing press to England in 1476, printing Chaucer's Canterbury Tales and Malory's Morte d'Arthur.
"The Miller's Tale" | Bawdy fabliau from Chaucer's Canterbury Tales involving a cuckolded carpenter and a misdirected kiss.
Gawain | Arthurian knight who accepts the Green Knight's beheading challenge in the alliterative romance bearing his name.
John Skelton | Early Tudor poet known for "Skeltonics," short, driving rhymed lines bridging medieval and Renaissance verse.
Julian of Norwich | Medieval English mystic and anchoress; Revelations of Divine Love, the earliest surviving English-language book by a woman.
King Arthur | Legendary British king whose court and knights, including Gawain, populate medieval English romance.
makars | Term for medieval and early-modern Scottish court poets, including Dunbar and Henryson.
Marie de France | Anglo-Norman poet at the English court who wrote the Lais, short verse romances.
Pearl | Anonymous elegiac dream-vision poem, attributed to the Gawain-poet, mourning a lost child or "pearl."
Piers Plowman | William Langland's allegorical dream-vision poem following Piers the Plowman's search for religious truth.
Sir Thomas More | Early Tudor humanist statesman and author of Utopia, executed under Henry VIII.
Troilus | Trojan prince whose doomed love for Criseyde is chronicled in Chaucer's Troilus and Criseyde.
Troilus and Criseyde | Chaucer's tragic romance of a Trojan prince betrayed by his beloved Criseyde for the Greek Diomedes.
"The Pardoner's Tale" | Chaucer's Canterbury tale of three revelers who find death by greedily fighting over gold.
Le Morte d'Arthur | Thomas Malory's compilation of Arthurian legend, printed by Caxton in 1485.
mystery plays | Medieval biblical drama cycles performed by town guilds, precursors to English secular theater.
Robin Hood | Legendary English outlaw of medieval ballads, robbing the rich to aid the poor from Sherwood Forest.
The Book of the Duchess | Chaucer's early dream-vision elegy, likely written for John of Gaunt's deceased first wife.
the General Prologue to the Canterbury Tales | Chaucer's opening frame introducing each of the pilgrims journeying to Canterbury.
The Miller | Drunken, bawdy pilgrim among Chaucer's Canterbury Tales narrators, teller of "The Miller's Tale."
Wakefield | Northern English town giving its name to the Wakefield Cycle of medieval mystery plays.

# Elizabethan & Jacobean Literature
> Poetry and drama of Shakespeare's era and the reigns of Elizabeth I and James I, from sonnet sequences to revenge tragedy.
Ben Jonson | Jacobean playwright and poet, Shakespeare's rival and friend; Volpone, The Alchemist, Every Man in His Humour.
Christopher Marlowe | Elizabethan dramatist and Shakespeare's rival; Doctor Faustus, Tamburlaine the Great, The Jew of Malta.
William Shakespeare | Elizabethan-Jacobean playwright and poet; tragedies, comedies, and histories plus 154 sonnets; central figure of English drama.
King Lear | Shakespeare tragedy of an aging king who divides his kingdom among his daughters and descends into madness.
Edmund Spenser | Elizabethan poet of the allegorical epic The Faerie Queene and the sonnet sequence Amoretti.

Hamlet | Shakespeare's tragedy of the Danish prince who feigns madness to avenge his father, murdered by Claudius.

Sir Philip Sidney | Elizabethan courtier-poet; sonnet sequence Astrophil and Stella, prose romance The Countess of Pembroke's Arcadia.
The Tempest | Shakespeare's late romance of exiled magician Prospero, his daughter Miranda, and spirits Ariel and Caliban.
As You Like It | Shakespeare pastoral comedy set in the Forest of Arden, featuring the cross-dressing Rosalind and melancholy Jaques.
Macbeth | Shakespeare tragedy of a Scottish general driven by prophecy and Lady Macbeth's ambition to murder King Duncan.
Richard III | Shakespeare history play of the scheming, physically deformed Richard's ruthless rise to the English throne.
John Webster | Jacobean revenge tragedian; The Duchess of Malfi, on a widowed duchess destroyed by her vengeful brothers.
Othello | Shakespeare tragedy of the Moorish general destroyed by Iago's manipulation into jealousy over Desdemona.
Titus Andronicus | Shakespeare's earliest, bloodiest tragedy, featuring the Roman general's revenge against Tamora and Aaron.
Volpone | Ben Jonson's satirical comedy of a Venetian miser who feigns death to swindle legacy-hunters.
Henry V | Shakespeare history play following the young king's rousing leadership at the Battle of Agincourt.
Measure for Measure | Shakespeare "problem play" of a deputy's abuse of power and a novice nun's moral test.
The Alchemist | Ben Jonson's comedy of con artists running elaborate scams from a house in plague-emptied London.
Love's Labour's Lost | Shakespeare comedy of a king and his lords who forswear women's company, only to promptly fall in love.
Sir John Falstaff | Shakespeare's comic, cowardly knight and companion to Prince Hal in the Henry IV plays.
The Merchant of Venice | Shakespeare comedy in which moneylender Shylock demands a pound of flesh from merchant Antonio.

Mark Antony | Roman triumvir whose love for Cleopatra destroys his political standing in Shakespeare's tragedy.
Philip Sidney | Elizabethan courtier-poet, author of Astrophil and Stella; usually styled Sir Philip Sidney.

Elizabeth I | Tudor queen whose reign gave its name to Elizabethan literature and who was celebrated in Spenser's Faerie Queene.
George Chapman | Elizabethan-Jacobean poet and playwright best known for his translation of Homer.
Malvolio | Puritanical, self-important steward tricked into absurd behavior in Shakespeare's Twelfth Night.
Mercutio | Romeo's witty, ill-fated friend in Shakespeare's Romeo and Juliet, killed by Tybalt.
Ophelia | Hamlet's beloved, driven to madness and drowning after her father Polonius's death in Shakespeare's tragedy.

Thomas Middleton | Prolific Jacobean playwright; The Changeling, A Chaste Maid in Cheapside, likely co-author of The Revenger's Tragedy.
Thomas Wyatt | Early Tudor courtier-poet who introduced the sonnet form to English poetry.

Astrophil and Stella | Sir Philip Sidney's pioneering English sonnet sequence chronicling a courtier's unrequited love.

Desdemona | Othello's devoted wife, falsely suspected of infidelity and murdered by him in Shakespeare's tragedy.
Philip Massinger | Jacobean-Caroline playwright; A New Way to Pay Old Debts, known for the villain Sir Giles Overreach.

# Metaphysical & Civil War-Era Poetry
> Seventeenth-century poets known for wit and conceits, alongside Milton and the literature surrounding the English Civil War.
John Donne | Founder of Metaphysical poetry; Holy Sonnets and elaborate conceits; later Dean of St. Paul's writing devotional prose.
John Milton | Puritan poet of the English Civil War era; epic Paradise Lost, Areopagitica, Samson Agonistes, "Lycidas."
Andrew Marvell | Metaphysical poet and Civil War-era politician; "To His Coy Mistress," "The Garden."

Oliver Cromwell | Puritan Lord Protector of England after the Civil War execution of Charles I; subject of Marvell's Horatian Ode.
Robert Herrick | Cavalier poet; "To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time," carpe-diem lyrics of nature and love.
"Lycidas" | Milton's pastoral elegy mourning a drowned Cambridge classmate under the shepherd name Lycidas.

Thomas Browne | 17th-century physician and prose stylist; Religio Medici, meditative essays on faith and mortality.
