British Literature questions draw most heavily on the Victorian and modernist canons, with Thomas Hardy, George Bernard Shaw, Harold Pinter, John Keats, John Donne, Gerard Manley Hopkins, Virginia Woolf, and Charles Dickens forming the most frequently asked answerlines. Poetry is tested across every era, from Old English alliterative verse and Chaucer's Middle English through the Metaphysical poets, the Romantics (Wordsworth, Coleridge, Blake, Byron, Shelley, Keats), and the Victorians (Tennyson, Browning, Arnold) to twentieth-century figures such as Yeats, Eliot, Auden, Larkin, and Heaney.

Drama clusters around two poles: Shakespeare and his Elizabethan-Jacobean contemporaries (Marlowe, Jonson, Webster, Middleton) on one end, and twentieth-century British and Irish playwrights — Shaw, Wilde, Beckett, Pinter, and Stoppard, largely under the banners of the Theatre of the Absurd and Angry Young Men — on the other. The novel is dominated by Victorian realists (Dickens, Eliot, the Brontes, Thackeray, Hardy) and by modernists and their heirs (Joyce, Woolf, Forster, Conrad, Lawrence), with a substantial contemporary tail of Booker-adjacent and postcolonial writers including Ishiguro, Rushdie, Zadie Smith, Atwood, and Mantel.

Ireland supplies an outsized share of the canon across every era — Swift and Sheridan in the eighteenth century, Wilde in the fin de siecle, Yeats, Joyce, and Beckett in modernism, and Heaney among Nobel laureates — reflecting the shared literary history bundled under "British Literature." Scotland (Burns, Scott, Muriel Spark) and Wales (Dylan Thomas) contribute smaller but recurring clusters. A long tail of generic motifs (gardens, the sea, swans, dreams) and non-British comparanda (Dante, Virgil, Moliere, Homer, the Greek and Roman classics) also recurs, usually as source material, analogues, or common-link scaffolding rather than as primary British-literature answers.
