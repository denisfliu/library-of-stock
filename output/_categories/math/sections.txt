# Foundations & Logic
> Set theory, axioms, and the logical bedrock underlying all of mathematics.
David Hilbert | German mathematician who posed 23 problems in 1900; foundational work on Hilbert spaces and formalism in logic.
continuum hypothesis | Claims no set has cardinality strictly between the integers and the reals; shown independent of ZFC by Godel and Cohen.
sets | Collections of distinct objects; the basic object of set theory, described via the Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms.
Georg Cantor | Founder of set theory; proved the reals are uncountable via diagonalization and introduced transfinite cardinal numbers.
axiom of choice | Set-theoretic axiom that any collection of nonempty sets has a choice function; independent of ZF, implies the Banach-Tarski paradox.
induction | Proof technique establishing a base case, then a step showing truth for n implies truth for n+1.
empty set | The unique set containing no elements, a subset of every set.
Kurt Godel | Logician whose incompleteness theorems show any consistent formal system contains truths it cannot prove.
Cantor set | Fractal subset of [0,1] formed by repeatedly removing middle thirds; uncountable yet has measure zero.
Cartesian product | Set of all ordered pairs, or tuples, formed from two or more sets.
finite | Describes a set or quantity with a limited, countable number of elements.
forcing | Technique developed by Paul Cohen to prove independence results in set theory, e.g. of the continuum hypothesis.
John von Neumann | Hungarian-American polymath with contributions to set theory, game theory, and computer architecture.
Banach-Tarski paradox | States a solid ball can be decomposed and reassembled into two balls identical to the original, using the axiom of choice.
closure | Smallest closed set containing a given set, or the property that an operation stays within a set.
countable | Describes a set whose elements can be put into one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers.
infinite | Describes a set or quantity without bound, explored via Cantor's theory of cardinalities.
intersection | Set of elements common to two or more sets.
power set | Set of all subsets of a given set, including the empty set and the set itself.
Well-Ordering Principle | States every nonempty set of positive integers has a least element; equivalent to induction.

# Number Theory
> The study of integers, primes, and the equations and conjectures built from them.
prime numbers | Integers greater than 1 divisible only by 1 and themselves; the building blocks of arithmetic via unique factorization.
Carl Friedrich Gauss | "Prince of mathematicians"; wrote Disquisitiones Arithmeticae, contributed to the normal distribution and Gaussian elimination.
Fermat's Last Theorem | States no positive integers a,b,c satisfy a^n+b^n=c^n for n>2; conjectured in 1637, proved by Andrew Wiles in 1994.
rational numbers | Numbers expressible as a ratio of integers, denoted Q; a countable set contrasted with the irrationals.
integers | The set of whole numbers including negatives and zero, denoted Z.
Pierre de Fermat | 17th-century French lawyer-mathematician; namesake of Fermat's Last Theorem, Fermat's little theorem, and early probability work.
real numbers | The complete ordered field including rationals and irrationals, denoted R.
Riemann zeta function | Complex function whose nontrivial zeros are conjectured, per the Riemann hypothesis, to have real part 1/2.
elliptic curves | Curves of form y^2=x^3+ax+b; central to the proof of Fermat's Last Theorem and to modern cryptography.
Chinese remainder theorem | Gives a unique solution modulo the product of pairwise coprime moduli for a system of simultaneous congruences.
complex numbers | Numbers of form a+bi with i=sqrt(-1); form an algebraically closed field, denoted C.
Srinivasa Ramanujan | Self-taught Indian mathematician, prolific in number theory and infinite series; mentored by G.H. Hardy.
Pell's equation | Diophantine equation x^2-Dy^2=1 with integer solutions found via continued fractions.
Riemann hypothesis | Conjecture that all nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function have real part 1/2; an unsolved Millennium Prize problem.
Diophantine equations | Polynomial equations for which only integer solutions are sought, named for Diophantus.
law of quadratic reciprocity | Gauss's theorem relating the solvability of quadratic congruences modulo two odd primes.
natural numbers | The positive integers, sometimes including zero, used for counting.
p-adic numbers | Number system extending the rationals using a notion of distance based on divisibility by a prime p.
prime | An integer greater than 1 with no positive divisors other than 1 and itself.
abc conjecture | States that for coprime integers a+b=c, the product of distinct prime factors of abc is rarely much smaller than c.
arithmetic progressions | Sequences with a constant difference between consecutive terms.
Euler totient function | Counts positive integers up to n coprime to n; central to RSA cryptography.
Euler's totient function | Counts positive integers up to n coprime to n; central to RSA cryptography.
fundamental theorem of arithmetic | States every integer greater than 1 has a unique factorization into primes.
greatest common divisor | Largest positive integer dividing two or more integers, found via the Euclidean algorithm.
Mersenne primes | Primes of the form 2^p-1, searched for via distributed computing projects like GIMPS.
perfect numbers | Positive integers equal to the sum of their proper divisors, e.g. 6 and 28.
real | Refers to the real numbers, the complete ordered field including rationals and irrationals.
arithmetic | Branch of mathematics dealing with numbers and basic operations; also used for number-theoretic study of integers.
Brahmagupta | 7th-century Indian mathematician who formalized rules for zero and negative numbers and gave a formula for cyclic quadrilateral area.
composite | A positive integer with more than two divisors, i.e. not prime.
continued fractions | Expressions representing numbers as nested fractions, used to approximate irrationals.
Johann Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet | German mathematician behind Dirichlet's theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions and the modern definition of a function.
prime factorization | Expression of an integer as a product of prime numbers.
primes | Integers greater than 1 divisible only by 1 and themselves.
sieve of Eratosthenes | Ancient algorithm for finding all primes up to a limit by iteratively marking multiples of each prime.
twin primes | Pairs of primes differing by two, e.g. 11 and 13; conjectured to be infinite in number.

# Algebra
> Groups, rings, fields, and the structures and symmetries that generalize arithmetic.
polynomials | Algebraic expressions of terms with variables raised to non-negative integer powers; central to factoring, roots, and interpolation.
eigenvalues | Scalars satisfying Av=v for a matrix and eigenvector; found via the characteristic polynomial, central to diagonalization.
determinant | Scalar computed from a square matrix encoding volume scaling and invertibility; zero determinant means the matrix is singular.
groups | Sets with an associative binary operation, identity, and inverses; the basic object of abstract algebra encoding symmetry.
commutative | Property that order of operation doesn't matter (ab=ba); defines commutative rings and abelian groups.
trace | Sum of a square matrix's diagonal entries, equal to the sum of its eigenvalues.
commutativity | Property that a binary operation's order doesn't affect the result (ab=ba).
Kernel | Set of elements mapped to the identity or zero by a homomorphism; measures the map's failure to be injective.
orthogonality | Property of vectors or functions with zero inner product; basis of orthogonal bases and Fourier analysis.
matrices | Rectangular arrays of numbers representing linear transformations, manipulated via addition, multiplication, and inversion.
Niels Henrik Abel | Norwegian mathematician who proved the general quintic has no algebraic solution; namesake of abelian groups.
orthogonal | Describes vectors with zero inner product, or matrices whose columns form an orthonormal basis.
spectrum | Set of eigenvalues of a linear operator or matrix, generalized in operator theory.
rings | Algebraic structures with two operations, addition and multiplication, generalizing the integers.
category theory | Branch studying mathematical structures via objects and morphisms, abstracting common patterns across fields.
characteristic polynomial | Polynomial det(A- I) whose roots are a matrix's eigenvalues.
conjugation | Operation gag^-1 in group theory, or the complex conjugate a-bi of a+bi.
fields | Algebraic structures where addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division (except by zero) are defined, e.g. Q, R, C.
lattices | Partially ordered sets where every pair of elements has a unique supremum and infimum.
matrix | Rectangular array of numbers representing a linear transformation.
quadratic | Describes a polynomial or equation of degree two, solved via the quadratic formula.
rank | Dimension of a matrix's column or row space, i.e. the number of linearly independent rows or columns.
roots | Solutions to an equation where a function equals zero.
vectors | Quantities with magnitude and direction, elements of a vector space combined via addition and scalar multiplication.
Alexander Grothendieck | 20th-century French-German mathematician who reshaped algebraic geometry via scheme theory and category theory.
associativity | Property that grouping of operations doesn't change the result: (ab)c=a(bc).
basis | Set of linearly independent vectors that span a vector space.
Camille Jordan | French mathematician; namesake of the Jordan normal form, Jordan curve theorem, and Jordan-Holder theorem.
Cauchy-Schwarz inequality | States the absolute value of an inner product of two vectors is at most the product of their norms.
diagonal | Entries of a matrix running from top-left to bottom-right, or Cantor's diagonal argument technique.
Evariste Galois | French mathematician who founded Galois theory linking field extensions to group theory, showing quintics unsolvable by radicals; died in a duel at 20.
group representations | Ways of expressing group elements as matrices acting on a vector space.
linear | Describes equations or functions of degree one, or maps preserving addition and scalar multiplication.
quaternions | Four-dimensional number system extending the complex numbers, discovered by Hamilton, with noncommutative multiplication.
roots of unity | Complex numbers that yield 1 when raised to a positive integer power; vertices of a regular polygon on the unit circle.
symmetric | Describes a matrix equal to its transpose, or an object invariant under some transformation.
vector spaces | Sets of vectors closed under addition and scalar multiplication, satisfying field-like axioms.
abelian | Describes a group whose operation is commutative, named for Niels Abel.
cross product | Binary operation on two vectors in three dimensions yielding a vector perpendicular to both.
eigenvalue | Scalar such that Av=v for some nonzero vector v and matrix A.
ideals | Special subsets of a ring closed under addition and absorption of multiplication by ring elements.
identity matrix | Square matrix with ones on the diagonal and zeros elsewhere, acting as the multiplicative identity.
injective | Describes a function that maps distinct inputs to distinct outputs, i.e. one-to-one.
inverse | Element or function that reverses another's effect, e.g. a matrix's inverse or a function's inverse.
projection | Linear map sending a vector space onto a subspace, idempotent when applied twice.
tensors | Multilinear generalizations of scalars, vectors, and matrices used in physics and differential geometry.
algebraic | Describes numbers or expressions that are roots of polynomial equations with rational coefficients.
Arthur Cayley | British mathematician and matrix algebra pioneer; namesake of the Cayley-Hamilton theorem and Cayley graphs.
basis set | Set of linearly independent vectors spanning a vector space.
centralizer | Subgroup of elements that commute with a given element or subset of a group.
characteristic | Smallest number of times one must add a ring's multiplicative identity to get zero, or zero if this never happens.
classification | Refers to results like the classification of finite simple groups, organizing structures into complete lists.
conjugate | Complex number a-bi paired with a+bi, or elements related by gag^-1 in group theory.
cyclic | Describes a group generated by a single element.
degree | Highest power of a variable in a polynomial, or the number of edges meeting at a graph vertex.
dot product | Operation on two vectors yielding a scalar, equal to the sum of products of corresponding components.
dual space | Vector space of linear functionals mapping a given vector space to its underlying field.
Emil Artin | 20th-century mathematician who contributed to class field theory and Artin's reciprocity law and co-developed modern algebra.
Emmy Noether | German mathematician with foundational work on Noetherian rings and Noether's theorem linking symmetries to conservation laws.
field | Algebraic structure with addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division, except by zero.
fundamental theorem of algebra | States every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has at least one complex root.
Galois | Evariste Galois, French mathematician who linked polynomial solvability to group theory.
Galois theory | Connects field extensions to group theory, explaining why quintic equations have no general radical solution.
Gram-Schmidt process | Algorithm converting a set of vectors into an orthogonal or orthonormal basis.
homomorphism | Structure-preserving map between two algebraic objects of the same type.
identity element | Element that leaves other elements unchanged under a given operation, e.g. 0 for addition or 1 for multiplication.
invertible | Describes a matrix or function that has an inverse.
irreducible | Describes a polynomial that cannot be factored into lower-degree polynomials over a given field.
Jordan-Holder theorem | States composition series of a finite group are unique up to permutation and isomorphism.
Lagrange's theorem | States the order of a subgroup divides the order of its finite parent group.
matrix decomposition | Factoring a matrix into a product of simpler matrices, e.g. LU, QR, or singular value decomposition.
Niels Abel | Norwegian mathematician who proved the general quintic is unsolvable by radicals; namesake of abelian groups.
norm | Function assigning a nonnegative length or size to vectors in a vector space.
normal subgroups | Subgroups invariant under conjugation by any element of the parent group; the kernels of homomorphisms.
positive definite | Describes a matrix whose associated quadratic form is always positive for nonzero vectors.
prime ideals | Ideals of a ring analogous to prime numbers, whose quotient rings are integral domains.
product | Result of multiplying two or more quantities.
Schur's lemma | States homomorphisms between irreducible representations are either zero or isomorphisms.
simple | Describes a group with no nontrivial normal subgroups, the building blocks of finite group classification.
singular values | Nonnegative square roots of eigenvalues of A*A, used in the singular value decomposition of a matrix.
Sophus Lie | Norwegian mathematician and founder of Lie groups and Lie algebras describing continuous symmetry.
sparse | Describes a matrix or data structure with mostly zero or absent entries.
sporadic groups | The 26 finite simple groups that don't fit into the standard infinite families, including the Monster group.
Sylow theorems | Results guaranteeing the existence and properties of subgroups of prime-power order within a finite group.
symmetric groups | Groups of all permutations of a finite set.
symmetry | Property of an object invariant under some transformation, studied via group theory.
William Rowan Hamilton | Irish mathematician who discovered quaternions and formulated Hamiltonian mechanics.

# Analysis & Calculus
> Limits, derivatives, integrals, series, and the machinery of continuous change.
Augustin-Louis Cauchy | French mathematician who rigorized analysis; namesake of Cauchy sequences, the Cauchy-Riemann equations, and Cauchy's integral formula.
continuity | Property of a function whose small input changes produce small output changes; formalized via epsilon-delta limits.
integration | Operation finding area under a curve or an antiderivative; inverse of differentiation via the fundamental theorem of calculus.
interpolation | Estimating values between known data points, e.g. via Lagrange polynomials or splines.
Leonhard Euler | Prolific 18th-century Swiss mathematician; introduced e and Euler's formula/identity, and founded graph theory via Konigsberg's bridges.
Fourier transform | Decomposes a function into constituent frequencies; foundational to signal processing and partial differential equations.
convergence | Property of a sequence or series approaching a limit; assessed via tests like the ratio and root tests.
differentiation | Operation finding a function's instantaneous rate of change; inverse of integration.
Taylor series | Represents a function as an infinite sum of terms built from its derivatives at a point.
gradient | Vector of partial derivatives pointing in the direction of steepest increase of a multivariable function.
Karl Weierstrass | "Father of modern analysis" who rigorized epsilon-delta calculus; gave an everywhere-continuous, nowhere-differentiable function.
convolution | Operation combining two functions to produce a third; central to signal processing and sums of independent random variables.
Dirac delta function | Generalized function that is zero everywhere except the origin and integrates to one; used to model an impulse.
Laplace transform | Integral transform converting functions of time into functions of complex frequency, used to solve differential equations.
derivative | The instantaneous rate of change of a function; the slope of its tangent line.
fixed points | Points mapped to themselves by a function; guaranteed to exist under conditions like Brouwer's fixed-point theorem.
numerical integration | Approximating definite integrals via methods like the trapezoidal rule or Simpson's rule.
completeness | Property of a metric space in which every Cauchy sequence converges within the space.
differentiable | Describes a function that possesses a derivative at a point or throughout its domain.
differential equations | Equations relating a function to its derivatives, used to model change over time as ODEs and PDEs.
exponential function | Function of form e^x (or a^x) characterized by a growth rate proportional to its own value.
Fourier series | Representation of a periodic function as a sum of sines and cosines.
logarithm | Inverse of exponentiation; log base b of x gives the power to which b must be raised to yield x.
power series | Infinite series of terms involving increasing powers of a variable, e.g. Taylor or Maclaurin series.
triangle inequality | States the length of one side of a triangle, or the norm of a sum, cannot exceed the sum of the other two.
bounded | Describes a set or function confined within some finite range.
Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi | German mathematician known for elliptic functions and the Jacobian matrix and determinant.
continuous | Property of a function with no abrupt jumps or breaks, formalized via limits.
gamma function | Extends the factorial to complex numbers.
Joseph-Louis Lagrange | 18th-century mathematician behind Lagrangian mechanics, Lagrange multipliers, and Lagrange's theorem in group theory.
Lagrange multipliers | Method for finding extrema of a function subject to equality constraints.
measure | Generalization of length, area, and volume assigning size to subsets of a space; foundation of Lebesgue integration.

analytic continuation | Technique extending the domain of an analytic function beyond its original definition.

integral | Value representing accumulated area under a curve; the antiderivative in indefinite form.
intermediate value theorem | States a continuous function on [a,b] takes every value between f(a) and f(b).
Isaac Newton | Co-inventor of calculus with Leibniz; developed the fundamental theorem of calculus and Newton's method.
natural logarithm | Logarithm base e, denoted ln(x); the inverse of the exponential function e^x.
separable | Describes a differential equation splittable into functions of each variable, or a space with a countable dense subset.
sine | Trigonometric function giving the y-coordinate of a point on the unit circle at a given angle.

analytic | Describes a function locally given by a convergent power series.

exponential | Describes growth proportional to current value, as in functions of form e^x.

harmonic | Refers to the divergent harmonic series of reciprocals, or harmonic functions satisfying Laplace's equation.

correlation | Statistical measure of the linear relationship between two variables.
