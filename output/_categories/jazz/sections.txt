# Early Jazz and Swing
> New Orleans jazz through the big-band swing era and its bandleaders.
Duke Ellington | Bandleader-composer who led his orchestra for decades at the Cotton Club; wrote "Take the A Train" and "Mood Indigo."
Louis Armstrong | New Orleans trumpeter and gravelly-voiced vocalist who popularized scat singing; led the Hot Five and Hot Seven.
"One O'Clock Jump" | Riff-based swing standard associated with Count Basie's orchestra.
Artie Shaw | Swing-era clarinetist and bandleader famous for "Begin the Beguine."
Bix Beiderbecke | Cornetist of the 1920s known for a lyrical, melodic improvisational style distinct from hot jazz's aggression.
Cab Calloway | Bandleader and showman known for the scat-filled "Minnie the Moocher" and its "hi-de-ho" call and response.
Count Basie | Kansas City bandleader-pianist known for a spare, riff-driven big-band swing style; led the orchestra behind "One O'Clock Jump."
Fats Waller | Stride pianist, organist, and comic vocalist known for "Ain't Misbehavin'" and "Honeysuckle Rose."

# Bebop
> The fast, harmonically complex 1940s rebellion against swing-era convention.
Dizzy Gillespie | Trumpeter and bebop co-founder known for his bent-bell trumpet and puffed cheeks; frequent partner of Charlie Parker.
Charlie Parker | Alto saxophonist and bebop pioneer nicknamed "Bird," known for blistering improvisation; namesake of the club Birdland.
Thelonious Monk | Pianist-composer known for angular melodies and dissonant harmony; wrote "'Round Midnight" and "Straight, No Chaser."
Bebop | Fast, harmonically complex jazz style of the 1940s pioneered by Parker, Gillespie, and Monk, emphasizing virtuosic improvisation.
Birdland | Legendary New York City jazz club named after Charlie "Bird" Parker, a hub of the bebop and hard bop scenes.
Bud Powell | Pianist who translated bebop's fast horn lines to the keyboard, influencing generations of jazz pianists.
Thelonius Monk | Pianist-composer known for angular melodies and dissonant harmony; wrote "'Round Midnight" and "Straight, No Chaser."

# Cool Jazz and Modal Jazz
> Miles Davis's relaxed 1950s cool sound and its move toward modal, scale-based improvisation.
Bill Evans | Lyrical pianist known for impressionistic harmony; played on Kind of Blue and led his own trio with Scott LaFaro.
Dave Brubeck | Pianist known for unusual time signatures, notably the 5/4 "Take Five" from the album Time Out.
Miles Davis | Trumpeter who shaped multiple jazz eras from cool jazz to modal jazz to fusion; led Kind of Blue and Bitches Brew.
Kind of Blue | Miles Davis's best-selling modal jazz album featuring Bill Evans and John Coltrane, including "So What."
Time Out | Dave Brubeck Quartet's album exploring unusual time signatures, including the 5/4 hit "Take Five."

# Hard Bop and Post-Bop
> Bebop's bluesier, gospel-inflected successor and the saxophone-led quartets that followed it.
John Coltrane | Tenor and soprano saxophonist known for "sheets of sound" improvisation; recorded the spiritual suite A Love Supreme.
Saxophone Colossus | Sonny Rollins's acclaimed album featuring the calypso-inflected tune "St. Thomas."
A Love Supreme | John Coltrane's four-part spiritual jazz suite expressing gratitude and devotion.
Freddie Hubbard | Trumpeter known for a bright, virtuosic tone on hard bop and post-bop recordings.
McCoy Tyner | Pianist known for modal, quartal-harmony playing as a member of John Coltrane's classic quartet.
Sonny Rollins | Tenor saxophonist known for extended unaccompanied solos and the calypso-tinged "St. Thomas."

# Free Jazz and the Avant-Garde
> Mid-century abandonment of fixed chord changes and conventional structure.
Charles Mingus | Virtuoso bassist-composer known for a volatile temperament and genre-blending works like Mingus Ah Um.
Ornette Coleman | Alto saxophonist who pioneered free jazz's abandonment of fixed chord changes; recorded The Shape of Jazz to Come.

# Jazz Fusion
> Jazz's merger with rock, funk, and electric instrumentation from the late 1960s onward.
Herbie Hancock | Pianist who moved from Miles Davis's quintet to electric funk-jazz fusion; composed "Watermelon Man" and the album Head Hunters.
Bitches Brew | Miles Davis's 1970 double album blending jazz improvisation with rock rhythms and electric instruments, a founding fusion text.
jazz fusion | Genre blending jazz improvisation with rock, funk, and electric instrumentation, pioneered by Miles Davis and Herbie Hancock.
Chick Corea | Pianist who played with Miles Davis before founding the fusion band Return to Forever.

# Jazz Vocalists
> Singers who defined jazz's vocal tradition, from scat to blues-inflected protest song.
Ella Fitzgerald | "The First Lady of Song," celebrated for pure tone, wide range, and scat singing virtuosity.
Nina Simone | Pianist-vocalist blending jazz, blues, and classical influences with civil-rights-themed songs like "Mississippi Goddam."
scat singing | Wordless vocal improvisation using nonsense syllables, popularized by Louis Armstrong and Ella Fitzgerald.
"Strange Fruit" | Haunting anti-lynching protest song made famous by Billie Holiday's stark vocal performance.
Smith | Likely refers to Bessie Smith, the "Empress of the Blues," a foundational blues vocalist whose recordings shaped early jazz singing.

# Latin, Caribbean, and Global Jazz
> Rhythms, countries, and scenes outside the United States that shaped jazz's development.
Bossa Nova | Brazilian fusion of samba rhythm and cool jazz harmony, popularized by Joao Gilberto and Antonio Carlos Jobim.
Cuba | Source of Afro-Cuban rhythms fused into jazz by Dizzy Gillespie and conga player Chano Pozo.
Brazil | Home of bossa nova and samba, whose rhythms fused with jazz through Jobim and Joao Gilberto.
California | Home to the West Coast cool jazz scene of the 1950s, centered around Los Angeles studio musicians.
calypso | Trinidadian rhythm and song style that influenced jazz tunes like Sonny Rollins's "St. Thomas."
duduk | Armenian double-reed woodwind occasionally incorporated into world-jazz fusion recordings.
flamenco | Spanish guitar and dance tradition that influenced jazz fusion, notably Miles Davis's Sketches of Spain.
France | Home to Gypsy jazz guitarist Django Reinhardt and violinist Stephane Grappelli's Quintette du Hot Club de France.
Germany | Home to the ECM record label, known for atmospheric European jazz recordings.
Japan | Home to a devoted jazz audience and record-collector culture, plus pianist Toshiko Akiyoshi.
Nigeria | Home to Afrobeat, Fela Kuti's genre fusing jazz, funk, and West African rhythms.
South Africa | Home to pianist Abdullah Ibrahim and trumpeter Hugh Masekela's township jazz tradition.

# Instruments, Standards, and Motifs
> The instruments, tunes, and general vocabulary that recur across jazz's subgenres.
piano | Central jazz instrument, showcased by soloists from Art Tatum and Bud Powell to Bill Evans and Herbie Hancock.
saxophone | Reed instrument central to jazz improvisation, epitomized by Charlie Parker's alto and John Coltrane's tenor and soprano playing.
trombone | Brass instrument with a sliding tube, prominent in swing-era big bands and Dixieland ensembles.
guitar | String instrument used in jazz from Django Reinhardt's Gypsy jazz to Wes Montgomery's octave technique.
trumpet | Brass instrument central to jazz solos, from Louis Armstrong and Dizzy Gillespie to Miles Davis.
clarinet | Reed instrument central to early New Orleans jazz and swing-era clarinetists like Benny Goodman.
drums | Percussion kit driving jazz rhythm, played by innovators like Max Roach, Art Blakey, and Elvin Jones.
jazz guitar | Guitar style within jazz spanning Django Reinhardt's Gypsy swing to Wes Montgomery's octave-based soloing.
double bass | Upright string bass providing jazz's walking-bass foundation, played by Charles Mingus and Ray Brown.
"Body and Soul" | Jazz standard famous for Coleman Hawkins's landmark 1939 tenor saxophone improvisation.
bass | Refers to the double bass or electric bass providing jazz's harmonic and rhythmic foundation.
Blues | Twelve-bar chord progression and vocal tradition foundational to jazz improvisation and repertoire.
Oscar Peterson | Canadian virtuoso pianist known for dazzling technique and swinging trio recordings.
vibraphone | Mallet percussion instrument prominent in jazz through players like Lionel Hampton and Milt Jackson.
"Moanin'" = moanin' | Hard bop standard composed by Bobby Timmons, popularized by Art Blakey's Jazz Messengers.
Cole | Likely refers to Nat "King" Cole, pianist-turned-vocalist known for smooth ballads like "Unforgettable."
George Gershwin | American composer who bridged jazz and classical music with Rhapsody in Blue and the opera Porgy and Bess.
improvisation | Spontaneous, in-the-moment composition central to jazz performance across all its subgenres.
violin | String instrument less common in jazz than others, notably played by Stephane Grappelli and Stuff Smith.
