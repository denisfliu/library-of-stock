Photography questions cluster around the medium's early fight for artistic legitimacy and its subsequent documentary turn. Alfred Stieglitz and Edward Steichen anchor the Photo-Secession and Pictorialist movement that sought to establish photography as fine art, while Paul Strand's shift toward sharply focused "straight photography" marks the transition into the documentary era. That documentary tradition dominates the mid-20th-century canon: Dorothea Lange, Walker Evans, and Margaret Bourke-White chronicled the Great Depression and rural poverty under the Farm Security Administration, with Lange's "Migrant Mother" as the era's single most recognizable image.

Photojournalism and war photography form a closely related cluster, led by Robert Capa and Henri Cartier-Bresson, both co-founders of the Magnum Photos cooperative, alongside Bourke-White's wartime work and the broader coverage of World War II and Vietnam. A smaller cluster covers experimental and avant-garde photography, led by Man Ray's Dadaist and Surrealist techniques and Brassai's atmospheric nighttime images of Paris. Portrait and street photography spans a wide range, from Julia Margaret Cameron's soft-focus 19th-century portraits to Diane Arbus's unflinching studies of marginalized subjects and Weegee's stark tabloid crime-scene images, while fashion and celebrity photography, represented by Annie Leibovitz and Vogue magazine, rounds out the unit's more contemporary end. Cities and landmarks — New York, Paris, Yosemite — recur as organizing answerlines tied to the photographers and movements associated with them.
