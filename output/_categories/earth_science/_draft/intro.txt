Questions on the Earth Science unit are dominated by surface and structural processes: glaciers are the single most frequent answerline, and basalt, soil, quartz, and salinity all recur heavily, reflecting a canon built around mineralogy, geomorphology, and the physical properties of Earth materials. Plate tectonics and Earth's interior form a closely related cluster — earthquakes, magma, metamorphism, seafloor spreading, subduction, and the mantle recur constantly, often tied to the named evidence (geomagnetic reversal, the Vine-Matthews-Morley hypothesis, mid-ocean ridges) that established the theory.

Atmospheric science and oceanography supply a second major bloc of answers. Tides, ocean currents, upwelling, and thermohaline circulation appear alongside atmospheric topics like jet streams, the Coriolis effect, lapse rate, and specific storm types (supercells, tornadoes, hurricanes), with named circulation features like El Nino, the Madden-Julian oscillation, and the Walker circulation recurring as more specific answers. Climate and paleoclimate contribute their own cluster — ice cores, Milankovitch cycles, ocean acidification, and radiative forcing — distinct from day-to-day meteorology but drawing on the same physical vocabulary.

Historical geology rounds out the canon with named periods and events: the Carboniferous, Permian, and Triassic periods, the Cambrian explosion, and the Permian-Triassic extinction all recur as answers keyed to their defining fossil record or environmental change, alongside dating methods like carbon-14 and tree rings. A smaller set of answerlines are the scientists and named hypotheses that structured the field — John Tuzo Wilson's transform faults, Darcy's law, and the uniformitarianism principle — typically appearing as common links tying together the mechanical and observational evidence that built the theory of plate tectonics.
