# Mineralogy & Petrology
> Minerals and the igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks they form.
basalt | Fine-grained, mafic volcanic rock forming oceanic crust and lava flows, rich in iron and magnesium.
quartz | Common silicate mineral (SiO2) with a Mohs hardness of 7, a major component of sandstone and granite.
iron | Abundant element forming Earth's core and key minerals like magnetite and hematite.
metamorphism | Process altering rock's mineral composition and texture under heat and pressure without melting.
feldspars | Most abundant mineral group in Earth's crust, key components of granite and basalt.
calcium carbonate | Compound forming limestone and marine shells, central to the ocean's carbonate system and acidification concerns.
limestone | Sedimentary rock composed mainly of calcium carbonate, often formed from marine organism shells.
obsidian | Naturally occurring volcanic glass formed from rapidly cooled felsic lava.
shale | Fine-grained sedimentary rock formed from compacted mud and clay, often fissile.
granite | Coarse-grained felsic igneous rock composed mainly of quartz and feldspar, forming much of continental crust.
gypsum | Soft sulfate mineral (calcium sulfate dihydrate) used in plaster and drywall, forming through evaporite deposition.
olivine | Green magnesium-iron silicate mineral common in Earth's mantle and mafic igneous rocks.
ophiolites | Sections of oceanic crust and upper mantle thrust onto continental crust, exposing deep Earth material.
aluminum | Abundant crustal metal found in minerals like feldspar and clays, extracted mainly from bauxite ore.
clay | Fine-grained sediment composed of hydrous silicate minerals, forming through weathering and central to shale and soil.
magnesium | Light metallic element abundant in mafic minerals like olivine and pyroxene.
sedimentary rocks | Rocks formed from compaction and cementation of sediment, often preserving fossils and strata.
calcite | Common carbonate mineral (calcium carbonate) forming limestone and marble, easily identified by its effervescence with acid.
carbonate | Mineral group containing the carbonate ion, including calcite and dolomite, forming much of Earth's sedimentary rock.
clays | Fine-grained hydrous silicate minerals formed by weathering, central to soil and sedimentary rock formation.
diagenesis | Physical and chemical changes that transform loose sediment into sedimentary rock.
igneous rocks | Rocks formed from the cooling and solidification of magma or lava.
metamorphic rock | Rock transformed from an existing rock type by heat, pressure, or chemically active fluids.
micas | Sheet silicate minerals with perfect cleavage, common in igneous and metamorphic rocks like granite and schist.
porosity | Measure of void space within a rock or sediment, determining its capacity to hold groundwater or hydrocarbons.
salt | Mineral (halite) formed through evaporation, dissolved in seawater and mined from evaporite deposits.
Bowen's reaction series | Sequence describing the order in which minerals crystallize from cooling magma, from high to low temperature.
chalk | Soft, white limestone formed from the calcite skeletons of marine microorganisms.
clay minerals | Fine-grained hydrous silicate minerals formed by the weathering of feldspar and other silicates.
color | Physical property used, though unreliably, to help identify minerals alongside streak, luster, and hardness.
crystal twinning | Phenomenon where two or more crystals of the same mineral grow together in a symmetrical intergrowth.
gneiss | Banded metamorphic rock formed under high temperature and pressure, typically from granite or sedimentary protoliths.
mafic | Describes igneous rocks and minerals rich in magnesium and iron, typically dark-colored, like basalt.
orthoclase | Potassium-rich feldspar mineral common in granite and a member of the Bowen's reaction series.
pyroxenes | Group of dark, mafic silicate minerals common in basalt and other igneous rocks.
sandstone | Sedimentary rock composed of cemented sand-sized mineral grains, primarily quartz.
sedimentary rock | Rock formed from compaction and cementation of sediment, often preserving fossils and strata.
sulfur | Yellow, non-metallic element found near volcanic vents and hot springs, forming its own native mineral.

# Plate Tectonics & Earth's Interior
> The structure of Earth's interior and the tectonic forces that shape its crust.
earthquakes | Sudden releases of energy along faults, generating seismic waves measured by magnitude scales.
seafloor spreading | Process by which new oceanic crust forms at mid-ocean ridges as plates diverge, key evidence for plate tectonics.
Earth's magnetic field | Field generated by convection in the liquid outer core, protecting Earth from solar wind and recorded in rock magnetism.
subduction | Process by which one tectonic plate slides beneath another, generating volcanism and deep earthquakes.
hotspots | Areas of volcanic activity from stationary mantle plumes rising beneath moving tectonic plates, as at Hawaii and Yellowstone.
mantle | Thick layer of hot, slowly flowing rock between Earth's crust and core, driving plate motion via convection.
mid-ocean ridges | Underwater mountain ranges where tectonic plates diverge and new oceanic crust forms.
orogeny | Mountain-building process driven by tectonic plate collisions, folding and uplifting crust.
subduction zones | Regions where one tectonic plate descends beneath another, producing volcanic arcs and deep-focus earthquakes.
tectonic plates | Rigid segments of Earth's lithosphere that move atop the asthenosphere, driving continental drift.
tsunamis | Large ocean waves typically triggered by underwater earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or landslides.
faults | Fractures in rock along which displacement has occurred, sites of earthquake generation.
anticlines | Upward-arching folds in rock layers formed by compressional tectonic stress.
asthenosphere | Ductile layer of the upper mantle beneath the lithosphere, allowing tectonic plates to move.
convection | Heat-driven circulation of mantle material thought to drive plate tectonic motion.
geomagnetic reversal | Change in the orientation of Earth's magnetic field, recorded in seafloor rock and used to date seafloor spreading.
isostasy | Gravitational equilibrium between Earth's crust and mantle, causing crust to rise or sink in response to loading.
plate tectonics | Theory that Earth's lithosphere is divided into moving plates whose interactions shape earthquakes, volcanism, and mountain building.
black smokers | Hydrothermal vents on the seafloor emitting mineral-rich, superheated water, often near mid-ocean ridges.
continental drift | Alfred Wegener's hypothesis that continents were once joined and have since moved apart, precursor to plate tectonics.
diapirs | Dome-shaped intrusions where a buoyant, ductile material like salt pierces overlying rock layers.
geomagnetic reversals | Changes in the orientation of Earth's magnetic field, recorded in seafloor rock and used to date seafloor spreading.
guyots | Flat-topped underwater seamounts formed by erosion of extinct volcanic islands before subsidence.
lithosphere | Rigid outer layer of Earth comprising the crust and uppermost mantle, divided into tectonic plates.
Love waves | Type of surface seismic wave causing horizontal shearing motion, generally more destructive than Rayleigh waves.
mantle plumes | Upwellings of abnormally hot rock within the mantle thought to generate volcanic hotspots.
Mohorovicic Discontinuity | Boundary between Earth's crust and mantle, marked by a change in seismic wave velocity.
oceanic crust | Thin, dense basaltic crust forming the ocean floor, continually created at mid-ocean ridges and destroyed at subduction zones.
outer core | Liquid iron-nickel layer of Earth's interior whose convective flow generates the planet's magnetic field.
seismic wave velocity | Speed at which seismic waves travel through Earth's layers, used to map internal structure.
seismic waves | Vibrations generated by earthquakes or explosions that travel through Earth, including P, S, and surface waves.
tectonic faults | Fractures in rock along which displacement has occurred, sites of earthquake generation.

# Volcanism
> Magma, lava, and the eruptive processes and landforms volcanoes produce.
magma | Molten rock beneath Earth's surface that erupts as lava or cools to form igneous rock.
tephra | Fragmented volcanic material ejected during an eruption, ranging from fine ash to large blocks.
Lava | Molten rock that has erupted onto Earth's surface, cooling to form extrusive igneous rock.
volcanic eruptions | Expulsions of magma, gas, and ash from a volcano, ranging from effusive lava flows to explosive pyroclastic events.
fumaroles | Vents in volcanic regions emitting steam and gases, often the final stage of volcanic activity.
pillow lava | Rounded, bulbous lava formations produced by underwater volcanic eruptions.
pyroclastic flow | Fast-moving, superheated current of gas and volcanic matter, one of the deadliest volcanic hazards.
Strombolian eruption | Volcanic eruption style characterized by intermittent, moderate explosions of gas bubbles bursting through lava.
volcanoes | Openings in Earth's crust through which magma, gas, and ash erupt, building characteristic landforms.

# Geomorphology & Surface Processes
> Weathering, erosion, glaciation, and the shaping of Earth's surface.
glaciers | Large, slow-moving masses of ice that carve landscapes via erosion and deposit moraines.
soil | Weathered surface layer of mineral and organic material supporting plant life, classified by horizons.
permafrost | Ground that remains frozen for at least two consecutive years, common in Arctic and alpine regions.
ice | Frozen water forming glaciers, sea ice, and permafrost, central to cryosphere processes.
sand | Granular sediment of rock and mineral particles, forming dunes and beaches through erosion and deposition.
sand dunes | Mounds of wind-deposited sand, shaped by processes like saltation.
weathering | Breakdown of rock at Earth's surface via physical, chemical, or biological processes.
Karst Topography | Landscape formed by dissolution of soluble bedrock like limestone, producing caves, sinkholes, and springs.
caves | Natural underground cavities often formed through the dissolution of soluble rock like limestone.
saltation | Process by which sediment grains bounce and hop along a surface, driven by wind or water.
ablation | Loss of ice or snow from a glacier via melting, evaporation, or calving.
Antarctica | Southernmost continent, almost entirely covered by an ice sheet holding most of Earth's fresh water.
deserts | Arid regions receiving little precipitation, shaped by wind erosion and deposition.
erosion | Process by which rock and soil are worn away and transported by wind, water, or ice.
icebergs | Large chunks of freshwater ice that have broken off glaciers or ice shelves to float in open water.
loess | Fine, wind-deposited sediment forming fertile, unstratified soil layers.
longshore drift | Movement of sediment along a coastline caused by waves approaching at an angle.
plucking | Glacial erosion process where a glacier freezes onto rock and pulls fragments away as it moves.
solifluction | Slow downslope movement of water-saturated soil, common in periglacial and permafrost regions.
bedrock | Solid rock underlying loose soil, sediment, or other surface material.
cirque | Bowl-shaped hollow carved by glacial erosion at the head of a mountain valley.
elevation | Height above sea level, influencing climate, atmospheric pressure, and landscape features.
humus | Dark organic matter in soil formed from decomposed plant and animal material.
mass wasting | Downslope movement of rock, soil, and debris under gravity, including landslides and mudflows.
moraines | Accumulations of rock debris deposited directly by a glacier, marking its extent or retreat.
pingos | Ice-cored mounds found in permafrost regions, formed by the freezing of groundwater beneath the surface.
sediment | Solid material transported and deposited by water, wind, or ice, the raw material of sedimentary rock.
soils | Weathered surface layers of mineral and organic material supporting plant life, classified by horizons.

# Hydrology & Oceanography
> Groundwater, rivers, oceans, and the circulation of Earth's water.
salinity | Measure of dissolved salt content in water, driving density-based ocean circulation.
aquifers | Underground layers of permeable rock or sediment that store and transmit groundwater.
tides | Periodic rise and fall of sea level caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon and Sun.
surface runoff | Water flowing over land after precipitation, feeding rivers and contributing to erosion.
ocean waves | Surface undulations of water driven by wind, generating coastal erosion and deposition.
upwelling | Process bringing cold, nutrient-rich deep water to the surface, boosting marine productivity.
ocean currents | Large-scale movements of seawater driven by wind, density differences, and Earth's rotation.
water | Essential compound cycling through Earth's hydrosphere via evaporation, precipitation, and runoff.
eutrophication | Excessive nutrient enrichment of water bodies causing algal blooms and oxygen depletion.
groundwater | Water stored beneath the surface in soil pores and rock fractures, accessed via wells and aquifers.
rivers | Flowing bodies of freshwater that shape landscapes through erosion, transport, and deposition of sediment.
turbidity | Measure of water clarity affected by suspended particles, often from sediment or algal growth.
thermohaline circulation | Global ocean circulation pattern driven by differences in water temperature and salinity.
Atlantic meridional overturning circulation | Major system of ocean currents, including the Gulf Stream, redistributing heat via density-driven circulation.
Atlantic Ocean | Second-largest ocean, separating the Americas from Europe and Africa, home to the Gulf Stream.
gyres | Large systems of rotating ocean currents driven by wind patterns and the Coriolis effect.
Algal Blooms | Rapid increase of algae in water, often driven by nutrient pollution, that can deplete oxygen and produce toxins.
dissolved oxygen | Amount of oxygen gas present in water, critical for aquatic life and affected by eutrophication.
estuaries | Coastal water bodies where freshwater rivers meet and mix with ocean saltwater.
floods | Overflow of water onto normally dry land, often caused by heavy precipitation or storm surge.
mangrove forests | Coastal wetland ecosystems of salt-tolerant trees that stabilize shorelines and buffer storm surges.
mean sea level | Average height of the ocean's surface, used as a reference datum for elevation and monitored for climate-driven rise.
oceanic currents | Large-scale movements of seawater driven by wind, density differences, and Earth's rotation.
river deltas | Landforms formed where a river deposits sediment as it enters a slower-moving body of water.
sea surface temperature | Temperature of the ocean's upper layer, a key indicator for phenomena like El Nino and hurricane formation.
vadose zone | Unsaturated subsurface zone between the ground surface and the water table.

# Atmospheric Science & Meteorology
> The atmosphere's structure, weather systems, and storms.
temperature | Fundamental atmospheric variable driving weather patterns, measured and modeled in forecasting.
clouds | Visible masses of condensed water droplets or ice crystals, classified by altitude and shape.
jet streams | Narrow bands of strong westerly winds in the upper troposphere that steer weather systems.
lapse rate | Rate at which atmospheric temperature decreases with altitude, key to atmospheric stability.
Coriolis effect | Apparent deflection of moving objects due to Earth's rotation, shaping wind and ocean current patterns.
Coriolis force | Apparent force causing moving objects to deflect due to Earth's rotation, shaping winds and currents.
ozone | Molecule (O3) concentrated in the stratosphere, absorbing harmful ultraviolet radiation.
weather forecasting | Prediction of atmospheric conditions using observational data and numerical models.
wind speed | Measurement of air movement rate, a key variable in storm intensity and weather prediction.
ionosphere | Upper atmospheric layer ionized by solar radiation, reflecting radio waves.
supercells | Severe, long-lived rotating thunderstorms capable of producing large hail and tornadoes.
tornadoes | Violently rotating columns of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground.
hurricanes | Powerful tropical cyclones fueled by warm ocean water, forming a calm central eye surrounded by intense storms.
lightning | Sudden electrostatic discharge within a thunderstorm, occurring between clouds or between cloud and ground.
snow | Precipitation of ice crystals, forming when atmospheric water vapor freezes directly.
trade winds | Persistent easterly surface winds near the equator, part of the Hadley cell circulation.
tropopause | Boundary between the troposphere and stratosphere, where temperature stops decreasing with altitude.
Troposphere | Lowest layer of Earth's atmosphere, where nearly all weather occurs.
cirrus clouds | Thin, wispy, high-altitude clouds composed of ice crystals.
cyclones | Large-scale systems of rotating winds around a low-pressure center, including hurricanes and typhoons.
Ferrel cell | Mid-latitude atmospheric circulation cell between the Hadley and polar cells, driving variable weather patterns.
Foehn wind | Warm, dry wind descending the leeward side of a mountain range after losing moisture on the windward side.
fog | Cloud of water droplets suspended near the ground, reducing visibility.
fronts | Boundaries between air masses of different temperature and density, driving much of mid-latitude weather.
graupel | Soft, small ice pellets formed when supercooled water droplets freeze onto a falling snowflake.
hail | Precipitation of ice balls formed within strong thunderstorm updrafts.
mesosphere | Atmospheric layer above the stratosphere where meteors burn up, the coldest layer of Earth's atmosphere.
rain | Liquid precipitation formed when water droplets in clouds grow large enough to fall.
red sprites | Large-scale electrical discharges occurring high above thunderstorms, a form of upper-atmospheric lightning.
Rossby waves | Large-scale meanders in the jet stream and ocean currents caused by Earth's rotation, influencing weather patterns.
synoptic scale | Meteorological scale of weather systems spanning hundreds to thousands of kilometers, like cyclones and fronts.
Thermosphere | Uppermost atmospheric layer with extremely high temperatures, where auroras occur and satellites orbit.
tornado | Violently rotating column of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground.
tropical cyclones | Large rotating storm systems fueled by warm ocean water, called hurricanes or typhoons regionally.
Walker circulation | Atmospheric circulation pattern over the tropical Pacific linked to El Nino and La Nina conditions.

# Climate & Paleoclimate
> Climate drivers, greenhouse gases, and the record of past climate change.
El Nino | Periodic warming of central and eastern Pacific surface waters, disrupting global weather patterns.
ocean acidification | Decrease in ocean pH from absorbing excess atmospheric CO2, threatening calcifying marine organisms.
ice cores | Cylindrical samples drilled from ice sheets, preserving trapped air bubbles used to reconstruct past climate.
Madden-Julian oscillation | Eastward-moving pattern of enhanced tropical rainfall and wind that influences global weather on a 30-60 day cycle.
albedo | Measure of a surface's reflectivity, with high values like ice and snow reflecting more incoming solar radiation.
atmospheric aerosols | Suspended particles in the atmosphere that can cool climate by reflecting sunlight or seed cloud formation.
carbon dioxide | Greenhouse gas central to the carbon cycle and modern climate change, absorbed by oceans and plants.
acid rain | Precipitation made acidic by atmospheric pollutants like sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides.
methane | Potent greenhouse gas released from wetlands, livestock, and permafrost thaw, far stronger than CO2 over short timescales.
Milankovitch cycles | Periodic variations in Earth's orbit and axial tilt that influence long-term climate patterns and glacial cycles.
oxygen gas | Atmospheric component produced by photosynthesis, whose rise in the Great Oxidation Event transformed early Earth's chemistry.
radiative forcing | Measure of the change in energy balance of Earth's atmosphere caused by factors like greenhouse gases.

# Geologic Time & Historical Geology
> The geologic periods, mass extinctions, and dating methods that chronicle Earth's history.
Carboniferous period | Paleozoic period (~359-299 Ma) known for extensive coal-forming swamp forests and giant arthropods.
Permian period | Last Paleozoic period (~299-252 Ma), ending in the largest mass extinction in Earth's history.
strata | Layers of sedimentary rock, whose sequence and characteristics record Earth's geologic history.
Triassic Period | First period of the Mesozoic era (~252-201 Ma), following the Permian extinction and preceding dinosaur diversification.
Cambrian explosion | Rapid diversification of complex animal life roughly 540 million years ago at the start of the Cambrian period.
Cambrian period | First period of the Paleozoic era (~541-485 Ma), marked by the rapid diversification of animal life.
carbon-14 | Radioactive isotope used to date organic materials up to about 50,000 years old.
fossils | Preserved remains or traces of ancient organisms, used to date rock strata and reconstruct past life.
Jurassic period | Middle period of the Mesozoic era (~201-145 Ma), marked by dinosaur dominance and the breakup of Pangaea.
meteorites | Extraterrestrial rock fragments that survive passage through the atmosphere to reach Earth's surface, some linked to mass extinctions.
Permian-Triassic extinction | The largest mass extinction in Earth's history, ending the Paleozoic era roughly 252 million years ago.
tree rings | Annual growth layers in trees used to date past events and reconstruct historical climate.
dating | Determining the age of rocks or fossils via methods like radiometric dating or stratigraphy.
Devonian period | Paleozoic period (~419-359 Ma) known as the "Age of Fishes," with early forests and tetrapods.
Eocene epoch | Cenozoic epoch (~56-34 Ma) marked by a warm climate and the diversification of mammals.
Pleistocene Epoch | Epoch (~2.6 Ma to 11,700 years ago) marked by repeated glacial cycles and the appearance of early humans.
stromatolites | Layered structures built by ancient microbial mats, among the oldest fossil evidence of life on Earth.

# Scientists & Foundational Concepts
> The researchers and theories underlying modern earth science.
Darcy's law | Equation describing the flow rate of fluid through a porous medium, foundational to groundwater hydrology.
John Tuzo Wilson | Canadian geophysicist who proposed transform faults and the Wilson cycle of ocean basin opening and closing.
remote sensing | Technique of gathering data about Earth's surface using satellite or airborne sensors rather than direct contact.
uniformitarianism | Principle that geologic processes observed today have operated similarly throughout Earth's history.
Vine-Matthews-Morley Hypothesis | Proposal explaining symmetric magnetic stripes on the seafloor as evidence for seafloor spreading.
