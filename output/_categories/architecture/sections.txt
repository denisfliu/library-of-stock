# Ancient and Classical Architecture
> Greek and Roman temples, engineering, and theory that codified the classical orders.
columns | Classical orders (Doric, Ionic, Corinthian) define column styles that originated in Greek and Roman temples.
domes | Curved roof form perfected by Roman engineers in the Pantheon and revived in the Renaissance (Florence) and Byzantine (Hagia Sophia) traditions.
Parthenon | Doric marble temple to Athena atop the Athenian Acropolis, designed by Ictinus and Callicrates; epitome of classical proportion.
Vitruvius | Roman architect-engineer whose treatise De Architectura defined firmitas, utilitas, venustas and inspired the Vitruvian Man.
Hadrian's villa | Sprawling 2nd-century retreat near Tivoli built by Roman emperor Hadrian, blending Greek and Egyptian architectural elements.

# Medieval and Gothic Architecture
> European church-building from Romanesque mass to Gothic verticality, light, and ornament.
cathedrals | Grand Christian churches, especially Gothic cathedrals like Chartres, known for ribbed vaults and flying buttresses.
churches | Christian worship buildings ranging from Romanesque and Gothic cathedrals to modern designs like Ando's Church of the Light.
Gothic | Style of pointed arches, ribbed vaults, and flying buttresses spanning medieval European cathedrals.
mosaics | Decorative tile technique prominent in Byzantine churches, from Ravenna's basilicas to Istanbul's Hagia Sophia.
Chartres Cathedral | French Gothic cathedral renowned for its stained glass and mismatched spires; a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
Gothic architecture | Style of pointed arches, ribbed vaults, and flying buttresses spanning medieval European cathedrals.
stained glass | Colored glass windows depicting religious scenes, a hallmark of Gothic cathedrals like Chartres.

# Renaissance and Baroque Architecture
> The revival of classical proportion in Italy and its spread and dramatization across Europe.
Andrea Palladio | Venetian Renaissance architect of symmetrical villas like the Villa Rotonda, codified in his Four Books of Architecture; namesake of Palladianism.
Filippo Brunelleschi | Early Renaissance architect-engineer who built Florence Cathedral's self-supporting dome using innovative herringbone brickwork.
Inigo Jones | First major English classicist architect, introduced Palladianism to Britain; designed the Queen's House and Banqueting House.
St. Paul's Cathedral | Christopher Wren's English Baroque London cathedral, rebuilt after the Great Fire with its iconic dome.
Donato Bramante | High Renaissance architect who designed the Tempietto and began the new design for St. Peter's Basilica.
Michelangelo | Renaissance master who designed St. Peter's Basilica's dome and the Laurentian Library's Mannerist staircase.

# Global and Non-Western Traditions
> Islamic, Buddhist, and Pre-Columbian architectural traditions outside the European canon.
Maya | Mesoamerican civilization whose stepped pyramids and temples appear at Chichen Itza and Tikal.
minarets | Tall, slender towers attached to mosques, traditionally used to call the faithful to prayer.
Alhambra | Moorish Nasrid palace-fortress in Granada, Spain, famed for intricate muqarnas and stucco ornamentation.
Chichen Itza | Maya city in the Yucatan featuring the stepped pyramid El Castillo, the Temple of Kukulcan.
muqarnas | Ornamental, honeycomb-like vaulting technique used in Islamic domes and portals.
stupas | Buddhist dome-shaped monuments housing relics, found across South and Southeast Asia.
Teotihuacan | Pre-Columbian Mexican city with the Pyramid of the Sun and Pyramid of the Moon along its Avenue of the Dead.

# Art Nouveau and Arts and Crafts
> Turn-of-the-century organic ornament and craft revival, from Barcelona to Glasgow.
Art Nouveau | Ornamental, organic-line movement of the 1890s-1910s; exemplified by Gaudi's Barcelona buildings and Victor Horta's Brussels townhouses.
Antoni Gaudi | Catalan architect of Barcelona's Sagrada Familia, Park Guell, and Casa Batllo, in the organic Catalan Modernisme idiom.
Charles Rennie Mackintosh | Scottish Art Nouveau architect-designer known for the Glasgow School of Art and distinctive ladder-back chairs.
Antoni Gaudi i Cornet | Catalan architect of Barcelona's Sagrada Familia, Park Guell, and Casa Batllo, in the organic Catalan Modernisme idiom.
La Sagrada Familia | Gaudi's still-unfinished Barcelona basilica, famed for its organic tree-like columns and elaborate facades.

# Modernism and the International Style
> The early-20th-century break with ornament toward function, steel, and glass.
Le Corbusier | Swiss-French pioneer of the International Style and Purism; designed Villa Savoye, Notre-Dame du Haut, and Unite d'Habitation; wrote Towards a New Architecture.
Louis Sullivan | Chicago School pioneer of "form follows function"; designed early skyscrapers like the Wainwright Building; mentored Frank Lloyd Wright.
Ludwig Mies van der Rohe | German-American minimalist of "less is more"; designed the Barcelona Pavilion, Seagram Building, and Farnsworth House.
Villa Savoye | Le Corbusier's 1931 Poissy villa embodying his "Five Points of Architecture": pilotis, ribbon windows, roof garden.
Buckminster Fuller | American inventor-futurist who popularized the geodesic dome as an efficient, lightweight structural system.
Louis Henry Sullivan | Chicago School pioneer of "form follows function"; designed early skyscrapers like the Wainwright Building; mentored Frank Lloyd Wright.
Richard Meier | American architect known for pristine white modernist buildings; designed the Getty Center; member of the "New York Five."
Bauhaus | German design school (1919-1933) founded by Walter Gropius, fusing art, craft, and industrial design into modernist principles.
International Style | Term coined by Philip Johnson and Henry-Russell Hitchcock for the stripped-down, glass-and-steel modernism of Le Corbusier, Mies, and Gropius.
Staatliches Bauhaus | German design school (1919-1933) founded by Walter Gropius, fusing art, craft, and industrial design into modernist principles.
Towards A New Architecture | Le Corbusier's 1923 manifesto declaring "a house is a machine for living in," a foundational modernist text.

# Organic and Regional Modernism
> Modernists who softened International Style rigor with nature, craft, and national character.
Frank Lloyd Wright | American architect of Prairie School and organic architecture; designed Fallingwater, the Robie House, and the Guggenheim Museum; ran Taliesin.
Alvar Aalto | Finnish architect and designer blending modernism with organic forms and natural materials; Paimio Sanatorium, Finlandia Hall.
Eero Saarinen | Finnish-American architect of swooping modernist forms; designed the TWA Flight Center, Gateway Arch, and Dulles Airport terminal.
Saarinen | Refers to Eero Saarinen, Finnish-American architect of swooping modernist forms; TWA Flight Center, Gateway Arch, Dulles Airport.
Jorn Utzon | Danish architect of the Sydney Opera House's sail-like concrete shells, a landmark of Expressionist modernism.
Taliesin | Frank Lloyd Wright's Wisconsin home and studio, later paired with the winter counterpart Taliesin West in Arizona.

# Brutalism and Postwar Concrete
> Raw-concrete monumentality of the mid-20th century, from housing blocks to government capitals.
Oscar Niemeyer | Brazilian modernist who designed most of Brasilia's government buildings with sweeping reinforced-concrete curves; collaborated with Lucio Costa.
brutalism | Mid-20th-century style of raw exposed concrete (beton brut); associated with Le Corbusier, Louis Kahn, and Moshe Safdie.
Tadao Ando | Japanese self-taught architect known for minimalist exposed-concrete buildings that manipulate natural light; Church of the Light.
Moshe Safdie | Israeli-Canadian architect famed for Habitat 67, a modular concrete housing complex built in Montreal for Expo 67.
concrete | Key building material of Brutalism and modern engineering, enabling reinforced and precast structural forms.
Louis Kahn | American architect of monumental, light-filled concrete and brick buildings; Salk Institute, Kimbell Art Museum, Bangladesh's National Assembly.
reinforced concrete | Structural material combining concrete with embedded steel rebar, enabling modernist and Brutalist forms.

# Postmodernism and Deconstructivism
> Late-20th-century reactions against modernist austerity, from historical irony to fractured form.
Zaha Hadid | Iraqi-British Deconstructivist known for flowing, parametric forms; designed the Heydar Aliyev Center; first woman to win the Pritzker Prize.
Frank Gehry | American-Canadian architect of swooping, titanium-clad Deconstructivist forms; designed the Guggenheim Bilbao and Walt Disney Concert Hall.
Rem Koolhaas | Dutch architect-theorist and OMA founder; wrote Delirious New York and S,M,L,XL; designed the CCTV Headquarters and Seattle Central Library.
Philip Johnson | American architect-critic who coined "International Style"; designed his own Glass House and the postmodern AT&T Building; first Pritzker laureate.
Daniel Libeskind | Polish-American Deconstructivist of jagged, symbolic forms; designed the Berlin Jewish Museum and the World Trade Center site master plan.
Frank Owen Gehry | American-Canadian architect of swooping, titanium-clad Deconstructivist forms; designed the Guggenheim Bilbao and Walt Disney Concert Hall.
One World Trade Center | Skyscraper built on the World Trade Center site following Daniel Libeskind's master plan, designed by David Childs.
postmodernism | Late-20th-century reaction against modernist austerity, embracing historical ornament and irony, led by Venturi and Johnson.

# High-Tech and Contemporary Architecture
> Structural expressionism and engineering-forward design from the 1970s to today's starchitects.
Renzo Piano | Italian High-Tech architect who co-designed the Centre Pompidou with Richard Rogers; also designed the Shard and the Whitney Museum.
Norman Foster | British High-Tech architect; designed 30 St Mary Axe (the Gherkin), the Reichstag dome renovation, and Hearst Tower.
Cesar Pelli | Argentine-American designer of skyscrapers including the Petronas Towers, a pioneer of postmodern curtain-wall towers.
I. M. Pei = I.M. Pei | Chinese-American modernist known for crisp geometric forms; designed the Louvre Pyramid and the Bank of China Tower.
Pompidou Center | Paris museum by Renzo Piano and Richard Rogers exposing its structural and mechanical systems on the exterior, launching High-Tech architecture.
Centre Georges Pompidou | Paris museum by Renzo Piano and Richard Rogers exposing its structural and mechanical systems on the exterior, launching High-Tech architecture.
Santiago Calatrava | Spanish architect-engineer known for skeletal, bird-like white structures; Valencia's City of Arts and Sciences, the WTC Oculus.
Shigeru Ban | Japanese architect known for humanitarian paper-tube and cardboard structures; Pritzker Prize laureate.

# Landscape Architecture and Urban Planning
> Designed parks, gardens, and city plans shaping the spaces around and between buildings.
Frederick Law Olmsted | American landscape architect who designed New York's Central Park with Calvert Vaux; pioneered City Beautiful-era planning.
Brasilia | Brazil's planned capital, laid out by Lucio Costa's Pilot Plan and filled with Oscar Niemeyer's modernist government buildings.
gardens | Designed outdoor spaces ranging from English landscape gardens to Islamic charbagh and Olmsted's American public parks.
Maya Lin | American designer of the Vietnam Veterans Memorial's minimalist black granite wall, chosen through a student design competition.
Lucio Costa | Brazilian urban planner who designed Brasilia's Pilot Plan, executed alongside Oscar Niemeyer's buildings.
