Architecture questions in quizbowl cluster heavily around 20th-century figures: Le Corbusier, Frank Lloyd Wright, and the International Style generation (Mies van der Rohe, Walter Gropius, Philip Johnson) anchor the modernist canon, while a second wave of starchitects — Zaha Hadid, Frank Gehry, Rem Koolhaas, Renzo Piano, Norman Foster, and Santiago Calatrava — dominates contemporary answerlines with signature museums, towers, and cultural centers. Named buildings recur as both standalone answers and supporting clues: Villa Savoye, Fallingwater, the Guggenheim Bilbao, the Sydney Opera House, and the Centre Pompidou each anchor their architect's identity while also surfacing in "building type" or "material" bonuses.

A substantial share of the canon predates the 20th century. Classical antiquity supplies the Parthenon, Vitruvius, and the Roman dome tradition; the medieval period contributes Gothic cathedrals like Chartres; and the Renaissance and Baroque periods bring Brunelleschi, Palladio, Bramante, and Wren. Non-Western traditions — Islamic ornament (the Alhambra, muqarnas, minarets), Buddhist stupas, and Pre-Columbian Mesoamerican pyramids (Teotihuacan, Chichen Itza) — form a smaller but consistent slice of the distribution, often tested through a single landmark rather than a named architect.

Cities and countries function as major organizing answerlines in their own right — Chicago, Barcelona, Brasilia, and Japan each gather clues about the buildings and movements associated with them rather than a single work. Generic building types and materials (cathedrals, airports, libraries, concrete, glass, domes, columns) round out the distribution, testing familiarity with architectural vocabulary and cross-era building categories rather than any one structure. Brutalism, Postmodernism, and Deconstructivism appear both as named movements and through their leading practitioners (Louis Kahn and Moshe Safdie for the former; Philip Johnson, Gehry, and Daniel Libeskind for the latter two), reflecting how mid-to-late-20th-century stylistic labels have become answerlines alongside the architects who defined them.
