# Classical Antiquity
> Greek and Roman poets, playwrights, and epics that predate the modern national literary canons.
Sappho | Archaic Greek lyric poet from Lesbos, c. 630 BCE, famed for passionate verse and the Sapphic stanza.

# French Literature
> Writers and works from the French literary tradition, from Renaissance satire to the modern novel.
French | Language of a literary tradition spanning Rabelais's satire, Voltaire's Enlightenment prose, and the modern French novel.
Francois Rabelais | French Renaissance satirist who wrote Gargantua and Pantagruel, known for grotesque humor and the character Panurge.

# German and Scandinavian Literature
> German-language and Nordic playwrights spanning epic theater and Scandinavian Realist drama.
Bertolt Brecht | German Marxist playwright who pioneered Epic Theatre's alienation effect; wrote Mother Courage and The Threepenny Opera.
ghosts | Refers to Henrik Ibsen's 1881 Norwegian play Ghosts, a Realist drama about hereditary syphilis and inherited moral corruption.

# Russian and Eastern European Literature
> Russian and neighboring literary traditions, centered on nineteenth-century prose satire and drama.
Nikolai Gogol | 19th-century Russian satirist; wrote the play The Government Inspector and novel Dead Souls, plus the story The Overcoat.

# British and Irish Literature
> Novelists, poets, and critics of England and Ireland, from the eighteenth century through Modernism.
Oscar Wilde | Irish aesthete and wit who wrote The Picture of Dorian Gray and The Importance of Being Earnest.
Virginia Woolf | English Modernist and Bloomsbury Group member; wrote Mrs Dalloway and To the Lighthouse using stream-of-consciousness technique.
Hamlet | Shakespeare's tragic Danish prince, driven by his father's ghost to seek revenge on Claudius.
D. H. Lawrence | English novelist linked to Georgian poetry and Modernism; wrote Sons and Lovers, Women in Love, and Lady Chatterley's Lover.
Ireland | Country whose literary tradition includes James Joyce, W. B. Yeats, Oscar Wilde, and Samuel Beckett.
Jane Austen | English novelist of manners; wrote Pride and Prejudice, Sense and Sensibility, and Emma using free indirect discourse.
London | City frequently the setting for English literature bonuses, from Dickens's novels to Woolf's Mrs Dalloway.
Matthew Arnold | Victorian poet-critic who wrote 'Dover Beach' and criticism like Culture and Anarchy; coined 'sweetness and light.'
Samuel Johnson | 18th-century English lexicographer and critic who compiled A Dictionary of the English Language; subject of Boswell's famous biography.

# American Literature
> United States writers spanning Gothic Romanticism, New Journalism, and contemporary satirical fiction.
Edgar Allan Poe | American Gothic writer who pioneered detective fiction; wrote The Tell-Tale Heart, The Raven, and other macabre tales.
George Saunders | Contemporary American short-story writer and satirist; wrote the Booker-winning novel Lincoln in the Bardo.
Florida | U.S. state frequently the setting or answer in bonuses on American regional literature, from Hurston to Hemingway's Key West years.
Joan Didion | American essayist and novelist of the New Journalism movement; wrote Slouching Towards Bethlehem and The Year of Magical Thinking.

# Latin American Literature
> Literary traditions of Mexico and the wider Spanish-speaking Americas.
Mexico | Country whose literary tradition includes Octavio Paz, Juan Rulfo, and Carlos Fuentes among its major modern writers.

# Middle Eastern and Jewish Literary Traditions
> Arabic-language and Yiddish literature, including classical storytelling and Ashkenazi Jewish fiction.
Arabic | Language of the literary tradition behind One Thousand and One Nights and classical Islamic poetry.
Yiddish | Language of Ashkenazi Jewish literature, home to Isaac Bashevis Singer and Sholem Aleichem's Tevye stories.

# South and East Asian Literature
> Literary traditions of China, Korea, and South Asia, including modernist and postcolonial writers.
China | Country whose literary tradition spans classical poetry, the Ming-era novel Journey to the West, and modern writer Lu Xun.
Lu Xun | Father of modern Chinese literature; wrote 'Diary of a Madman' and 'The True Story of Ah Q.'
Salman Rushdie | British-Indian novelist of Magical Realism; wrote Midnight's Children and The Satanic Verses, which prompted a fatwa against him.
South Korea | Country whose literary tradition includes Han Kang's The Vegetarian and a wave of postwar Korean fiction.

# Literary Common Links and Motifs
> Cross-cutting bonus answerlines built around a shared theme, setting, or figure rather than a single tradition.
dogs | Common-link answerline connecting literary dogs, such as Homer's Argos in the Odyssey and Buck in The Call of the Wild.
letters | Common-link answerline for epistolary works composed of letters, such as Dracula, Les Liaisons Dangereuses, and The Color Purple.
Ludwig van Beethoven | German composer (1770-1827) bridging the Classical and Romantic eras in Vienna; wrote the opera Fidelio and the Ninth Symphony.
