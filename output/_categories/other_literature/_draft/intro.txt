"Other Literature" spans literary traditions and cross-cutting bonus formats that fall outside the separately organized British, American, Russian, French, and German literature categories. Recurring answerlines include the archaic Greek lyric poet Sappho, the German epic-theater dramatist Bertolt Brecht, and English writers such as Oscar Wilde, Virginia Woolf, D. H. Lawrence, Jane Austen, Matthew Arnold, and Samuel Johnson, whose British and Irish tradition supplies the largest single cluster of authors in the corpus.

American literature forms a secondary cluster, running from Edgar Allan Poe's Gothic tales through Joan Didion's New Journalism to the contemporary satire of George Saunders, with the state of Florida recurring as a regional setting. Russian, German, and Scandinavian drama appear through Nikolai Gogol and Bertolt Brecht and through Henrik Ibsen's 1881 play Ghosts, while France is represented by the Renaissance satirist Francois Rabelais and by French-language literature more broadly.

A substantial share of answerlines are countries and languages rather than individual authors — China, French, Arabic, Ireland, Mexico, South Korea, and Yiddish — reflecting bonus questions that survey an entire national or linguistic tradition, such as Chinese literature via Lu Xun, South Asian and postcolonial writing via Salman Rushdie, Latin American letters via Mexico, and Jewish literature via Yiddish, rather than a single author or work. A smaller set of common-link and thematic answerlines — dogs, ghosts, letters, Hamlet, and even the composer Ludwig van Beethoven — connect works and figures across several of these traditions by a shared motif rather than by nationality or genre.
