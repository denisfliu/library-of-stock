Religion questions at this level draw most heavily on the Abrahamic faiths and the Dharmic traditions of South Asia, with the remainder distributed across East Asian religion, new and restorationist movements, and African-diaspora syncretic traditions. Judaism, Christianity, and Islam each contribute a dense layer of named scripture (the Torah, individual New Testament epistles, the Qur'an and hadith), festivals (Passover, Yom Kippur, and Sukkot; Easter and Pentecost; Ramadan and Eid), and biblical or Qur'anic figures who recur across all three canons, such as Abraham, Moses, and Gabriel. Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, and Sikhism supply their own recurring cast — Shiva, Vishnu, and Krishna; the Buddha and his bodhisattvas across Theravada, Mahayana, and Vajrayana schools; Mahavira and the Tirthankaras; and Guru Nanak's line of ten Sikh Gurus — bound together by shared concepts like karma, samsara, and moksha.

Beyond the largest traditions, the unit surfaces a long tail of smaller but distinctive faiths: Zoroastrianism's fire temples and dualism of Ahura Mazda and Ahriman; Baha'i, Mormonism, Christian Science, and Scientology as products of 19th- and 20th-century religious innovation; and syncretic New World religions — Vodou, Santeria, Candomble, and Umbanda — that blend West African orisha and loa worship with Roman Catholic saints. East Asian religion appears chiefly through Shinto kami worship, Confucian filial piety, and Taoist cosmology, often in combination rather than isolation.

A significant share of answerlines are not proper names at all but cross-tradition motifs: sacred numbers (three, five, seven, forty), natural symbols (fire, water, mountains, trees), and shared practices (fasting, pilgrimage, animal sacrifice, divination) that quizbowl regularly clues as common links spanning several religions at once. Countries and cities likewise recur as anchors for a tradition's geography — Jerusalem, Mecca, and Varanasi among them — more often than as free-standing topics in their own right.
