Ancient History questions in this unit concentrate heavily on classical Greece and Rome's rivalry with Carthage, with the Greek poleis Sparta, Athens, Corinth, Syracuse, and Thebes among the single most-asked answerlines, alongside Carthage itself. Sparta's militarism and helot system, Athens's democracy and empire under Pericles, and the Peloponnesian War that pitted the two powers against each other form one dense cluster, while the Persian Wars against Darius I and Xerxes I supply another, tied together by figures such as Themistocles, Leonidas, and the battles of Marathon, Thermopylae, Salamis, and Plataea. Alexander the Great and the Hellenistic world that followed his conquests, including Ptolemaic Egypt, the Seleucid Empire, and Antigonid Macedon, form a third major cluster bridging Greek and Near Eastern history.

Rome's canon divides between the Republic and the Empire. Republican material centers on the Punic Wars against Hannibal and Carthage, the political crises of the Gracchi, Marius, and Sulla, and the fall of the Republic through Pompey, Caesar, and the triumvirates. Imperial material runs from Augustus's founding of the Principate through the Julio-Claudian, Flavian, and Severan dynasties, the Crisis of the Third Century, and Constantine's refounding of the empire on Christian and Constantinopolitan lines into late antiquity. The Achaemenid, Parthian, and Sassanid Persian empires recur throughout as Rome's perennial eastern rival, from Crassus's disaster at Carrhae to Valerian's capture by Shapur I.

A substantial share of answerlines fall outside the Greco-Roman core: the river-valley civilizations of Mesopotamia and Egypt (Sumer, Akkad, Babylon, Assyria, the Hittites), peoples on the periphery of the classical world (Gauls, Britons, Germanic tribes, Scythians, Numidians, Dacians, and the Indus Valley and Maurya civilizations of South Asia), and a long tail of lower-frequency answerlines covering political and social institutions (archon, ephors, Roman Senate, Praetorian Guard, dictator) and everyday material culture (coins, chariots, purple dye, war elephants) that recur across many individual topics rather than anchoring their own.
