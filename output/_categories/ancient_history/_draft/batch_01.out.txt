Sparta	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Dominant Peloponnesian city-state known for its rigid militarism, dual kingship, and helot-worked land; led the anti-Persian and anti-Athenian coalitions.
Carthage	Carthage and the Punic Wars	Phoenician-founded North African city-state whose Mediterranean trade empire sparked three Punic Wars against Rome before its destruction in 146 BCE.
Athens	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Direct-democracy city-state led by Pericles; center of classical culture and the Delian League, defeated by Sparta in the Peloponnesian War.
Corinth	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Wealthy, well-situated Peloponnesian trade city whose quarrel with Corcyra helped trigger the Peloponnesian War; later destroyed by Rome in 146 BCE.
Alexander the Great	Alexander and the Hellenistic World	Macedonian king who conquered the Achaemenid Persian Empire under Darius III and pushed to India before dying at Babylon in 323 BCE.
Pompey the Great	The Roman Republic	Roman general and triumvir who defeated Mithridates VI and the Mediterranean pirates, then lost to Caesar at Pharsalus and was murdered in Egypt.
Syracuse	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Powerful Sicilian Greek city-state that repelled the Athenian Sicilian Expedition and later fought both Carthage and Rome.
Thebes	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Boeotian city-state that broke Spartan hegemony at Leuctra under Epaminondas, spearheaded by the Sacred Band.
Helots	Institutions, Society, and Material Culture	Enslaved serf population of Messenia and Laconia bound to Spartan-owned land, periodically rising in revolt against their masters.
Pyrrhus of Epirus	Alexander and the Hellenistic World	Hellenistic king whose costly victories over Rome at Heraclea and Asculum gave rise to the term "Pyrrhic victory."
Sicily	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Mediterranean island whose Greek colonies, especially Syracuse, and Carthaginian holdings made it a recurring battleground through the Punic Wars.
Solon	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Athenian statesman whose early-6th-century reforms canceled debts (seisachtheia) and reorganized citizenship by property class.
Pericles	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Athenian statesman who led the city's Golden Age and the early Peloponnesian War, funding the Parthenon's construction.
Rhodes	Alexander and the Hellenistic World	Hellenistic island city-state famed for its Colossus and navy, allied with Rome against Macedon and the Seleucids.
Trajan	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Emperor who brought Rome to its greatest territorial extent, conquering Dacia and much of Parthian Mesopotamia.
Lydia	Ancient Near East and Egypt	Wealthy Anatolian kingdom of King Croesus, famed for early coinage, conquered by Cyrus the Great of Persia in 546 BCE.
Marcus Licinius Crassus	The Roman Republic	Wealthiest man in Rome and member of the First Triumvirate with Caesar and Pompey; crushed Spartacus's revolt but died at Carrhae against Parthia.
Marcus Tullius Cicero	The Roman Republic	Roman orator, consul, and philosopher who exposed the Catiline conspiracy and was killed in the Second Triumvirate's proscriptions.
Philip II of Macedon	Alexander and the Hellenistic World	Alexander the Great's father, who unified Greece after Chaeronea and built the Macedonian phalanx army.
Sassanid Empire	Persia and the Iranian World	Last pre-Islamic Persian empire, founded by Ardashir I, which fought chronic wars with Rome and captured Emperor Valerian.
Themistocles	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Athenian statesman who built the fleet that won at Salamis during the Persian Wars, later ostracized and exiled to Persia.
Argos	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Ancient Peloponnesian city-state, rival of Sparta, associated with the mythic hero Perseus and early Mycenaean kingship.
Cyrus the Great	Persia and the Iranian World	Founder of the Achaemenid Persian Empire, who conquered Lydia and Babylon and freed the Jews from Babylonian captivity.
Parthian Empire	Persia and the Iranian World	Iranian empire of horse-archers and cataphracts that annihilated Crassus at Carrhae and repeatedly clashed with Rome over Armenia.
Alcibiades	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Charismatic and treacherous Athenian general of the Peloponnesian War, involved in the Sicilian Expedition, who defected to Sparta and then Persia.
Constantine the Great	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Emperor who legalized Christianity, won at the Milvian Bridge, and founded Constantinople as the empire's new capital.
Demosthenes	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Athenian orator whose Philippics warned against Philip II of Macedon's growing power.
Diocletian	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Emperor who ended the Crisis of the Third Century by instituting the Tetrarchy and persecuting Christians.
Gaius Julius Caesar	The Roman Republic	Roman general and dictator who conquered Gaul, crossed the Rubicon, and was assassinated on the Ides of March.
Gaius Marius	The Roman Republic	Roman general who reformed the legions to accept landless citizens and fought Sulla in Rome's first civil war.
Hadrian	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Emperor who built a wall across northern Britain and consolidated the empire's frontiers rather than expanding further.
Macedonia	Alexander and the Hellenistic World	Northern Greek kingdom of Philip II and Alexander the Great, later a Roman province after the Battle of Pydna.
Miletus	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Wealthiest Ionian Greek city, home to early philosophers Thales and Anaximander; its revolt sparked the Ionian Revolt against Persia.
Minoan Civilization	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Bronze Age Aegean civilization centered on Crete and the palace at Knossos, predating and influencing Mycenaean Greece.
Peloponnesian War	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Late-5th-century BCE war between Athens's and Sparta's alliances, chronicled by Thucydides, ending in Athens's defeat.
Caligula	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Erratic early Julio-Claudian emperor known for extravagance and cruelty, assassinated by the Praetorian Guard.
chariots	Institutions, Society, and Material Culture	Horse-drawn war and racing vehicles used from Bronze Age Near Eastern battles like Kadesh to Roman circus races.
Cleisthenes	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Athenian reformer credited with founding democracy by reorganizing citizens into ten tribes and instituting ostracism.
cuneiform	Ancient Near East and Egypt	Wedge-shaped script pressed into clay tablets, invented in Sumer and adapted for Akkadian, Hittite, and other Near Eastern languages.
Egypt	Ancient Near East and Egypt	Nile River civilization of pharaohs and pyramids, later a Ptolemaic Hellenistic kingdom and then a Roman province.
Gaius Suetonius Tranquillus	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Roman biographer whose Lives of the Twelve Caesars chronicles emperors from Julius Caesar to Domitian.
Gaul	Peoples on the Periphery	Celtic-inhabited region roughly corresponding to modern France, conquered by Julius Caesar in the Gallic Wars.
Hispania	Peoples on the Periphery	Roman name for the Iberian Peninsula, contested in the Punic Wars and later fully absorbed into the Roman Republic.
Ionian Revolt	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Revolt of Ionian Greek cities led by Miletus against Persian rule, whose suppression helped trigger the Persian Wars.
Jugurtha	Peoples on the Periphery	Numidian king whose war against Rome was fought by Marius and Sulla, ending in his capture and a triumph for Rome.
ostracism	Institutions, Society, and Material Culture	Athenian practice of exiling a citizen for ten years by popular vote, used against Themistocles and other leaders.
Polybius	The Roman Republic	Greek historian and hostage at Rome whose Histories chronicled Rome's rise to Mediterranean dominance through the Punic Wars.
Samnites	Peoples on the Periphery	Early Italic people of the central Apennines, conquered by Rome across three Samnite Wars.
Samos	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Ionian island city-state, home to Pythagoras, ruled by the tyrant Polycrates, later part of the Delian League.
Sea Peoples	Ancient Near East and Egypt	Mysterious confederation of seaborne raiders whose invasions around 1200 BCE contributed to the Bronze Age Collapse; repelled by Ramesses III.
Second Punic War	Carthage and the Punic Wars	War in which Hannibal crossed the Alps and won at Cannae before Scipio Africanus defeated him at Zama.
Social War	The Roman Republic	War of 91-88 BCE in which Rome's Italian allies fought for citizenship rights, ending with citizenship's extension.
Spartacus	The Roman Republic	Thracian gladiator who led the Third Servile War, a major slave revolt eventually crushed by Crassus.
Strabo	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Greek geographer of the Augustan age whose Geographica surveyed the known world from Britain to India.
Theodosius I	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Last emperor to rule a united Roman Empire; made Nicene Christianity the state religion and divided the empire between his sons.
Ugarit	Ancient Near East and Egypt	Bronze Age Levantine city-state whose alphabetic cuneiform tablets preserve early Canaanite myth and the Ugaritic alphabet.
Vandals	Peoples on the Periphery	Germanic people who crossed into North Africa, sacked Rome in 455 CE, and were later destroyed by Belisarius's reconquest.
Aurelian	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Emperor who reunited the empire by defeating the breakaway Palmyrene and Gallic empires during the Crisis of the Third Century.
Battle of Adrianople	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	378 CE battle in which Emperor Valens was killed and the Goths crushed the Roman army, a major turning point in imperial decline.
Battle of Plataea	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	479 BCE battle in which the combined Greek army decisively defeated the Persians, ending Xerxes I's invasion.
Cambyses II	Persia and the Iranian World	Son of Cyrus the Great who conquered Egypt, founding Persia's Twenty-Seventh Dynasty there.
Claudius	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Julio-Claudian emperor who conquered Britain and expanded the imperial bureaucracy despite a reputation for physical infirmity.
copper	Institutions, Society, and Material Culture	Early worked metal, alloyed with tin to form bronze; heavily mined on Cyprus and central to Bronze Age trade networks.
Dacians	Peoples on the Periphery	People north of the Danube under King Decebalus, conquered by Trajan and commemorated on Trajan's Column.
Delian League	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Athenian-led naval alliance against Persia whose treasury's move to Athens and use for the Parthenon fueled Spartan resentment.
Domitian	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Autocratic Flavian emperor assassinated after a paranoid reign of persecution, succeeded by Nerva.
Elam	Ancient Near East and Egypt	Ancient civilization centered at Susa in southwestern Iran, predating and later absorbed into the Persian Empire.
Etruscans	The Roman Republic	Pre-Roman civilization of central Italy whose kings (the Tarquins) once ruled Rome and whose culture shaped early Roman religion and architecture.
Goths	Peoples on the Periphery	Germanic people split into Visigoths and Ostrogoths, who defeated Rome at Adrianople and later sacked Rome under Alaric.
Halicarnassus	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Carian coastal city in Anatolia, birthplace of Herodotus and site of the Mausoleum, one of the Seven Wonders.
Macedon	Alexander and the Hellenistic World	Northern Greek kingdom of Philip II and Alexander the Great, later a Roman province after the Battle of Pydna.
Melos	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Aegean island whose people, refusing to submit to Athens, were massacred and enslaved per Thucydides's "Melian Dialogue."
Nero	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	Last Julio-Claudian emperor, blamed for the Great Fire of Rome and remembered for persecuting Christians and his artistic excess.
Ptolemy	Alexander and the Hellenistic World	Ptolemy I Soter, Alexander's general who took Egypt as satrap and founded the Ptolemaic dynasty.
Publius Clodius Pulcher	The Roman Republic	Populist tribune and rival of Cicero, whose gang violence with Milo's led to his death and Cicero's Pro Milone.
Septimius Severus	The Roman Empire and Late Antiquity	African-born emperor who founded the Severan dynasty after the Year of the Five Emperors, expanding the empire militarily.
Susa	Persia and the Iranian World	One of the Achaemenid Empire's capitals, in ancient Elam, terminus of the Royal Road from Sardis.
Thucydides	Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age	Athenian general and historian whose History of the Peloponnesian War is a foundational work of political realism.
Ur	Ancient Near East and Egypt	Sumerian city-state, traditionally Abraham's home, famous for its ziggurat and royal tombs excavated by Leonard Woolley.
agoge	Institutions, Society, and Material Culture	Spartan state-run education and training regimen that molded boys into soldiers from around age seven.
