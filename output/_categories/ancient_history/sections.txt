# Ancient Near East and Egypt
> Mesopotamia's Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian, and Assyrian city-states and empires alongside pharaonic Egypt and their Levantine neighbors.
Lydia | Wealthy Anatolian kingdom of King Croesus, famed for early coinage, conquered by Cyrus the Great of Persia in 546 BCE.
cuneiform | Wedge-shaped script pressed into clay tablets, invented in Sumer and adapted for Akkadian, Hittite, and other Near Eastern languages.
Egypt | Nile River civilization of pharaohs and pyramids, later a Ptolemaic Hellenistic kingdom and then a Roman province.
Sea Peoples | Mysterious confederation of seaborne raiders whose invasions around 1200 BCE contributed to the Bronze Age Collapse; repelled by Ramesses III.
Ugarit | Bronze Age Levantine city-state whose alphabetic cuneiform tablets preserve early Canaanite myth and the Ugaritic alphabet.
Elam | Ancient civilization centered at Susa in southwestern Iran, predating and later absorbed into the Persian Empire.
Ur | Sumerian city-state, traditionally Abraham's home, famous for its ziggurat and royal tombs excavated by Leonard Woolley.
Ashurbanipal | Last great king of the Neo-Assyrian Empire, whose library at Nineveh preserved the Epic of Gilgamesh.
Assyrian Empire | Militaristic Mesopotamian empire centered at Nineveh and Assur, known for brutal conquest before its collapse under Babylonian and Median pressure.
Babylon | Mesopotamian city on the Euphrates, capital under Hammurabi and Nebuchadnezzar II, later conquered by Cyrus the Great.
Battle of Kadesh | Bronze Age battle between Ramesses II's Egypt and the Hittite Empire, resulting in history's earliest surviving peace treaty.
Hittite empire | Bronze Age Anatolian empire that sacked Babylon, fought Egypt at Kadesh, and collapsed amid the Bronze Age Collapse.
Phoenicians | Levantine maritime traders from Tyre and Sidon who spread the alphabet and founded Carthage.
Sargon of Akkad | Founder of the Akkadian Empire, often considered history's first empire-builder, who united the Sumerian city-states.
Akkadian Empire | Semitic empire founded by Sargon, uniting Sumer and Akkad and considered history's earliest empire.
Amarna | Short-lived Egyptian capital built by pharaoh Akhenaten for his monotheistic worship of the sun-disc Aten.
Gutians | Mountain people who overthrew the Akkadian Empire and briefly ruled Mesopotamia before being expelled.
Hammurabi | Babylonian king famed for his law code inscribed on a stele, one of the earliest written legal codes.
Hyksos | Foreign dynasty of likely Semitic origin that ruled Lower Egypt during its Second Intermediate Period.
Lagash | Early Sumerian city-state, ruled by figures like Gudea, with some of the earliest known legal and building records.
Phoenicia | Levantine coastal region of city-states like Tyre and Sidon that spread the alphabet and founded Mediterranean colonies including Carthage.
Syria | Levantine region, Seleucid heartland and later a major Roman province.
Tiglath-Pileser III | Neo-Assyrian king whose administrative and military reforms built the empire's most effective phase of expansion.
Tyre | Phoenician port city famed for purple dye, besieged and captured by Alexander the Great after a long siege.
Amarna Letters | Cuneiform diplomatic correspondence between Egypt's pharaohs and Near Eastern rulers, found at Akhenaten's capital.
Byblos | Phoenician port city that gave its name to the word "Bible" through its papyrus and book trade.
Canaan | Levantine region encompassing the Phoenician coast and biblical Israel, contested by Egypt and Near Eastern powers.
Hittite Kingdom | Bronze Age Anatolian empire that sacked Babylon, fought Egypt at Kadesh, and collapsed amid the Bronze Age Collapse.
Hittites | Bronze Age Anatolian empire that sacked Babylon, fought Egypt at Kadesh, and collapsed amid the Bronze Age Collapse.
Kadesh | Bronze Age battle between Ramesses II's Egypt and the Hittite Empire, resulting in history's earliest surviving peace treaty.
Kish | Early Sumerian city-state, traditionally considered the first seat of kingship after the mythical Flood.
Libya | North African region bordering Egypt, home to Berber peoples and the Greek colony of Cyrene.
Mittani | Bronze Age Hurrian kingdom in northern Mesopotamia and Syria, rival and later ally of Egypt against the Hittites.
Naram-Sin | Akkadian king, grandson of Sargon, who claimed divinity and expanded the empire to its greatest extent.
Nebuchadnezzar II | Neo-Babylonian king who destroyed Jerusalem's Temple and built the traditional Hanging Gardens and the Ishtar Gate.
Neo-Assyrian Empire | Militaristic Mesopotamian empire centered at Nineveh and Assur, known for brutal conquest before its collapse under Babylonian and Median pressure.
Nile River | River whose annual floods enabled Egyptian agriculture and civilization along its banks.
Sargon II | Neo-Assyrian king who conquered the Israelite kingdom of Israel and built the palace at Dur-Sharrukin.
Turkey | Modern name for Anatolia, home in antiquity to the Hittites, Lydia, Ionian Greek cities, and Troy.

# Persia and the Iranian World
> The Achaemenid, Parthian, and Sassanid empires that ruled the Iranian plateau and repeatedly clashed with the Greek and Roman worlds.
Sassanid Empire | Last pre-Islamic Persian empire, founded by Ardashir I, which fought chronic wars with Rome and captured Emperor Valerian.
Cyrus the Great | Founder of the Achaemenid Persian Empire, who conquered Lydia and Babylon and freed the Jews from Babylonian captivity.
Parthian Empire | Iranian empire of horse-archers and cataphracts that annihilated Crassus at Carrhae and repeatedly clashed with Rome over Armenia.
Cambyses II | Son of Cyrus the Great who conquered Egypt, founding Persia's Twenty-Seventh Dynasty there.
Susa | One of the Achaemenid Empire's capitals, in ancient Elam, terminus of the Royal Road from Sardis.
Darius I | Achaemenid king who organized the empire into satrapies, built the Royal Road, and was defeated at Marathon.
Medes | Iranian people whose empire, centered at Ecbatana, was overthrown by Cyrus the Great to found Achaemenid Persia.
Xerxes I | Achaemenid king who invaded Greece with a massive army, winning at Thermopylae but losing at Salamis and Plataea.
Battle of Cunaxa | 401 BCE battle where Cyrus the Younger died trying to seize the Persian throne from Artaxerxes II, chronicled by Xenophon.
Behistun Inscription | Trilingual cuneiform inscription of Darius I carved into a cliff face, key to the decipherment of cuneiform.
Achaemenid Empire | First Persian Empire, founded by Cyrus the Great, stretching from India to the Aegean, ended by Alexander the Great.
Ardashir I | Founder of the Sassanid Empire, who overthrew the last Parthian king, Artabanus V.
Ctesiphon | Parthian and later Sassanid capital on the Tigris, repeatedly captured by Roman emperors.
Iran | Modern name for the Persian homeland, seat of the Achaemenid, Parthian, and Sassanid empires.
Persian Empire | First Persian Empire, founded by Cyrus the Great, stretching from India to the Aegean, ended by Alexander the Great.
Surena | Parthian noble general who orchestrated the destruction of Crassus's army at Carrhae.

# Greece: Bronze Age through Classical Age
> The Aegean world from Minoan Crete and Mycenaean palaces through the poleis of Sparta, Athens, Corinth, and Thebes, the Persian Wars, and the Peloponnesian War.
Sparta | Dominant Peloponnesian city-state known for its rigid militarism, dual kingship, and helot-worked land; led the anti-Persian and anti-Athenian coalitions.
Athens | Direct-democracy city-state led by Pericles; center of classical culture and the Delian League, defeated by Sparta in the Peloponnesian War.
Corinth | Wealthy, well-situated Peloponnesian trade city whose quarrel with Corcyra helped trigger the Peloponnesian War; later destroyed by Rome in 146 BCE.
Syracuse | Powerful Sicilian Greek city-state that repelled the Athenian Sicilian Expedition and later fought both Carthage and Rome.
Thebes | Boeotian city-state that broke Spartan hegemony at Leuctra under Epaminondas, spearheaded by the Sacred Band.
Sicily | Mediterranean island whose Greek colonies, especially Syracuse, and Carthaginian holdings made it a recurring battleground through the Punic Wars.
Solon | Athenian statesman whose early-6th-century reforms canceled debts (seisachtheia) and reorganized citizenship by property class.
Pericles | Athenian statesman who led the city's Golden Age and the early Peloponnesian War, funding the Parthenon's construction.
Themistocles | Athenian statesman who built the fleet that won at Salamis during the Persian Wars, later ostracized and exiled to Persia.
Argos | Ancient Peloponnesian city-state, rival of Sparta, associated with the mythic hero Perseus and early Mycenaean kingship.
Alcibiades | Charismatic and treacherous Athenian general of the Peloponnesian War, involved in the Sicilian Expedition, who defected to Sparta and then Persia.
Demosthenes | Athenian orator whose Philippics warned against Philip II of Macedon's growing power.
Miletus | Wealthiest Ionian Greek city, home to early philosophers Thales and Anaximander; its revolt sparked the Ionian Revolt against Persia.
Minoan Civilization | Bronze Age Aegean civilization centered on Crete and the palace at Knossos, predating and influencing Mycenaean Greece.
Peloponnesian War | Late-5th-century BCE war between Athens's and Sparta's alliances, chronicled by Thucydides, ending in Athens's defeat.
Cleisthenes | Athenian reformer credited with founding democracy by reorganizing citizens into ten tribes and instituting ostracism.
Ionian Revolt | Revolt of Ionian Greek cities led by Miletus against Persian rule, whose suppression helped trigger the Persian Wars.
Samos | Ionian island city-state, home to Pythagoras, ruled by the tyrant Polycrates, later part of the Delian League.
Battle of Plataea | 479 BCE battle in which the combined Greek army decisively defeated the Persians, ending Xerxes I's invasion.
Delian League | Athenian-led naval alliance against Persia whose treasury's move to Athens and use for the Parthenon fueled Spartan resentment.
Halicarnassus | Carian coastal city in Anatolia, birthplace of Herodotus and site of the Mausoleum, one of the Seven Wonders.
Melos | Aegean island whose people, refusing to submit to Athens, were massacred and enslaved per Thucydides's "Melian Dialogue."
Thucydides | Athenian general and historian whose History of the Peloponnesian War is a foundational work of political realism.
Battle of Salamis | 480 BCE naval battle in which Themistocles's Athenian fleet destroyed Xerxes I's Persian navy in the straits.
Battle of Thermopylae | Mountain pass where Leonidas and 300 Spartans made their last stand against Xerxes I's invading Persian army.
Corinthian War | Early-4th-century BCE war pitting Sparta against a coalition of Athens, Thebes, Corinth, and Argos, backed by Persian gold.
Herodotus | "Father of History" whose Histories chronicle the Persian Wars and describe Egypt, Persia, and Scythia.
Lesbos | Aegean island home to the poet Sappho, contested between Athens and Sparta during the Peloponnesian War.
Magna Graecia | Greek-colonized region of southern Italy and Sicily, including Syracuse and Tarentum.
Megara | Peloponnesian city-state whose trade embargo by Athens (the Megarian Decree) helped spark the Peloponnesian War.
Nicias | Cautious Athenian general who negotiated the Peace of Nicias and died leading the disastrous Sicilian Expedition.
Thermopylae | Mountain pass where Leonidas's Spartans held off Xerxes I's Persian army in 480 BCE.
Xenophon | Athenian soldier-historian whose Anabasis recounts the Ten Thousand's march after Cyrus the Younger's failed revolt.
Battle of Aegospotami | Final naval battle of the Peloponnesian War, where Lysander's Spartan fleet destroyed Athens's navy in 405 BCE.
Battle of Arginusae | Athenian naval victory over Sparta whose commanding generals were controversially executed for failing to rescue survivors.
Battle of Leuctra | 371 BCE battle in which Epaminondas's Thebans broke Spartan military dominance using the oblique phalanx.
Battle of Marathon | 490 BCE battle in which Athenian hoplites defeated Darius I's first Persian invasion force.
Dorians | Greek-speaking people whose migration is traditionally linked to the Bronze Age collapse and the settlement of Sparta and Crete.
Lycurgus | Semi-legendary Spartan lawgiver credited with founding Sparta's militaristic constitution and the agoge.
Mantinea | Site of major Peloponnesian battles, including one where Epaminondas died winning a second victory over Sparta.
Pausanias | Spartan regent-general who commanded the victory at Plataea, later accused of conspiring with Persia and starved to death.
Salamis | Strait where the Greek fleet under Themistocles destroyed Xerxes I's Persian navy in 480 BCE.
Sicilian Expedition | Disastrous Athenian invasion of Sicily during the Peloponnesian War, ending in the total destruction of the expedition force at Syracuse.
Thera | Aegean volcanic island, modern Santorini, whose massive Bronze Age eruption is linked to Minoan decline and Atlantis legends.
Thrasybulus | Athenian democratic leader who overthrew the Thirty Tyrants and restored democracy after the Peloponnesian War.
anabasis | Greek term for "the march up-country," most famously Xenophon's account of the Ten Thousand's retreat after Cunaxa.
Aspasia | Influential companion of Pericles, a learned Milesian woman associated with rhetoric and philosophy in classical Athens.
Cimon | Athenian statesman and general who led the Delian League against Persia after the Persian Wars.
Corcyra | Greek colony, modern Corfu, whose conflict with Corinth over Epidamnus helped trigger the Peloponnesian War.
Draco | Early Athenian lawgiver whose harshly punitive law code gave rise to the word "draconian."
Elis | Peloponnesian region that administered the sanctuary and games at Olympia.
Epaminondas | Theban general whose victory at Leuctra broke Spartan hegemony, using the innovative oblique phalanx formation.
Ephialtes | Athenian democratic reformer who stripped the aristocratic Areopagus of most of its power shortly before his assassination.
Euboea | Large Greek island off Attica and Boeotia, site of early colonization and later contested territory in Greek wars.
Knossos | Palatial center of Minoan Crete, associated with the legendary Labyrinth and King Minos, excavated by Arthur Evans.
Linear B | Syllabic script used by Mycenaean Greeks to record an early form of Greek, deciphered by Michael Ventris.
Lysander | Spartan admiral who destroyed the Athenian fleet at Aegospotami, ending the Peloponnesian War, and installed the Thirty Tyrants.
Massalia | Greek colony, modern Marseille, founded by Phocaean settlers on the southern coast of Gaul.
Miltiades | Athenian general who commanded the victory over Darius I's Persians at the Battle of Marathon in 490 BCE.
Mycenae | Bronze Age palatial center of legendary King Agamemnon, giving its name to Mycenaean civilization.
Olympia | Sanctuary of Zeus in Elis that hosted the ancient Olympic Games.
Olympic Games | Panhellenic athletic festival held every four years at Olympia in honor of Zeus.
Piraeus | Athens's port city, connected to the capital by the Long Walls and home to its war fleet.
Pisistratus | Athenian tyrant who seized power three times, patronized the arts, and paved the way for later democratic reforms.
Sacred Band of Thebes | Elite Theban infantry unit of 150 paired lovers, decisive at Leuctra and annihilated at Chaeronea.
Thirty Tyrants | Pro-Spartan oligarchic regime installed at Athens after the Peloponnesian War, overthrown by Thrasybulus.
Timoleon | Corinthian general who liberated Syracuse from tyranny and defeated a Carthaginian invasion of Sicily.

Alexander the Great | Macedonian king who conquered the Achaemenid Persian Empire under Darius III and pushed to India before dying at Babylon in 323 BCE.
Pyrrhus of Epirus | Hellenistic king whose costly victories over Rome at Heraclea and Asculum gave rise to the term "Pyrrhic victory."
Rhodes | Hellenistic island city-state famed for its Colossus and navy, allied with Rome against Macedon and the Seleucids.
Philip II of Macedon | Alexander the Great's father, who unified Greece after Chaeronea and built the Macedonian phalanx army.
Macedonia | Northern Greek kingdom of Philip II and Alexander the Great, later a Roman province after the Battle of Pydna.
Macedon | Northern Greek kingdom of Philip II and Alexander the Great, later a Roman province after the Battle of Pydna.
Ptolemy | Ptolemy I Soter, Alexander's general who took Egypt as satrap and founded the Ptolemaic dynasty.
Antioch | Seleucid capital in Syria founded by Seleucus I Nicator, later one of the Roman Empire's greatest eastern cities.
Battle of Ipsus | 301 BCE battle among Alexander's successors in which Antigonus was killed, reshaping the Hellenistic world.
Cassander | Successor of Alexander who took control of Macedon and had Alexander's wife Roxana and son murdered.
Epirus | Northwestern Greek kingdom ruled by Pyrrhus, famed for his costly wars against Rome.
Seleucid Empire | Hellenistic successor state founded by Seleucus I, stretching from Anatolia to India, eventually absorbed by Rome and Parthia.
Seleucus I Nicator | Alexander's general who founded the Seleucid Empire and lost eastern territory to Chandragupta Maurya.
Achaean League | Hellenistic-era confederation of Peloponnesian city-states that resisted, then was crushed by, Rome in 146 BCE.
Antigonid Dynasty | Macedonian royal house descended from Alexander's general Antigonus, ruling Macedon until Rome's conquest at Pydna.
Battle of Gaugamela | 331 BCE battle in which Alexander decisively defeated Darius III, effectively ending the Achaemenid Empire.
Battle of the Hydaspes River | Alexander's costly victory over King Porus in India, after which his exhausted army refused to march further.
Cleopatra VII Philopator | Last active ruler of Ptolemaic Egypt, allied with Julius Caesar and then Mark Antony before her defeat at Actium and suicide.
death of Alexander the Great | Alexander's sudden death in Babylon in 323 BCE, possibly from fever or poison, which fractured his empire among his generals.
Pergamon | Hellenistic kingdom in Anatolia famed for its library, Altar of Zeus, and eventual bequest to Rome.
Philip | Likely Philip II of Macedon, Alexander the Great's father, who unified Greece after the Battle of Chaeronea.
Philip II | Philip II of Macedon, Alexander the Great's father, who unified Greece after the Battle of Chaeronea.
Philip V of Macedon | Macedonian king defeated by Rome in the Second Macedonian War, ending Macedon's independence.
Ptolemy I Soter | Alexander's general who took Egypt as satrap and founded the Ptolemaic dynasty, along with Alexandria's Museum and Library.

> The Phoenician colony of Carthage and its generals, navies, and long struggle with Rome across three Punic Wars.
Carthage | Phoenician-founded North African city-state whose Mediterranean trade empire sparked three Punic Wars against Rome before its destruction in 146 BCE.
Second Punic War | War in which Hannibal crossed the Alps and won at Cannae before Scipio Africanus defeated him at Zama.
First Punic War | Rome and Carthage's earliest war (264-241 BCE), fought mainly over Sicily and at sea, won by Rome.
Hamilcar Barca | Carthaginian general of the First Punic War and father of Hannibal, who rebuilt Carthaginian power in Spain.
Africa | Roman province established on former Carthaginian territory after the Third Punic War, a major grain source for Rome.
Balearic Islands | Mediterranean islands famed for their skilled slingers, who served as mercenaries in Carthaginian and Roman armies.
Battle of Cannae | Hannibal's double-envelopment annihilation of a Roman army during the Second Punic War, 216 BCE.
Battle of Ilipa | Scipio Africanus's decisive victory over Carthaginian forces in Spain during the Second Punic War.
Hannibal Barca | Carthaginian general who crossed the Alps with war elephants and crushed Rome at Cannae during the Second Punic War.

Gaius Julius Caesar | Roman general and dictator who conquered Gaul, crossed the Rubicon, and was assassinated on the Ides of March.

Spartacus | Thracian gladiator who led the Third Servile War, a major slave revolt eventually crushed by Crassus.

Sulla | Roman dictator and rival of Marius who marched on Rome, proscribed his enemies, and reformed the constitution before retiring.
Cicero | Roman orator, consul, and philosopher who exposed the Catiline conspiracy and was killed in the Second Triumvirate's proscriptions.

Marcus Livius Drusus | Reformist Roman tribune whose proposals to extend citizenship to Italian allies led to his assassination and the Social War.
Quintus Sertorius | Roman general who led a breakaway state in Hispania against the Sullan regime until his assassination.

Fulvia | Politically active wife of Mark Antony (and earlier Clodius), who raised troops against Octavian in the Perusine War.

Julia | Caesar's daughter and Pompey's wife, whose death in childbirth helped sever the political alliance between the two men.
Julius Caesar | Roman general and dictator who conquered Gaul, crossed the Rubicon, and was assassinated on the Ides of March.

Augustus | Rome's first emperor, born Octavian, who defeated Mark Antony and Cleopatra at Actium and established the Principate.

Tiberius Julius Caesar Augustus | Formal imperial name of Tiberius, Augustus's reclusive and suspicious successor, who retired to Capri.
Titus Flavius Caesar Vespasianus Augustus | Formal imperial name of Titus, who completed the Colosseum and presided over the eruption of Vesuvius.
Valerian | Emperor captured alive by the Sassanid king Shapur I, reportedly used as a human footstool, a humiliating first for Rome.

# Peoples on the Periphery
> The Gauls, Britons, Germanic tribes, Huns, Scythians, Numidians, and other peoples who bordered, invaded, or were conquered by the classical Mediterranean powers, along with early South Asia and the Americas.
Gaul | Celtic-inhabited region roughly corresponding to modern France, conquered by Julius Caesar in the Gallic Wars.
Hispania | Roman name for the Iberian Peninsula, contested in the Punic Wars and later fully absorbed into the Roman Republic.
Jugurtha | Numidian king whose war against Rome was fought by Marius and Sulla, ending in his capture and a triumph for Rome.
Samnites | Early Italic people of the central Apennines, conquered by Rome across three Samnite Wars.
Vandals | Germanic people who crossed into North Africa, sacked Rome in 455 CE, and were later destroyed by Belisarius's reconquest.
Dacians | People north of the Danube under King Decebalus, conquered by Trajan and commemorated on Trajan's Column.
Goths | Germanic people split into Visigoths and Ostrogoths, who defeated Rome at Adrianople and later sacked Rome under Alaric.
Bithynia | Anatolian kingdom bequeathed to Rome by its last king, Nicomedes IV, in 74 BCE; later governed by Pliny the Younger.
Black Sea | Sea rimmed by Greek colonies, Scythian territory, and Pontic kingdoms including Mithridates VI's realm.
Brennus | Gallic chieftain who sacked Rome around 390 BCE, reputedly declaring "vae victis" ("woe to the vanquished").
Dacia | Region north of the Danube conquered by Trajan and later abandoned under Gothic pressure by Aurelian.
druids | Celtic priestly class among the Gauls and Britons, suppressed by Rome and described by Julius Caesar.
Kushan Empire | Central and South Asian empire descended from Yuezhi nomads that patronized Buddhism along the Silk Road.
Mithridates VI | King of Pontus who fought three wars against Rome and reputedly immunized himself against poison.
Palmyra | Syrian desert city ruled by Queen Zenobia, whose breakaway empire was crushed by Aurelian.
Arminius | Germanic chieftain who annihilated three Roman legions under Varus at the Teutoburg Forest in 9 CE.
Boudica | Iceni queen in Roman Britain who led a major revolt burning Londinium before her defeat.
Boudicca | Iceni queen who led a major British revolt against Roman rule before her defeat.
Chandragupta Maurya | Founder of India's Maurya Empire, who defeated Alexander's successor Seleucus I and unified much of the subcontinent.
Decebalus | Dacian king who resisted then fell to Trajan's conquest, commemorated on Trajan's Column.
Gauls | Celtic peoples of Gaul and beyond, conquered by Caesar and earlier sackers of Rome under Brennus.
Gnaeus Julius Agricola | Roman general who governed and campaigned in Britain, subject of his son-in-law Tacitus's biography.
Illyrians | Peoples of the western Balkans, conquered by Rome after the Illyrian Wars.
Indus River Valley Civilization | Bronze Age civilization of cities like Mohenjo-daro and Harappa in the Indus basin, predating classical India.
Indus Valley civilization | Bronze Age civilization of cities like Mohenjo-daro and Harappa in the Indus basin, predating classical India.
Scythians | Nomadic horse-archer people of the Eurasian steppe, described by Herodotus, who resisted Darius I's invasion.
Thrace | Region north of the Aegean home to warlike tribes, birthplace of Spartacus, later a Roman province.
Visigoths | Western Gothic people who sacked Rome under Alaric in 410 and later founded a kingdom in Spain.
Alaric I | Visigothic king who sacked Rome in 410 CE, the first such sack in nearly 800 years.
Clovis culture | Prehistoric Paleo-Indian culture identified by distinctive fluted stone points, among the earliest well-documented cultures in North America.
Germania | Region east of the Rhine home to Germanic tribes, described in Tacitus's ethnography of the same name; never fully conquered by Rome.
Great Britain | Northern island partially conquered by Rome after Claudius's invasion, home to the Britons and site of Boudica's revolt.
Huns | Nomadic steppe people under Attila who invaded the late Roman Empire before being checked at the Catalaunian Plains.
India | South Asian subcontinent reached by Alexander's campaigns and home to the Maurya Empire under Chandragupta.
Mithridates VI of Pontus | King of Pontus who fought three wars against Rome and reputedly immunized himself against poison.
Numidia | North African kingdom of Berber horsemen under kings like Masinissa and Jugurtha, allied with and later subdued by Rome.
Olmecs | Earliest major Mesoamerican civilization, known for colossal stone heads, predating the Maya and Aztec.
Petra | Rock-cut capital of the Nabataean kingdom, a wealthy caravan trade hub later annexed by Rome.
Tigranes the Great | King of Armenia who built a short-lived empire before being defeated by Lucullus and Pompey.

# Institutions, Society, and Material Culture
> Political offices, social classes, religious practices, and the everyday technologies, goods, and structures that organized ancient life.
Helots | Enslaved serf population of Messenia and Laconia bound to Spartan-owned land, periodically rising in revolt against their masters.
chariots | Horse-drawn war and racing vehicles used from Bronze Age Near Eastern battles like Kadesh to Roman circus races.
ostracism | Athenian practice of exiling a citizen for ten years by popular vote, used against Themistocles and other leaders.
copper | Early worked metal, alloyed with tin to form bronze; heavily mined on Cyprus and central to Bronze Age trade networks.
agoge | Spartan state-run education and training regimen that molded boys into soldiers from around age seven.
Bronze | Copper-tin alloy that gave its name to the Bronze Age and armed early Near Eastern and Mycenaean warriors.
coins | Minted currency beginning in Lydia under Croesus, later standardized as Athenian owls and Roman denarii.
Gerousia | Spartan council of elders that, alongside the kings and ephors, governed the state and proposed laws.
gold | Precious metal that funded ancient treasuries and empires, from Lydian riches to Persian darics and Roman aurei.
latifundia | Large slave-worked agricultural estates that concentrated land ownership in the late Roman Republic.
ships | Vessels ranging from Phoenician traders to Athenian triremes that decided battles like Salamis.
tombs | Burial monuments ranging from Egyptian pyramids to Mycenaean tholos tombs and the Mausoleum at Halicarnassus.
plebeians | Common citizen class of Rome who won political rights from the patricians through the Conflict of the Orders.
Pontifex Maximus | Chief priest of Roman state religion, an office later absorbed into the emperor's own titles.
Praetorian Guard | Elite imperial bodyguard that both protected and repeatedly deposed and installed Roman emperors.
Triumphs | Ceremonial processions honoring victorious Roman generals, parading captives and spoils through the city.
Vestal Virgins | Rome's priestesses of Vesta, sworn to chastity and tending the sacred hearth fire, severely punished for its violation.
agora | Central public square of a Greek city-state, used for markets, assembly, and civic life.
Amber | Fossilized resin traded along routes from the Baltic to the Mediterranean, prized as jewelry in antiquity.
archon | Chief magistrate of Athens, originally a king's role, later a rotating annual office.
Boule | Athenian council of 500 citizens chosen by lot that prepared business for the assembly.
cataphracts | Heavily armored cavalry, especially of Parthia and Sassanid Persia, with mailed horse and rider.
chickens | Domesticated fowl used in Roman augury (the sacred chickens) to divine military and political decisions.
Debt Slavery | Practice of enslaving debtors, common across the ancient world until reforms like Solon's seisachtheia abolished it at Athens.
denarius | Standard silver coin of the Roman Republic and early Empire.
dictator | Roman republican office granting a single man temporary absolute power in emergencies, later abused by Sulla and Caesar.
earthquakes | Natural disasters recorded across antiquity, including the mid-5th-century BCE quake that triggered a major Helot revolt against Sparta.
ephors | Five annually elected Spartan officials who checked the power of the kings.
fasces | Bundle of rods with an axe carried before Roman magistrates, symbolizing their power to punish and execute.
gladiators | Combatants who fought in Roman arenas for entertainment, ranging from slaves and captives to volunteers.
jade | Precious stone especially prized in ancient China and Mesoamerica for ritual and burial objects.
Jupiter | Chief god of the Roman pantheon, equated with the Greek Zeus, honored on the Capitoline Hill.
libraries | Text collections such as the Library of Alexandria and Ashurbanipal's library at Nineveh.
Lupercalia | Roman fertility festival in February involving priests running through the streets striking onlookers with goatskin thongs.
olive oil | Staple Mediterranean product and major trade good, especially associated with Athens and its sacred olive trees.
Periplus | Genre of ancient sailing-directions text, most famously the Periplus of the Erythraean Sea describing Indian Ocean trade routes.
phalanx | Dense infantry formation of overlapping shields and spears, used by Greek hoplites and perfected by Philip II's Macedon.
pomerium | Sacred boundary of the city of Rome within which armed troops and burials were forbidden.
ports | Harbor infrastructure central to Mediterranean trade, from Piraeus and Ostia to Carthage's circular naval harbor.
purple | Tyrian dye extracted from murex snails, an extremely costly color reserved for royalty and high officials.
quaestor | Entry-level Roman magistracy overseeing financial administration, the first rung of the cursus honorum.
Roman Forum | Central public square of Rome housing temples, law courts, and political assembly spaces.
Roman Senate | Advisory body of Rome's elite that guided policy through the Republic and continued, in diminished form, into the Empire.
shields | Defensive armor central to hoplite and legionary equipment, such as the Greek aspis and Roman scutum.
swords | Weapons like the Greek xiphos and Roman gladius central to ancient infantry combat.
togas | Distinctive draped woolen garment worn by Roman male citizens as a mark of civic status.
war elephants | Battlefield animals used by Pyrrhus, Hannibal, and Hellenistic armies, notably at the Battle of the Hydaspes and Zama.
