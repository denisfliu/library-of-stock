# Ancient Greek Philosophy
> The Pre-Socratics, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, and the Hellenistic schools that first framed metaphysics, ethics, and logic.
Aristotle | Student of Plato who founded the Peripatetic school and formal logic; wrote the Nicomachean Ethics and Politics; theory of four causes.
Stoicism | Hellenistic school founded by Zeno of Citium teaching virtue and acceptance of fate; later exemplified by Seneca, Epictetus, and Marcus Aurelius.
Plato | Student of Socrates; theory of Forms, the philosopher-kings of the Republic, and the allegory of the cave.
atoms | Indivisible material particles posited by Democritus and Leucippus, later adopted in Epicurus's atomism.
Socrates | Athenian philosopher who left no writings; the elenchus method; tried and executed on charges of impiety.
love | Examined as eros in Plato's Symposium, where Diotima's ladder ascends from bodies to the Form of Beauty.
Parmenides | Pre-Socratic of Elea; argued being is one and unchanging, and that change is mere illusion.
Epicurus | Founder of Epicureanism; pleasure as absence of pain, atomism, and dispelling fear of death and the gods.
cynicism | Hellenistic school founded by Antisthenes, exemplified by Diogenes of Sinope's ascetic rejection of convention.
Nicomachean Ethics | Aristotle's ethical treatise defining virtue as a mean between extremes, culminating in the contemplative life.
Sophists | Itinerant Greek teachers of rhetoric such as Protagoras and Gorgias, criticized by Socrates for relativism over truth.
Heraclitus of Ephesus | Pre-Socratic of flux ("no man steps in the same river twice") who identified the logos governing change.
Phaedo | Plato's dialogue on Socrates's last day, arguing for the soul's immortality before his execution.
The Republic = Republic | Plato's dialogue on justice, the ideal city, and the philosopher-king, containing the allegory of the cave.
Zeno of Elea | Pre-Socratic student of Parmenides; paradoxes of motion, including Achilles and the tortoise, defending monism.
categories | Aristotle's classification of the basic kinds of being, such as substance and quality, later reworked by Kant.
Empedocles | Pre-Socratic who proposed the four classical elements, earth, air, fire, and water, governed by Love and Strife.
Heraclitus | Pre-Socratic of Ephesus known for flux, the unity of opposites, and the governing logos.
rhetoric | Art of persuasion theorized in Aristotle's Rhetoric and contested by Socrates against the Sophists.
the soul = soul | Subject of Plato's tripartite soul (reason, spirit, appetite) and Aristotle's soul as the form of a living body.
virtues | Character traits at the mean between excess and deficiency in Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics.
Euthyphro | Plato's dialogue asking whether the pious is loved by the gods because it is pious, or pious because loved.
Symposium = The Symposium | Plato's dialogue on love (eros), featuring Aristophanes's myth of split humans and Diotima's ladder of love.
Marcus Tullius Cicero | Roman statesman and Academic Skeptic who transmitted Greek philosophy to Latin readers in works like De Officiis.
water | Thales's proposed arche, or first principle, of all things, inaugurating Pre-Socratic natural philosophy.
Epicureanism | Hellenistic school founded by Epicurus teaching pleasure and freedom from disturbance (ataraxia) as the good.
Diogenes Laertius | Greek biographer whose Lives of the Eminent Philosophers is a chief source on the ancient philosophers' lives.
Meno | Plato's dialogue asking whether virtue can be taught, featuring the slave-boy demonstration of recollection.
nous | Greek term for intellect or reason; the highest human faculty in Aristotle, the second hypostasis after the One in Plotinus.
Sextus Empiricus | Ancient Pyrrhonist skeptic whose Outlines of Pyrrhonism systematized suspension of judgment (epoche).
Theaetetus | Plato's dialogue examining and rejecting several definitions of knowledge, including true belief with an account.
Aristotle of Stagira | Full epithet of the student of Plato who founded the Peripatetic school and formal logic.
Crito | Plato's dialogue where Socrates, awaiting execution, argues one must obey the state's laws despite an unjust verdict.
Politics | Aristotle's treatise classifying constitutions and arguing man is by nature a "political animal."
Timaeus | Plato's dialogue on cosmology, the demiurge, and the world's construction from geometric forms.
beauty | Central aesthetic concept; Plato's Form of Beauty in the Symposium and Kant's disinterested judgment of taste.
forms | Plato's eternal, unchanging archetypes of which physical things are imperfect copies.
logos | Heraclitus's term for the rational principle governing cosmic change, later central to Stoic physics.
Pythagoras of Samos | Pre-Socratic who held numbers are the essence of reality; founded a religious-philosophical brotherhood.
syllogisms | Aristotle's deductive argument form, as in "all men are mortal," founding formal logic.
ataraxia | Hellenistic ideal of untroubled tranquility sought alike by Epicureans and Skeptics.
eudaimonia | Greek term for flourishing or living well, the highest good in Aristotle's ethics.
Gorgias | Sophist rhetorician who argued nothing exists, or if it does, it cannot be known or communicated.
medicine | Greek medical theory, associated with Hippocrates and Galen, was closely intertwined with early natural philosophy.
motion | Subject of Zeno's paradoxes questioning its coherence, and of Aristotle's physics of actuality and potentiality.
Phaedrus | Plato's dialogue on rhetoric, madness, and love, containing the charioteer allegory of the soul.
Protagoras | Sophist known for "man is the measure of all things," a relativist epistemology.
Pythagoras | Pre-Socratic who held numbers are the essence of reality; founded a religious-philosophical brotherhood.
virtue | Central concept of Aristotle's ethics, a disposition at the mean between excess and deficiency.
virtue ethics | Aristotelian approach to ethics focused on character rather than rules or consequences, revived by Anscombe and MacIntyre.
De rerum natura | Lucretius's Epicurean poem expounding atomism and denying divine intervention or an afterlife.
Diogenes of Sinope | Cynic philosopher who reputedly lived in a barrel and mocked convention and Alexander the Great alike.
Eleatic school | Pre-Socratic school of Parmenides and Zeno arguing reality is a single, unchanging being.
Epictetus | Roman Stoic ex-slave whose Enchiridion teaches focusing only on what is "up to us."
immortality of the soul | Argued for by Plato in the Phaedo through arguments from recollection and the soul's affinity with the Forms.
Marcus Aurelius Antoninus Augustus | Full title of the Roman Stoic emperor-philosopher of the Meditations.
Meditations | Marcus Aurelius's Stoic private reflections on virtue, mortality, and duty.
mimesis | Concept of imitation central to Plato's critique of art and Aristotle's Poetics on tragedy.
Peripatetic School | Aristotle's school at the Lyceum, named for its walking style of teaching.

ships | Subject of the "ship of Theseus" paradox questioning identity through the replacement of parts.
syllogism | Aristotle's deductive argument form, as in "all men are mortal," founding formal logic.

Alcibiades | Athenian statesman and student of Socrates, featured in Plato's Symposium.
Allegory of the Cave | Plato's Republic image of prisoners mistaking shadows for reality, illustrating the ascent to knowledge of the Forms.
apeiron | Anaximander's term for the "boundless" or indefinite substance underlying all things.

Cyrene | City home to the Cyrenaic school founded by Aristippus, which taught hedonism as the immediate pursuit of pleasure.
Democritus | Pre-Socratic atomist who, with Leucippus, held that reality consists of indivisible atoms moving in the void.
Diogenes | Cynic philosopher of Sinope who reputedly lived in a barrel and mocked convention and Alexander the Great alike.
doxa | Greek term for common opinion or belief, contrasted by Plato with genuine knowledge, or episteme.
Elea | Southern Italian city home to the Eleatic school of Parmenides and Zeno.
Enchiridion | Epictetus's Stoic manual on focusing only on what is "up to us."
epoche | Term for suspension of judgment used by ancient skeptics, later adapted by Husserl as phenomenological "bracketing."
hedonism | View that pleasure is the sole intrinsic good, advocated by the Cyrenaics and, in refined form, the Epicureans.
mathematical Platonism | View that mathematical objects exist as abstract, mind-independent entities, tracing to Plato's theory of Forms.
Oedipus | Figure from Greek tragedy sometimes invoked in philosophical discussions of fate, guilt, and self-knowledge.
Seneca the Younger | Roman Stoic statesman and playwright whose letters and essays treat tranquility, mortality, and virtue.
Thales of Miletus | First Pre-Socratic philosopher, who held water as the arche, or first principle, of all things.
tragedy | Dramatic genre analyzed by Aristotle's Poetics as effecting catharsis through pity and fear.
akrasia | Greek term for weakness of will, acting against one's own better judgment, discussed by Socrates and Aristotle.
Anaxagoras | Pre-Socratic who introduced nous, or mind, as the ordering principle of the cosmos.
anger | Discussed in Seneca's On Anger as a passion to be mastered through Stoic reason.
atomism | Pre-Socratic doctrine of Democritus and Leucippus that reality consists of indivisible particles moving in the void.
circle | Geometric figure invoked in Plato's theory of Forms as an example of a perfect, unattainable ideal.
contradiction | Governed by Aristotle's law of non-contradiction, foundational to classical logic.
De Anima | Aristotle's treatise on the soul as the form of a living body, distinguishing nutritive, sensitive, and rational functions.
Glaucon | Plato's brother and interlocutor in the Republic who poses the ring of Gyges challenge to justice.
gods | Subject of the Euthyphro's dilemma questioning whether the pious is loved by the gods because pious, or pious because loved.
hylomorphism | Aristotle's doctrine that substances are compounds of matter (hyle) and form (morphe).
Lucretius | Roman Epicurean poet whose De rerum natura expounds atomism and denies divine intervention.
Marcus Aurelius | Roman Stoic emperor whose private reflections form the Meditations.
paradoxes | Category including Zeno's paradoxes of motion and the liar paradox, testing the limits of logic and language.
Parmenides of Elea | Pre-Socratic of Elea who argued being is one and unchanging, and that change is mere illusion.
pleasure | Central good in Epicurean and Cyrenaic ethics, analyzed critically by Aristotle as accompanying, not constituting, activity.
poets | Famously banished from Plato's ideal city in the Republic for imitating rather than knowing truth.
Quintilian | Roman rhetorician whose Institutio Oratoria systematized classical rhetorical education.
recollection | Plato's theory that learning is remembering knowledge the soul possessed before birth, illustrated in the Meno.
relativism | View that truth or morality is relative to a perspective, associated with the Sophist Protagoras.
The Apology of Socrates | Plato's account of Socrates's trial defense against charges of impiety and corrupting the youth.
the Myth of Er | Plato's closing myth in the Republic depicting the afterlife's judgment and choice of reincarnated lives.
Theophrastus | Aristotle's successor as head of the Lyceum, whose Characters catalogues moral types.

Thrasymachus | Sophist in Plato's Republic who argues justice is merely "the advantage of the stronger."
Zeno | Name shared by Zeno of Elea, famed for paradoxes of motion, and Zeno of Citium, founder of Stoicism.
a ship | Likely references the "ship of Theseus" paradox questioning identity through the replacement of parts.

contradictions | Governed by Aristotle's law of non-contradiction, foundational to classical logic.

horse | Likely references Plato's charioteer allegory of the soul in the Phaedrus, with its unruly and noble horses.

kings | Central to Plato's Republic, whose ideal city is ruled by philosopher-kings possessing knowledge of the Good.
man | Subject of Protagoras's dictum "man is the measure of all things."
