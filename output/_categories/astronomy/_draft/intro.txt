Questions on the Astronomy unit cluster heavily around extragalactic and high-energy phenomena alongside the solar system's familiar bodies. Quasars are the single most frequent answerline, and neutron stars, gamma-ray bursts, supermassive black holes, and pulsars all recur often, reflecting how much of the canon draws on compact objects and the extreme physics of accretion. Solar system topics remain a steady presence throughout — Jupiter, Uranus, Venus, Mars, Neptune, comets, asteroids, and sunspots all appear regularly, usually keyed to a distinguishing physical feature or moon.

Stellar astrophysics forms another dense cluster: star formation, luminosity, main-sequence evolution, nucleosynthesis, and the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram recur constantly, often paired with specific stellar remnants like white dwarfs and Type Ia supernovae. Cosmology contributes a smaller but conceptually central group of answers — the cosmic microwave background, cosmic inflation, dark matter, gravitational lensing and waves, and the Big Bang itself — probing the large-scale structure and history of the universe rather than individual objects.

Exoplanets, astrobiology, and observational technique account for a further slice of the canon: detection methods like radial velocity and transits, systems such as TRAPPIST-1 and Gliese 581, and instruments from the Hubble and James Webb Space Telescopes to Gaia and WMAP. Historical and contemporary astronomers appear as common-link answers tying together their signature discoveries — Hubble and the expanding universe, Kepler and planetary motion, Chandrasekhar and the white dwarf mass limit, Vera Rubin and dark matter — while a broad layer of physical concepts (redshift, the Doppler effect, magnetic fields, general relativity) supplies the vocabulary connecting all of the above.
