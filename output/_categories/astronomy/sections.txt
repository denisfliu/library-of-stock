# Solar System
> Planets, moons, and small bodies orbiting the Sun.
Jupiter | Largest planet in the solar system, a gas giant with the Great Red Spot storm and the four large Galilean moons.
Comets | Icy small bodies that develop comas and tails on approaching the Sun, originating in the Kuiper belt or Oort cloud.
Uranus | Ice giant planet with an extreme roughly 98-degree axial tilt, discovered by William Herschel in 1781.
asteroids | Small rocky bodies orbiting the Sun, concentrated mainly in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.
Venus | Second planet from the Sun, with a thick CO2 atmosphere causing a runaway greenhouse effect and surface temperatures over 460 C.
Mars | Fourth planet from the Sun, the "Red Planet," home to the solar system's largest volcano, Olympus Mons.
Neptune | Outermost ice giant planet, discovered via its gravitational effect on Uranus; orbited by the moon Triton.
sunspots | Temporary dark regions on the Sun's photosphere caused by magnetic flux concentrations, following an 11-year cycle.
Titan | Saturn's largest moon, with a thick nitrogen atmosphere and lakes of liquid methane and ethane.
Enceladus | Saturn's icy moon with active water-ice geysers erupting from its south pole, suggesting a subsurface ocean.
Triton | Neptune's largest moon, likely a captured Kuiper belt object with a retrograde orbit and cryovolcanic activity.
Ceres | Largest object in the asteroid belt, classified as a dwarf planet.
corona | The Sun's outermost atmospheric layer, visible during total solar eclipses and far hotter than the photosphere.
Kuiper belt | Disk of icy bodies beyond Neptune's orbit, home to Pluto and the source of many short-period comets.
Saturn | Ringed gas giant planet, second largest in the solar system, with the moons Titan and Enceladus.
Charon | Pluto's largest moon, massive enough relative to Pluto that the pair orbit a common barycenter outside Pluto.
Mercury | Smallest and innermost planet, with extreme temperature swings and a perihelion precession explained by general relativity.
Pluto | Dwarf planet in the Kuiper belt, formerly the ninth planet, with its largest moon Charon.
the Sun | Our solar system's G-type main-sequence star, the source of nearly all energy reaching Earth.
chondrites | Stony meteorites containing chondrules, among the most primitive, unaltered material from the early solar system.
cryovolcanism | Eruption of volatile materials like water or ammonia from icy moons such as Enceladus and Triton.
Europa | Jupiter's icy moon believed to harbor a subsurface liquid water ocean beneath its cracked ice shell.
Ganymede | Jupiter's largest moon and the largest moon in the solar system, bigger than Mercury, with its own magnetic field.
Late Heavy Bombardment | Hypothesized period of intense asteroid and comet impacts on the inner planets roughly 4 billion years ago.
meteorites | Fragments of asteroids or other bodies that survive passage through Earth's atmosphere to reach the surface.
Opik-Oort Cloud | Hypothesized spherical shell of icy bodies far beyond the Kuiper belt, source of long-period comets.
planetary migration | Process by which planets change their orbital distance over time through interactions with a protoplanetary disk or other bodies.
planetary rings | Disks of debris and ice orbiting a planet, most prominently around Saturn.
planets | Large bodies orbiting a star that have cleared their orbital neighborhood, per the IAU definition.
solar wind | Stream of charged particles continuously flowing outward from the Sun's corona.
1 Ceres | Largest object in the asteroid belt, the first asteroid discovered and now classified as a dwarf planet.
auroras | Luminous displays in a planet's upper atmosphere caused by charged solar wind particles interacting with its magnetic field.
Earth's Moon | Earth's only natural satellite, likely formed from debris after a giant impact with a Mars-sized body, Theia.
gas giants | Large planets composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, including Jupiter and Saturn.
Io | Jupiter's innermost large moon, the most volcanically active body in the solar system due to tidal heating.
KREEP | Geochemical component of some lunar rocks rich in potassium, rare earth elements, and phosphorus.
Oort cloud | Hypothesized spherical shell of icy bodies far beyond the Kuiper belt, source of long-period comets.
orbital resonance | Gravitational interaction where orbiting bodies exert regular periodic influence, locking their orbital periods into simple ratios.
space debris | Artificial objects such as defunct satellites and fragments orbiting Earth.
Theia | Hypothesized Mars-sized protoplanet that collided with early Earth, forming the Moon.
Yarkovsky effect | Force on a rotating small body caused by anisotropic thermal radiation, slowly altering its orbit.

# Stars & Stellar Evolution
> The life cycles of stars, from protostars through the main sequence to giant phases.
star formation | Process by which stars condense from collapsing clouds of gas and dust within molecular clouds.
stellar wind | Outflow of charged particles from a star's outer layers, driving mass loss especially in giant and massive stars.
main sequence | Stage of stable hydrogen-fusing stars forming a diagonal band on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.
nucleosynthesis | Process by which elements are formed via nuclear fusion in stars or in the early universe.
Taurus | Zodiac constellation containing the Pleiades and Hyades star clusters, the bright star Aldebaran, and the Crab Nebula.
Wolf-Rayet stars | Massive, hot, evolved stars losing mass rapidly via strong stellar winds, precursors to some supernovae.
accretion | Process by which matter falls onto a star, black hole, or protoplanetary disk, releasing gravitational energy.
hydrostatic equilibrium | Balance between inward gravity and outward pressure that maintains a star's stable structure.
accretion disks | Rotating disks of matter spiraling inward onto a star, black hole, or protoplanet, releasing energy through friction.
Cepheid variables | Pulsating stars whose period directly relates to luminosity, used as standard candles for measuring cosmic distances.
convection | Energy transport mechanism via bulk motion of plasma, occurring in stellar envelopes and cores.
instability strip | Region of the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram where stars pulsate, including Cepheids and RR Lyrae variables.
stars | Self-luminous spheres of plasma fusing hydrogen, and eventually heavier elements, in their cores.
binary star system | Two stars gravitationally bound and orbiting a common center of mass.
Binary Stars | Systems of two stars gravitationally bound and orbiting a common center of mass.
brown dwarf | Substellar object too low in mass to sustain stable hydrogen fusion, bridging giant planets and true stars.
Population III Stars | Hypothesized first generation of stars formed from pristine, metal-free gas after the Big Bang.
protostars | Early stage of star formation where a collapsing gas cloud has not yet begun stable hydrogen fusion.
red giants | Evolved, expanded, cooled stars that have exhausted core hydrogen and begun shell fusion.
triple alpha process | Nuclear fusion reaction combining three helium nuclei into carbon in the cores of evolved stars.
Vega | Bright, nearby A-type star that served as a historical photometric standard and will be the pole star around 13,000 CE.
Betelgeuse | Red supergiant star in Orion nearing the end of its life, expected to explode as a supernova.
binary star systems | Systems of two stars gravitationally bound and orbiting a common center of mass.
brown dwarfs | Substellar objects too low in mass to sustain stable hydrogen fusion.
Carbon stars | Evolved giant stars with more carbon than oxygen in their atmospheres, giving them a deep red color.
helium flash | Sudden ignition of helium fusion in the degenerate core of a low-mass red giant.
Hertzsprung-Russell diagram | Plot of stellar luminosity versus temperature, revealing the main sequence and giant and dwarf branches.
HR diagram | Plot of stellar luminosity versus temperature, revealing the main sequence and giant and dwarf branches.
kappa mechanism | Opacity-driven process causing stellar pulsations in variable stars like Cepheids.
nebulae | Clouds of gas and dust in space, sites of star formation or remnants of stellar death.
open clusters | Loosely bound groups of young stars formed from the same molecular cloud.
planetary nebulae | Glowing shells of gas ejected by low-to-medium mass stars near the end of their lives, leaving a white dwarf core.
red giant branch | Evolutionary stage where a star expands and cools after exhausting core hydrogen, fusing hydrogen in a shell.
star clusters | Gravitationally bound groups of stars, either loose open clusters or dense globular clusters.
variable stars | Stars whose brightness fluctuates over time due to pulsation, eclipses, or eruptive events.

# Stellar Remnants & Compact Objects
> White dwarfs, neutron stars, black holes, and the supernovae that produce them.
neutron stars | Extremely dense remnants of core-collapse supernovae, composed almost entirely of neutrons; observed as pulsars when rotating.
pulsars | Rapidly rotating neutron stars emitting beams of radiation observed as regular pulses.
black holes | Regions of spacetime with gravity so strong not even light can escape, formed from collapsed massive stars or larger processes.
Crab Nebula | Supernova remnant from a star that exploded in 1054 CE, observed by Chinese astronomers; houses the Crab Pulsar.
supernovae | Explosive deaths of massive stars or accreting white dwarfs, briefly outshining entire galaxies and seeding heavy elements.
white dwarf | Dense remnant core of a low-to-medium mass star left after it sheds its outer layers as a planetary nebula.
Type Ia supernovae | Thermonuclear explosions of white dwarfs exceeding the Chandrasekhar limit, used as standard candles to measure cosmic distances.
white dwarfs | Dense remnant cores of low-to-medium mass stars supported by electron degeneracy pressure.
core collapse | Sudden implosion of a massive star's iron core that triggers a Type II supernova.
neutron star | Extremely dense remnant of a core-collapse supernova, composed almost entirely of neutrons.
radio pulsars | Rotating neutron stars detected via regular pulses of radio emission.
supernova | Explosive death of a massive star or accreting white dwarf, briefly outshining an entire galaxy.
supernovas | Explosive deaths of massive stars or accreting white dwarfs.

# Galaxies & Large-Scale Structure
> Galaxies, clusters, and the cosmic web that organizes matter on the largest scales.
elliptical galaxies | Smooth, ellipsoidal galaxies with little gas or ongoing star formation, composed largely of older stars.
spiral galaxies | Galaxies with a flattened, rotating disk and spiral arms of gas, dust, and young stars, like the Milky Way.
Magellanic Clouds | Two irregular dwarf galaxies orbiting the Milky Way, visible from the Southern Hemisphere.
globular clusters | Dense, roughly spherical collections of old stars orbiting in a galaxy's halo.
Andromeda Galaxy | Nearest large spiral galaxy to the Milky Way, on a collision course with it in several billion years.
galaxies | Massive gravitationally bound systems of stars, gas, and dark matter, ranging from dwarf to giant elliptical and spiral forms.
galaxy clusters | Largest gravitationally bound structures in the universe, containing hundreds to thousands of galaxies bound by dark matter.
galaxy mergers | Collisions and combinations of galaxies over cosmic time, often triggering starbursts and reshaping galactic structure.
Zone of Avoidance | Region of the sky obscured by the Milky Way's disk, hiding extragalactic objects behind dust and stars.
cosmic voids | Vast, roughly empty regions of space between filaments of galaxies in the cosmic web.
galactic halo | Roughly spherical region surrounding a galaxy's disk containing old stars, globular clusters, and dark matter.
galaxy filaments | Thread-like large-scale structures of galaxies forming the cosmic web, separated by voids.
Large Magellanic Cloud | Largest satellite galaxy of the Milky Way, site of supernova 1987A.
Milky Way | Our home barred spiral galaxy, containing the solar system within one of its spiral arms.
Sagittarius A* | Supermassive black hole at the center of the Milky Way, imaged directly by the Event Horizon Telescope.
spiral galaxy | Galaxy with a flattened, rotating disk and spiral arms of gas, dust, and young stars.
galactic filaments | Thread-like large-scale structures of galaxies forming the cosmic web.
galaxy formation | Processes by which galaxies assembled from primordial density fluctuations and dark matter halos.
Great Attractor | Massive gravitational anomaly pulling the Milky Way and nearby galaxies toward it, obscured by the Zone of Avoidance.
Local Group | Galaxy group containing the Milky Way, Andromeda, and dozens of smaller galaxies.
Milky Way Galaxy | Our home barred spiral galaxy containing the solar system.
Omega Centauri | Largest known globular cluster orbiting the Milky Way, possibly the remnant core of a dwarf galaxy.
velocity dispersion | Spread of velocities among stars or galaxies in a system, used to estimate mass via gravitational dynamics.

# Active Galaxies & High-Energy Phenomena
> Quasars, blazars, and the extreme energetics of accreting supermassive black holes.
quasars | Extremely luminous active galactic nuclei powered by supermassive black holes accreting matter; among the most distant observable objects.
gamma ray bursts | Extremely energetic explosions, the brightest electromagnetic events known, linked to collapsing massive stars or neutron star mergers.
supermassive black holes | Black holes of millions to billions of solar masses found at the centers of most galaxies, powering active galactic nuclei.
Active Galactic Nuclei | Compact, extremely luminous regions at galactic centers powered by accretion onto a supermassive black hole.
blazars | Active galactic nuclei with a relativistic jet pointed nearly directly at Earth, showing rapid variability.
quasar | Extremely luminous active galactic nucleus powered by accretion onto a supermassive black hole.
Fast Radio Bursts | Brief, intense pulses of radio emission from extragalactic sources, of still-debated origin.

# Cosmology
> The origin, expansion, and large-scale evolution of the universe as a whole.
cosmic microwave background radiation | Relic thermal radiation from about 380,000 years after the Big Bang, key evidence for the hot Big Bang model.
Dark Matter | Unseen mass inferred from galactic rotation curves and gravitational lensing that does not interact via electromagnetism.
cosmic inflation | Theorized period of exponential expansion in the universe's first fraction of a second, explaining its flatness and uniformity.
gravitational lensing | Bending of light by massive objects predicted by general relativity, used to detect dark matter and distant galaxies.
reionization | Epoch when ultraviolet radiation from the first stars and galaxies ionized neutral hydrogen throughout the universe.
Big Bang | Event marking the origin of the universe's expansion from a hot, dense state roughly 13.8 billion years ago.
Lambda-CDM model | Standard cosmological model combining a cosmological constant (dark energy) with cold dark matter to explain cosmic structure and expansion.
CMBR | Abbreviation for the cosmic microwave background radiation, relic heat from the early universe.
redshift | Stretching of light to longer wavelengths due to the expansion of the universe or relative motion; used to measure cosmic distances.
dark energy | Mysterious component driving the accelerating expansion of the universe, inferred from Type Ia supernova observations.
Big Bang nucleosynthesis | Production of light elements, hydrogen, helium, and some lithium, in the first few minutes after the Big Bang.
cosmological constant | Term in Einstein's field equations representing a constant energy density of space, now associated with dark energy.
epoch of reionization | Period when radiation from the first stars and galaxies ionized the neutral hydrogen filling the universe.
Expansion of the universe | Increase in distance between distant galaxies over time, discovered by Hubble and now accelerating due to dark energy.
Gunn-Peterson trough | Absorption feature in distant quasar spectra indicating neutral hydrogen in the intergalactic medium before reionization.
Hubble constant | Rate of the universe's expansion, relating a galaxy's recession velocity to its distance.
Hubble's constant | Rate of the universe's expansion, relating a galaxy's recession velocity to its distance.
peculiar velocity | A galaxy's motion relative to the smooth Hubble flow of cosmic expansion, caused by local gravitational effects.

# Exoplanets & Astrobiology
> Planets beyond the solar system and the search for life elsewhere.
exoplanets | Planets orbiting stars other than the Sun, detected via transit photometry, radial velocity, and direct imaging.
radial velocity | Method of detecting exoplanets by measuring a star's wobble via Doppler shifts in its spectrum.
extrasolar planets | Planets orbiting stars other than the Sun.
transits | Method of detecting exoplanets by observing periodic dimming as a planet passes in front of its star.
Gliese 581 | Red dwarf star hosting multiple exoplanets, some once considered potentially within its habitable zone.
panspermia | Hypothesis that life or its building blocks can spread between planets or star systems.
TRAPPIST-1 | Ultra-cool red dwarf star hosting seven roughly Earth-sized planets, several within its habitable zone.

# Observational Astronomy & Instruments
> Telescopes, spacecraft, and the techniques used to observe the sky.
radio waves | Long-wavelength electromagnetic radiation used via radio telescopes to study pulsars, the CMB, and galactic structure.
21-centimeter line | Radio emission line from neutral hydrogen's hyperfine transition, used to map galactic structure and the early universe.
James Webb Space Telescope | Infrared space telescope launched in 2021, successor to Hubble, studying early galaxies and exoplanet atmospheres.
X-rays | High-energy electromagnetic radiation used to study black holes, neutron stars, and hot gas in galaxy clusters.
Gaia | European Space Agency mission mapping precise positions, distances, and motions of over a billion stars.
infrared | Electromagnetic radiation used to observe cool objects, dust-obscured star formation, and distant redshifted galaxies.
Hubble Space Telescope | Optical/UV/infrared space telescope launched in 1990, producing landmark deep-field images and precision distance measurements.
Lyman-alpha line | Spectral emission line of hydrogen used to detect and study distant galaxies and the intergalactic medium.
Spitzer Space Telescope | NASA infrared space telescope that studied cool objects like brown dwarfs, dusty disks, and distant galaxies.
WMAP | NASA satellite that mapped the cosmic microwave background with high precision, refining cosmological parameters.
light curves | Graphs of an object's brightness over time, used to detect transits, variable stars, and supernovae.
New Horizons | NASA spacecraft that performed the first flyby of Pluto in 2015 and later the Kuiper belt object Arrokoth.
radio | Long-wavelength electromagnetic radiation studied via radio telescopes to probe pulsars, galaxies, and the CMB.
Radio Astronomy | Branch of astronomy observing celestial objects via radio-wavelength emission.

# Astronomers & Physicists
> The scientists whose discoveries and namesakes define the field.
Edwin Hubble | American astronomer who showed galaxies lie beyond the Milky Way and discovered the universe's expansion via Hubble's Law.
Vera Rubin | American astronomer whose galaxy rotation curve observations provided key evidence for dark matter.
Johannes Kepler | German astronomer who formulated three laws of planetary motion describing elliptical orbits around the Sun.
Chushiro Hayashi | Japanese astrophysicist known for the Hayashi track describing the pre-main-sequence contraction of protostars.
Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar | Indian-American astrophysicist who derived the mass limit above which a white dwarf cannot support itself against collapse.
Galileo Galilei | Italian astronomer who used the telescope to discover Jupiter's four largest moons and support heliocentrism.
Herschel | William Herschel, discoverer of Uranus and infrared radiation, or the Herschel Space Observatory that studied cool infrared sources.
William Herschel | German-British astronomer who discovered Uranus in 1781 and infrared radiation.

# Physical Concepts
> The physical quantities and processes — mass, temperature, gravity, radiation — that underlie astronomical phenomena.
luminosity | Total energy a star radiates per unit time; combines with temperature via the Stefan-Boltzmann law to place stars on the HR diagram.
gravitational waves | Ripples in spacetime from accelerating massive objects like merging black holes, first directly detected by LIGO in 2015.
metallicity | Proportion of a star's or galaxy's mass composed of elements heavier than hydrogen and helium.
helium | Second most abundant element in the universe, produced in Big Bang nucleosynthesis and by stellar fusion.
blue | Color associated with hot, high-temperature stars per Wien's law and blackbody radiation.
distance | Fundamental astronomical measurement determined via parallax, standard candles, or redshift.
hydrogen | Most abundant element in the universe, fuel for stellar fusion via the proton-proton chain and CNO cycle.
temperature | Determines a star's color and spectral classification; central axis of the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.
cosmic dust | Fine particles of solid material in interstellar space, contributing to the extinction and reddening of starlight.
mass | Fundamental property determining a star's fate, luminosity, and lifetime.
gamma rays | Highest-energy electromagnetic radiation, produced in processes like supernovae, pulsars, and gamma-ray bursts.
carbon | Key element produced via the triple-alpha process in stellar cores, essential for organic chemistry.
Doppler effect | Shift in observed wavelength due to relative motion, used to measure radial velocities and redshift.
interstellar medium | Matter, gas and dust, that fills the space between stars within a galaxy.
magnetic field | Field generated by moving charges, shaping phenomena from sunspots and stellar winds to pulsar emission and galactic structure.
tidal forces | Differential gravitational forces that stretch bodies, responsible for tides, tidal locking, and tidal heating of moons.
velocity | Rate and direction of an object's motion, measured via Doppler shifts or proper motion.
astronomical unit | Unit of distance equal to the average Earth-Sun distance, about 150 million kilometers.
cosmic rays | High-energy particles, mostly protons, arriving from space and originating from sources like supernovae.
dark | Often refers to the universe's unseen components, dark matter and dark energy.
ionosphere | Layer of a planet's atmosphere ionized by solar radiation, affecting radio wave propagation.
iron | Heaviest element commonly produced by stellar fusion; its buildup in a star's core signals an imminent core-collapse supernova.
Lagrange points | Five points in a two-body system where gravitational and centrifugal forces balance, allowing stable spacecraft positions.
lithium | Light element produced in Big Bang nucleosynthesis, with an observed abundance discrepancy from predictions.
proper motion | Apparent angular movement of a star across the sky over time, relative to more distant background objects.
alpha particles | Helium nuclei, emitted in radioactive decay and involved in stellar nucleosynthesis reactions.
color | Indicator of a star's surface temperature, measured via color indices on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.
dust | Fine solid grains in the interstellar or protoplanetary medium that absorb and scatter starlight.
general relativity | Einstein's theory describing gravity as the curvature of spacetime by mass and energy.
gravity | Fundamental force of attraction between masses, governing orbits, stellar structure, and cosmic evolution.
hydrogen and helium | The two lightest and most abundant elements in the universe, produced primarily in the Big Bang.
ionization | Process by which atoms lose or gain electrons, key to spectral classification and the epoch of reionization.
methane | Simple hydrocarbon found in the atmospheres of giant planets and moons like Titan.
neutrinos | Nearly massless, weakly interacting particles produced in nuclear fusion and supernovae.
opacity | Measure of how strongly matter absorbs radiation, governing energy transport within stars.
pressure | Force per unit area that, along with gravity, determines stellar structure and hydrostatic equilibrium.
red | Color associated with cool, low-temperature stars per Wien's law.
rotation | Spin of a celestial body about its axis, affecting shape, magnetic field generation, and pulsar timing.
stellar mass | Fundamental property of a star determining its luminosity, temperature, and evolutionary fate.
tidal force | Differential gravitational force stretching a body, responsible for tides and tidal heating.
tides | Periodic rise and fall of ocean levels caused by differential gravitational pull, mainly from the Moon and Sun.
