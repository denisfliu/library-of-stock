Psychology questions in this unit cluster heavily around a small set of towering figures and the schools they founded. Sigmund Freud and Jean Piaget are the two most-asked answers by a wide margin, reflecting the canon's emphasis on psychoanalysis and stage-based developmental theory; Daniel Kahneman, Albert Bandura, and B.F. Skinner (asked under both his full name and initials) round out the most frequently cited individual thinkers. Alongside named thinkers, a large share of clues target general topics and processes rather than people — memory, emotions, personality, attention, and stress recur across many different questions as connective or common-link material rather than as answers tied to a single researcher.

The canon draws most heavily on twentieth-century American and European experimental and clinical psychology. Landmark studies are asked both for their designers and as standalone answerlines: Milgram's obedience studies, Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment, Asch's conformity line-judging task, Sherif's Robbers Cave Experiment, and Festinger's cognitive dissonance work (including the doomsday-cult study written up in When Prophecy Fails) form the backbone of the social psychology material. Developmental questions center on Piaget's stages, Kohlberg's moral dilemmas, Erikson's psychosocial stages, and attachment research built on Harlow's monkey studies, while behaviorist material runs from Pavlovian and Thorndike-style animal conditioning through Watson and Skinner to Bandura's Bobo doll experiment on observational learning.

Clinical and abnormal psychology is well represented through named disorders (schizophrenia, depression, PTSD, OCD, bipolar disorder, autism spectrum disorder, borderline personality disorder) and the diagnostic apparatus of the DSM, alongside therapeutic figures such as Aaron Beck and Albert Ellis for cognitive-behavioral approaches. Humanistic and positive psychology appear through Maslow, Carl Rogers, Martin Seligman, and Viktor Frankl, while personality and testing questions draw on trait theory, factor analysis, and instruments like the Rorschach and Thematic Apperception Test. A smaller but consistent thread of cognitive science questions covers memory research (Ebbinghaus, Loftus), perceptual and Gestalt phenomena (Wertheimer, Kohler, the phi phenomenon), and judgment and decision-making under Kahneman's heuristics-and-biases program.
