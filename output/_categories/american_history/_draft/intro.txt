American History questions in this unit draw most heavily on geography: individual states and cities, led by California, Chicago, Texas, Georgia, New York, and San Francisco, anchor a large share of the canon, since tossups and bonuses on state history routinely braid together colonial founding, statehood, and signature later events. Layered onto this geographic backbone is a dense presidential and political roster running from the colonial and Revolutionary founders through every twentieth- and twenty-first-century administration, with Andrew Jackson, Abraham Lincoln, Franklin Roosevelt, and Ronald Reagan among the most frequently asked named individuals. Native American nations and leaders, from the Iroquois Confederacy and Cherokee to the Sioux, Apache, and Nez Perce, recur throughout as both agents and victims of the expansion narrative.

The canon clusters especially densely around a handful of eras: the sectional crisis, Civil War, and Reconstruction generate a large fraction of all questions, spanning battles, generals, constitutional amendments, and the politics of emancipation and its betrayal. The Gilded Age and the conquest of the West supply a similarly rich vein of industrialists, labor conflicts, immigrant groups, and Indian Wars, while the twentieth century contributes a steady stream of material on the Progressive Era, the Depression and New Deal, World War II, and the intertwined stories of the Cold War and the civil rights movement. Supreme Court cases, from Marbury v. Madison to Brown v. Board and Roe v. Wade, and recurring institutions like the NAACP, the Ku Klux Klan, and the Federal Reserve appear across multiple eras rather than in any single one.

More recent decades are represented mainly through presidencies, scandals, and social movements, including Watergate, the Reagan era, the end of the Cold War, and the politics of the 1990s and 2000s, though this material is proportionally thinner than the nineteenth- and mid-twentieth-century core. Colonial topics, by contrast, are a small but steady presence, covering Jamestown, Puritan New England, and recurring conflicts with Native nations that set patterns replayed throughout later expansion.
