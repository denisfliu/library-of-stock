Opera questions span composers, individual works, arias, singers, and terminology. Composer answerlines ask for the figures who defined national schools and eras — Monteverdi, Purcell, and Handel in the Baroque, Rameau and Gluck bridging into Mozart's Classical era, Rossini through Verdi and Puccini in 19th- and early 20th-century Italy, Meyerbeer, Bizet, Massenet, and Offenbach in France, Wagner, Strauss, and Berg in the German tradition, Mussorgsky and Smetana further east, and Sullivan, Britten, Glass, Adams, and Adès in English-language opera. Individual-opera answerlines cover plot, characters, and famous excerpts (Rigoletto, Carmen, Tristan und Isolde), while a smaller set of answers names specific arias ("Vesti la giubba," "Nessun dorma"), singers (Maria Callas), or concepts (verismo, castrato, patter song).

The canon is dominated by Italian opera: Verdi and Puccini are the two most-asked composers, and their signature works — La Traviata, Rigoletto, Aida, La bohème, Tosca, Turandot — recur throughout the bel canto and verismo sections, alongside deeper cuts like Verdi's Nabucco, Il trovatore, and Otello and Puccini's Manon Lescaut. Mozart is the leading Classical-era composer, his three collaborations with librettist Da Ponte (The Marriage of Figaro, Don Giovanni, Così fan tutte) appearing more often than his German Singspiele or the early opera seria Idomeneo. Bel canto composers Rossini, Bellini, and Donizetti form a distinct cluster bridging Classical and Romantic Italy, each represented by both a signature tragedy and a lighter comedy, while the verismo generation — Puccini, plus the single-opera answers Cavalleria Rusticana, I Pagliacci, La Gioconda, and La Wally — marks Italian opera's turn toward realism.

Beyond Italy, answers cluster by national tradition. French opera centers on Bizet's Carmen and the Grand Opera spectacle of Meyerbeer's Les Huguenots, with Gounod's Faust and Roméo et Juliette, Massenet's Thaïs, and Offenbach's The Tales of Hoffmann rounding out the group. German-language opera is anchored by Wagner's Ring cycle and other music dramas and Richard Strauss's psychologically intense scores, with Beethoven's sole opera Fidelio, Berg's atonal Wozzeck, and Zimmermann's Die Soldaten marking its 20th-century edge.

A newer cluster of national traditions rounds out the field. Russian and Eastern European answers include Mussorgsky's Boris Godunov, Smetana's Czech nationalism, Dvořák's Rusalka, and Prokofiev's The Love for Three Oranges. English-language opera runs from Gilbert and Sullivan's Victorian operettas through Gershwin's Porgy and Bess and Menotti's Amahl and the Night Visitors to the 20th-century figures Britten, Glass, and Adams and the contemporary Thomas Adès. Throughout, castrato, Maria Callas, coloratura soprano, patter song, sleepwalking, and specific arias represent the category's attention to performance practice and vocal terminology alongside composers and works.
