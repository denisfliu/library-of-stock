# Silent Film, German Expressionism, and Soviet Montage
> Early cinema's foundational silent-era movements, from slapstick to Expressionist shadow to Soviet editing theory.
Charlie Chaplin | British-born silent film star and director of the Tramp character; directed and starred in The Great Dictator and Modern Times.
Sergei Eisenstein | Soviet director-theorist of montage editing; directed Battleship Potemkin, famed for its Odessa Steps sequence.
Fritz Lang | German Expressionist director who later worked in Hollywood film noir; directed Metropolis and M.
Buster Keaton | American silent-era physical comedian and director known for deadpan stunts; starred in The General.
Battleship Potemkin | Eisenstein's silent Soviet propaganda film famed for its Odessa Steps montage sequence.
Nosferatu | Murnau's unauthorized silent adaptation of Dracula, featuring the vampire Count Orlok.
The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari | German Expressionist silent film with distorted, painted sets, framed as a madman's asylum story.
The Great Dictator | Chaplin's sound-era satire in which he plays both a Jewish barber and a Hitler-parodying dictator.
Dziga Vertov | Soviet documentarian and montage theorist who directed the experimental Man with a Movie Camera.
Man with a Movie Camera | Vertov's experimental Soviet documentary showcasing city life through innovative montage and self-reflexive technique.

# Classic Hollywood and Film Noir
> The studio-era system, its stars and genres, and the shadowy crime films it produced.
Orson Welles | American director-actor whose Citizen Kane pioneered deep-focus cinematography; also directed Touch of Evil.
Alfred Hitchcock | British-American "Master of Suspense" director of Vertigo, Psycho, and Rear Window.
film noir | Shadowy, morally ambiguous crime genre of 1940s-50s Hollywood, marked by chiaroscuro lighting and femme fatales.
Vertigo | Hitchcock's psychological thriller about a detective's obsessive pursuit of a woman he believes is dead, plagued by acrophobia.
Citizen Kane | Welles's landmark film about newspaper tycoon Charles Foster Kane, told through flashbacks probing the mystery of "Rosebud."
Marlene Dietrich | German-American actress known for sultry roles in The Blue Angel and Josef von Sternberg's films.
Preston Sturges | American writer-director of fast-talking screwball comedies like Sullivan's Travels and The Lady Eve.
Sunset Boulevard | Billy Wilder's noir about a faded silent film star, Norma Desmond, who ensnares a struggling screenwriter.
Audrey Hepburn | Actress known for Roman Holiday and Breakfast at Tiffany's, a defining star of 1950s-60s Hollywood.
Barbara Stanwyck | American actress known for femme fatale roles, notably in Double Indemnity.
Billy Wilder | Austrian-American director of sharp-witted films across noir and comedy; directed Double Indemnity and Some Like It Hot.
Casablanca | Wartime romance set in Vichy-controlled Morocco, starring Humphrey Bogart and Ingrid Bergman.
Douglas Sirk | German-American director of glossy Technicolor melodramas like All That Heaven Allows and Imitation of Life.
Ernst Lubitsch | German-American director known for sophisticated romantic comedies with the "Lubitsch touch"; directed Ninotchka.
Frank Capra | American director of populist, sentimental films like It's a Wonderful Life and Mr. Smith Goes to Washington.
Rear Window | Hitchcock's thriller about a wheelchair-bound photographer who suspects a neighbor of murder while watching through his window.
The Birds | Hitchcock's thriller in which a California coastal town is besieged by unexplained, violent bird attacks.

# Italian Neorealism and Postwar Italian Cinema
> Postwar Italy's turn to street-level realism and its evolution into Fellini's and Antonioni's art cinema.
Federico Fellini | Italian director who moved from neorealism to surreal autobiographical spectacle; directed La Dolce Vita and 8½.
Michelangelo Antonioni | Italian director of alienation-themed art films; directed L'Avventura and Blow-Up.
Vittorio De Sica | Italian Neorealist director of Bicycle Thieves, following a poor father's search for his stolen bicycle.
La Dolce Vita | Fellini's episodic film following a tabloid journalist through Rome's decadent high society.
Roberto | Refers to Roberto Rossellini, Italian Neorealist director of Rome, Open City and Paisan.
8½ | Fellini's self-reflexive film about a director suffering creative block while making his next picture.
Bicycle Thieves | De Sica's Neorealist film about a poor father searching Rome for his stolen bicycle needed for work.
Marcello Mastroianni | Italian actor and frequent Fellini collaborator, starring in La Dolce Vita and 8½.
Pier Paolo Pasolini | Italian director-poet known for provocative, politically charged films like Salo and The Gospel According to St. Matthew.
Roberto Rossellini | Italian Neorealist director of Rome, Open City and Paisan, chronicling wartime and postwar Italy.

# French New Wave and French Cinema
> The critics-turned-directors who broke studio convention, and the French tradition around them.
Jean-Luc Godard | Leading French New Wave director known for jump cuts and self-reflexive style; directed Breathless and Weekend.
Francois Truffaut | French New Wave director and critic; directed The 400 Blows and Jules et Jim.
French New Wave | Late-1950s movement of young critics-turned-directors (Godard, Truffaut, Rivette) rejecting studio convention for personal, self-reflexive filmmaking.
Jacques Rivette | French New Wave critic-turned-director known for long, labyrinthine films like Celine and Julie Go Boating.
Agnes Varda | French New Wave-adjacent director often called its "grandmother"; directed Cleo from 5 to 7.
Breathless | Godard's jump-cut-driven French New Wave crime film about a petty criminal and his American girlfriend.
Cleo from 5 to 7 | Varda's real-time film following a singer awaiting cancer test results through Paris.
Jean Renoir | French poetic-realist director and forerunner of the New Wave; directed The Rules of the Game and Grand Illusion.
The 400 Blows | Truffaut's semi-autobiographical film following troubled youth Antoine Doinel, ending on a famous freeze-frame.
The Rules of the Game | Jean Renoir's satirical French ensemble drama about aristocrats and servants at a country estate, exposing prewar social hypocrisy.

# European Art Cinema
> Postwar auteurs across Sweden, Spain, Germany, and Eastern Europe pursuing personal, philosophical filmmaking.
Ingmar Bergman | Swedish director of stark psychological and existential dramas; directed Persona, The Seventh Seal, and Wild Strawberries.
Luis Bunuel | Spanish Surrealist director who collaborated with Salvador Dali on Un Chien Andalou; later worked in Mexico and France.
Rainer Werner Fassbinder | Prolific New German Cinema director known for melodramas critiquing postwar West German society.
Persona | Bergman's experimental film about a mute actress and her nurse whose identities blur.
Carl Theodor Dreyer | Danish director known for austere, spiritually intense films; directed The Passion of Joan of Arc.
Werner Herzog | New German Cinema director known for obsessive protagonists and jungle epics like Fitzcarraldo and Aguirre.
Andrei Tarkovsky | Soviet director of slow, meditative, image-driven films; directed Andrei Rublev, Solaris, and Stalker.
Satantango | Bela Tarr's over seven-hour Hungarian slow-cinema epic shot in stark black and white.
Jeanne Dielman | Chantal Akerman's minimalist film observing a widow's domestic routine over three days.
Salvador Dali | Spanish Surrealist painter who co-wrote and co-directed Un Chien Andalou with Luis Bunuel.
The Passion of Joan of Arc | Dreyer's silent film using intense close-ups to depict Joan of Arc's trial and execution.
Blue | Refers to Kieslowski's Three Colors: Blue, following a widow processing grief after her husband and daughter's deaths.
Jean Cocteau | French Surrealist writer-director known for Beauty and the Beast and Orpheus.
Krzysztof Kieslowski | Polish director of the Three Colors trilogy and The Decalogue, exploring moral and existential themes.
Lars von Trier | Danish director and Dogme 95 co-founder known for provocative films like Melancholia and Dancer in the Dark.
Milos Forman | Czech New Wave director who later worked in Hollywood; directed One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest and Amadeus.
Pedro Almodovar | Spanish director of vividly colored melodramas exploring gender and desire; directed Talk to Her and All About My Mother.
Portrait of a Lady on Fire | Celine Sciamma's French period drama about a painter falling for the noblewoman she has been secretly hired to paint.
Robert Bresson | French director known for austere, minimalist style using non-professional actors; directed Au Hasard Balthazar.
Surrealism | Film movement rendering dreamlike, irrational imagery, launched by Bunuel and Dali's Un Chien Andalou.
Suspiria | Dario Argento's Italian giallo horror film about an American ballet student uncovering a coven at her German dance academy.
Un Chien Andalou | Bunuel and Dali's Surrealist short film famous for its shocking eyeball-slicing opening image.

# New Hollywood
> The late-1960s to 1970s American renaissance of director-driven, genre-subverting studio films.
Martin Scorsese | American director of gritty crime and character studies; directed Taxi Driver, Goodfellas, and Raging Bull.
Stanley Kubrick | Meticulous American auteur working across genres; directed 2001: A Space Odyssey, A Clockwork Orange, and Barry Lyndon.
Taxi Driver | Scorsese's film starring Robert De Niro as alienated cabbie Travis Bickle, who plots to assassinate a senator and rescue a child prostitute.
Bonnie and Clyde | Arthur Penn's crime film about the Depression-era outlaw couple, a landmark of graphic violence in American cinema.
Barry Lyndon | Kubrick's candlelit period drama following an Irish rogue's rise through 18th-century society.
The Godfather | Coppola's Mafia epic following the Corleone family, starring Marlon Brando and Al Pacino.
2001: A Space Odyssey | Kubrick's science fiction epic featuring the rogue AI HAL 9000 and a mysterious monolith guiding human evolution.
A Clockwork Orange | Kubrick's dystopian adaptation following ultraviolent delinquent Alex, subjected to the aversion-based Ludovico Technique.
Apocalypse Now | Coppola's Vietnam War epic loosely adapting Heart of Darkness, following a mission to assassinate the renegade Colonel Kurtz.
Bullitt | Steve McQueen action film famed for its extended San Francisco car chase sequence.
Chinatown | Polanski's neo-noir following detective Jake Gittes into a web of Los Angeles water rights corruption.
Francis Ford Coppola | American director of the Godfather trilogy and the Vietnam War epic Apocalypse Now.
The Graduate | Mike Nichols's film about a directionless college graduate's affair with an older woman, Mrs. Robinson, and her daughter.

# American Independent and Experimental Cinema
> Avant-garde, DIY, and post-studio American filmmaking outside the major genre pipeline.
David Lynch | American surrealist director of unsettling, dreamlike narratives; directed Eraserhead, Blue Velvet, and Mulholland Drive.
Spike Lee | American director exploring race and urban life; directed Do the Right Thing and BlacKkKlansman.
Woody Allen | American writer-director of neurotic comedies; directed Annie Hall and Manhattan.
Andy Warhol | Pop artist who also made avant-garde experimental films like Sleep and Empire.
Do the Right Thing | Spike Lee's film depicting rising racial tension on a Brooklyn block during a heat wave.
John Cassavetes | American independent film pioneer known for raw, improvisational dramas like Faces and A Woman Under the Influence.
Maya Deren | Ukrainian-American avant-garde filmmaker known for the surreal short Meshes of the Afternoon.
Quentin Tarantino | American director known for nonlinear structure and genre pastiche; directed Pulp Fiction and Kill Bill.
Terrence Malick | American director known for poetic, nature-focused visuals and voiceover; directed Days of Heaven and The Tree of Life.
Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind | Michel Gondry film written by Charlie Kaufman about a couple who erase each other from their memories.
Sofia Coppola | American director known for atmospheric, mood-driven films like Lost in Translation and The Virgin Suicides.
There Will Be Blood | Paul Thomas Anderson's film following ruthless oilman Daniel Plainview's rise in early-20th-century California.
Wavelength | Michael Snow's structural film consisting of a single 45-minute zoom across a room.

# East Asian Cinema
> Japan, Hong Kong, South Korea, Taiwan, and China's directors, genres, and national film movements.
Akira Kurosawa | Japanese director of samurai epics blending Western influence with jidaigeki tradition; directed Rashomon, Seven Samurai, and Yojimbo.
Bong Joon-ho | South Korean director blending genre and social critique; directed Parasite and Memories of Murder.
Wong Kar-Wai | Hong Kong director of romantic, visually stylized films; directed Chungking Express and In the Mood for Love.
samurai | Warrior figures central to Japanese jidaigeki films, epitomized by Kurosawa's Seven Samurai and Yojimbo.
Chungking Express | Wong Kar-wai's dual-story Hong Kong romance shot in a kinetic, impressionistic style.
Ran | Kurosawa's late epic adapting King Lear to feudal Japan, depicting a warlord's sons warring over his kingdom.
Toshiro Mifune | Japanese actor and frequent Kurosawa collaborator, starring in Seven Samurai, Yojimbo, and Rashomon.
wuxia | Chinese martial-arts genre of chivalrous swordfighters, exemplified by Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon.
Yasujiro Ozu | Japanese director known for quiet family dramas shot with a static, low "tatami" camera; directed Tokyo Story.
Yi Yi | Edward Yang's Taiwanese family drama following three generations of a Taipei household.
Ang Lee | Taiwanese-American director spanning wuxia (Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon) and American drama (Brokeback Mountain).
Hayao Miyazaki | Japanese Studio Ghibli director of animated films like Spirited Away and My Neighbor Totoro.
In the Mood for Love | Wong Kar-wai's Hong Kong drama about two neighbors who bond over their spouses' affair.
Parasite | Bong Joon-ho's class-satire thriller about a poor family infiltrating a wealthy household; first non-English-language film to win Best Picture.
Park Chan-wook | South Korean director known for stylish, violent thrillers; directed Oldboy and The Handmaiden.
Rashomon | Kurosawa's film presenting a murder and assault from four contradictory eyewitness perspectives.
Yojimbo | Kurosawa's film about a masterless samurai who manipulates two rival gangs against each other in a small town.

# World and Global Cinema
> Directors and national cinemas from South Asia, the Middle East, Africa, and Latin America.
Satyajit Ray | Bengali director of the Apu Trilogy, a leading figure of Indian Parallel Cinema.
Abbas Kiarostami | Iranian director known for minimalist, self-reflexive films; directed Close-Up and Taste of Cherry.
Close-Up | Kiarostami's docufiction blending real events and reenactment about a man who impersonated a famous director.
Jane Campion | New Zealand director known for The Piano and Power of the Dog; second woman to win the Palme d'Or.
Alejandro Jodorowsky | Chilean-French surrealist director of midnight-movie cult films like El Topo and The Holy Mountain.
Fantastic Planet | French-Czech animated film depicting enslaved humans on an alien planet ruled by giant blue creatures.
Ousmane Sembene | Senegalese "father of African cinema," director of Xala and Black Girl, also a novelist.
Touki Bouki | Senegalese film by Djibril Diop Mambety following a young couple dreaming of escaping Dakar for Paris.
Uncle Boonmee | Apichatpong Weerasethakul's Thai film about a dying man visited by ghosts and past lives, winner of the Palme d'Or.

# Genre Cinema
> Popular genres — westerns, musicals, epics, horror, and science fiction — spanning eras and countries.
Westerns | American genre of frontier gunslinger films, from John Ford's classics to Sergio Leone's spaghetti westerns.
High Noon | Western shot in near real time about a marshal facing outlaws alone as the town's citizens refuse to help.
All That Jazz | Bob Fosse's autobiographical musical about a workaholic director-choreographer confronting mortality.
John Ford | American director who defined the Western genre with Monument Valley landscapes; directed Stagecoach and The Searchers.
Lawrence of Arabia | David Lean's sweeping desert epic dramatizing T.E. Lawrence's role in the Arab Revolt against the Ottomans.
42nd Street | Depression-era backstage musical with Busby Berkeley choreography, credited with reviving the film musical genre.
Bob Fosse | American director-choreographer of movie musicals; directed Cabaret and All That Jazz.
Dorothy | The Wizard of Oz's young protagonist, swept to the magical land of Oz by a Kansas tornado.
Gentlemen Prefer Blondes | Howard Hawks musical comedy starring Marilyn Monroe as a gold-digging showgirl singing "Diamonds Are a Girl's Best Friend."
movie musicals | Genre integrating song and dance into narrative, from Golden Age Hollywood spectacles to Bob Fosse's darker takes.
Sergio Leone | Italian director who created the "Spaghetti Western" with the Dollars Trilogy and Once Upon a Time in the West.
The Matrix | Wachowskis' science fiction film about a hacker discovering reality is a simulated construct controlled by machines.

# Film Theory, Technique, and Festivals
> Critics, theorists, editing and camera techniques, and the festivals that anoint prestige cinema.
Cannes Film Festival | Prestigious annual French festival awarding the Palme d'Or, a major launching point for international art cinema.
montage | Editing technique juxtaposing shots to create meaning, theorized and pioneered by Soviet directors like Eisenstein.
Andre Bazin | French film critic and theorist who championed realism and mentored the future French New Wave directors.
Andrew Sarris | American critic who popularized auteur theory in the United States.
animation | Frame-by-frame film medium spanning Disney features, Miyazaki's Studio Ghibli, and stop-motion techniques.
dutch angle | Camera technique tilting the frame off the horizontal axis to convey unease, associated with German Expressionism and noir.
film editing | Post-production process of assembling shots, foundational to both montage theory and continuity style.
Laura Mulvey | British feminist film theorist who coined "the male gaze" in her essay on visual pleasure and narrative cinema.
