# Classical Antiquity
> Greek and Roman poets, dramatists, and epics that form the bedrock of the Western literary tradition.

# Medieval Literature
> Epics, romances, and courtly verse from pre-1400 Europe outside Italy, including chansons de geste and troubadour lyric.

# Italian Literature
> From Dante and Boccaccio's founding of vernacular Italian letters through Pirandello, Calvino, and Eco's postmodern experiments.

# Spanish & Portuguese Literature
> The Iberian tradition spanning Cervantes and the Spanish Golden Age to Lorca, Pessoa, and Saramago.

# French Literature
> France's continuous literary dominance from Classicism and the Enlightenment through Romanticism, Realism, Symbolism, and the Theatre of the Absurd.

# German-Language Literature
> Writers of Germany, Austria, and Switzerland spanning Sturm und Drang through Weimar Classicism, Romanticism, and Modernism.

# Russian Literature
> The nineteenth-century golden age of Pushkin, Gogol, Tolstoy, and Dostoevsky through Soviet-era and dissident writing.

# Scandinavian Literature
> Norwegian, Swedish, Danish, and Icelandic authors, centered on Ibsen and Strindberg's revolutions in modern drama.

# Central & Eastern European Literature
> Czech, Polish, Hungarian, Balkan, and modern Greek writers, many shaped by twentieth-century totalitarianism and exile.

# British & Irish Literature
> English and Irish authors who recur in this unit as cross-national common links and points of comparison.

# Other
> Recurring words, themes, and historical references that surface across many European literature questions without anchoring a single author or work.
