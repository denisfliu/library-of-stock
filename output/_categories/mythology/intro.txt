Mythology questions draw overwhelmingly from the Greco-Roman canon, where the Olympian gods, the Trojan War cycle, and the labors of Heracles account for the single largest share of answerlines. Figures like Zeus, Apollo, Athena, and Odysseus recur constantly, alongside the interlocking genealogies of the Theban and Trojan royal houses and the Argonaut and Perseus cycles. Roman variants of the same gods (Jupiter, Juno, Venus) and the founding legends of Rome, from Aeneas to Romulus and Remus, extend this cluster further.

The second major bloc is Norse mythology, built around Odin, Thor, and Loki and the cosmology of Yggdrasil's nine worlds, culminating in Ragnarok. Egyptian mythology forms a tight, frequently recurring cycle around Osiris, Isis, Horus, and Seth, paired with the solar theology of Ra. Hindu and South Asian mythology is anchored by the two great epics, the Ramayana and Mahabharata, and by Vishnu's avatars and Shiva's family. Celtic and Arthurian material spans Irish heroic cycles like Cu Chulainn's and the medieval Arthurian romance tradition of the Round Table and the Grail quest.

Beyond these five dominant traditions, the canon broadens considerably: Mesopotamian myth (Gilgamesh, Enuma Elish, Inanna's descent), East Asian traditions (Shinto kami, Chinese creation myths, Journey to the West), Mesoamerican and Andean cosmologies (Aztec, Mayan, and Incan), and Slavic and Finnish material (the Kalevala, Baba Yaga) each supply a smaller but consistent stream of answerlines. African, Oceanian, and Native American traditions round out the world-mythology coverage, most often through trickster and creator figures.

Cutting across all of these traditions is a large body of cross-cultural motif answerlines — recurring animals (snakes, dragons, horses), elements (fire, water, thunder), numbers, and story types (flood myths, decapitation, transformation into trees or stars) that questions frequently ask about as common links spanning multiple mythological systems rather than any single tradition.
