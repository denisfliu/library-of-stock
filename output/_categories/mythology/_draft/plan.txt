# Greek and Roman Mythology
> The largest and most heavily tested cluster: the Olympian pantheon, the Trojan War cycle, the labors of Heracles, and the founding legends of Rome.

# Norse and Germanic Mythology
> Odin, Thor, and Loki preside over the nine realms of Yggdrasil, building toward the apocalyptic battle of Ragnarok.

# Celtic and Arthurian Legend
> Irish, Welsh, and British cycles spanning the Ulster Cycle's Cu Chulainn, the Mabinogion, and the Knights of the Round Table.

# Egyptian Mythology
> The Osiris-Isis-Horus-Seth cycle and the solar theology of Ra anchor a pantheon tied to the Nile, kingship, and the afterlife.

# Near Eastern and Mesopotamian Mythology
> Sumerian, Babylonian, Canaanite, and Persian traditions, from Gilgamesh's quest for immortality to Marduk's defeat of Tiamat.

# Hindu and South Asian Mythology
> The Vedic pantheon and the epics Ramayana and Mahabharata, following Vishnu's avatars, Shiva's family, and the Pandava-Kaurava rivalry.

# East Asian Mythology
> Chinese, Japanese, and Korean traditions: Shinto kami and Amaterasu, Chinese creation and immortal lore, and Korea's founding myth.

# Mesoamerican and Andean Mythology
> Aztec, Mayan, and Incan cosmologies centered on Quetzalcoatl, Tezcatlipoca, the Popol Vuh's Hero Twins, and the Inca sun cult.

# Slavic and Finno-Ugric Mythology
> Eastern and Northern European traditions spanning Slavic gods like Perun and Baba Yaga and the Finnish Kalevala's Vainamoinen.

# African, Oceanian, and Indigenous American Mythology
> Trickster and creator figures from Africa, the Pacific, Australia, and Native American and Arctic traditions.

# Symbols, Creatures, and Recurring Motifs
> Cross-cultural animals, objects, numbers, and story motifs (floods, dragons, tricksters) that recur across many mythological systems.
