Auditory Fine Arts questions in quizbowl draw overwhelmingly on the Western classical tradition, spanning roughly nine centuries from Hildegard von Bingen and Gregorian chant to living composers such as John Adams and Kaija Saariaho. The heaviest concentration of tossups falls on the Classical-into-Romantic core: Mozart, Beethoven, Brahms, Schubert, and Schumann anchor the German-Austrian symphonic and Lied traditions, while Bach, Handel, and Vivaldi represent the Baroque, and Haydn bridges the two eras. Opera forms a second major axis that cuts across every period, from Monteverdi's L'Orfeo and Handel's oratorios through Mozart's comic operas, Verdi and Puccini's Italian repertoire, Wagner's music dramas, and twentieth-century works by Britten and Glass.

A large share of the canon reflects composers who wrote in an explicitly national or folk idiom: the Russian Mighty Five (Mussorgsky, Rimsky-Korsakov, Borodin), Czech nationalists Dvorak and Smetana, Scandinavian figures Grieg and Sibelius, Hungarian pioneers Bartok and Kodaly, and English pastoral composers Vaughan Williams, Holst, and Elgar. Early twentieth-century French Impressionism (Debussy, Ravel) and the Ballets Russes collaborations of Stravinsky feed into a broader modernist cluster built around the Second Viennese School (Schoenberg, Berg, Webern) and later minimalists (Reich, Glass, Adams), who remain frequent answers in questions on contemporary concert music.

Beyond named composers and works, a substantial fraction of answerlines are generic: individual instruments (violin, clarinet, piano, harpsichord), musical forms and theory terms (sonata form, fugue, rondo, sonata, symphony, string quartet), and the orchestras, halls, and cities most associated with performance history (Vienna, Leipzig, Boston, Carnegie Hall). A distinct but smaller cluster covers American vernacular music, principally jazz, running from Armstrong and Ellington through Parker, Davis, and Coltrane, along with ragtime, ragtime-adjacent popular song, ballet, and Broadway musical theater, reflecting the unit's secondary emphasis on American concert and popular idioms alongside its European classical core.
