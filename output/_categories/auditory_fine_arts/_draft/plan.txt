# Medieval and Renaissance
> Chant, polyphony, and early vocal music from Hildegard through Palestrina, Byrd, and the Renaissance madrigalists and motet composers.

# Baroque
> Bach, Handel, Vivaldi, and the rise of tonal counterpoint, concerto grosso, fugue, and other instrumental forms of the 17th and early 18th centuries.

# Classical Era
> Haydn, Mozart, and early Beethoven establish the symphony, string quartet, and sonata form in Vienna.

# Romanticism
> Nineteenth-century German and Austrian symphonists, virtuoso pianists, and song composers who expanded harmony, orchestral scale, and emotional expression.

# Opera
> Opera composers, works, singers, and terminology spanning Monteverdi's earliest experiments through twentieth-century stage works, across every national tradition.

# National Schools and Folk Traditions
> Composers who drew on folk material and national identity, from the Russian Mighty Five to Czech, Scandinavian, Iberian, English, and Latin American nationalists.

# Impressionism and the Ballets Russes
> French tone-color innovators and the Russian-French ballet collaborations that reshaped early twentieth-century orchestral color and rhythm.

# Twentieth-Century Modernism
> Atonality, serialism, and experimentalism from the Second Viennese School through Ives, Bartok's mature works, and the postwar avant-garde.

# Minimalism and Contemporary Classical
> Repetitive structures and living composers, from Reich and Glass's minimalism to present-day concert-hall composers.

# Jazz and American Vernacular Music
> Ragtime, blues, and jazz from Armstrong and Ellington through bebop, cool jazz, and fusion, alongside American musical theater and popular song.

# Instruments, Forms, and Musical Institutions
> Individual instruments, generic musical forms and theory terms, and the orchestras, halls, cities, and performers tied to classical music life.
