# Boasian Foundations and Cultural Evolutionism
> The late-19th- and early-20th-century founders who established cultural relativism against earlier unilinear evolutionist schemes.
Franz Boas | German-American founder of cultural relativism and four-field anthropology; mentored Benedict, Mead, and Kroeber at Columbia.
culture | Core anthropological concept theorized as a learned, patterned, relativistic way of life by Boas, Tylor, and Kroeber.
anthropology | Holistic study of humankind spanning cultural, biological, linguistic, and archaeological subfields, founded by Boas and Tylor.
Alfred Kroeber | American Boasian anthropologist at Berkeley who studied California Native peoples and befriended Ishi, the last Yahi.
Ishi | Last surviving member of the Yahi people of California; lived at Kroeber's Berkeley museum after 1911.
Lewis Henry Morgan | 19th-century American evolutionist who studied Iroquois kinship terminology; wrote Ancient Society, influencing Marx and Engels.
The Golden Bough | James Frazer's comparative study tracing an evolutionary progression from magic to religion to science.
Zora Neale Hurston | Boas-trained African-American anthropologist and Harlem Renaissance writer who studied Haitian vodou and Southern folklore.
Edward Burnett Tylor | British founder of cultural evolutionism who broadly defined "culture" and theorized animism as religion's origin.
Primitive Culture | Edward Tylor's 1871 book defining culture and proposing animism as religion's evolutionary origin.
California | U.S. state where Kroeber documented Native peoples, including Ishi, the last Yahi, at Berkeley's museum.
cultural relativism | Boas's principle that cultures should be understood on their own terms rather than judged by outside standards.
Gene Weltfish | Boas-trained American anthropologist who studied Pawnee culture and co-wrote The Races of Mankind against scientific racism.
salvage ethnography | Boasian practice of urgently documenting cultures believed to be vanishing, as in Kroeber's work on Ishi.
The Golden Bough: A Study in Comparative Religion | Full title of James Frazer's comparative study tracing an evolution from magic through religion to science.

# British Social Anthropology and Functionalist Fieldwork
> Functionalist and structural-functionalist theorists who pioneered long-term participant-observation fieldwork in Melanesia and Africa.
Bronislaw Malinowski | Polish-British functionalist who pioneered intensive participant-observation fieldwork among the Trobriand islanders; wrote Argonauts of the Western Pacific.
magic | Ritual practice studied by Malinowski as Trobriand gardening magic, distinguished functionally from religion and science.
Trobriand islanders | Melanesian people off Papua New Guinea studied by Malinowski, famed for the Kula ring gift-exchange system.
ethnography | Descriptive fieldwork method of long-term participant observation pioneered by Malinowski's Trobriand research.
Alfred Reginald Radcliffe-Brown | British structural-functionalist who studied the Andaman Islanders, emphasizing social structure's role in maintaining stability.
E. E. Evans-Pritchard | British social anthropologist who studied the Nuer and Azande of Sudan, writing on witchcraft and segmentary lineage.
gift giving | Reciprocal exchange practice central to Malinowski's Trobriand Kula ring fieldwork.
Nuer | South Sudanese cattle-herding people studied by Evans-Pritchard for segmentary lineage and time-reckoning systems.
Alfred Radcliffe-Brown | Variant name for the British structural-functionalist who studied the Andaman Islanders and social structure's function.
Argonauts of the Western Pacific | Malinowski's 1922 ethnography establishing participant-observation method, describing the Trobriand Kula ring exchange.
Bronislaw Kasper Malinowski | Full name of the Polish-British founder of functionalist fieldwork methodology in the Trobriand Islands.
Dobu Island | Melanesian society near the Trobriands studied by Reo Fortune, cited in Benedict's Patterns of Culture as "paranoid."
Edmund Leach | British social anthropologist who studied Kachin political systems in Highland Burma, oscillating between hierarchy and equality.
Facing Mount Kenya | Jomo Kenyatta's ethnography of Kikuyu life and custom, written as a student under Malinowski.
Kula ring | Ceremonial gift-exchange system of shell valuables among Trobriand islanders, described in Malinowski's Argonauts of the Western Pacific.

# American Culture-and-Personality School
> Boas's students who linked culture to individual psychology and national character through fieldwork in Oceania and East Asia.
Ruth Benedict | American Boas student who contrasted Apollonian and Dionysian cultures in Patterns of Culture; also wrote The Chrysanthemum and the Sword.
Margaret Mead | American Boasian ethnographer of adolescence and gender in Samoa and New Guinea; wrote Coming of Age in Samoa.
Gregory Bateson | British-American anthropologist who studied Bali and the Iatmul of New Guinea; coined "schismogenesis"; married to Margaret Mead.
New Guinea | Melanesian fieldwork site for Mead's gender studies and Bateson's Iatmul research on the Sepik River.
Bali | Indonesian island studied by Mead and Bateson in Balinese Character, an early photographic ethnography.
play | Concept theorized by Bateson as a metacommunicative frame, related to Geertz's later "deep play" analysis.
The Chrysanthemum and the Sword | Ruth Benedict's 1946 study of Japanese national character, contrasting shame and guilt cultures.
The Chrysanthemum and the Sword: Patterns of Japanese Culture | Full title of Ruth Benedict's 1946 wartime study of Japanese culture and national character.
Coming of Age in Samoa | Margaret Mead's 1928 study arguing Samoan adolescence lacked Western storm-and-stress, later disputed by Derek Freeman.
Patterns of Culture | Ruth Benedict's 1934 book comparing Zuni, Kwakiutl, and Dobu societies as integrated cultural "configurations."
Zuni | Pueblo people of the American Southwest described by Ruth Benedict as an "Apollonian" culture in Patterns of Culture.

# French Structuralism and Exchange Theory
> French thinkers who modeled kinship, myth, and gift exchange as expressions of an underlying, universal structure.
Claude Levi-Strauss | French structuralist who analyzed myth and kinship via binary oppositions; wrote Tristes Tropiques and The Savage Mind.
Marcel Mauss | French sociologist, nephew of Durkheim, who wrote The Gift, analyzing reciprocity and obligation in exchange.
kinship | System of descent and marriage relations analyzed structurally by Levi-Strauss as governed by exchange and alliance.
marriage | Kinship institution analyzed via incest taboos and exchange theory by Levi-Strauss and structural-functionalists.
gifts | Objects of reciprocal exchange theorized by Mauss as carrying obligations to give, receive, and repay.
incest | Taboo universally regulated by kinship rules, theorized by Levi-Strauss as the origin of exchange and alliance.

# Symbolic and Interpretive Anthropology
> Approaches treating culture as a web of symbols, rituals, and meanings to be interpreted rather than causally explained.
Clifford Geertz | American interpretive anthropologist who treated culture as a web of meaning requiring "thick description"; studied Balinese cockfights and Java.
"Deep Play: Notes on the Balinese Cockfight" | Clifford Geertz's 1972 essay interpreting Balinese cockfights as symbolic status drama, modeling "thick description."
Java | Indonesian island studied by Geertz in The Religion of Java and his agricultural involution thesis.
Mary Douglas | British symbolic anthropologist who wrote Purity and Danger, analyzing pollution, taboo, and classificatory boundaries.
shamans | Ritual specialists who mediate spirit worlds, studied cross-culturally as healers and religious intermediaries.
symbols | Central unit of analysis for interpretive anthropologists like Geertz, who read culture as a system of symbols.
taboo | Prohibition concept theorized by Mary Douglas via pollution and boundary-marking in Purity and Danger.
witches | Accused practitioners of harmful magic studied by Evans-Pritchard among the Azande as a coherent belief system.
fairy tales | Folk narratives analyzed structurally and comparatively for recurring motifs, as in Joseph Campbell's monomyth work.
Islam | World religion studied anthropologically for ritual practice, veiling, and comparative religious meaning.
Joseph Campbell | American mythologist who proposed the comparative "monomyth" hero's journey across world fairy tales and myths.
religions | Systems of belief and ritual analyzed comparatively, from Frazer's evolutionary scheme to Geertz's symbolic approach.
rituals | Patterned symbolic practices analyzed by interpretive anthropologists for the meanings and social functions they enact.
thick description | Clifford Geertz's interpretive method of richly contextualizing observed behavior to recover its layered cultural meaning.
veils | Garments studied anthropologically as markers of religious practice, modesty, and gendered social meaning, notably in Islam.

# Materialist and Political-Economic Anthropology
> Theorists explaining custom through ecology, economy, and power, and later critiques of capitalism, debt, and violence.
Marshall Sahlins | American anthropologist who studied Pacific chiefdoms and potlatch; proposed hunter-gatherers as the "original affluent society."
David Graeber | American anarchist anthropologist who wrote Debt: The First 5,000 Years; associated with Occupy Wall Street.
Yanomamo | Amazonian people of Venezuela and Brazil studied by Napoleon Chagnon, who emphasized their endemic warfare.
cannibalism | Practice debated by materialist anthropologists like Harris over ecological versus symbolic explanations for the custom.
Marvin Harris | American founder of cultural materialism, explaining customs like sacred cows through ecological and economic causes.
potlatch | Pacific Northwest Coast gift-giving feast redistributing wealth, analyzed by Boas and later Sahlins.
violence | Theme central to Chagnon's Yanomamo studies and debates over the biological versus cultural origins of human aggression.
cultural materialism | Marvin Harris's theory explaining cultural practices as adaptations to material and ecological conditions.
Napoleon Chagnon | American anthropologist controversial for depicting the Yanomamo as "the fierce people" shaped by chronic warfare.
agriculture | Subsistence strategy analyzed through materialist ecological anthropology and studies of intensification and involution.

# Biological and Evolutionary Anthropology
> The study of human physical evolution, extinct hominins, and living primates through fossils, tools, and field observation.
Olduvai Gorge | Tanzanian site where the Leakeys found early hominin fossils including Homo habilis and Paranthropus boisei.
Jane Goodall | British primatologist who studied chimpanzee tool use and social behavior at Gombe Stream, Tanzania.
Neanderthals | Extinct hominin species that overlapped and interbred with early Homo sapiens in Ice Age Europe.
Africa | Continent central to human origins research at sites like Olduvai Gorge and to hominin fossil finds.
Dian Fossey | American primatologist who studied mountain gorillas in Rwanda; wrote Gorillas in the Mist.
Homo erectus | Extinct hominin species that first left Africa, used fire and Acheulean tools, ancestral to later humans.
stone | Material of early hominin tool industries, such as the Acheulean tools associated with Homo erectus.
teeth | Skeletal evidence used to reconstruct diet, age, and species identity in fossil hominin analysis.

# Contemporary and Postmodern Anthropology
> Later-20th- and 21st-century theorists addressing globalization, science studies, and the performance of everyday social life.
Arjun Appadurai | Indian-American anthropologist who theorized globalization via cultural "scapes" in Modernity at Large.
Erving Goffman | Canadian-American sociologist who theorized dramaturgical self-presentation in The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life.
anthropocene | Proposed geological epoch of pervasive human ecological impact, addressed by contemporary environmental anthropology.
Bruno Latour | French science-and-technology-studies scholar who wrote We Have Never Been Modern and developed actor-network theory.
Michael Taussig | Australian anthropologist known for work on shamanism, colonial violence, and mimesis in South America.

# Ethnographic Regions and Peoples
> Places and peoples that recur across the unit as canonical fieldwork sites and geographic answerlines.
United States of America | Home institution and fieldwork base for the Boasian and culture-and-personality anthropologists dominating this unit.
Haiti | Caribbean nation studied by Zora Neale Hurston, whose Tell My Horse documented vodou and folklore.
Kalahari Desert | Southern African desert home to the San, subjects of hunter-gatherer ethnography and affluence debates.
Brazil | South American nation home to Amazonian peoples including the Yanomamo studied by Chagnon.
Indonesia | Southeast Asian nation encompassing Bali and Java, key fieldwork sites for Mead, Bateson, and Geertz.
Nanook of the North | Robert Flaherty's 1922 silent documentary depicting, partly staged, the life of an Inuit family.
Australian Aborigines | Indigenous Australian peoples studied for totemism and kinship, influencing Durkheim's and Levi-Strauss's theories.
Inuit | Arctic peoples of North America depicted in Nanook of the North and studied for cold-climate subsistence adaptation.
Republic of Indonesia | Formal name of the Southeast Asian nation encompassing Bali and Java, key fieldwork sites in this unit.
