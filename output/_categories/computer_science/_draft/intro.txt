Questions on the Computer Science unit draw most heavily on core algorithms and data structures, with strings, pointers, recursion, and Turing machines among the single most frequent answerlines. Sorting algorithms (quicksort, mergesort), graph algorithms (Dijkstra's, depth-first search), and canonical structures like trees, heaps, stacks, and hash tables recur across nearly every batch, reflecting how much of the canon is built on the standard undergraduate algorithms curriculum. Theory of computation supplies a closely related cluster — NP, NP-complete, Big O notation, the halting problem, and the Boolean satisfiability problem — probing the formal boundaries of what algorithms can efficiently solve.

Programming languages and paradigms form a second major cluster, spanning widely used languages (Python, Java, JavaScript, C, C++) as well as functional languages and concepts (Haskell, LISP, monads, lambda calculus, currying) that get asked about disproportionately relative to their real-world popularity. Systems topics — threads, caches, CPUs, operating systems, TCP/IP, and concurrency primitives like semaphores and locks — cover the hardware and infrastructure layer beneath the language layer, while databases contribute a smaller, well-defined cluster built around SQL, relational joins, and transactional consistency.

Artificial intelligence and machine learning make up a growing share of the canon, from classical techniques (decision trees, support vector machines, genetic algorithms) to the neural network concepts increasingly central to the field (attention, overfitting, stochastic gradient descent, convolution). Cryptography and security, and graphics and compression, each supply smaller specialized clusters — hash functions and one-way functions on one side, ray tracing and Huffman coding on the other. Finally, a steady stream of answerlines are historical figures and organizations: Alan Turing, Donald Knuth, Edsger Dijkstra, Claude Shannon, John von Neumann, and John McCarthy recur as common-link answers tying together the algorithms, machines, and languages that bear their names, alongside companies like Google, Microsoft, and Intel.
