Physics questions in quizbowl draw heavily on the standard undergraduate curriculum, with the heaviest concentration in quantum mechanics, thermodynamics and statistical mechanics, and classical mechanics. Common-link and single-clue answerlines alike tend to be concepts and quantities rather than people: entropy, angular momentum, magnetic field, and quantum tunneling anchor the highest-frequency tossups, while foundational equations (the Schrodinger equation, Maxwell's equations, Navier-Stokes equations) and named effects (the Hall effect, photoelectric effect, Doppler effect) recur across difficulty levels. Statistical and condensed-matter physics contribute a dense cluster of terms — partition functions, phase transitions, superconductivity, ferromagnetism, and the Ising model — reflecting the subject's importance in modern research-level quizbowl.

Particle and nuclear physics form a second major cluster, built around the Standard Model's constituents (quarks, leptons, gluons, the Higgs boson) and its historical discoveries (the muon, positron, neutrino, and various mesons and baryons). Relativity and cosmology answerlines range from Einstein's field equations and time dilation to black holes, gravitational waves, and the cosmological constant. Optics and fluid mechanics, though smaller clusters, supply reliably recurring terms like diffraction, refractive index, viscosity, and the Reynolds number.

Named physicists appear far less often than concepts, and when they do, they are usually tied to a single signature contribution: Einstein to relativity, Feynman to quantum electrodynamics and his diagrams, Boltzmann and Gibbs to statistical mechanics, Maxwell to electromagnetism, Dirac and Heisenberg to the mathematical foundations of quantum theory. The canon is broad rather than deep at the middle frequencies, with hundreds of answerlines appearing only a handful of times — specific effects, theorems, equations, and constants that reward detailed knowledge of a particular subfield.
