# International Relations Theory
> Core concepts and schools of IR — realism, neorealism, liberal institutionalism, and constructivism — and the theorists who built them.
anarchy | Absence of a central global authority above sovereign states; foundational assumption shared by realist and liberal IR theory.
power | Central IR concept denoting capacity to influence others' behavior; analyzed as hard, soft, or structural power.
realism | School of IR emphasizing state self-interest, anarchy, and power politics; associated with Morgenthau, Waltz, and Mearsheimer.
John Mearsheimer | American political scientist and leading offensive realist; author of The Tragedy of Great Power Politics.
nuclear weapons | Central to deterrence theory and mutually assured destruction; studied by Thomas Schelling and Kenneth Waltz on stability.
war | Central IR subject studied through causal theories such as the democratic peace and power transition theory.
Kenneth Waltz | American political scientist, founder of neorealism; author of Theory of International Politics and Man, the State, and War.
Neorealism | Structural realist school founded by Kenneth Waltz, emphasizing the anarchic international system over unit-level factors.
the state | Sovereign political entity and primary unit of IR analysis; per Weber, holds a monopoly on legitimate force within its territory.
constructivism | IR school holding that state identities and interests are socially constructed; associated with Alexander Wendt.
liberalism | IR school emphasizing cooperation, institutions, and interdependence, contrasted with realism; associated with Robert Keohane.
security | Central IR concept spanning national and international security studies, including securitization theory.
hegemony | Dominance of one state or actor over a system; central to hegemonic stability theory and Gramsci's cultural hegemony.
international relations | Academic discipline studying interactions between states, encompassing realism, liberalism, and constructivism.
Robert Keohane | American political scientist, co-founder of neoliberal institutionalism; co-authored Power and Interdependence with Joseph Nye.
social constructivism | IR school holding that state identities and interests are shaped by shared ideas and norms rather than fixed material power.
soft power | Concept coined by Joseph Nye describing influence through attraction and persuasion rather than coercion.

states | Sovereign entities that are the primary unit of analysis in international relations theory.
Thomas Schelling | American economist and game theorist, Nobel laureate for work on strategic behavior and nuclear deterrence; wrote The Strategy of Conflict.

# Comparative Politics & Democracy
> Institutions and processes of democratic government: elections, parties, federalism, and the ideologies that animate them.
political parties | Organizations that aggregate interests and contest elections; party systems classified as two-party, dominant-party, or multiparty.
democracy | Government based on popular sovereignty and free elections; contrasted with autocracy and measured by indices like Freedom House.
voting | Mechanism of political participation studied through electoral systems, turnout patterns, and rational choice models.
elections | Formal process of selecting representatives, studied via electoral system design, turnout, and campaign behavior.
democracies | Studied comparatively, notably in Democratic Peace Theory's claim that they rarely fight one another.
gerrymandering | Manipulation of electoral district boundaries to favor a party, via techniques like packing and cracking.
liberal | Political ideology emphasizing individual rights, limited government, and free markets; contrasted with conservatism and realism.
federalism | System dividing power between central and regional governments, exemplified by the United States and Canada.
Francis Fukuyama | American political scientist known for "The End of History and the Last Man," arguing liberal democracy is history's ideological endpoint.
voters | Studied through theories of voting behavior, party identification, and rational choice models.

# Law & Legal Systems
> Civil and criminal law concepts spanning torts, contracts, criminal liability, and the common-law tradition.
Tort law | Branch of civil law addressing civil wrongs such as negligence, covering duty of care and liability for damages.
contracts | Area of civil law governing legally binding agreements, requiring offer, acceptance, and consideration.
Mens rea | Latin for "guilty mind"; the mental-state element of criminal liability, contrasted with actus reus.
common law | Legal system based on judicial precedent, originating in England, contrasted with civil law systems.
copyright | Legal protection granting authors exclusive rights over creative works, central to intellectual property law and policy.
negligence | Tort standard based on failure to exercise reasonable care, requiring duty, breach, causation, and damages.
criminal law | Body of law defining crimes and punishments, distinguished from civil law by concepts like mens rea and actus reus.
juries | Bodies of citizens who decide facts at trial, central to common-law adversarial legal systems.
pornography | Subject of legal debates over obscenity and free speech, notably in Catharine MacKinnon's feminist legal theory.
victims of crime | Subject of victimology, studying the psychological, social, and legal treatment of crime victims.

# Media & Communications Studies
> Theorists and concepts explaining how mass media, propaganda, and public opinion shape society and politics.
Marshall McLuhan | Canadian media theorist who coined "the medium is the message" and "global village"; wrote Understanding Media and The Gutenberg Galaxy.
media | Broad object of study covering mass communication's effects on public opinion, framing, and agenda-setting.
propaganda | Communication used to influence public opinion, studied via Edward Bernays and Walter Lippmann on opinion manipulation.
television | Broadcast medium studied for its effects on political communication, public opinion, and cultivation theory.
video games | Studied for effects on behavior and political socialization, and as a medium of mass communication.
Walter Lippmann | American journalist and commentator, author of Public Opinion (1922), which introduced "stereotype" in its social science sense.

# Sociology & Social Theory
> Social theory and critical thought on class, gender, and nationhood, from Marx to Butler to Anderson.
Karl Marx | German philosopher and economist (b. 1818), founder of Marxism and historical materialism; co-wrote The Communist Manifesto and Das Kapital.
feminism | Social and political movement/theory analyzing gender inequality, spanning waves from suffrage to Judith Butler's queer theory.
Gender Trouble | 1990 book by Judith Butler introducing the concept of gender performativity, foundational to queer theory.
heterosexuality | Studied as a social institution and construct, notably via Adrienne Rich's concept of "compulsory heterosexuality."
Imagined Communities | 1983 book by Benedict Anderson arguing nations are socially constructed communities imagined through print capitalism.
marriage | Studied as a legal and social institution, including debates over its legal definition and same-sex marriage.

# Education & Pedagogy
> Educational theory and pedagogical methods, centered on child-centered and progressive approaches to schooling.
Maria Montessori | Italian physician and educator who developed the Montessori Method, a child-centered pedagogy of self-directed, hands-on learning.
education | Field covering pedagogy, curriculum, and schooling systems, studied by theorists such as Montessori and Dewey.

# Business, Management & Globalization
> Organizational management theory and case studies in globalization and cultural standardization.
management | Field concerned with organizing and directing organizations, including theories of leadership and organizational behavior.
McDonald's | Case study in globalization and standardization, notably George Ritzer's "McDonaldization" thesis.

# Countries & Case Studies
> Nations and cities recurring as comparative case studies across political science and sociology.
Canada | North American federal parliamentary democracy studied for its multiparty system, bilingualism, and federal structure.
China | Studied comparatively for authoritarian one-party governance and rapid state-led economic development.
Japan | Studied for its parliamentary system, long LDP dominant-party rule, and postwar constitution.
Israel | Studied for parliamentary coalition politics and its central role in Middle Eastern international relations.
New York City | Studied as a global city and case study in urban politics and governance.
