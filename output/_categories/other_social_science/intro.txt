Questions in Other Social Science draw heavily on international relations theory, with the realist-liberal-constructivist debate forming the unit's densest cluster. Core concepts like anarchy, power, hegemony, and security recur alongside the theorists who formalized them: Kenneth Waltz's neorealism, Robert Keohane's neoliberal institutionalism, John Mearsheimer's offensive realism, and Alexander Wendt's constructivism. Nuclear deterrence and game theory, associated especially with Thomas Schelling, form a related strategic-studies thread.

A second major cluster covers domestic political institutions and processes — democracy, elections, voting, political parties, federalism, and gerrymandering — often studied comparatively across named states such as Canada, China, Japan, and Israel, which recur as case studies in both comparative politics and international relations. Francis Fukuyama's thesis on the endpoint of liberal democracy bridges this cluster with IR theory.

Law questions form a distinct thread built around civil and criminal doctrine: torts, contracts, negligence, mens rea, common law, criminal law, copyright, and the role of juries, generally tested through definitional and case-based clues rather than named jurists. Media and communications studies contribute a third cluster anchored by Marshall McLuhan and Walter Lippmann, with propaganda, television, and public opinion as recurring subjects.

Sociology and social theory appear through canonical texts and concepts — Karl Marx's foundational class analysis, Judith Butler's Gender Trouble, Benedict Anderson's Imagined Communities, and debates over feminism and heterosexuality as social institutions — while education (Maria Montessori) and business/management, including McDonald's as a globalization case study, round out the unit's remaining answerlines.
