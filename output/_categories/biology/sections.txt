# Cell Biology

liver | Organ of detoxification, bile production, glycogen storage, and urea cycle activity; composed mainly of hepatocytes.
acetylcholine | Neurotransmitter at neuromuscular junctions and in the parasympathetic nervous system; broken down by acetylcholinesterase.
hippocampus | Brain structure central to memory formation and spatial navigation; site of long-term potentiation studies.
Alzheimer's disease | Neurodegenerative disorder marked by amyloid-beta plaques and tau tangles, causing progressive memory loss.
dopamine | Neurotransmitter involved in reward and motor control; depleted in Parkinson's disease.
retina | Light-sensing tissue at the back of the eye containing rod and cone photoreceptors with opsins.
serotonin | Neurotransmitter regulating mood and digestion; the target of many antidepressants.
adrenal glands | Endocrine glands atop the kidneys secreting cortisol, aldosterone, and epinephrine.
heart | Muscular four-chambered pump driving circulation; its conduction system includes the bundle of His and Purkinje fibers.
multiple sclerosis | Autoimmune disease attacking myelin sheaths in the central nervous system, disrupting nerve signaling.
Kidneys | Paired organs filtering blood via nephrons, regulating fluid balance and producing urine.
platelets | Cell fragments involved in blood clotting, aggregating at sites of vascular injury.
skin | Largest organ, providing a barrier via the epidermis and dermis; site of melanin pigmentation.
kidney | Organ filtering blood via nephrons; regulates water and electrolyte balance and produces urine.
long term potentiation | Persistent strengthening of synapses, especially in the hippocampus, underlying learning and memory.
lungs | Paired respiratory organs where alveoli exchange oxygen and carbon dioxide with blood.
neurons | Electrically excitable cells transmitting signals via axons and dendrites using action potentials.
Parkinson's disease | Neurodegenerative disorder from dopaminergic neuron loss in the substantia nigra, linked to alpha-synuclein.
placenta | Organ exchanging nutrients, gas, and waste between mother and fetus during pregnancy.
red blood cells | Anucleate, hemoglobin-filled cells that carry oxygen through the bloodstream.
circadian rhythms | Roughly 24-hour biological cycles regulated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus and clock genes.
hypothalamus | Brain region linking nervous and endocrine systems, regulating temperature, hunger, and hormone release via the pituitary.
pancreas | Organ secreting digestive enzymes and the hormones insulin and glucagon from its islets.

sodium | Cation central to the sodium-potassium pump and neuronal action potentials.

axons | Long neuronal projections that conduct action potentials away from the cell body.

metastasis | Spread of cancer cells from a primary tumor to distant tissues.

cone snails | Predatory marine snails that use potent venom peptides to capture prey.
feathers | Keratin structures unique to birds, used for flight, insulation, and display.
fireflies | Beetles that produce bioluminescent light via luciferase for mate attraction.
flight | Locomotion through the air, evolved independently in insects, birds, and bats.
gizzard | Muscular digestive organ in birds and some other animals that grinds food.
hagfish | Jawless, eel-like marine vertebrate known for producing defensive slime.
honey bees | Eusocial insects that communicate food locations via the waggle dance.
Hydra | Small freshwater cnidarian known for regenerative ability and radial symmetry.
mantis shrimp | Crustacean known for extremely fast raptorial strikes and complex compound eyes.
marsupials | Mammal group giving birth to underdeveloped young that continue developing in a pouch.
mollusks | Phylum including snails, bivalves, and cephalopods, typically with a muscular foot and mantle.
molting | Periodic shedding of an exoskeleton or outer covering to allow growth, as in arthropods.
monarch butterfly | Migratory butterfly species whose larvae feed on toxic milkweed, deterring predators.
nematodes | Roundworms, including the model organism C. elegans.
pigeons | Bird species notable for homing ability and historical use in genetics and behavior research.
platypus | Egg-laying monotreme mammal with a duck-like bill and venomous spurs.
rabbits | Lagomorph mammals known for rapid reproduction and coprophagy.
Radula | Rasping, tooth-covered feeding organ found in most mollusks.
schooling | Coordinated group swimming behavior in fish that reduces predation risk.
Sea otters | Marine mammal keystone species that controls sea urchin populations, protecting kelp forests.
swim bladder | Gas-filled organ in bony fish that controls buoyancy.
wings | Appendages enabling flight, evolved independently in insects, birds, and bats.

# Scientists & History of Biology
> Researchers, historic experiments, and discoveries that shaped the field.
Frederick Sanger | Biochemist who developed DNA and protein sequencing methods, winning two Nobel Prizes.
Meselson-Stahl experiment | Classic experiment using density-labeled DNA that confirmed semiconservative replication.
Camillo Golgi | Italian scientist who developed silver staining and discovered the Golgi apparatus.
Charles Darwin | Naturalist who proposed natural selection as the mechanism of evolution in On the Origin of Species.
Gregor Mendel | Augustinian monk whose pea plant experiments established the laws of inheritance.
Luria-Delbruck experiment | Fluctuation test demonstrating that bacterial mutations arise randomly, not in response to selection.
Ronald Fisher | Statistician and geneticist who unified Mendelian genetics with Darwinian evolution.
Santiago Ramon y Cajal | Neuroscientist who established the neuron doctrine using Golgi staining.
Barbara McClintock | Geneticist who discovered transposable elements, "jumping genes," in maize.
James Watson AND Francis Crick | Scientists who determined the double-helix structure of DNA in 1953.
John Maynard Smith | Evolutionary biologist who applied game theory to evolution, developing the evolutionarily stable strategy concept.
Lynn Margulis | Biologist who championed the endosymbiotic theory of mitochondrial and chloroplast origin.
Miller-Urey experiment | Classic experiment simulating early Earth conditions that produced amino acids from simple gases.
Motoo Kimura | Population geneticist who proposed the neutral theory of molecular evolution.
Stephen Jay Gould | Paleontologist and evolutionary biologist who co-developed the theory of punctuated equilibrium.
William Donald Hamilton | Evolutionary biologist who formalized kin selection with Hamilton's rule.
Haldane | J.B.S. Haldane, geneticist who helped found population genetics and proposed early theories on the origin of life.
John Snow | Physician who traced a London cholera outbreak to contaminated water, founding modern epidemiology.
Konrad Lorenz | Ethologist who studied imprinting in birds and co-founded the field of ethology.
Richard Dawkins | Evolutionary biologist known for popularizing the gene-centered view of evolution in The Selfish Gene.
Thomas Hunt Morgan | Geneticist who used Drosophila to establish the chromosomal theory of inheritance.
W. D. Hamilton | Evolutionary biologist who formalized kin selection with Hamilton's rule.
