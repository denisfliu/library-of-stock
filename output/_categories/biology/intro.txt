Biology questions in this unit draw most heavily on molecular and cell biology: the machinery of DNA replication, transcription, and translation; the organelles and processes that keep a eukaryotic cell alive; and the laboratory techniques (PCR, gel electrophoresis, CRISPR, sequencing, blotting) used to study them. Biochemistry and metabolism form a closely related cluster, covering amino acids, enzymes, and the central pathways of glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, and photosynthesis.

Human physiology is the single largest content area, spanning every organ system: nervous (neurons, neurotransmitters, brain structures), endocrine (hormones and glands), circulatory, digestive, respiratory, renal, and reproductive. Associated diseases and disorders, from Alzheimer's and Parkinson's to genetic conditions like cystic fibrosis and sickle cell anemia, are frequently asked alongside the normal anatomy and physiology they disrupt. Immunology, covering antibodies, immune cell types, and autoimmune and infectious disease, is closely intertwined with both physiology and microbiology.

Genetics and evolutionary biology form another major cluster, ranging from Mendelian inheritance and population genetics to natural selection, speciation, and molecular evolution, frequently illustrated through model organisms such as Drosophila, E. coli, C. elegans, and Arabidopsis. Microbiology and virology cover bacteria, archaea, fungi, protists, and viruses, including the diseases they cause and the techniques (Gram staining, culturing) used to study them. Plant biology and ecology appear as smaller but consistent clusters, the former centered on plant anatomy and hormones, the latter on population and community-level interactions.

A modest set of questions asks about non-human animal diversity and behavior, and about the scientists and landmark experiments that built the field, including Mendel, Darwin, Watson and Crick, McClintock, and the Meselson-Stahl and Miller-Urey experiments. Across all these areas, the canon is dominated by a long tail of frequently recurring single terms rather than a small number of dominant answers, reflecting the breadth of introductory and molecular biology as tested subject matter.
