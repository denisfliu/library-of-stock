Chemistry questions at this level draw heavily on physical chemistry and organic chemistry, with a dense secondary layer of inorganic, analytical, and biochemical material. The most-asked answers include broad conceptual terms such as free radicals, chirality, molecular orbitals, aromaticity, and Gibbs free energy alongside instrumental techniques like mass spectrometry, NMR spectroscopy, and chromatography. Individual elements form one of the largest recurring clusters, with transition metals (copper, iron, gold, palladium, nickel) and common nonmetals (boron, sulfur, mercury, nitrogen) tested on their characteristic reactivity, oxidation states, and industrial or biological roles.

Organic chemistry contributes the single largest share of the canon: functional groups (esters, ethers, epoxides, amines), structural concepts (aromaticity, chirality, hyperconjugation, conformational analysis), and a long tail of named reactions and reagents (Diels-Alder, Wittig, Grignard, aldol condensation, Wittig, Suzuki, and dozens of others tied to specific chemists) recur across question sets. Physical chemistry supplies the thermodynamic and kinetic framework — Gibbs free energy, entropy, equilibrium constants, activation energy, and rate laws — while a closely related cluster of quantum chemistry and spectroscopy questions covers molecular orbital theory, VSEPR geometry, and the spectroscopic methods (NMR, IR, mass spec, X-ray crystallography) used to characterize compounds.

Inorganic and organometallic chemistry is anchored by coordination complexes, ligand field theory, and catalytic cycles involving palladium, platinum, and other transition metals, often overlapping with industrial processes like the Haber-Bosch and Contact processes. Analytical techniques (titration, distillation, extraction, chromatography) and materials science (polymers, graphene, fullerenes, crystal structures) each form smaller but consistent clusters, as does biochemistry, represented by amino acids, proteins, enzymes, and sugars. A long tail of eponymous chemists, named equations, and lower-frequency common-link answers — including numeric and color answerlines — rounds out the remainder of the canon.
