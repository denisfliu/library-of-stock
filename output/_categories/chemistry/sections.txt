# Physical Chemistry: Thermodynamics & Kinetics
> Energy, equilibrium, and rate concepts governing whether and how fast reactions proceed.
adsorption | Adhesion of molecules to a surface, described by Langmuir and BET isotherms; underlies heterogeneous catalysis.
Gibbs free energy | Thermodynamic potential (G = H - TS) predicting reaction spontaneity at constant temperature and pressure.
catalysts | Substances that speed reactions by lowering activation energy without being consumed; homogeneous or heterogeneous.
transition states | Highest-energy configuration along a reaction coordinate between reactants and products, central to transition state theory.
equilibrium constant | Ratio (K) of product to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, related to Gibbs free energy via delta G = -RT ln K.
chemical potential | Partial molar Gibbs free energy of a species, governing direction of mass transfer and phase equilibrium.
diffusion | Net movement of particles from high to low concentration, described by Fick's laws; underlies effusion and mixing.
enthalpy | State function (H = U + PV) representing heat content at constant pressure; sign shows exo- or endothermic reactions.
entropy | Measure of disorder or accessible microstates (S), which increases in spontaneous processes per the second law.
vapor pressure | Pressure of a substance's vapor in equilibrium with its liquid or solid, linked to boiling point via Clausius-Clapeyron.
activation energy | Minimum energy barrier reactants must overcome to reach the transition state, per the Arrhenius equation.
reaction rate | Speed at which reactants convert to products, expressed as a rate law dependent on concentration and rate constant.
Arrhenius equation | Relates rate constant to temperature and activation energy, k = Ae^(-Ea/RT).
surface tension | Cohesive force at a liquid's surface minimizing surface area, responsible for water striders and meniscus formation.
temperature | Measure of average kinetic energy of particles, governing reaction rate, equilibrium position, and phase behavior.
kinetic isotope effect | Change in reaction rate from isotopic substitution, used to probe whether bond-breaking occurs in the rate-determining step.
calorimetry | Measurement of heat absorbed or released in a reaction, using devices like bomb or coffee-cup calorimeters.
fugacity | Effective pressure of a real gas that corrects for non-ideal behavior in thermodynamic equations.
melting point | Temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid, used to assess purity of organic compounds.
pressure | Force per unit area exerted by gas molecules, related to volume and temperature via the ideal gas law.
reaction rate constant | Proportionality factor (k) in a rate law linking reaction rate to reactant concentrations, temperature-dependent.
transition state | Highest-energy configuration along a reaction coordinate between reactants and products, central to transition state theory.
azeotropes | Liquid mixtures that boil at constant composition, unable to be separated by simple fractional distillation.
concentration | Amount of solute per unit volume or amount of solution, a key variable in rate laws and equilibrium expressions.
activity coefficient | Factor correcting concentration for non-ideal behavior in solution, relating activity to molar concentration.
activity coefficients | Factors correcting concentration for non-ideal behavior in solution, relating activity to molar concentration.
critical point | Temperature and pressure above which distinct liquid and gas phases no longer exist, forming a supercritical fluid.
phase diagrams | Graphs of temperature versus pressure showing regions of solid, liquid, and gas phases and their boundaries.
Raoult's Law | States that a solvent's vapor pressure in a solution equals its mole fraction times its pure vapor pressure.
solubility | Maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a solvent at equilibrium, governed by like-dissolves-like polarity rules.
heat capacity | Amount of heat needed to raise a substance's temperature by one degree, measured at constant volume or pressure.
mixing | Combination of substances accompanied by an entropy increase, described thermodynamically by the Gibbs free energy of mixing.
partition coefficient | Ratio of a solute's concentration between two immiscible phases at equilibrium, quantifying relative solubility.
femtochemistry | Study of chemical reactions on femtosecond timescales using ultrafast laser spectroscopy to observe transition states directly.
lattice energy | Energy released when gaseous ions combine to form an ionic solid, related to ion charge and radius.
osmotic pressure | Pressure needed to prevent solvent flow across a semipermeable membrane, a colligative property proportional to solute concentration.
van der Waals equation | Modified ideal gas law correcting for molecular volume and intermolecular attraction between real gas particles.
internal energy | State function (U) representing total kinetic and potential energy of a system's particles, changed by heat and work.
nucleation | Initial formation of a new phase, such as a crystal or bubble, from a supersaturated or supercooled system.
pre-exponential factor | Frequency term (A) in the Arrhenius equation representing collision frequency and orientation probability.
rate constant | Proportionality factor (k) in a rate law linking reaction rate to reactant concentrations, temperature-dependent.
reaction quotient | Ratio (Q) of product to reactant concentrations at any point in a reaction, compared to K to predict direction.
sublimation | Direct phase transition from solid to gas without passing through the liquid state, as with dry ice.
volume | Space occupied by a substance, related to pressure and temperature via the ideal gas law.
deposition | Direct phase transition from gas to solid without passing through the liquid state, the reverse of sublimation.
Gibbs phase rule | Formula (F = C - P + 2) relating degrees of freedom to the number of components and phases at equilibrium.
Henry's Law | States that a gas's solubility in a liquid is proportional to its partial pressure above the liquid.
Le Chatelier's principle | States that a system at equilibrium shifts to counteract an imposed change in concentration, pressure, or temperature.
Lennard-Jones potential | Mathematical model of intermolecular potential energy combining short-range repulsion and long-range van der Waals attraction.
reaction order | Exponent on a reactant's concentration in a rate law, determined experimentally rather than from stoichiometry.
surface area | Extent of exposed surface, which increases reaction rate in heterogeneous catalysis and adsorption.

boiling point | Temperature at which a liquid's vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure, causing it to vaporize throughout the liquid.
Clausius-Clapeyron equation | Relates a substance's vapor pressure to temperature via its enthalpy of vaporization, describing the liquid-gas phase boundary.
compressibility factor | Ratio (Z) quantifying deviation of a real gas from ideal gas behavior, equal to one for an ideal gas.
eutectic point | Composition and temperature at which a mixture has the lowest possible melting point among all its compositions.
evaporation | Phase transition of surface liquid molecules to vapor below the boiling point, driven by molecular kinetic energy.

chemical reactions | Processes converting reactants into products via bond breaking and forming, governed by thermodynamics and kinetics.

calorimeter | Device measuring heat absorbed or released in a reaction, such as bomb or coffee-cup designs.

# Organic Chemistry: Structure & Stereochemistry
> Functional groups, conformations, and stereochemical concepts that define organic molecules.
chirality | Property of molecules lacking a mirror-image superimposability; chiral centers give enantiomers that rotate polarized light oppositely.
aromaticity | Extra stability of planar, fully conjugated rings obeying Huckel's 4n+2 electron rule, exemplified by benzene.
benzene | Six-membered aromatic hydrocarbon ring (C6H6) with delocalized pi electrons; parent compound of aromatic chemistry.
alkenes | Hydrocarbons with carbon-carbon double bonds, undergoing addition reactions like hydrogenation, halogenation, and hydration.
alkynes | Hydrocarbons with carbon-carbon triple bonds, linear at sp-hybridized carbons, with acidic terminal C-H bonds.
ethers | Functional group with oxygen bonded to two carbons (R-O-R'), including cyclic crown ethers and diethyl ether solvent.
esters | Functional group from acid-alcohol condensation (RCOOR'), often fragrant, hydrolyzed back to carboxylic acid and alcohol.
solvents | Liquids dissolving reactants, classed as polar protic, polar aprotic, or nonpolar, strongly influencing reaction mechanism and rate.
cyclohexane | Six-membered saturated ring adopting chair, boat, and twist-boat conformations to minimize steric and torsional strain.
acetic acid | Simple carboxylic acid (CH3COOH), the sour component of vinegar; a weak acid forming acetate esters.
epoxides | Strained three-membered cyclic ethers highly reactive to nucleophilic ring-opening, formed by alkene epoxidation.
Acetone | Simplest ketone (CH3COCH3), a common polar aprotic solvent and a product of fat metabolism.
hyperconjugation | Stabilizing overlap of a sigma bond with an adjacent empty or antibonding orbital, stabilizing carbocations and alkenes.
carbonyl | Functional group with a carbon-oxygen double bond (C=O), the core of aldehydes, ketones, esters, and amides.
ethanol | Simple alcohol (C2H5OH) produced by fermentation, used as a solvent, fuel additive, and disinfectant.
resonance | Delocalization of electrons across multiple Lewis structures, stabilizing systems like benzene and carboxylate ions.
amines | Nitrogen-containing functional group derived from ammonia, acting as weak bases and nucleophiles.
