# Latin American Literature
> The Latin American Boom and its magical-realist inheritors dominate this unit, anchored by Borges, Neruda, and García Márquez.

# African Literature
> Nigerian and South African writers lead a broad postcolonial African tradition spanning oral epic to apartheid-era fiction.

# Middle Eastern & Persian Literature
> Arabic, Persian, and Turkish traditions cluster around Mahfouz's Cairo, Sufi poets like Rumi, and Pamuk's Istanbul.

# South & Southeast Asian Literature
> Indian writers in English and Bengali, alongside classical Sanskrit drama and Filipino and Indonesian fiction, run from Kalidasa to Rushdie.

# East Asian Literature
> Chinese, Japanese, and Korean traditions range from classical Tang poetry and the Four Great Classical Novels to Kawabata and Murakami.

# Caribbean Literature
> Anglophone and Francophone island writers -- Walcott, Naipaul, Cesaire -- explore colonialism, creolization, and diaspora.

# Commonwealth Literature: Australia, Canada & New Zealand
> A smaller but distinct Anglophone Commonwealth tradition, from Mansfield and White to Atwood, Munro, and Peter Carey.

# Classical Antiquity
> Greek tragedy and comedy, Roman poetry, and ancient Near Eastern epic supply a comparative layer beneath the modern unit.

# Continental European Literature
> Kafka, Tolstoy, Goethe, Camus, and other non-English-language European writers recur as a comparative layer within the unit.

# Movements, Motifs & Common Links
> Recurring objects, themes, and literary movements -- magical realism, Negritude, the Boom, common-link animals and colors -- tie works together across regions.
