# Colonial and Early National Literature
> Colonial Puritan verse and early national prose forge an American voice, from Bradstreet's devotional poetry through Irving's and Cooper's frontier tales.
Anne Bradstreet | Puritan poet, the first published poet of the American colonies; questions cite The Tenth Muse Lately Sprung Up in America, "To My Dear and Loving Husband," and "Upon the Burning of Our House."
- Simon Bradstreet = Anne Bradstreet's husband | Her husband, addressed in "To My Dear and Loving Husband" and "A Letter to Her Husband, Absent upon Publick Employment"; subject of a John Berryman homage.
Edward Taylor | Puritan minister-poet unpublished until the 20th century; "Huswifery" ("Make me, O Lord, thy spinning wheel complete") and his Preparatory Meditations exemplify his knotty "American Baroque" style.
Philip Freneau | "Poet of the American Revolution"; wrote "The Wild Honey Suckle," the captivity account The British Prison-Ship, and the graveyard meditation "The Indian Burying Ground."
- "The Indian Burying Ground" | Poem on a Native burial site where the dead sit upright; "Reason's self shall bow the knee / To shadows and delusions" recurs in clues.
Joel Barlow | Early national poet-diplomat; wrote the mock-epic "The Hasty Pudding" praising corn and the more ambitious epic The Columbiad on Christopher Columbus.
Mercy Otis Warren | Revolutionary-era playwright and historian; satirized Massachusetts governor Thomas Hutchinson as "Rapatio" in The Adulateur, and wrote an early History of the American Revolution.
Benjamin Franklin | Wrote under pseudonyms like "Silence Dogood" and "Poor Richard"; Poor Richard's Almanack and his posthumously published Autobiography are his most-clued works.
Charles Brockden Brown | Early American Gothic novelist; in Wieland the biloquist Carwin dupes siblings Clara and Theodore, while Edgar Huntly and the yellow-fever novel Arthur Mervyn are also clued.
Gothic fiction | Common-link genre spanning Brockden Brown's Wieland, its European models Walpole and Radcliffe, and later American horror writers like Poe.
Washington Irving | Early national sketch writer behind "Rip Van Winkle" and "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow" from The Sketch Book; also wrote A History of New York as Diedrich Knickerbocker.
- "Rip Van Winkle" | Henpecked villager who drinks with Hendrick Hudson's ghostly crew and sleeps twenty years, waking after the American Revolution.
- "The Devil and Tom Walker" | Miser Tom Walker trades his soul to "Old Scratch" for Captain Kidd's buried treasure and becomes a ruthless moneylender.
James Fenimore Cooper | Author of the Leatherstocking Tales starring Natty Bumppo — The Last of the Mohicans, The Deerslayer, The Pioneers; Twain's essay on his "literary offenses" is a stock clue.
- The Last of the Mohicans | Second Leatherstocking Tale; Magua's massacre outside Fort William Henry and the clifftop deaths of Uncas and Cora close the novel.
- Natty Bumppo | Cooper's frontiersman hero, called Hawkeye, Deerslayer, or the trapper across the five Leatherstocking Tales; travels with Mohican companion Chingachgook.
- The Deerslayer | Earliest-set Leatherstocking Tale; young Bumppo earns his nickname at Glimmerglass Lake alongside Hurry Harry and the Hutter sisters, Hetty and Judith.
Susanna Rowson | First bestselling American novelist; her sentimental Charlotte Temple, about a schoolgirl seduced by John Montraville, was often called the country's first novel.
- Charlotte Temple | Title schoolgirl seduced by soldier John Montraville and abandoned in New York, dying after giving birth to her daughter Lucy.

# Transcendentalism and the American Renaissance
> Poe, Hawthorne, and Melville plumb sin and obsession in the Dark Romantic mode, while Emerson, Thoreau, and Margaret Fuller's Transcendentalist circle prizes self-reliant intuition.
Transcendentalism | Idealist literary movement centered on Emerson, Thoreau, and Fuller's Dial circle and the utopian Brook Farm community; stressed intuition and the individual soul over doctrine.
Ralph Waldo Emerson | Central Transcendentalist essayist; "Self-Reliance," Nature with its "transparent eyeball," "The American Scholar," and the Divinity School Address dominate clues.
- "Self-Reliance" | Emerson's essay urging nonconformity and self-trust ("Trust thyself: every heart vibrates to that iron string"); with Nature, his best-known work.
Henry David Thoreau | Transcendentalist who lived at Walden Pond; Walden and "Civil Disobedience" provide nearly all clues, often alongside his night in jail and Emerson's patronage.
- Walden | Thoreau's memoir of his two years, two months, and two days at Walden Pond, including the chapters "Economy" and "Brute Neighbors"; champions living "deliberately."
- Walden Pond | Concord pond, owned by Emerson, where Thoreau built his cabin and watched the "Battle of the Ants."
- "Civil Disobedience" | Essay defending refusal to pay taxes that support slavery and the Mexican War, inspired by Thoreau's night in jail.
Margaret Fuller | Transcendentalist first editor of The Dial; wrote the feminist Woman in the Nineteenth Century (expanded from "The Great Lawsuit") and the travelogue Summer on the Lakes.
Louisa May Alcott | Author of Little Women and its sequels; questions cite the four March sisters, Hospital Sketches, and the sensation fiction she published as A. M. Barnard.
- Little Women | Novel of the four March sisters' Civil War-era coming of age; Jo rejects Laurie's proposal and marries Professor Bhaer instead.
- Josephine March | Tomboyish second March sister who sells her hair for money, burns Amy's manuscript, and later runs the Plumfield school for boys.
- March family | Common-link family of sisters Meg, Jo, Beth, and Amy in Little Women; Beth dies of scarlet fever after delivering charity to the Hummels.
Edgar Allan Poe = Edgar Allen Poe | Master of Gothic tales and musical verse: "The Raven," "The Tell-Tale Heart," "The Fall of the House of Usher," and "The Cask of Amontillado," plus detective Auguste Dupin.
- "Ligeia" | Opium-addled narrator's dead first wife Ligeia returns from beyond, reincarnated in his second wife Rowena's body; recites the poem "The Conqueror Worm."
- "The Conqueror Worm" | Poem embedded in "Ligeia" describing angels who watch a "blood-red thing" devour actors in a theater; ends, "the hero [is] the Conqueror Worm."
- the house of Usher | Ancestral mansion of twins Roderick and Madeline in "The Fall of the House of Usher"; collapses into a tarn after Madeline's premature burial.
- "The Cask of Amontillado" | Montresor lures the boastful Fortunato into the catacombs with promised wine and walls him up alive in revenge.
- "The Pit and the Pendulum" | Inquisition prisoner narrowly escapes a swinging blade and closing walls before General Lasalle's army frees him from Toledo.
- "A Descent into the Maelstrom" | Fisherman narrator's hair turns white surviving a Norwegian whirlpool by lashing himself to a cask, unlike his drowned brother.
- "The Imp of the Perverse" | Essay-turned-story on the perverse urge to self-destruct; its narrator murders with a poisoned candle, then confesses despite getting away with it.
- "The Bells" | Onomatopoeic poem coining "tintinnabulation," cataloguing silver, golden, brazen, and iron bells across four stanzas of mounting terror.
- "The Raven" | Grieving narrator questions a talking raven perched on a bust of Pallas about his lost love Lenore; it answers only "Nevermore."
- C. Auguste Dupin | Poe's "ratiocination"-practicing detective of "The Murders in the Rue Morgue," "The Purloined Letter," and "The Mystery of Marie Rogêt."
- The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym of Nantucket | Poe's sole completed novel, ending abruptly as a shrouded white giant appears near the South Pole; Toni Morrison's Playing in the Dark reads its racial imagery.
- Arthur Gordon Pym | Poe's only novel's title character, a Nantucket stowaway; survives mutiny and cannibalism before the eerie white figure appears near the South Pole.
Nathaniel Hawthorne | Dark romancer of Puritan New England: The Scarlet Letter, The House of the Seven Gables, and tales like "Young Goodman Brown," "The Minister's Black Veil," and "Rappaccini's Daughter."
- The Scarlet Letter | Hawthorne's novel of Hester Prynne, Pearl, Dimmesdale, and Chillingworth in Puritan Boston; the three scaffold scenes and "The Custom-House" introduction recur in clues.
- Hester Prynne | Adulteress who embroiders the scarlet "A," raises daughter Pearl, and shields former lover Dimmesdale until his scaffold confession and death.
- "The Custom House" | Autobiographical preface to The Scarlet Letter; narrator finds Surveyor Jonathan Pue's manuscript and a scarlet "A" while working at the Salem customhouse.
- The House of the Seven Gables | Novel of the cursed Pyncheon family mansion, the aged Hepzibah, and her cousin Judge Pyncheon's mysterious death in his ancestral chair.
- "Rappaccini's Daughter" | Padua tale of Beatrice, poisoned by her father Dr. Rappaccini's toxic garden, who dies drinking a rival scientist's antidote.
- "Young Goodman Brown" | Puritan Goodman Brown leaves wife Faith for a satanic forest ritual, leaving him unable to trust his neighbors' piety afterward.
- "The Minister's Black Veil" | Reverend Hooper dons an unexplained black veil for life, unsettling his congregation and fiancée Elizabeth until his deathbed.
- "The Birth-Mark" | Scientist Aylmer, aided by servant Aminadab, kills his wife Georgiana trying to remove her "Crimson Hand" birthmark with an elixir.
- "The May-Pole of Merry Mount" | Puritan leader John Endicott cuts down the title Maypole and breaks up the wedding revels of Edgar and Edith.
- "Dr. Heidegger's Experiment" = Dr. Heidegger | Doctor gives elderly friends Fountain-of-Youth water, reviving a withered rose and their youth before both fade again; owns a "Forbear!"-uttering Hippocrates bust.
Herman Melville | Author of Moby-Dick, Billy Budd, "Bartleby, the Scrivener," and "Benito Cereno"; clues range from the early Polynesian novels Typee and Omoo to The Confidence-Man.
- Moby-Dick | Melville's whaling epic narrated by Ishmael; Ahab, Queequeg, Starbuck, the Pequod, and chapters like "The Whiteness of the Whale" are stock clues.
- Ishmael | Narrator of Moby-Dick ("Call me Ishmael"), a damp-November misanthrope who befriends Queequeg and survives the Pequod's sinking by clinging to a coffin.
- Captain Ahab | Monomaniacal, peg-legged Pequod captain hunting Moby Dick for biting off his leg; dies harpooning the whale, strangled by his own line.
- Queequeg | Tattooed Pacific islander harpooner and prince of Kokovoko who worships the idol Yojo; his coffin later saves Ishmael's life.
- Benito Cereno | Novella in which Amasa Delano boards the San Dominick without realizing captain Cereno has been deposed by Babo's slave revolt.
- Billy Budd = Billy Budd, Sailor | Handsome sailor impressed onto the Bellipotent who fatally strikes master-at-arms John Claggart after a false mutiny accusation, then hangs.
- John Claggart | Bellipotent master-at-arms who falsely accuses Billy Budd of mutiny and is killed by Budd's punch; Melville's narrator cites his "natural depravity."
- "Bartleby, the Scrivener: A Story of Wall Street" | Wall Street copyist who "would prefer not to" work, and eventually starves in the Tombs; the narrator recalls his rumored Dead Letter Office past.
- The Piazza Tales | Melville story collection including "Benito Cereno," "Bartleby, the Scrivener," "The Bell-Tower," and the Galapagos sketches "The Encantadas."

# Nineteenth-Century Poetry
> American poetry's founding century: the moralizing popular verse of Bryant and the Fireside Poets gives way to Whitman's sprawling free verse and Dickinson's compressed, dash-driven lyrics.
William Cullen Bryant | Early Romantic poet and longtime New York Evening Post editor; "Thanatopsis," drafted in his teens, and "To a Waterfowl" are his near-universal clues.
- "Thanatopsis" | Meditation on death urging readers to join the "innumerable caravan" and lie down "to pleasant dreams"; Greek title means a view of death.
Henry Wadsworth Longfellow | The most famous Fireside Poet: The Song of Hiawatha, Evangeline, "Paul Revere's Ride," and "A Psalm of Life" supply most clues.
- Hiawatha | Ojibwe legendary hero of Longfellow's epic, raised by Nokomis on Lake Superior's shore; woos Minnehaha in trochaic tetrameter verse.
- The Song of Hiawatha | Longfellow's epic in unrhymed trochaic tetrameter modeled on the Finnish Kalevala; follows the Ojibwe hero Hiawatha through legend and courtship.
- "A Psalm of Life" | Longfellow's exhortation that "Life is real! Life is earnest!," urging readers to leave "footprints on the sands of time."
- "The Village Blacksmith" | Longfellow poem of a smith who "looks the whole world in the face, for he owes not any man," working his forge beneath a chestnut tree.
- Evangeline, A Tale of Acadie | Longfellow's narrative poem of Acadian lovers separated by the British expulsion; Evangeline searches for Gabriel for decades.
- Tales of a Wayside Inn | Longfellow's frame narrative of travelers swapping stories, including the collection's most famous tale, "Paul Revere's Ride."
John Greenleaf Whittier | Quaker abolitionist Fireside Poet; the winter idyll Snow-Bound anchors nearly every question, with "Barbara Frietchie" and "Maud Muller" as supporting clues.
James Russell Lowell | Fireside poet and first editor of the Atlantic Monthly; A Fable for Critics satirizes his contemporaries, and The Biglow Papers use Yankee dialect.
Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr. = Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr. | Fireside poet and Boston physician who coined "anesthesia" and "Boston Brahmin"; "Old Ironsides," "The Chambered Nautilus," The Autocrat of the Breakfast-Table.
- "The Chambered Nautilus" | Meditation on a "ship of pearl" mollusk shell, urging the soul to "build thee more stately mansions" as the creature's spiral grows.
Walt Whitman | Free-verse poet of Leaves of Grass; "Song of Myself," the Lincoln elegies "O Captain! My Captain!" and "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd," and "Crossing Brooklyn Ferry" recur.
- Leaves of Grass | Whitman's lifelong, endlessly revised collection, first published 1855 with a preface calling America "essentially the greatest poem"; contains "Song of Myself."
- "Song of Myself" | Fifty-two-section centerpiece of Leaves of Grass; "I celebrate myself, and sing myself," the child asking "What is the grass?," and the "barbaric yawp."
- "I Sing the Body Electric" | Catalogues the human body, including a slave-auction scene; Ray Bradbury reused the title for a story and collection.
- "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd" | Lincoln elegy in which a hermit thrush sings "Come lovely and soothing death" as the speaker mourns "with ever-returning spring."
- "Crossing Brooklyn Ferry" | Speaker addresses future ferry riders, "Flood-tide below me!," insisting "distance avails not" across "scores or hundreds of years."
- "Out of the Cradle Endlessly Rocking" | Boy on Paumanok's shore hears a mockingbird mourn its lost mate, learning the sea's "low and delicious word death."
- grass | Whitman's central image, "the beautiful uncut hair of graves" in "Song of Myself"; also titles a Carl Sandburg poem set at Austerlitz and Waterloo.
- "I Hear America Singing" | Catalogues workers' "varied carols" — mechanics, carpenters, boatmen, shoemakers — each singing what belongs to them alone.
- "When I Heard the Learn'd Astronomer" | Speaker flees a lecture's "proofs, the figures" for the "mystical moist night-air" to look "in perfect silence at the stars."
- lilacs | Purple flowers of the Lincoln elegy's title, a sprig broken for the passing coffin; also title Amy Lowell's New England poem.
- Paumanok | Whitman's Algonquian name for his birthplace, Long Island; setting of "Out of the Cradle Endlessly Rocking" and "Starting from Paumanok."
the Soul | Common-link answerline: Whitman "loafes and invites" it in "Song of Myself," and Dickinson's poems personify it "shutting the Door" and selecting "her own Society."
Emily Dickinson | The most-asked answer in American literature: the reclusive Amherst poet of "Because I could not stop for Death," "I heard a Fly buzz," and "Hope is the thing with feathers," identified by dashes and slant rhyme.
- "Because I could not stop for Death" | Death personified as a courteous carriage driver who takes the speaker past a school, fields of grain, and the setting sun toward eternity.
- The Master | Mysterious addressee of three passionate, unsent Dickinson letters signed "Daisy"; scholars have proposed Charles Wadsworth, Samuel Bowles, Otis Lord, or God.
- "I taste a liquor never brewed" | Speaker as an "Inebriate of air" and "Debauchee of Dew," drunk on nature; ends with "Seraphs swing their snowy Hats" at her leaning against the sun.
- Fame | Common-link concept in several Dickinson poems: "a bee" with "a song," "a sting," "a wing"; also called "a fickle food" that "does not stay."
Emma Lazarus | Jewish American poet of "The New Colossus," inscribed on the Statue of Liberty's pedestal ("give me your tired, your poor"); also wrote Songs of a Semite.
James Whitcomb Riley | "Hoosier poet" from Indiana known for dialect verse; "Little Orphant Annie" and "The Raggedy Man," and championed fellow dialect poet Paul Laurence Dunbar.

# Realism, Regionalism, and Naturalism
> The post-Civil War turn from Romantic idealism to hard-eyed observation: Howells and James's psychological realism, regional local color, and naturalism's deterministic pessimism through World War I.
realism | Post-Civil War movement toward objective, everyday subject matter and plain style, reacting against Romantic idealization; Howells and James are its leading theorists.
William Dean Howells | The "Dean of American Letters," realist novelist and Atlantic Monthly editor; The Rise of Silas Lapham and A Hazard of New Fortunes are the core clues.
- Silas Lapham | Self-made paint magnate whose social climbing and a shady land deal test his morals in Howells's The Rise of Silas Lapham.
- A Hazard of New Fortunes | Basil March starts a New York magazine amid the class tensions of an 1890s streetcar strike that turns violent.
Henry James | Expatriate master of the psychological novel: The Portrait of a Lady, The Turn of the Screw, The Ambassadors, Daisy Miller, and The Wings of the Dove.
- Daisy Miller | Novella of a brash, unchaperoned American girl in Europe whose flirtations scandalize expatriate society; she dies of "Roman fever."
- The Ambassadors | Lambert Strether travels to Paris to retrieve Chad Newsome for his fiancée, but falls under the city's spell himself.
- The Turn of The Screw -> The Turn of the Screw | Ghost story of a governess convinced her charges Miles and Flora are possessed by the dead servants Quint and Jessel.
- "The Beast in the Jungle" | Novella of John Marcher, who waits his life for a catastrophic fate and realizes too late it was letting May Bartram's love pass him by.
- The Bostonians | Feminist reformer Olive Chancellor and her cousin Basil Ransom vie for the affections of the young orator Verena Tarrant.
- The Portrait of a Lady | Isabel Archer inherits a fortune and is manipulated by Madame Merle into marrying the cold aesthete Gilbert Osmond.
- Washington Square | Plain, wealthy Catherine Sloper is courted by the fortune-hunting Morris Townsend against her father's wishes.
- The Wings of the Dove | Dying heiress Milly Theale is courted by Merton Densher, who is secretly engaged to the scheming Kate Croy.
Mark Twain | Samuel Clemens, humorist of the Mississippi: Adventures of Huckleberry Finn, Tom Sawyer, "The Celebrated Jumping Frog of Calaveras County," A Connecticut Yankee, and Pudd'nhead Wilson.
- Jim | Enslaved man who escapes down the Mississippi with Huck Finn; Percival Everett's 2024 novel James retells the story from his viewpoint.
- The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn | Twain's novel of Huck and Jim rafting down the Mississippi; the Duke and the Dauphin, the Grangerford feud, and "All right, then, I'll go to hell" recur as clues.
- Huckleberry Finn | Huck himself, the novel's narrator, first introduced as Tom Sawyer's friend in The Adventures of Tom Sawyer.
- Tom Sawyer | Huck's mischievous friend who engineers Jim's overly elaborate "rescue" at novel's end; gets lost in a cave with Becky Thatcher in his own book.
- Roughing It | Twain's semi-autobiographical account of his years prospecting and reporting across the Nevada silver-mining West.
- "The Celebrated Jumping Frog of Calaveras County" | Twain's breakout tall tale of Jim Smiley's trained frog Dan'l Webster, secretly weighted down with buckshot by a rival.
- "The Man That Corrupted Hadleyburg" | A stranger's sack of "gold" coins exposes the hypocrisy of the town's nineteen supposedly incorruptible leading citizens.
- Hadleyburg | The self-righteous town whose leading citizens are exposed as frauds by a stranger's revenge scheme in Twain's "The Man That Corrupted Hadleyburg."
- A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court | Hank Morgan is transported to Camelot, where his modern know-how and gunpowder upend Arthurian society.
- The Innocents Abroad | Twain's satirical travelogue of an American pleasure cruise through Europe and the Holy Land.
Harriet Beecher Stowe | Abolitionist author of Uncle Tom's Cabin, the century's best-selling novel; clues add A Key to Uncle Tom's Cabin, Dred, and Oldtown Folks.
- Uncle Tom's Cabin = Uncle Tom's Cabin; or, Life Among the Lowly | Stowe's 1852 anti-slavery novel: Tom, Little Eva, Eliza crossing the icy Ohio, Topsy, and the brutal overseer Simon Legree are the standard clues.
- Uncle Tom | Enslaved man of unwavering Christian faith whose death at Simon Legree's hands made his name a byword, now pejorative, for servile deference.
Bret Harte | Local colorist of Gold Rush California; "The Luck of Roaring Camp" and "The Outcasts of Poker Flat" provide nearly every clue.
- "The Outcasts of Poker Flat" | Gold Rush outcasts, including the gambler John Oakhurst and the prostitute Duchess, are exiled from camp and die in a mountain snowstorm.
- "The Luck of Roaring Camp" | A mining camp adopts an orphaned baby, "the Luck," whose death in a flood devastates the reformed miners.
Sarah Orne Jewett | Maine regionalist of The Country of the Pointed Firs and "A White Heron," in which young Sylvia refuses to reveal the heron's nest to a visiting ornithologist.
- The Country of the Pointed Firs | Loosely linked sketches of a summer visitor's stay in the Maine fishing village of Dunnet Landing.
- "A White Heron" | Young Sylvia climbs a great pine to spot a rare white heron, then refuses to reveal its nest to a visiting ornithologist.
Joel Chandler Harris | Georgia journalist who recorded plantation folktales as the Uncle Remus dialect stories of the trickster Br'er Rabbit outwitting Br'er Fox.
Kate Chopin | Louisiana local colorist; The Awakening plus the stories "The Story of an Hour" and "Désirée's Baby" make her a fixture at every difficulty.
- The Awakening | Chopin's 1899 novel of Edna Pontellier's sexual and artistic awakening at Grand Isle, ending as she swims out into the Gulf; Robert Lebrun and Mademoiselle Reisz appear in clues.
- "Desiree's Baby" -> "Désirée's Baby" | Armand rejects his wife Désirée over their baby's complexion, then discovers after her disappearance that his own mother was Black.
- "The Story of An Hour" -> "The Story of an Hour" | Louise Mallard secretly rejoices at news of her husband's death, then dies of shock when he turns out alive.
- Desiree -> Désirée | Adopted foundling wife of Armand Aubigny, cast out over her baby's race in Chopin's "Désirée's Baby."
- Edna Pontellier | Protagonist of The Awakening who leaves her family in pursuit of artistic and sexual independence, ending in her swim into the Gulf.
Charlotte Perkins Gilman | Feminist writer of "The Yellow Wallpaper" and the all-female utopia Herland; her treatise Women and Economics also surfaces in Social Science questions.
- "The Yellow Wallpaper" = The Yellow Wallpaper | Woman confined for a "rest cure" comes to see a creeping woman trapped behind the wallpaper; protests S. Weir Mitchell's treatment of nervous disorders.
O. Henry | Pen name of William Sydney Porter, the twist-ending story writer: "The Gift of the Magi," "The Ransom of Red Chief," and "The Cop and the Anthem"; he began writing in prison.
- "The Ransom of Red Chief" | Two kidnappers abduct a small-town boy so unruly that they end up paying his father to take him back.
- "The Gift Of The Magi" -> "The Gift of the Magi" | Della sells her hair to buy Jim a watch chain, while he sells his watch to buy her hair combs.
Ambrose Bierce | Sardonic Civil War veteran: "An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge," "Chickamauga," and The Devil's Dictionary; his 1913 disappearance into revolutionary Mexico is a favorite clue.
- "An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge" | Confederate saboteur Peyton Farquhar imagines a miraculous escape while being hanged from the title bridge; the escape is his dying hallucination.
- "Chickamauga" | A deaf-mute boy plays soldier in the woods, then wanders a corpse-strewn battlefield to find his own home burned and mother dead.
- The Old Gringo | Carlos Fuentes's novel fictionalizing Bierce's own disappearance in the Mexican Revolution, following an old American drawn into Pancho Villa's forces.
Stephen Crane | Naturalist who died at twenty-eight: The Red Badge of Courage, Maggie: A Girl of the Streets, "The Open Boat," and the poetry collection War Is Kind.
- The Red Badge of Courage | Crane's Civil War novel of Henry Fleming, who flees his first battle and envies the wounded their "red badge"; the tall soldier Jim Conklin's death is a stock clue.
- "The Open Boat" | Four men, based on Crane's own shipwreck, struggle in a small dinghy through rough seas; only the oiler drowns just short of shore.
- The Black Riders and Other Lines | Crane's spare, unrhymed free-verse debut poetry collection, including a poem about a man eating his own heart in the desert.
- "The Bride Comes to Yellow Sky" | Marshal Jack Potter brings home a new bride just as gunman Scratchy Wilson comes looking for a showdown, which the marriage defuses.
- Maggie Johnson | Slum girl driven to prostitution and death after her seduction by Pete in Crane's Maggie: A Girl of the Streets.
- Maggie: A Girl of the Streets | Naturalist novella of a Bowery girl's ruin after abandonment by her lover Pete; self-published by Crane under a pseudonym.
Frank Norris | Naturalist of McTeague and the unfinished "Epic of Wheat" trilogy (The Octopus, The Pit), depicting deterministic economic forces crushing individuals.
- McTeague | Dentist McTeague murders his miserly wife Trina for her lottery winnings and dies handcuffed to his rival Marcus in Death Valley.
Jack London | Klondike naturalist: The Call of the Wild's dog Buck, White Fang, "To Build a Fire," and The Sea-Wolf's captain Wolf Larsen dominate questions.
- "To Build a Fire" | An unnamed man ignores warnings and freezes to death in the Yukon cold after failing to relight his fire, accompanied only by his wolf-dog.
- The Call of the Wild | Domesticated dog Buck is stolen and sold into the Klondike, gradually reverting to his wild wolf ancestry.
- The Sea-Wolf | Humphrey van Weyden is shipwrecked aboard the seal-hunting schooner Ghost, ruled by the brutal, Nietzschean Captain Wolf Larsen.
- The Iron Heel | Dystopian novel narrated through Avis Everhard's rediscovered memoir of a fascist oligarchy crushing an American socialist revolution.
Theodore Dreiser = Theodore Herman Albert Dreiser | Naturalist of Sister Carrie, tracing Carrie Meeber's rise and Hurstwood's ruin, and An American Tragedy, in which Clyde Griffiths lets Roberta Alden drown.
- Sister Carrie | Carrie Meeber rises from rural poverty to stage stardom in Chicago and New York while her lover Hurstwood steals money and sinks into ruin.
- An American Tragedy | Clyde Griffiths lets pregnant, working-class Roberta Alden drown rather than lose his chance at a wealthy fiancée; based on a real murder case.
- Jennie Gerhardt | Poor Ohio girl bears an illegitimate daughter and endures years as a rich man's mistress in Dreiser's second novel.
Upton Sinclair | Muckraking naturalist best known for The Jungle's exposé of the meatpacking industry; also wrote the oil-industry novel Oil! and the polemic The Brass Check.
- The Jungle | Lithuanian immigrant Jurgis Rudkus endures Chicago meatpacking-plant horrors before finding hope in socialism; helped spur the Pure Food and Drug Act.
- Oil! | Bunny Ross navigates his oil-baron father's business and his own radical politics in Sinclair's novel, loosely adapted into There Will Be Blood.
Edith Wharton | Novelist of Old New York society: The House of Mirth, The Age of Innocence with Newland Archer and Ellen Olenska, and the bleak New England novella Ethan Frome.
- The House of Mirth | Wharton's novel of Lily Bart's social fall in New York, ending with her chloral overdose; Lawrence Selden and the tableaux vivants scene are common clues.
- The Age of Innocence | Newland Archer, engaged to conventional May Welland, falls for her scandalous cousin Countess Ellen Olenska in 1870s New York.
- Ethan Frome | Title farmer's affair with his wife Zeena's cousin Mattie Silver ends in a failed suicide-pact sled crash that leaves both maimed.
- Countess Ellen Olenska | Scandalous, separated cousin who draws Newland Archer's forbidden love away from his fiancée May in The Age of Innocence.
- Lily Bart | Beautiful but penniless socialite whose gambling debts and dashed marriage prospects doom her to a fatal overdose in The House of Mirth.
- The Custom of the Country | Ruthlessly ambitious Undine Spragg claws upward through a string of marriages in Wharton's satire of American social climbing.
- "Roman Fever" | Two old friends reminisce on a Roman terrace, revealing a decades-old rivalry and a secret about a daughter's true parentage.
Willa Cather = Willa Sibert Cather | Novelist of the Nebraska prairie and the Southwest: My Ántonia, O Pioneers!, The Song of the Lark, and Death Comes for the Archbishop.
- O Pioneers! | First of Cather's prairie trilogy: Alexandra Bergson builds up the Nebraska farm, while her brother Emil and Marie Shabata are shot under a mulberry tree by Marie's husband Frank.
- The Song of the Lark | Thea Kronborg, a small-town Colorado girl, develops her voice and leaves for Chicago to become an opera singer.
- Death Comes for the Archbishop | Episodic novel of Bishop Jean Marie Latour establishing a Catholic diocese in the New Mexico desert.
- My Antonia -> My Ántonia | Jim Burden narrates his boyhood friendship with the resilient Bohemian immigrant girl Ántonia Shimerda on the Nebraska prairie.
- The Professor's House | Godfrey St. Peter grows detached from his family, dwelling instead on student Tom Outland's discovery of Blue Mesa cliff dwellings.
Henry Adams | Historian and memoirist, grandson and great-grandson of presidents; The Education of Henry Adams and Mont-Saint-Michel and Chartres are his major prose works.

Sophie's Choice | William Styron's Brooklyn-set novel narrated by Stingo; Auschwitz survivor Sophie Zawistowski's fatal wartime choice and her volatile lover Nathan Landau.
Nat Turner | Leader of an 1831 Virginia slave revolt whose "confessions" to lawyer Thomas Gray inspired William Styron's controversial novel The Confessions of Nat Turner.

# African American Literature: Wheatley to the Harlem Renaissance
> African American letters from Phillis Wheatley's colonial-era poetry through slavery-era narratives to the 1920s Harlem Renaissance's flowering of poetry and fiction.
Jupiter Hammon | Enslaved poet from Long Island whose 1760 "An Evening Thought" was the first poem published by an African American; also wrote "An Address to Miss Phillis Wheatley."
Phillis Wheatley = Phyllis Wheatley | African-born enslaved poet of revolutionary Boston, first African American to publish a poetry book; wrote "On Being Brought from Africa to America."
Frederick Douglass | Escaped slave, orator, and editor of The North Star; his 1845 Narrative, the fight with slave-breaker Covey, and "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" are the clues.
slave narratives | Common-link genre spanning Douglass, Harriet Jacobs's Incidents, Olaudah Equiano, and Solomon Northup, often narrated or published under a pseudonym while still enslaved.
Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl | Harriet Jacobs's narrative, published as "Linda Brent," of seven years hiding in her grandmother's garret to escape the predatory Dr. Flint.
W. E. B. Du Bois | Sociologist-activist and NAACP co-founder; The Souls of Black Folk introduced "double consciousness" and "the Veil," and his fiction included the story "The Comet."
- The Souls of Black Folk | Du Bois's 1903 essay collection on "double consciousness," "the color line," and the "sorrow songs," including the story "Of the Coming of John."
Charles Chesnutt = Charles W. Chesnutt = Charles Waddell Chesnutt | Author of The Conjure Woman and The House Behind the Cedars, dramatizing Reconstruction-era racial passing and folk conjure tales.
- The House Behind the Cedars | Novel of Rena Walden, who passes as white to become George Tryon's "queen of love and beauty" until he learns her race and breaks their engagement.
Paul Laurence Dunbar | First nationally famous Black American poet; "We Wear the Mask" and "Sympathy" ("I know why the caged bird sings") anchor nearly every question.
- "We Wear the Mask" | Poem whose title object "grins and lies, hides our cheeks and shades our eyes" while masking the speaker's true suffering from the world.
- "Sympathy" | Poem of a caged bird who sings "a prayer... from his heart's deep core"; its refrain gave Maya Angelou her memoir title, I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings.
- Dunbar | Married surname shared by the poet and his first wife, Alice Dunbar-Nelson (born Alice Ruth Moore), author of Violets and Other Tales.
James Weldon Johnson | Writer of "Lift Every Voice and Sing," the novel The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man, and the sermon-poems of God's Trombones; also an NAACP leader and diplomat.
- the Ex-Colored Man | Unnamed narrator who passes as white after witnessing a lynching in Georgia, ultimately confessing he sold his "birthright for a mess of pottage."
Harlem Renaissance | The 1920s flowering of Black art centered in upper Manhattan: Hughes, Hurston, McKay, Countee Cullen, and Alain Locke's anthology The New Negro are the standard clues.
Langston Hughes | Poet laureate of the Harlem Renaissance: "The Negro Speaks of Rivers," "Harlem," "I, Too," "The Weary Blues," and the Jesse B. Semple ("Simple") stories.
- "The Weary Blues" | Poem of a pianist "swaying to and fro on his rickety stool," playing a "drowsy syncopated tune" on Lenox Avenue until he sleeps "like a rock."
- "Theme For English B" | Poem addressed to a white instructor; the speaker, the "only colored student" in his class, insists "that's American" of what the two share.
blues | Common-link musical genre clued through Hughes's poetry and Baldwin's story "Sonny's Blues," typically via a "drowsy syncopated tune" and the AAB stanza form.
Zora Neale Hurston | Anthropologist-novelist from Eatonville, Florida: Their Eyes Were Watching God, the folklore collection Mules and Men, and the essay "How It Feels to Be Colored Me."
- Their Eyes Were Watching God | Hurston's novel of Janie Crawford's three marriages; the pear tree, the mule of Eatonville, the hurricane, and the shooting of rabid Tea Cake are the standard clues.
- Sweat | Short story of Delia Jones, whose washing "sweat" pays for her house while husband Sykes dies from a rattlesnake he planted to kill her.
- "The Gilded Six-Bits" | Story set in Eatonville where Joe pays Missie May with a fake gold coin after catching her affair with ice-cream parlor owner Otis Slemmons.
- Eatonville, Florida | Hurston's all-Black hometown, setting of Their Eyes Were Watching God and "The Gilded Six-Bits" and subject of "How It Feels to Be Colored Me."
- Tea Cake | Janie's much-younger third husband in Their Eyes Were Watching God, shot by her in self-defense after a rabid dog bite drives him mad.
Claude McKay | Jamaican-born Harlem Renaissance poet of the defiant sonnet "If We Must Die" and "America" ("this cultured hell that tests my youth"), plus the novel Home to Harlem.
- Jamaica | McKay's island homeland, evoked in his dialect collection Songs of Jamaica and the novel Banana Bottom, set in its title village.
- "If We Must Die" | Sonnet urging "Kinsmen" to face "the murderous, cowardly pack," fighting back rather than dying "like hogs...in an inglorious spot."
Countee Cullen | Harlem Renaissance poet of the collection Color and its sonnet "Yet Do I Marvel"; "Incident" recalls a childhood racial slur suffered on a trip to Baltimore.
- "Yet Do I Marvel" | Sonnet on God's inscrutable ways, from Tantalus to the blind mole, before the "curious thing" of making "a poet black, and bid him sing."
Jean Toomer | Harlem Renaissance author of Cane, mixing poetry, prose, and drama; grandson of Louisiana governor P. B. S. Pinchback, later a disciple of George Gurdjieff.
- Cane | 1923 collection of vignettes, poems, and the closing play "Kabnis," opening with "Karintha" and including "Georgia Dusk" and "Blood-Burning Moon."
Nella Larsen | Harlem Renaissance novelist of Passing and Quicksand, exploring racial ambiguity and light-skinned Black women caught between white and Black worlds.
- passing -> Passing | Novel of Irene Redfield and the racially ambiguous Clare Kendry, who dies falling from a window after her white husband learns she is Black.
- Quicksand | Novel of Helga Crane, half-Danish and half-Black, who drifts from the Tuskegee-like school Naxos through Chicago, Harlem, and Copenhagen before marrying a rural minister.

# Postwar African American Literature
> From Richard Wright's naturalist protest fiction and the essays of Baldwin and Ellison through Toni Morrison's Nobel-winning novels to the poets, critics, and novelists carrying that tradition forward today.
Richard Wright | Naturalist of Black protest fiction: Native Son, the memoir Black Boy, and the story collection Uncle Tom's Children with "Big Boy Leaves Home."
- Native Son | Wright's novel of Bigger Thomas, who accidentally smothers Mary Dalton in Chicago and is defended by Boris Max; its three books are Fear, Flight, and Fate.
- Bigger Thomas | Fearful protagonist who kills Mary Dalton and his girlfriend Bessie Mears in Native Son; defended by lawyer Boris Max, sentenced to death.
- Black Boy | Wright's memoir of poverty and violence in Jim Crow Mississippi and Memphis; its long-suppressed second part, American Hunger, covers Chicago's Communist Party.
Ralph Ellison = Ralph Waldo Ellison | Author of Invisible Man and the essay collection Shadow and Act; the posthumous novel Juneteenth was assembled from decades of drafts.
- Invisible Man | 1952 novel of an unnamed narrator's journey from a Southern "battle royal" through the Brotherhood in Harlem, ending underground in a room lit by 1,369 bulbs.
James Baldwin = James Arthur Baldwin | Essayist-novelist of Go Tell It on the Mountain, Giovanni's Room, and The Fire Next Time; the story "Sonny's Blues" is a stock short-fiction clue.
- Go Tell It on the Mountain | Semi-autobiographical novel of John Grimes's religious awakening in a Harlem church on his fourteenth birthday; his abusive stepfather is preacher Gabriel.
- Giovanni's Room | Novel of the American David's Paris affair with the Italian bartender Giovanni, executed for murder, while his fiancee Hella is away in Spain.
- The Fire Next Time | Two-part essay collection: "My Dungeon Shook," a letter to Baldwin's nephew, and "Down at the Cross," recounting a dinner with Elijah Muhammad.
- "Sonny's Blues" | Story of a Harlem algebra teacher whose heroin-addicted jazz-pianist brother Sonny plays with Creole's band in a Greenwich Village club.
- "Notes of a Native Son" | Title essay of Baldwin's 1955 collection; recalls his father's funeral coinciding with the 1943 Harlem race riot and a jug of water thrown at a waitress.
Gwendolyn Brooks | First Black Pulitzer winner, for Annie Allen; "We Real Cool" ("We / Die soon") and the Chicago world of A Street in Bronzeville dominate clues.
- "We Real Cool" | Eight-line poem on "the pool players, Seven at the Golden Shovel," each line but the last ending in "We"; inspired Terrance Hayes's golden shovel form.
Robert Hayden | First Black poet to hold the Library of Congress poetry consultantship; "Those Winter Sundays" ("love's austere and lonely offices") and the slave-ship poem "Middle Passage" are the clues.
- "Those Winter Sundays" | Poem on a father who rose early to heat the house in "blueblack cold," his "love's austere and lonely offices" going unthanked.
Amiri Baraka | Black Arts Movement founder, formerly LeRoi Jones: the play Dutchman, in which Lula murders Clay on a subway, and the controversial poem "Somebody Blew Up America."
Ishmael Reed | Black Arts-adjacent postmodern satirist; coined "Neo-HooDooism" and the "neo-slave narrative" (Flight to Canada), with the houngan Papa LaBas recurring across several novels.
- Mumbo Jumbo | Novel of Papa LaBas battling the secret Wallflower Order to stop "Jes Grew," a virus spreading 1920s Black cultural and musical expression.
Toni Morrison | Nobel laureate whose Beloved, Song of Solomon, The Bluest Eye, Sula, and Jazz make her the most-asked postwar American novelist.
- Beloved | Morrison's novel of Sethe, haunted at 124 Bluestone Road by the daughter she killed rather than return to slavery; Paul D, Denver, and the Sweet Home plantation are stock clues.
- Sethe | Beloved's protagonist, modeled on Margaret Garner; kills her daughter with a saw rather than let the Schoolteacher return them to the Sweet Home plantation.
- 124 Bluestone Road | Beloved's haunted Cincinnati address, "spiteful" and "full of a baby's venom," which scares off Sethe's sons Howard and Buglar.
- Paul D | Beloved's former Sweet Home fellow slave who becomes Sethe's lover at 124 and drives out the house's ghost.
- Song of Solomon | Morrison's novel of Milkman Dead's journey south to trace the flying African Solomon; his aunt Pilate, born without a navel, and the insurance agent's opening leap are clues.
- milkman | Common-link nickname: Macon "Milkman" Dead III, who searches for Pilate's gold in Song of Solomon, and the title stalker of Anna Burns's Troubles-era Booker winner Milkman.
- The Bluest Eye | Morrison's first novel: Pecola Breedlove prays for blue eyes in Lorain, Ohio; partly narrated by Claudia MacTeer and framed by a distorted Dick-and-Jane primer.
- Sula | Novel of Sula Peace and Nel Wright's friendship in the Bottom; Shadrack's yearly "National Suicide Day" and the drowning of Chicken Little recur.
- Sula Peace | Title character who drowns Chicken Little as a child, sleeps with Nel's husband Jude, and returns to Medallion amid a "plague of robins."
Alice Walker | Author of The Color Purple; the story "Everyday Use," the essays of In Search of Our Mothers' Gardens, and her role reviving Hurston's reputation also appear.
- The Color Purple | Walker's epistolary Pulitzer winner: Celie's letters to God and from her missionary sister Nettie in Africa, with blues singer Shug Avery and the abusive Mister.
Audre Lorde | Self-described "Black, lesbian, mother, warrior, poet"; the essay "The Master's Tools Will Never Dismantle the Master's House," The Cancer Journals, and the "biomythography" Zami.
Maya Angelou | Memoirist-poet of I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings; "Still I Rise," "Phenomenal Woman," and reading "On the Pulse of Morning" at Clinton's inauguration recur.
- I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings | First of Angelou's seven memoirs: her Stamps, Arkansas childhood, rape by Mr. Freeman that leaves her mute, and becoming San Francisco's first Black streetcar conductor.
- "On the Pulse of Morning" | Inaugural poem urging listeners to "lift up your eyes" and "lift up your hearts," spoken by "A Rock, A River, A Tree"; read at Clinton's 1993 inauguration.
Nikki Giovanni | Black Arts poet of "Nikki-Rosa" and "Knoxville, Tennessee"; delivered the "We Are Virginia Tech" chant-poem after the 2007 campus shooting.
Terrance Hayes | Poet of Lighthead, winner of the 2010 National Book Award, and American Sonnets for My Past and Future Assassin; invented the golden shovel form from Brooks's "We Real Cool."
Claudia Rankine | Poet-essayist of the book-length "American Lyric" Citizen, addressing Serena Williams and Trayvon Martin in the second person; also wrote Just Us.
Octavia Butler | Afrofuturist author of Kindred and the Earthseed novels Parable of the Sower and Parable of the Talents; also wrote the novella "Bloodchild."
- Kindred | Neo-slave narrative in which writer Dana time-travels from 1970s Los Angeles to save her slaveholding ancestor Rufus Weylin on a Maryland plantation.
Colson Whitehead | Back-to-back Pulitzer winner for The Underground Railroad, which makes the railroad literal, and The Nickel Boys, based on the Dozier School; earlier wrote The Intuitionist and Zone One.
- The Intuitionist | Debut novel of Lila Mae Watson, the first Black female elevator inspector, whose intuitionist school of inspection rivals the rationalist Empiricists.
Jesmyn Ward | Mississippi novelist of Salvage the Bones and the National Book Award-winning Sing, Unburied, Sing; also edited the essay collection The Fire This Time.
- Salvage the Bones | Novel of pregnant teenager Esch Batiste and her brother Skeetah's prize pit bull China, set around Hurricane Katrina's landfall.
Percival Everett | Metafictional satirist of Erasure, whose academic Thelonious "Monk" Ellison pens the parody novel "My Pafology"; also wrote Dr. No and the Huck Finn retelling James.
James McBride | Author of the memoir The Color of Water, the John Brown novel The Good Lord Bird, and Deacon King Kong, set in a Brooklyn housing project.
Ta-Nehisi Coates | Atlantic writer of Between the World and Me, a letter to his son Samori, and the essay "The Case for Reparations"; also wrote the novel The Water Dancer.
The Sellout | Paul Beatty's 2016 Booker winner: the narrator tries reintroducing segregation to the disincorporated Los Angeles ghetto of Dickens and keeps Hominy as a slave.
A Lesson Before Dying | Ernest Gaines's novel of schoolteacher Grant Wiggins mentoring the wrongly condemned Jefferson, urged to die "like a man," not a "hog."
bell hooks | Feminist theorist, pen name lowercase to honor her great-grandmother; wrote Ain't I a Woman?, Teaching to Transgress, and All About Love.
Henry Louis Gates | Harvard critic of The Signifying Monkey, which theorizes Black "double-voiced" texts via the trickster figure Esu-Elegbara; co-edited the Norton Anthology of African-American Literature.

# American Drama
> American drama's twentieth century, from O'Neill's tragic naturalism through Group Theatre social realism and the Williams-Miller golden age to August Wilson's century-spanning Pittsburgh Cycle.
Eugene O'Neill = Eugene Gladstone O'Neill | America's first great playwright and its only dramatist Nobel laureate: Long Day's Journey into Night, The Iceman Cometh, The Hairy Ape, Desire Under the Elms, and Mourning Becomes Electra.
- The Iceman Cometh | O'Neill play set in Harry Hope's saloon, where salesman Hickey urges the regulars to abandon their "pipe dreams"; he is revealed to have murdered his wife Evelyn.
- Harry Hope's saloon | Agoraphobic Harry Hope's Raines-Law bar, setting of The Iceman Cometh, where Hickey confronts the regulars' "pipe dreams" and Don Parritt leaps to his death.
- Hickey | Charismatic salesman in The Iceman Cometh who urges Harry Hope's regulars to abandon their "pipe dreams," then confesses to murdering his wife Evelyn.
- Long Day's Journey into Night | O'Neill's posthumously produced autobiographical play: miserly actor James Tyrone, his morphine-addicted wife Mary, and sons Jamie and consumptive Edmund across one day in Connecticut.
- Mary Tyrone | Morphine-addicted matriarch of Long Day's Journey into Night; the family maid Cathleen and an offstage foghorn accompany her retreat into the past.
- fog | Recurring O'Neill motif: shrouds Anna Christie's coastal setting and keeps the morphine-addicted Mary Tyrone awake via foghorn in Long Day's Journey into Night.
- The Hairy Ape | O'Neill play of stoker Yank, rejected by Mildred Douglas and the IWW alike, who is crushed to death after freeing a gorilla from its cage.
- The Emperor Jones | O'Neill play in which ex-Pullman porter Brutus Jones, ruling a Caribbean island, flees into the forest pursued by "Little Formless Fears" and is killed by a silver bullet.
- Anna Christie | O'Neill play in which the title former prostitute reunites with her father, barge captain Chris Christopherson, and falls for the Irish stoker Mat Burke.
- Strange Interlude | O'Neill's Pulitzer-winning play of Nina Leeds, whose soliloquies interrupt the dialogue; she marries Sam Evans but conceives her son with Dr. Ned Darrell.
- Desire Under the Elms | O'Neill's Hippolytus-inspired tragedy: young wife Abbie Putnam seduces stepson Eben Cabot on the family farm, then smothers their child to prove her love.
- Ah, Wilderness! | O'Neill's only comedy: Richard Miller's Fourth of July coming-of-age in Connecticut with sweetheart Muriel McComber; the title quotes the Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam.
Clifford Odets | Group Theatre social dramatist of the Depression: Waiting for Lefty, Awake and Sing!, and Golden Boy, about violinist-turned-boxer Joe Bonaparte.
- Waiting for Lefty | Odets's agitprop one-act at a taxi-union meeting awaiting organizer Lefty Costello, who is found shot dead; it ends with the audience-rousing cry of "Strike!"
- Awake and Sing! | Odets's Depression-era Bronx family drama: the Marxist grandfather Jacob commits suicide so his insurance money can free grandson Ralph; daughter Hennie is pregnant.
- Golden Boy | Odets's Group Theatre hit: violinist Joe Bonaparte gives up music for boxing and Lorna Moon, then dies crashing his car after a fatal bout.
Lillian Hellman | Playwright of The Little Foxes, The Children's Hour, and Watch on the Rhine; her memoir Pentimento and her defiant letter to HUAC also appear as clues.
- The Little Foxes | Hellman's play about the grasping Hubbard family of the post-Reconstruction South; Regina Giddens lets her husband Horace die by withholding his heart medicine.
- The Children's Hour | Hellman play in which student Mary Tilford's lie that teachers Karen and Martha are lovers destroys their school; Martha confesses her feelings and kills herself.
- Watch on the Rhine | Hellman play in which anti-fascist Kurt Muller and wife Sara harbor refugees from Nazi Germany; Kurt kills the blackmailing Romanian count Teck de Brancovis.
Tennessee Williams | Southern lyric dramatist: A Streetcar Named Desire, The Glass Menagerie, Cat on a Hot Tin Roof, Suddenly, Last Summer, and The Night of the Iguana.
- A Streetcar Named Desire | Williams play in which Blanche DuBois arrives at Stella and Stanley Kowalski's Elysian Fields apartment; "STELLA!", the paper lantern, and "the kindness of strangers" are giveaways.
- Blanche DuBois | Fading Southern belle of A Streetcar Named Desire; raped by brother-in-law Stanley Kowalski, she is led away insisting she has "always depended on the kindness of strangers."
- Stella Kowalski | Pregnant sister of Blanche DuBois in A Streetcar Named Desire; her name is famously bellowed by husband Stanley Kowalski after their poker-night fight.
- The Glass Menagerie | Williams's "memory play": narrator Tom Wingfield, his mother Amanda, his fragile sister Laura with her glass unicorn, and the gentleman caller Jim O'Connor.
- Laura Wingfield | Painfully shy "Blue Roses" of The Glass Menagerie; her glass unicorn's broken horn seals the heartbreak of the gentleman caller Jim O'Connor's visit.
- Cat on a Hot Tin Roof | Williams play set at Big Daddy Pollitt's birthday on a Mississippi Delta plantation; alcoholic Brick, his wife Maggie "the Cat," and the truth about Skipper drive the plot.
- "mendacity" | Cat on a Hot Tin Roof's central theme: Brick blames it for labeling his bond with Skipper as gay, and for Big Daddy's cancer denial.
- The Night of the Iguana = Night of the Iguana | Williams play set at Maxine Faulk's Costa Verde hotel in Mexico: defrocked Reverend Shannon, artist Hannah Jelkes, and poet grandfather Nonno; ends as Shannon unties the title reptile.
- The Rose Tattoo | Williams's only Tony-winning play: widow Serafina delle Rose falls for banana-truck driver Alvaro after her husband Rosario's affair and death are revealed.
- Suddenly, Last Summer | Williams play in which Mrs. Venable seeks a lobotomy for niece Catherine Holly to silence her account of cousin Sebastian Venable being devoured by children.
Arthur Miller = Arthur Asher Miller | Playwright of Death of a Salesman, The Crucible, All My Sons, and A View from the Bridge; his marriage to Marilyn Monroe and HUAC testimony appear as biographical clues.
- Death of a Salesman | Miller's tragedy of Willy Loman and his sons Biff and Happy; "attention must be paid," the rubber hose, and the Requiem's "We're free and clear" are stock clues.
- Willy Loman | Death of a Salesman's title character, an aging traveling salesman whose broken refrigerator and rubber hose in the basement foreshadow his suicide for insurance money.
- Biff Loman | Willy Loman's elder son in Death of a Salesman; discovering his father's affair with The Woman derails his University of Virginia football scholarship.
- The Crucible | Miller's Salem witch-trial allegory of McCarthyism: John Proctor's cry "Because it is my name!", the accuser Abigail Williams, and Giles Corey pressed to death demanding "more weight."
- John Proctor | The Crucible's protagonist, whose affair with Abigail Williams fuels the accusations; he tears up his false confession, crying "I have given you my soul; leave me my name!"
- Tituba | Enslaved woman in The Crucible who admits giving Betty chicken blood and accuses Goody Good and Goody Osborne, sparking the Salem witchcraft hysteria.
- All My Sons | Miller play about Joe Keller, who shipped cracked cylinder heads that killed twenty-one pilots; his son Larry's suicide letter forces the recognition that "they were all my sons."
- A View from the Bridge | Miller play of Red Hook longshoreman Eddie Carbone, whose jealous obsession with niece Catherine leads him to inform on immigrants Marco and Rodolpho.
- After the Fall | Miller's semi-autobiographical play narrated by lawyer Quentin, whose failed marriages (one echoing Marilyn Monroe) unfold before the silhouette of a Nazi camp tower.
Lorraine Hansberry | First Black woman to have a play on Broadway, with A Raisin in the Sun; The Sign in Sidney Brustein's Window and To Be Young, Gifted and Black also appear.
- A Raisin in the Sun | Hansberry's play about the Youngers' insurance money and move to white Clybourne Park: Walter Lee's liquor-store dream, Beneatha, Mama's plant, and Karl Lindner's buyout offer.
- Beneatha Younger | Aspiring doctor in A Raisin in the Sun who favors Nigerian suitor Joseph Asagai over the assimilated George Murchison; wears her hair natural.
- The Sign in Sidney Brustein's Window | Hansberry's last play, set among Greenwich Village intellectuals: publisher Sidney Brustein backs populist candidate Wally O'Hara; completed by husband Robert Nemiroff after her death.
August Wilson | Author of the ten-play Pittsburgh Cycle chronicling each decade of twentieth-century Black life: Fences, The Piano Lesson, Ma Rainey's Black Bottom, and Joe Turner's Come and Gone.
- Fences | Wilson's Pulitzer winner about ex-Negro Leagues slugger Troy Maxson, who builds a fence, taunts Death, and blocks his son Cory's football scholarship; Gabriel opens the gates at the end.
- Troy Maxson | Protagonist of Fences: ex-Negro Leagues slugger turned garbage collector who blocks son Cory's football scholarship and fathers Raynell with his mistress Alberta.
- The Piano Lesson | Wilson play in which Boy Willie wants to sell the piano carved with the family's history, which his sister Berniece refuses to touch; it ends with the exorcism of Sutter's ghost.
- Ma Rainey's Black Bottom | Wilson's only Pittsburgh Cycle play set outside Pittsburgh: at a Chicago recording session, trumpeter Levee stabs bandleader Toledo after Ma Rainey fires him.
- Ma Rainey | Blues singer of Ma Rainey's Black Bottom who refuses to record without a Coca-Cola and fires the hot-tempered trumpeter Levee from her band.
- Joe Turner's Come and Gone | Pittsburgh Cycle entry set in the 1910s: conjure man Bynum aids Herald Loomis, freed from Joe Turner's chain gang, in finding his lost wife.
- Gem of the Ocean | Cycle's earliest chronological entry: 285-year-old "washer of souls" Aunt Ester sends Citizen Barlow to the City of Bones aboard a paper boat.
- Aunt Ester | Recurring centuries-old healer of the Pittsburgh Cycle, living at 1839 Wylie Avenue; most prominent in Gem of the Ocean, guiding Citizen Barlow.
- King Hedley | Name shared by two Pittsburgh Cycle characters: an ex-con who sells stolen refrigerators in King Hedley II, and Seven Guitars' killer of Floyd Barton.
- Pittsburgh Cycle | August Wilson's ten-play series, one set per decade of twentieth-century Black life; Gem of the Ocean and Radio Golf bookend it, Aunt Ester recurs.
- Pittsburgh | Common link built on August Wilson's ten-play cycle in the Hill District, plus Chabon's The Mysteries of Pittsburgh, Cather's "Paul's Case," and Annie Dillard's An American Childhood.
Edward Albee = Edward Franklin Albee III | Playwright bridging absurdism and domestic drama: Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?, The Zoo Story, A Delicate Balance, and The Goat, or Who Is Sylvia?
- Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf? | Albee's play of George and Martha's all-night "Fun and Games" with guests Nick and Honey, climaxing in the "killing" of George and Martha's imaginary son.
- A Delicate Balance | Albee's 1967 Pulitzer winner: Harry and Edna move in with Tobias and Agnes, fleeing a nameless terror, displacing daughter Julia from her room.
- Seascape | Albee play in which retired couple Nancy and Charlie, resting on a beach, meet the evolved lizard couple Leslie and Sarah and discuss evolution.
- The Zoo Story = Zoo Story | Albee's one-act on a Central Park bench: drifter Jerry tells the "story of Jerry and the dog," provokes publisher Peter, and impales himself on the knife he makes Peter hold.
- The Sandbox | Albee one-act in which Mommy and Daddy dump Grandma in the title sandbox as the calisthenics-performing Young Man, the Angel of Death, comes for her.
- The Goat | Albee play in which Pritzker-winning architect Martin confesses an affair with a goat named Sylvia; wife Stevie kills and drags the animal onstage in revenge.
David Mamet = David Alan Mamet | Playwright of clipped, profane "Mamet speak": Glengarry Glen Ross, American Buffalo, Oleanna, and Speed-the-Plow.
- Glengarry Glen Ross | Mamet's Pulitzer winner about desperate Chicago real-estate salesmen — Shelly Levene, Ricky Roma, the stolen Glengarry leads, and the film's added "Always be closing" speech.
- American Buffalo | Mamet play set in a junk shop: Don and Teach plot to steal back a rare buffalo nickel, botching the burglary after distrusting young Bobby.
- Oleanna | Two-hander in which student Carol accuses professor John of sexual harassment; the escalating conflict ends as John violently assaults her with a chair.
Dion Boucicault | Irish-American actor-manager and prolific melodramatist; wrote The Octoroon and the Irish drama The Colleen Bawn.
The Octoroon | Boucicault melodrama set on the Terrebonne plantation; Zoe, one-eighth Black, cannot marry the man she loves under slavery-era law.
Our American Cousin | Tom Taylor's comedy of the bumbling Lord Dundreary; the play Lincoln was watching at Ford's Theatre when assassinated.
David Belasco | Turn-of-the-century producer-director famed for hyper-realistic staging; wrote the source plays behind Puccini's Madama Butterfly and The Girl of the Golden West.
Susan Glaspell | Provincetown Players co-founder; wrote Trifles and won the Pulitzer for Alison's House, based on Emily Dickinson's life.
Trifles | Glaspell's one-act in which farmwives Mrs. Peters and Mrs. Hale piece together Minnie Wright's motive for murder from kitchen "trifles."
Elmer Rice | Expressionist playwright of The Adding Machine; also won the Pulitzer for the tenement drama Street Scene, later a Kurt Weill opera.
The Adding Machine | Rice's expressionist play in which the clerk Mr. Zero murders his boss after being replaced by the title device.
Machinal | Sophie Treadwell's expressionist play, loosely based on the Ruth Snyder case, of a stifled "Young Woman" who murders her husband.
Maxwell Anderson | Verse dramatist who drew on the Sacco and Vanzetti case for Winterset; also wrote Both Your Houses and, with Kurt Weill, Lost in the Stars.
Winterset | Anderson's verse drama fictionalizing Sacco and Vanzetti: Mio Romagna seeks to clear his executed father, opposed by the murderer Trock Estrella.
Robert Sherwood | Algonquin Round Table playwright and FDR speechwriter; three-time Drama Pulitzer winner for Idiot's Delight, Abe Lincoln in Illinois, and There Shall Be No Night.
Orson Welles | Directed the all-Black "Voodoo" Macbeth and The Cradle Will Rock for the Federal Theatre Project before Citizen Kane and War of the Worlds.
method acting | Lee Strasberg's Group Theatre-rooted technique using "affective memory"; associated with Odets's Waiting for Lefty and the Actors Studio.
The Man Who Came to Dinner | Kaufman and Hart comedy: acerbic critic Sheridan Whiteside, injured en route to dinner, torments his Ohio hosts as houseguest.
Our Town | Wilder's play narrated by the Stage Manager in Grover's Corners, New Hampshire; Emily Webb marries George Gibbs, dies, and relives her twelfth birthday.
- the Stage Manager = stage manager -> The Stage Manager | Narrates Grover's Corners, marries George and Emily, and answers "the saints and poets, maybe" to Emily's question.
- Emily Webb | Our Town's heroine, who marries George Gibbs, dies in childbirth, and relives her twelfth birthday from the grave.
The Skin of Our Teeth | Wilder's fourth-wall-breaking allegory of the Antrobus family of Excelsior, New Jersey, surviving an ice age, flood, and war with maid Sabina.

# Postwar Poetry and the Beats
> Postwar American verse fractures into confessional self-exposure, Beat countercultural sprawl, and New York School wit, before opening into today's laureates and performance poetry.
Sylvia Plath | Confessional poet of Ariel — "Daddy," "Lady Lazarus" ("I eat men like air") — and The Colossus; The Bell Jar and her marriage to Ted Hughes appear constantly.
- The Bell Jar | Plath's autobiographical novel of Esther Greenwood's New York magazine internship, breakdown, and shock treatment; first published under the pseudonym Victoria Lucas.
- Esther Greenwood | Narrator of The Bell Jar; a magazine intern under Jay Cee who undergoes electroconvulsive therapy after a breakdown and suicide attempt.
- Ariel | Plath's posthumous collection, published by Hughes; its title poem is a fierce dawn horse ride "into the red eye, the cauldron of morning."
- "Daddy" | Plath poem addressing her dead father as a "man in black with a Meinkampf look"; opens "You do not do, you do not do."
- Sylvia Plath's father | Otto Plath, recalled as a "bag full of God" and "panzer-man" in "Daddy," and mourned in "Electra on Azalea Path" and "Little Fugue."
- "Lady Lazarus" | Plath poem of serial near-death and rebirth: "Out of the ash / I rise with my red hair / And I eat men like air."
- Ted Hughes | English poet and Plath's husband; his Birthday Letters answered her confessional poems decades later, and his Crow followed her 1963 suicide.
Elizabeth Bishop | Meticulous poet of "One Art" ("the art of losing isn't hard to master"), "The Fish," "In the Waiting Room," and "The Moose"; lived for years in Brazil with Lota de Macedo Soares.
- "One Art" | Villanelle listing escalating losses, from door keys to "a continent"; written after Lota de Macedo Soares's suicide, ending "(Write it!)".
- "The Armadillo" | Poem of the title animal fleeing Brazilian fire-balloons at a saint's-day festival; dedicated to Robert Lowell, who answered it with "Skunk Hour."
- "The Fish" | Poem of an old, battle-scarred fish held boatside and released once the speaker sees "rainbow, rainbow, rainbow" in the oil-slicked water; Marianne Moore wrote a same-titled poem.
Robert Lowell = Robert Traill Spence Lowell IV | Founder of confessional poetry with Life Studies; "Skunk Hour," "For the Union Dead," and "Memories of West Street and Lepke," from his conscientious-objector jail term, recur.
- "Skunk Hour" | Lowell poem set on Nautilus Island, ending as a skunk "jabs her wedge-head in a cup / of sour cream"; dedicated to Elizabeth Bishop, answering her "The Armadillo."
- "For the Union Dead" | Poem on the Robert Gould Shaw memorial, "a fishbone in the city's throat," set against a Boston parking garage under construction.
- Life Studies | Lowell's confession-launching 1959 collection; includes the prose memoir "91 Revere Street" and poems on his mental illness, ending with "Skunk Hour."
Anne Sexton | Confessional poet who began writing in therapy: To Bedlam and Part Way Back, the Pulitzer-winning Live or Die, and the fairy-tale revisions of Transformations.
John Berryman | Confessional poet of The Dream Songs, starring Henry and the minstrel-voiced "Mr. Bones," and Homage to Mistress Bradstreet; he jumped from a Minneapolis bridge.
- The Dream Songs | Berryman's 385-poem sequence for alter ego Henry and his blackface interlocutor "Mr. Bones"; twelve elegies were dedicated to Delmore Schwartz.
Confessional Poetry | Movement label for intensely autobiographical postwar verse; Lowell's Life Studies, Plath, Sexton, Berryman, and W. D. Snodgrass's Heart's Needle are the canonical clues.
Delmore Schwartz | New York poet-critic whose story "In Dreams Begin Responsibilities" and poem "In the Naked Bed, In Plato's Cave" made him the model for Saul Bellow's Humboldt's Gift.
- "In Dreams Begin Responsibilities" | Schwartz's title story: a man watches a film of his parents' courtship and disastrous marriage, shouting warnings at the screen.
Randall Jarrell | Poet-critic best known for the five-line "The Death of the Ball Turret Gunner," which ends "they washed me out of the turret with a hose"; also wrote Pictures from an Institution.

Theodore Roethke | Michigan-born poet of "My Papa's Waltz," the whiskey-drunk father who "beat time" on the speaker's head; also wrote "The Waking" and the greenhouse poem "Root Cellar."
John Ashbery | New York School poet whose Self-Portrait in a Convex Mirror, on Parmigianino's painting, swept the Pulitzer, National Book Award, and NBCC; famously difficult and prolific.

Frank O'Hara | New York School poet and MoMA curator; Lunch Poems pieces like "The Day Lady Died," on Billie Holiday's death, and "Why I Am Not a Painter" recur.

- "Howl" | Ginsberg's Beat epic beginning "I saw the best minds of my generation destroyed by madness"; addresses Carl Solomon ("I'm with you in Rockland"), rails against Moloch, and survived a 1957 obscenity trial.

spoken word poetry | Broader performance genre of poets reading aloud to audience approval, from Louise Bennett-Coverley's dialect verse to Gil Scott-Heron; slam poetry is its competitive, judged form.

# Postwar Fiction (1945-1980)
> Fiction's postwar boom: suburban short stories give way to Jewish-American social novels, postmodern maximalism, and Vietnam-era New Journalism blurring fact and fiction.
J. D. Salinger | Reclusive author of The Catcher in the Rye and the Glass family stories — Franny and Zooey and "A Perfect Day for Bananafish," ending in Seymour's suicide.

- Franny Glass | Youngest Glass sibling whose breakdown over The Way of a Pilgrim, recited to boyfriend Lane Coutell, is chronicled in Franny and Zooey.

# Contemporary Fiction (1980-present)
> American fiction since 1980 ranges from postmodern maximalism and Southern/Western violence through immigrant, Native, and autofictional voices reshaping the contemporary novel and short story.
David Foster Wallace | Postmodern maximalist of Infinite Jest, set partly at Enfield Tennis Academy; also wrote the essay "A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again" and the unfinished The Pale King.
- Infinite Jest | Wallace's doorstop novel of the Incandenza family, wheelchair-bound Quebecois separatists, and a lethally entertaining "samizdat" film cartridge.
Raymond Carver = Raymond Clevie Carver Jr. | Minimalist "dirty realist" story writer, heavily edited by Gordon Lish: "Cathedral," "Where I'm Calling From," and "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love."
- "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love" = What We Talk About When We Talk About Love | Title story: cardiologist Mel and Terri, plus Nick and Laura, drink gin and argue about love, recalling an old couple hurt in a crash.
- "Cathedral" | A blind man, Robert, guides the narrator's hand to draw a cathedral together on paper while high.
- "Where I'm Calling From" | Set at a drying-out facility; an alcoholic narrator listens to fellow patient J.P.'s life story.
Cormac McCarthy | Novelist of violent Souths and Southwests: Blood Meridian with Judge Holden, the Border Trilogy beginning with All the Pretty Horses, No Country for Old Men, and The Road.
- The Road | Post-apocalyptic Pulitzer winner following an unnamed father and son "carrying the fire" toward the coast, pushing a shopping cart through the ash.
- All the Pretty Horses | Border Trilogy opener: John Grady Cole rides into Mexico, falls for ranch owner's daughter Alejandra, and is imprisoned.
- Judge Holden | Hairless, giant, erudite member of Glanton's scalp-hunting gang; declares "war is god" and shoots the ex-priest Tobin.
- No Country For Old Men | Llewelyn Moss stumbles on a drug deal's cash, pursued by the coin-flipping killer Anton Chigurh; Sheriff Bell narrates.
- Blood Meridian | Ultraviolent Western following "the kid" through Glanton's scalp-hunting gang and its philosophizing judge across the Texas-Mexico borderlands.
Don DeLillo | Postmodernist of White Noise, Underworld — which opens at the "Shot Heard 'Round the World" baseball game — Libra, and Mao II.
- White Noise | National Book Award-winning satire of Hitler-studies professor Jack Gladney, the Airborne Toxic Event, and the fear-of-death drug Dylar.
- Underworld | Waste-management executive Nick Shay, haunted by wife Marian's affair, anchors a Cold War epic opening with Cotter Martin catching a home-run ball.
Jhumpa Lahiri | Chronicler of Bengali American life: the Pulitzer-winning Interpreter of Maladies, The Namesake with Gogol Ganguli, and later books written in Italian.
Joyce Carol Oates | Hyper-prolific novelist; "Where Are You Going, Where Have You Been?" with Arnold Friend, the National Book Award winner them, and Blonde are the usual clues.
- "Where Are You Going, Where Have You Been?" | Teenager Connie, alone at home, is menaced by the sinister, possibly supernatural stranger Arnold Friend.
Michael Chabon | Pulitzer winner for The Amazing Adventures of Kavalier & Clay, about Golden Age comic-book creators; The Yiddish Policemen's Union and The Mysteries of Pittsburgh also come up.
- The Amazing Adventures of Kavalier and Clay | Joe Kavalier escapes Prague for New York, teams with cousin Sam Clay to create the superhero comic The Escapist.
- The Yiddish Policemen's Union | Alternate history set in a Jewish settlement in Sitka, Alaska; detective Meyer Landsman investigates a murder amid looming resettlement.
Jonathan Franzen = Jonathan Earl Franzen | Novelist of Midwestern family sagas: The Corrections, about the Lambert family, and Freedom; his Oprah's Book Club snub is a recurring clue.
- Freedom | Novel of Patty and Walter Berglund's marriage and environmentalism; Patty gets a basketball scholarship to Minnesota.
- "Mr. Difficult" | Essay contrasting "Status" and "Contract" models of fiction while attacking William Gaddis's hard-to-finish novel J R.
Sherman Alexie | Spokane/Coeur d'Alene writer of The Lone Ranger and Tonto Fistfight in Heaven, the basis for the film Smoke Signals, and The Absolutely True Diary of a Part-Time Indian.
George Saunders | Satirical story writer of Tenth of December and CivilWarLand in Bad Decline; won the Booker Prize for Lincoln in the Bardo, narrated by graveyard ghosts.
Alice Munro = Alice Ann Munro | Canadian short story writer and 2013 Nobel laureate; "The Bear Came Over the Mountain," Lives of Girls and Women, and Dance of the Happy Shades recur as clues.
Amy Tan | Chinese American novelist best known for The Joy Luck Club; her fiction often traces immigrant mothers and American-born daughters across generations.
- The Joy Luck Club | Four Chinese immigrant mothers and their American-born daughters trade stories over mahjong; Jing-mei "June" Woo travels to China to meet her half-sisters.
Jamaica Kincaid | Antiguan American writer known for the prose poem "Girl" and the novel Annie John, drawing on a mother-daughter relationship in the Caribbean.
- "Girl" | A mother's stream of instructions to her daughter, from washing clothes to "the kind of woman the baker won't let near the bread."
John Irving = John Winslow Irving | Novelist of The World According to Garp, The Cider House Rules, and A Prayer for Owen Meany, often set in New England.
- The World According to Garp | T.S. Garp, son of feminist Jenny Fields, becomes a writer; the "Under Toad" and the Ellen Jamesians recur.
- The Cider House Rules | Orphan Homer Wells is trained in abortion medicine by Dr. Larch at a Maine orphanage, then works at an apple orchard.
- Owen Meany | Tiny-voiced Owen believes he is God's instrument and dies saving Vietnamese children; "keep passing the open windows" recurs.
Barbara Kingsolver | Novelist of The Poisonwood Bible, about the missionary Price family in the Congo, and the Pulitzer-winning Demon Copperhead, a Dickens-inspired Appalachian retelling.
Jennifer Egan | Novelist of A Visit from the Goon Squad, a Pulitzer-winning story cycle told partly through a slideshow chapter, following music-industry figures across decades.
- A Visit From the Goon Squad | Interlinked chapters follow kleptomaniac Sasha and record executive Bennie Salazar across decades; one chapter is a PowerPoint slideshow.
Jonathan Safran Foer | Novelist of Everything Is Illuminated and Extremely Loud and Incredibly Close; also writes food-politics nonfiction like Eating Animals.
- Extremely Loud and Incredibly Close | Nine-year-old Oskar Schell searches New York for a lock matching a key left by his father, who died on 9/11.
Louise Erdrich | Ojibwe novelist of Love Medicine and The Round House; her 2020 novel The Night Watchman draws on her grandfather's fight against Native termination policy.
- Love Medicine | Story cycle opening with June Kashpaw's death walking home in a snowstorm, following intertwined Ojibwe families on a North Dakota reservation.
Erdrich | Common-link surname spanning sisters Louise, the celebrated novelist, and Heid, an Ojibwe poet; both are tied to Minneapolis's Birchbark Books.
Margaret Atwood | Canadian novelist of The Handmaid's Tale and the speculative Oryx and Crake, often clued for dystopian and environmental themes.
- Oryx and Crake | Post-apocalyptic novel following Snowman/Jimmy, his genius friend Crake, and the genetically engineered Crakers after a bioengineered plague.
Sandra Cisneros | Chicana writer of The House on Mango Street, a vignette novel narrated by young Esperanza Cordero in a Chicago barrio.
- The House on Mango Street | Vignette novel narrated by Esperanza Cordero, a girl coming of age in a Chicago Latino neighborhood.
Annie Dillard | Nonfiction writer of Pilgrim at Tinker Creek, a Pulitzer-winning meditation on nature observed near her Virginia home.
Lydia Davis | Master of extremely short flash fiction, including "Samuel Johnson Is Indignant"; also translated Proust's Swann's Way and Flaubert's Madame Bovary.
Ocean Vuong | Vietnamese American poet and novelist of On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous, an epistolary novel addressed to the narrator's illiterate mother.
- Night Sky With Exit Wounds | Ocean Vuong's 2016 debut collection responding to Rilke ("Suppose you do change your life"); includes "Telemachus" and "Aubade with Burning City."
Paul Auster | Postmodern novelist of The New York Trilogy and 4 3 2 1, blending detective-story tropes with metafictional identity games.
- The New York Trilogy | Three linked detective novellas — City of Glass, Ghosts, and The Locked Room — that dissolve identity and authorship.
Tim O'Brien | Vietnam War novelist of The Things They Carried, blurring memoir and fiction; also wrote Going After Cacciato.
- The Things They Carried | Linked stories cataloging soldiers' physical and emotional burdens; Lieutenant Jimmy Cross blames himself for Kiowa's death in a sewage field.
Anne Carson | Canadian classicist and poet of the verse novel Autobiography of Red, following the winged monster Geryon; also wrote the brother-elegy Nox.
House of Leaves | Mark Z. Danielewski's typographically experimental horror novel about the Navidson family's house, which is larger inside than out.
Jane Smiley | Novelist of A Thousand Acres, a Pulitzer-winning King Lear retelling set on an Iowa farm.
Junot Diaz | Dominican American novelist of The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao, following a nerdy Dominican American and his family's curse, or fuku.
Kathy Acker | Punk postmodernist who used cut-up technique in Blood and Guts in High School and rewrote Don Quixote as an American woman.
Maxine Hong Kingston | Chinese American memoirist of The Woman Warrior, blending myth and autobiography around the legend of Fa Mu Lan.
- The Woman Warrior: Memoirs of a Girlhood Among Ghosts | Memoir blending the narrator's Chinese American girlhood with the legend of the woman warrior Fa Mu Lan and the fearsome aunt "No Name Woman."
Bless Me, Ultima | Rudolfo Anaya's Chicano coming-of-age novel: Antonio Marez is mentored by the curandera Ultima and learns of the sacred Golden Carp.
Denis Johnson | Story writer of Jesus' Son and the National Book Award-winning Vietnam epic Tree of Smoke.
- Jesus' Son | Linked stories narrated by an unnamed addict nicknamed "Fuckhead," including "Emergency" with the orderly Georgie.
Homegoing | Yaa Gyasi's debut novel tracing two half-sisters, Effia and Esi, and their descendants from the Gold Coast slave trade to America.
Lorrie Moore | Short story writer known for wordplay and dark comedy, including the collection Birds of America.
Patricia Lockwood | Poet-novelist of "Rape Joke" and the memoir Priestdaddy, about growing up with a married Catholic priest father; also wrote the internet-age novel No One Is Talking About This.

Alison Bechdel | Graphic memoirist of Fun Home, about her closeted gay father and a family funeral home; the "Bechdel test" bears her name.
Art Spiegelman | Graphic novelist of Maus, depicting the Holocaust with Jews as mice and Nazis as cats through his father Vladek's story.
Ben Lerner | Poet-novelist of Leaving the Atocha Station and 10:04; The Topeka School follows his autofictional alter ego Adam Gordon.
Donna Tartt | Novelist of The Secret History, about classics students who murder a classmate, and the Pulitzer-winning The Goldfinch.
Emily St. John Mandel | Novelist of Station Eleven, following a Shakespearean traveling troupe after a flu pandemic collapses civilization.
James Welch | Blackfeet novelist of Winter in the Blood, set on the Fort Belknap Reservation, and the historical epic Fools Crow.

Marilynne Robinson | Novelist of Gilead, an epistolary novel from an aging Congregationalist minister to his young son.

Ottessa Moshfegh | Novelist of My Year of Rest and Relaxation, about a woman who sleeps for a year, and the unsettling Eileen.

# Genre Fiction: Science Fiction, Horror, and Crime
> American genre writing from Golden Age science fiction through mid-century gothic horror to the hardboiled detective novel.
Ray Bradbury | Science-fiction lyricist: Fahrenheit 451 with fireman Guy Montag, The Martian Chronicles, Something Wicked This Way Comes, and Dandelion Wine.
- Fahrenheit 451 | Dystopia of fireman Guy Montag, who burns books at the title temperature, guided by Professor Faber against a society that outlaws reading.
- Something Wicked This Way Comes | Dark fantasy of boys Will Halloway and Jim Nightshade menaced by Mr. Dark's sinister carnival, Cooger & Dark's Pandemonium Shadow Show.
- "The Veldt" | Story of a nursery that renders children's African-veldt fantasies real, ending with the parents devoured by illusory lions.
- "There Will Come Soft Rains" | Story of an automated house that keeps its routines after a nuclear war kills its inhabitants, leaving only their silhouettes seared onto a wall.
- The Martian Chronicles | Linked stories of human colonization of Mars, from telepathic Martians' near-extinction to settlers rebuilding Earth culture on the red planet.
Isaac Asimov | Prolific SF grandmaster: the Foundation series built on Hari Seldon's psychohistory, the Three Laws of Robotics from I, Robot, and the story "Nightfall."
Ursula K. Le Guin | Master of anthropological SF and fantasy: The Left Hand of Darkness, The Dispossessed, the Earthsea books, and "The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas."
- Omelas | Utopian city whose happiness depends on a suffering scapegoat child locked in a closet, in "The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas."
Ted Chiang | Chinese American SF author of "Story of Your Life," basis for the film Arrival, in which linguist Louise Banks decodes the alien language Heptapod B; also "Tower of Babylon" and Exhalation.
William Gibson | Cyberpunk pioneer who coined "cyberspace" in "Burning Chrome"; Neuromancer follows hacker Case and the augmented "razorgirl" Molly Millions through the Sprawl.
Harlan Ellison | New Wave SF author of "I Have No Mouth, and I Must Scream," in which the sentient supercomputer AM tortures the last five humans alive; edited the anthology Dangerous Visions.
A Canticle for Leibowitz | Walter M. Miller Jr.'s post-nuclear novel of a monastic order preserving human knowledge across centuries, told in sections "Fiat Homo," "Fiat Lux," "Fiat Voluntas Tua."
Shirley Jackson | Gothic writer of "The Lottery," which drew furious New Yorker mail over Tessie Hutchinson's stoning, The Haunting of Hill House, and We Have Always Lived in the Castle.
- "The Lottery" | Village ritual in which townspeople draw slips of paper, ending with the "winner" Tessie Hutchinson stoned by her neighbors and children.
- The Haunting of Hill House | Novel of parapsychologist John Montague's supernatural investigation at Hill House, where the fragile Eleanor Vance is drawn toward her death.
- Shirley | Alias for the author herself, subject of Ruth Franklin's biography, Susan Scarf Merrell's novel of a couple moving into her house, and a Josephine Decker biopic.
- We Have Always Lived in the Castle | Novel narrated by Merricat Blackwood, whose family was poisoned with arsenic-laced sugar; she and sister Constance live isolated afterward.
- Hill House | The "not sane" mansion itself, investigated by parapsychologist John Montague and cared for by the Dudleys; setting of, and distinct from, the novel titled for it.
H. P. Lovecraft = Howard Phillips Lovecraft | Cosmic horror author of the Cthulhu Mythos; "The Call of Cthulhu," At the Mountains of Madness, and the Necronomicon's "Great Old Ones."
Stephen King | Prolific horror author of Carrie, The Shining, and It, set largely in fictional Maine towns like Castle Rock and Derry; also wrote the memoir On Writing.
Raymond Chandler | Hardboiled crime novelist who defined the noir detective in his essay "The Simple Art of Murder"; The Big Sleep and The Long Goodbye.
- Philip Marlowe | Chandler's Los Angeles private eye, hired by General Sternwood in The Big Sleep and searching for the vanished Terry Lennox in The Long Goodbye.
The Maltese Falcon | Dashiell Hammett's novel of detective Sam Spade pursuing a valuable statuette after his partner Miles Archer's murder; femme fatale Brigid O'Shaughnessy.
Chester Himes | African American author of Harlem detective novels starring Coffin Ed Johnson and Gravedigger Jones; also wrote If He Hollers Let Him Go.
"The Most Dangerous Game" | Richard Connell's story of hunter Rainsford, shipwrecked on Ship-Trap Island and hunted for sport by General Zaroff.
westerns | Common-link genre spanning Owen Wister's The Virginian, Zane Grey's Riders of the Purple Sage, Charles Portis's True Grit, and Larry McMurtry's Lonesome Dove.

# Places in American Literature
> Cities, states, regions, and countries that recur as common-link answers, tying together works set there or written about them.
New York City | Ubiquitous common link: Whitman's ferries, Hart Crane's bridge, the Harlem Renaissance, Bartleby's Wall Street, and Gatsby's West Egg parties all feed clues.
Brooklyn | Common link: Whitman's "Crossing Brooklyn Ferry," Hart Crane's The Bridge (named for the Brooklyn Bridge), Betty Smith's A Tree Grows in Brooklyn, and Lethem's Fortress of Solitude.
Harlem | Usually Hughes's poem asking "What happens to a dream deferred?" — "does it dry up / like a raisin in the sun?" — from Montage of a Dream Deferred; sometimes the neighborhood as a common link.
Chicago = Chicago, Illinois | Common link: Sandburg's "Hog Butcher for the World" poem, Sister Carrie, Native Son, The Adventures of Augie March, A Raisin in the Sun, and Upton Sinclair's The Jungle.
Boston = Boston, Massachusetts | Home of the "Boston Brahmins" of Holmes's Elsie Venner, James's The Bostonians (Olive Chancellor and Verena Tarrant), Bellamy's Looking Backward, and Lowell's "For the Union Dead."
San Francisco | Common link: the Beats at City Lights and the Six Gallery "Howl" reading, Amy Tan's Chinatown, Hammett's The Maltese Falcon, and Kerouac's Big Sur trips.
Los Angeles | Common link: Didion's Play It As It Lays and The White Album, Nathanael West's The Day of the Locust, Chandler's Philip Marlowe novels, and Pynchon's Inherent Vice.
New Orleans | Common link: Toole's A Confederacy of Dunces, Percy's The Moviegoer, and Tennessee Williams's Vieux Carré, Orpheus Descending, and A Streetcar Named Desire.
California | Common link via Steinbeck's valleys, Didion's essays, Bret Harte's mining camps, the Los Angeles of Chandler and Nathanael West, and "A Supermarket in California."
Massachusetts | Common link: Puritan Boston in The Scarlet Letter, Dickinson's Amherst, Thoreau's Walden Pond, Lowell's "For the Union Dead," and the Salem of The Crucible.
Mississippi | Common link covering both state and river: Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha, home state of Welty and Wright, and Twain's Life on the Mississippi and Huck's raft.
Florida | Common link: Hurston's Eatonville in Their Eyes Were Watching God, Stevens's "The Idea of Order at Key West," Nilo Cruz's Anna in the Tropics, and Hiaasen's crime novels.
Georgia | Common link: Mitchell's Gone with the Wind, O'Connor's home state, Toomer's Cane, and McCullers's The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter.
Maine | Common link: Sarah Orne Jewett's Dunnet Landing in The Country of the Pointed Firs, Strout's Olive Kitteridge, and Millay's Mount Battie in "Renascence."
Iowa | Common link: Robinson's Gilead and Smiley's A Thousand Acres, both set here, plus the Iowa Writers' Workshop that trained Carver and Jorie Graham.
America = United States of America | Common link on the country itself: Whitman's "I Hear America Singing," Hughes's "I, Too, Sing America," Ginsberg's "America," McKay's sonnet "America," and Dos Passos's U.S.A. trilogy.
Canada | Common link as a destination of escape: fugitive slaves head there in Uncle Tom's Cabin and Beloved, and it recurs as a border-crossing setting and refuge across American fiction.
Mexico | Common link: Tennessee Williams's The Night of the Iguana, McCarthy's All the Pretty Horses, Chandler's The Long Goodbye, and the crossover poets Sor Juana Ines de la Cruz and Octavio Paz.
Paris | Expatriate common link: Hemingway's A Moveable Feast, Stein's salon at 27 rue de Fleurus, Baldwin's Giovanni's Room, and Henry Miller's Tropic of Cancer.
France | Common link as the expatriate destination behind the Paris entries above: the country Hemingway, Stein, Baldwin, and Miller wrote from or about.
China | Common link led by Pearl S. Buck's The Good Earth and her Nobel Prize, plus Amy Tan's mother-daughter novels and Maxine Hong Kingston's The Woman Warrior.
Africa | Common link: Wheatley's "On Being Brought from Africa to America," Cullen's "Heritage" ("What is Africa to me?"), and the Caribbean poet Derek Walcott's "A Far Cry from Africa."
Louisiana | Setting of Kate Chopin's The Awakening and "Désirée's Baby," and of Ignatius Reilly's New Orleans in A Confederacy of Dunces.
Mississippi River | Twain's Life on the Mississippi and the raft of Huckleberry Finn; also Faulkner's "Old Man" and Eliot's "The Dry Salvages."
Salem, Massachusetts | Hawthorne's "Young Goodman Brown" and The House of the Seven Gables; site of the 1692 witch trials dramatized in Miller's The Crucible.
Key West | Wallace Stevens's "The Idea of Order at Key West" and Elizabeth Bishop's island poems; longtime home of Hemingway.
Native American reservations | Setting of Erdrich's Love Medicine, Silko's Ceremony, and Alexie's The Lone Ranger and Tonto Fistfight in Heaven and The Absolutely True Diary of a Part-Time Indian.
plantations | Setting of Uncle Tom's Cabin and Chesnutt's Conjure Woman tales; also the lost estates of The Little Foxes and A Streetcar Named Desire.
Ohio | Sherwood Anderson's fictional Winesburg; also home state in Toni Morrison's Sula and The Bluest Eye and David Foster Wallace's The Broom of the System.
Illinois | Sandburg's "Chicago" and Masters's Spoon River Anthology; Chicago setting of A Raisin in the Sun and Shepard's Buried Child.
Oklahoma | Dust Bowl home of Steinbeck's Joad family; also Momaday's Kiowa homeland and the Lynn Riggs play behind the musical Oklahoma!
Philadelphia | Franklin's Autobiography and Charles Brockden Brown's Gothic novels; site of the yellow fever epidemic in Arthur Mervyn.
Washington, D.C. | Setting of Hellman's Watch on the Rhine, Edward P. Jones's Lost in the City, and Saunders's Lincoln in the Bardo.
Brooklyn Bridge | Title structure of Hart Crane's The Bridge; also lends its name to Arthur Miller's A View from the Bridge.
Los Angeles, California | Home of Nathanael West's Day of the Locust and Bukowski's Henry Chinaski; setting of Fitzgerald's The Last Tycoon.
Alaska | Setting of Chabon's alternate-history The Yiddish Policemen's Union and Krakauer's Into the Wild; homesteader poet John Haines's home state.
hospitals | Setting of One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest, Vonnegut's "2 B R 0 2 B," and hospital scenes in Angels in America.
Italy | Setting of Henry James's Daisy Miller and The Portrait of a Lady, Hawthorne's "Rappaccini's Daughter," and Hemingway's postwar stories.

Babylon | Title city of Fitzgerald's "Babylon Revisited" and Stephen Vincent Benét's "By the Waters of Babylon."
Mars | Setting of Bradbury's The Martian Chronicles and Tracy K. Smith's Pulitzer-winning Life on Mars.
Vietnam | Birth country of poet Ocean Vuong and novelist Viet Thanh Nguyen, whose The Sympathizer follows a Vietnamese narrator to Los Angeles.
Coney Island | Common-link Brooklyn amusement district: Ferlinghetti's *A Coney Island of the Mind*, Schwartz's "In Dreams Begin Responsibilities," Heller's memoir *Then and Now*.
Chinatown | Common-link ethnic enclave: Frank Chin's *The Year of the Dragon*, Charles Yu's screenplay-format novel, Polanski's neo-noir film of the same name.
Hollywood | Common-link film-industry setting: West's *The Day of the Locust*, Fitzgerald's unfinished *The Last Tycoon*, Didion's *Play It as It Lays*.
Indian reservation | Common-link Native setting: Sherman Alexie's Spokane-reservation stories, Erdrich's North Dakota novels, Momaday's *House Made of Dawn*.
Alexandria | Egyptian city common-linking Cavafy's poems, Mark Doty's AIDS-elegy collection *My Alexandria*, and Durrell's *Alexandria Quartet*.
Egypt | Common-link setting of H.D.'s *Helen in Egypt* and *Kora in Hell*, and of Naguib Mahfouz's Cairo-set novels.
Argentina | Common-link gaucho-literature homeland of Güiraldes's *Don Segundo Sombra*, Ernesto Sábato, and Manuel Puig.

Texas | Setting of Cormac McCarthy's Blood Meridian, All the Pretty Horses, and No Country for Old Men; also McMurtry's Lonesome Dove.

Poland | Setting of Uris's Mila 18 and part of Wiesel's Night; homeland of poets Adam Mickiewicz and Zbigniew Herbert.

# History and Culture through Literature
> Wars, historical figures, and the institutions — magazines, prizes, critics — that American literature keeps returning to.
Vietnam War | Common link anchored by Tim O'Brien's The Things They Carried and Going After Cacciato, with veteran poets like Yusef Komunyakaa ("Facing It") close behind.
American Civil War | Common link: The Red Badge of Courage, Whitman's Drum-Taps and hospital service, Benét's John Brown's Body, "An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge," and Cold Mountain.
World War I | Common link: Dos Passos's U.S.A. trilogy (Nineteen-Nineteen), e.e. cummings's imprisonment recounted in The Enormous Room, and Hemingway's A Farewell to Arms.
World War II | Common link: Vonnegut's Slaughterhouse-Five, Randall Jarrell's "The Death of the Ball Turret Gunner," and Auden's "September 1, 1939."
9/11 | Common link: DeLillo's Falling Man, Jonathan Safran Foer's Extremely Loud and Incredibly Close, Pynchon's Bleeding Edge, and Amiri Baraka's "Somebody Blew Up America."
American Revolution | Common link: Philip Freneau, "poet of the Revolution," Emerson's "Concord Hymn," Longfellow's "Paul Revere's Ride," and the wartime theater bans that Cato defied at Valley Forge.
the revolution | Common link on revolution as an abstract, ongoing event rather than one war: Gil Scott-Heron's "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised" and a Joy Harjo poem on its "real" form.
Christopher Columbus | Common link: Hart Crane's The Bridge ("Ave Maria"), Joel Barlow's epic The Vision of Columbus, Philip Roth's Goodbye, Columbus, and Wheatley's Columbia-as-goddess.
George Washington | Common link: Phillis Wheatley's "To His Excellency General Washington" (Columbia's champion) and Parson Weems's myth-making biography.
Thomas Jefferson | Common link: William Wells Brown's Clotel (fictionalizing Sally Hemings's daughter), Jefferson's own Notes on the State of Virginia, and his dismissive verdict on Phillis Wheatley's poetry.
Abraham Lincoln | Literary common link: Whitman's elegies "O Captain! My Captain!" and "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd," Sandburg's multivolume biography, Lindsay's "Abraham Lincoln Walks at Midnight," and Saunders's Lincoln in the Bardo.
John Brown | Common link on the abolitionist in literature: Benét's epic John Brown's Body, Thoreau's "A Plea for Captain John Brown," and James McBride's The Good Lord Bird.
Daniel Webster | Common link: Benét's "The Devil and Daniel Webster" (defending Jabez Stone), Whittier's poem "Ichabod," and the jumping frog Twain names after him.
Adolf Hitler | Common link as an object of academic study inside fiction: DeLillo's White Noise (Professor Gladney's "Hitler studies") and Running Dog, and W. D. Snodgrass's The Führer Bunker.
Helen of Troy | Mythological common link: H.D.'s "Helen" and "Helen in Egypt," Poe's "To Helen," Hart Crane's "For the Marriage of Faustus and Helen," and W. C. Williams's "Asphodel, That Greeny Flower."
The New Yorker | The magazine as an answerline: it published "The Lottery" to record hate mail, ran Salinger, Cheever, and Updike's stories, devoted an issue to Hersey's "Hiroshima," and employed Parker and Thurber.
Esquire | Common link: published Raymond Carver's minimalist stories under editor Gordon Lish, ran Fitzgerald's "The Crack-Up" essays, and printed Mailer's and Gay Talese's New Journalism pieces.
Harold Bloom | Yale literary critic of The Anxiety of Influence and The Western Canon; appears both as a direct answerline and as a book characters read in campus fiction.
United States Poet Laureate | Institution/prize common link: the Library of Congress post held by Hayden (first Black laureate), Harjo (first Native American), and recent holders Limón and Sze.
Chinese-American | Common link on the ethnicity of Amy Tan, Maxine Hong Kingston, David Henry Hwang, and Frank Chin, whose works explore immigrant identity and generational conflict.
Jesus Christ | Common-link religious figure: nativity and Passion plays, Adrienne Kennedy's Funnyhouse of a Negro, Lew Wallace's Ben-Hur.
Euclid | Ancient Greek geometer invoked in Edna St. Vincent Millay's sonnet "Euclid alone has looked on Beauty bare" and a Vachel Lindsay poem about him.
Marilyn Monroe | Film icon and third wife of Arthur Miller; recurs as a biographical clue attached to his plays.
Rosenberg | Surname of executed Cold War spies Julius and Ethel; their ghosts haunt Roy Cohn in Angels in America, and their execution opens Plath's The Bell Jar.
Iraq War | Backdrop for veteran drama and fiction: Hudes's Water by the Spoonful, Joseph's Bengal Tiger at the Baghdad Zoo, Klay's Redeployment, Powers's The Yellow Birds.
slaves | Common link on enslavement: Jacobs-Jenkins's An Octoroon, Harris's Slave Play, August Wilson's Joe Turner's Come and Gone, Butler's Kindred.
Harvard University | Setting or alma mater across fiction and essay: Quentin Compson's college, Emerson's "American Scholar" address, poets Tracy K. Smith and Natasha Trethewey.
The Atlantic | Boston abolitionist magazine first edited by James Russell Lowell; published Howe's "Battle Hymn of the Republic" and Coates's "The Case for Reparations."
newspapers | Common-link industry: Ida B. Wells's and Nellie Bly's journalism, West's Miss Lonelyhearts advice column, Jefferson's "newspapers without government" line.
Helen Vendler | Influential American poetry critic; her readings of Dickinson, Plath, and other poets recur as biographical or critical clues.
The New York Times | Newspaper of record clued via its crossword, style magazine, and the viral 2021 article "Who Is the Bad Art Friend?"
The Paris Review | Literary magazine whose "Writers at Work"/"Art of Fiction" interview series and 2017 editorial scandal are common-link subjects.
assassination of Abraham Lincoln | Elegized in Whitman's "O Captain! My Captain!" and "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd"; dramatized in Parks's *Topdog/Underdog* and *Oh, Mary!*.
Captain William Kidd | Executed pirate whose buried-treasure legend inspires Irving's "The Devil and Tom Walker" and Poe's "The Gold-Bug."
The Vietnam War | Common-link conflict spanning O'Brien's *The Things They Carried*, Denis Johnson's "Emergency," Rabe's war-plays trilogy, and Lauro's *A Piece of My Heart*.
1980s | Common-link AIDS-crisis decade of Kushner's *Angels in America*, Kramer's *The Normal Heart*, Wasserstein's *The Heidi Chronicles*, Henley's *Crimes of the Heart*.
jazz | Common-link art form in Morrison's *Jazz*, Petry's *The Street*, Walker's *The Color Purple*, and Ellison's essay collection *Living with Music*.
Bob Dylan | Nobel Prize-winning singer-songwriter; clued via his Beat-influenced lyrics, "Brownsville Girl" co-write with Sam Shepard, and the 2016 Literature Prize controversy.
Frank Sinatra | Singer profiled without an interview in Gay Talese's "Frank Sinatra Has a Cold," a founding text of New Journalism.
Elaine Showalter | American feminist critic who coined "gynocriticism"; author of *A Literature of Their Own* and *The Female Malady*.
The Atlantic Monthly | Boston magazine that published Rebecca Harding Davis, Charles Chesnutt, and Julia Ward Howe's "Battle Hymn of the Republic."
Native Americans | Ethnic group of authors Sherman Alexie, N. Scott Momaday, and Joy Harjo, and subject of Longfellow's "Song of Hiawatha."
the American Civil War | Backdrop for Melville's Battle-Pieces, Howe's "Battle Hymn," Bierce's war stories, and plays on Lincoln's assassination.
lynching | Subject of poems by Claude McKay and Countee Cullen and stories by Richard Wright and James Baldwin ("Going to Meet the Man").
Scopes Monkey Trial | 1925 Tennessee evolution trial covered by H.L. Mencken, fictionalized in Lawrence and Lee's Inherit the Wind.
The Great Depression | Setting of Steinbeck's Of Mice and Men and The Grapes of Wrath, Odets's plays, and Kennedy's Ironweed.
Spanish Civil War | Conflict behind Hemingway's For Whom the Bell Tolls and Orwell's Homage to Catalonia; killed poet Federico García Lorca.
the Holocaust | Subject of Wiesel's memoir Night, Spiegelman's Maus, and Foer's Everything Is Illuminated.
Leon Trotsky | Assassinated Soviet revolutionary dramatized in David Ives's All in the Timing ("Variations on the Death of Trotsky").
September 11 attacks | Subject of DeLillo's Falling Man, Foer's Extremely Loud and Incredibly Close, and Pynchon's Bleeding Edge.
University of Iowa | Home of the Iowa Writers' Workshop, long directed by Paul Engle; faculty have included Vonnegut, Carver, and Robinson.

# Words, Names, and Things
> Bare first names, letters, surnames, and everyday objects, animals, and concepts that quizbowl turns into cross-author common links.
John | First-name common link spanning many authors and characters named John; answerlines this bare usually come from questions accepting any "John," so treat it as a grab-bag rather than one canonical topic.
Robert | First-name common link — Frost, Lowell, Penn Warren, and Hayden among poets alone; like "John," it reflects shared-name common-link questions rather than a single canonical answer.
James | As a bare answerline this has usually meant Henry James via surname-accepting clues, but since 2024 it may also be Percival Everett's Booker-winning novel James — check the clues.
Philip | First-name common link: Philip Roth and the colonial "poet of the Revolution" Philip Freneau are the most frequent referents; check clues to distinguish.
Eugene | First-name common link, almost always the playwright Eugene O'Neill (Long Day's Journey into Night, Mourning Becomes Electra) rather than any other Eugene.
Lowell | Surname shared by the Imagist poet Amy, the Confessional poet Robert ("For the Union Dead," "Skunk Hour"), and their ancestor James Russell Lowell.
Brown | Surname common link: rival "first American novel" claimants William Hill Brown and Charles Brockden Brown, plus Sterling Brown, Jericho Brown, and William Wells Brown's Clotel.
Wright | Surname common link: Richard Wright (Native Son, Black Boy) alongside the unrelated poets James and Charles Wright.
Crane | Surname common link: the poets Hart Crane (The Bridge, "Chaplinesque") and Stephen Crane (The Red Badge of Courage, "In the Desert").
e | Shared-initial common link: e. e. cummings's lowercase signature, E. L. Doctorow (Ragtime, City of God), and E. B. White (Charlotte's Web, The Elements of Style).
American | Adjective common link: Roth's American Pastoral, Mencken's The American Language, Hayden's collection American Journal, and Rankine's "American Lyric" subtitles.
Alice Adams | Name shared by two unrelated entities: the small-town title heroine of Booth Tarkington's Pulitzer-winning novel Alice Adams, and the short-story author Alice Adams (Superior Women).
Death | Personified common link: Dickinson's kindly stopping carriage driver, Whitman's dark mother in "Lilacs," and Fences' Troy Maxson wrestling Death, plus countless title clues.
autobiography | Genre common link: Franklin's Autobiography, The Autobiography of Malcolm X, Stein's The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas, and Johnson's The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man.
baseball | Common link: Malamud's The Natural, Thayer's "Casey at the Bat," the Polo Grounds prologue of DeLillo's Underworld, Roth's The Great American Novel, and Kinsella's Shoeless Joe.
boxing | Common link: Hemingway's "The Killers" and The Sun Also Rises, Odets's Golden Boy, Lipsyte's The Contender, and the boxing writing of Liebling and Joyce Carol Oates.
alcoholism | Common link: O'Neill's The Iceman Cometh, Fitzgerald's Tender Is the Night, Jack London's John Barleycorn, Charles Jackson's The Lost Weekend, and Carver's recovery stories.
bars | Common link: the "End of the Line Cafe" of O'Neill's The Iceman Cometh, Auden's "one of the dives / On Fifty-Second Street," and Lynn Nottage's Sweat.
teachers | Common link: Hellman's The Children's Hour, Irving's Ichabod Crane, Ernest Gaines's Grant Wiggins, and Strout's Olive Kitteridge.
soldiers | Common link: Dos Passos's Newsreel sections, the returning Native veterans of Silko's Ceremony and Momaday's House Made of Dawn, and O'Brien's The Things They Carried.
letters | Common link: Plath's posthumous Letters Home, Barth's epistolary novel LETTERS, Poe's "The Purloined Letter," and Robinson's epistolary Gilead.
birds | Common link: Poe's raven, Dickinson's "Hope is the thing with feathers," Jewett's white heron, the caged bird of Dunbar and Angelou, and Whitman's mockingbird.
bees | Common link: Dickinson's tipsy bees, Plath's late "Bee" sequence in Ariel, Whittier's "Telling the Bees," and Sue Monk Kidd's The Secret Life of Bees.
hawks | Common link: Robinson Jeffers's "Hurt Hawks" and his inhumanist creed ("I'd sooner... kill a man than a hawk"), and a companion James Wright poem.
dogs | Common link: Pynchon's recurring talking dogs — Vineland's epigraph, Mason & Dixon's "Learned English Dog," and Against the Day's Pugnax.
trees | Common link: Frost's "Birches," Kilmer's "Trees," Betty Smith's A Tree Grows in Brooklyn, and the chokecherry tree of Morrison's Beloved.
plums | Object common link: William Carlos Williams's "This Is Just to Say," the icebox note that became an internet meme, and its companion poem "To a Poor Old Woman."
snow | Common link: Frost's "Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening," Whittier's "Snow-Bound," Longfellow's "The Cross of Snow," and Hemingway's "The Snows of Kilimanjaro."
rain | Common link: Bradbury's "There Will Come Soft Rains" and "The Long Rain," Hemingway's A Farewell to Arms, and the thunder's rain in Eliot's The Waste Land.
fire | Common link: Baldwin's The Fire Next Time, the one-issue Harlem Renaissance magazine Fire!!, and the purifying fire of Eliot's "Little Gidding."
summer | Common link: Tennessee Williams's Suddenly Last Summer and Summer and Smoke, Wharton's Summer, and Bradbury's Dandelion Wine.
dreams | Common link: Hughes's "Harlem" ("What happens to a dream deferred?") and his Montage of a Dream Deferred, and Berryman's Dream Songs.
hope | Common link: Dickinson's "'Hope' is the thing with feathers," and O'Neill's Harry Hope, who runs the bar in The Iceman Cometh.
love | Common link: Carver's "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love," Auden's "O Tell Me the Truth About Love," and Erdrich's Love Medicine.
ghosts | Common link: Morrison's Beloved, Henry James's The Turn of the Screw, and Roth's Exit Ghost.

singing | Common link: Whitman's "I Hear America Singing" and "Song of Myself," Hughes's "I, Too, Sing America," and Dunbar's "Sympathy" (the caged bird "sings").
blue | Common link: Didion's Blue Nights, Maggie Nelson's Bluets, Morrison's The Bluest Eye, and the fading blue eyes of the Doctor T. J. Eckleburg billboard in Gatsby.
eyes | Common link: the billboard eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg in The Great Gatsby and the title organ of Hurston's Their Eyes Were Watching God.

George | Common-link first name: George in Albee's Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf, husband of Martha, who taunts guests Nick and Honey with an invented dead son.

Henry | Common-link first name: John Berryman's put-upon alter ego "Henry," addressed as "Mr. Bones," across The Dream Songs.
Richard | Common-link first name: poets Richard Wilbur, Richard Eberhart, and Richard Siken; also novelist Richard Brautigan of Trout Fishing in America.

Harriet | Common-link first name: Harriet Beecher Stowe (Uncle Tom's Cabin), Harriet Jacobs (Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl), and Harriet Wilson, the first Black novelist published in the US.

names | Common-link motif of naming: DeLillo's novel The Names, Lahiri's The Namesake, Adam naming the animals in Genesis.

abortion | Common-link procedure in Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants," Irving's The Cider House Rules, and Hansberry's A Raisin in the Sun.
