American Literature questions span every form the category recognizes — lyric and narrative poetry, the novel and short story, drama, and a native tradition of essayistic nonfiction — and quizbowl organizes almost all of it into the same handful of movements that literary history uses: a colonial beginning, a nineteenth-century flowering split between prose romance and poetry, a realist-to-modernist turn, and a broad postwar field that fragments into competing schools of fiction, poetry, and drama. Emily Dickinson tops the frequency table outright, and poets crowd the upper ranks alongside novelists and playwrights to a degree unusual among national literatures, which is why poetry gets treated as its own recurring thread rather than folded into prose-era sections.

The colonial and early national period is thin but distinctive: Puritan devotional poets Anne Bradstreet and Edward Taylor, the enslaved poet Phillis Wheatley, Benjamin Franklin's autobiographical persona, and the early tales of Washington Irving ("Rip Van Winkle," "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow") and James Fenimore Cooper's Leatherstocking novels (Natty Bumppo, The Last of the Mohicans) carry the era. Transcendentalism supplies the philosophical core of the mid-century — Emerson's essays and Thoreau's Walden and "Civil Disobedience" — while the American Renaissance proper is a fiction cluster answering to Hawthorne (The Scarlet Letter, "Young Goodman Brown," "Rappaccini's Daughter") and Melville (Moby-Dick, "Bartleby, the Scrivener," Billy Budd). Nineteenth-century poetry runs in parallel: Whitman's Leaves of Grass and Dickinson's lyrics anchor it, Poe supplies both verse ("The Raven") and a separate strand of Gothic and detective short fiction, and the fireside poets — Longfellow, Whittier, Bryant — round out the schoolroom canon.

Late-century realism (Twain, Henry James, Howells), regionalism (Kate Chopin's The Awakening, Sarah Orne Jewett, Bret Harte's local color), and naturalism (Stephen Crane, Frank Norris, Theodore Dreiser, Jack London) mark literature's turn toward documentary detail and social determinism, setting up the modernist rupture. Modernist fiction is the Lost Generation's territory — Fitzgerald's Gatsby and Tender Is the Night, Hemingway's spare Paris-and-war novels, Gertrude Stein's expatriate salon, Sherwood Anderson's Winesburg, Ohio — while modernist poetry answers to a denser, more technical set of names: Eliot and Pound as the movement's theorists, Wallace Stevens and William Carlos Williams as its most-asked American-based figures, plus Marianne Moore, H.D. and Imagism, and Hart Crane's The Bridge.

Two lineages cut across these eras rather than sitting inside a single decade. Southern literature is a self-contained regional canon running from Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha novels through Flannery O'Connor's grotesque short stories, Robert Penn Warren, Eudora Welty, Carson McCullers, and — as a later inheritor of the mode — Cormac McCarthy. African American literature forms its own continuous arc: Wheatley and Frederick Douglass's slave narrative at one end, Du Bois and Dunbar bridging to the Harlem Renaissance of Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, and Jean Toomer, and then a postwar continuation — Ellison's Invisible Man, Baldwin, Richard Wright, Toni Morrison, Alice Walker, Gwendolyn Brooks, Maya Angelou — that is asked about as heavily as any other twentieth-century cluster.

American drama is unusually prominent for a national literature, holding its own slot across O'Neill, Tennessee Williams, Arthur Miller, Albee, Lorraine Hansberry, and August Wilson, plus individually-answered plays. Postwar poetry splits into the confessional line (Lowell, Plath, Sexton, Berryman) and the Beats (Ginsberg's "Howl," Kerouac's On the Road), while postwar fiction (1945–1980) runs from Salinger and Bellow through Vonnegut, Pynchon, Updike, and Roth, and contemporary fiction since 1980 continues it through DeLillo, David Foster Wallace, Jhumpa Lahiri, and Colson Whitehead. A genre-fiction strand — Poe's detective tales at its root, then Bradbury, Le Guin, and Lovecraft in science fiction and horror, Chandler in hardboiled crime — sits alongside the literary mainstream. Finally, a large body of common-link material recurs across all of these writers: places (New York City, California, Mississippi, Harlem), historical backdrops (the Civil War, the World Wars, Vietnam), bare first names shared by multiple characters, and the occasional non-American author (Tolstoy, Kafka, Borges) who surfaces because an American work engages with them directly.
