# American Literature overview — section skeleton (fixed; do not invent new sections)

1. Colonial and Early National Literature
   Pre-1830 writing: Puritans (Bradstreet, Edwards, Mather), Franklin, Irving,
   Cooper, Freneau, Charles Brockden Brown, early republic figures.
2. Transcendentalism and the American Renaissance
   Emerson, Thoreau, Fuller, Hawthorne, Melville, Poe, Alcott; the answerline
   "Transcendentalism" itself goes here.
3. Nineteenth-Century Poetry
   Whitman, Dickinson, and the Fireside Poets (Longfellow, Bryant, Whittier,
   Holmes, James Russell Lowell), Lazarus, Hiawatha etc.
4. Realism, Regionalism, and Naturalism
   Twain, Henry James, Howells, Wharton, Stephen Crane, Chopin, Jewett, Harte,
   Bierce, Gilman, Dreiser, Jack London, Upton Sinclair, O. Henry, Cather,
   Harriet Beecher Stowe / Uncle Tom's Cabin.
5. Modernist Fiction and the Lost Generation
   Hemingway, Fitzgerald, Stein, Dos Passos, Sherwood Anderson, Sinclair Lewis,
   Steinbeck, Nathanael West, Thornton Wilder's novels (his plays -> 10).
6. Modernist Poetry
   Pound, T. S. Eliot, Stevens, William Carlos Williams, Frost, Moore, Millay,
   cummings, H.D., Hart Crane, E. A. Robinson, Sandburg, Lindsay, Masters,
   Amy Lowell, Auden; "Imagism" goes here.
7. Southern Literature
   Faulkner, Flannery O'Connor, Welty, Robert Penn Warren, McCullers,
   Katherine Anne Porter, Walker Percy, Styron, Toole, Capote.
8. African American Literature: Wheatley to the Harlem Renaissance
   Wheatley, Douglass, slave narratives, Chesnutt, Dunbar, Du Bois,
   James Weldon Johnson, Hughes, Hurston, McKay, Cullen, Toomer, Larsen;
   "Harlem Renaissance" itself goes here.
9. Postwar African American Literature
   Richard Wright, Ellison, Baldwin, Gwendolyn Brooks, Hayden, Morrison,
   Angelou, Alice Walker, Baraka, Lorde, Ishmael Reed, Octavia Butler,
   Colson Whitehead, Jesmyn Ward, Coates, Rankine.
10. American Drama
   ALL playwrights and plays, including African American drama: O'Neill,
   Wilder's plays, Odets, Hellman, Tennessee Williams, Arthur Miller, Albee,
   Hansberry, August Wilson, Mamet, Shepard, Kushner, Nottage, Suzan-Lori
   Parks, Hwang, Neil Simon, David Ives. Play characters too (e.g. Roy Cohn).
11. Postwar Poetry and the Beats
   Robert Lowell, Bishop, Plath, Sexton, Berryman, Jarrell, Schwartz, Roethke,
   "Confessional Poetry"; the Beats (Ginsberg, Kerouac incl. On the Road,
   Burroughs, Ferlinghetti, Snyder), O'Hara, Ashbery, Rich, Bukowski, Merrill,
   Wilbur, and later poets (Harjo, Oliver, Collins, Glück, Dove).
12. Postwar Fiction (1945-1980)
   Salinger, Bellow, Philip Roth, Updike, Cheever, Malamud, Mailer, Vonnegut,
   Heller, Kesey, Nabokov, Pynchon, Barth, Barthelme, Algren, Didion, Sontag,
   Tom Wolfe, New Journalism, Hunter S. Thompson.
13. Contemporary Fiction (1980-present)
   DeLillo, Cormac McCarthy, David Foster Wallace, Franzen, Chabon, Saunders,
   John Irving, Joyce Carol Oates, Amy Tan, Cisneros, Lahiri, Diaz, Alexie,
   Kincaid, and Canadian authors (Munro, Atwood) unless era dictates otherwise.
14. Genre Fiction: Science Fiction, Horror, and Crime
   Lovecraft, Bradbury, Asimov, Heinlein, Le Guin, Philip K. Dick, Stephen
   King, Shirley Jackson, Chandler, Hammett, Highsmith, Gibson.
15. Common-Link and Miscellaneous Answers
   True cross-era common-links only: letters ("e", "W"), bare first names
   (John, James, Robert, Philip), places clued across many works (New York
   City, California, Chicago, Harlem, Massachusetts...), things (birds, rain,
   plums, death, baseball), wars (American Civil War, World War I, Vietnam
   War), historical figures clued through literature (Lincoln, Washington,
   John Brown), foreign authors appearing via American-lit questions (Kafka,
   Shakespeare, Virginia Woolf, Walter Scott — CONSULT the questions first),
   magazines (The New Yorker, The Atlantic), Yiddish, autobiography.

Placement principles:
- Movements/genres belong with their era section, not in 15.
- A work, poem, story, or character is a NESTING CANDIDATE under its author
  when the author is also a curated answerline (e.g. Sula Peace -> Toni
  Morrison; Ishmael -> Herman Melville; "Howl" -> Allen Ginsberg).
- When era/section is genuinely arguable, prefer the section where the
  author's most-clued works sit; consult the captured questions when unsure.
