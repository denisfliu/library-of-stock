# Places in American Literature

New York | State setting of Irving's "Rip Van Winkle" and "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow," Cather's "Paul's Case," and Kushner's Angels in America.
Maryland | Setting of Barth's Chesapeake novels: The Sot-Weed Factor, The Floating Opera, The End of the Road, "Lost in the Funhouse."
Texas | Setting of Cormac McCarthy's Blood Meridian, All the Pretty Horses, and No Country for Old Men; also McMurtry's Lonesome Dove.
New Orleans, Louisiana | Setting of Walker Percy's The Moviegoer, Toole's A Confederacy of Dunces, and Algren's A Walk on the Wild Side.
Las Vegas | Setting of Hunter S. Thompson's Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas, chronicling Raoul Duke and Dr. Gonzo's drug-fueled trip.
San Francisco, California | Beat Generation hub (City Lights Bookstore); setting of Didion's "Slouching Towards Bethlehem" and Maupin's Tales of the City.
Oakland, California | Gertrude Stein's hometown ("there is no there there"), the title source for Tommy Orange's novel There There.
Puerto Rico | Homeland of Nuyorican and island-born playwrights: Piñero's Short Eyes, Hudes's Water by the Spoonful, and Lin-Manuel Miranda's heritage.
Scotland | Homeland of poets Robert Burns, Hugh MacDiarmid, and Edwin Muir, and novelists James Kelman and Irvine Welsh (Trainspotting).
Sweden | Premiere venue for O'Neill's Long Day's Journey Into Night; homeland of Selma Lagerlöf and Scandinavian figures in Crane's "The Blue Hotel."
Poland | Setting of Uris's Mila 18 and part of Wiesel's Night; homeland of poets Adam Mickiewicz and Zbigniew Herbert.

# History and Culture through Literature

The Atlantic Monthly | Boston magazine that published Rebecca Harding Davis, Charles Chesnutt, and Julia Ward Howe's "Battle Hymn of the Republic."
Native Americans | Ethnic group of authors Sherman Alexie, N. Scott Momaday, and Joy Harjo, and subject of Longfellow's "Song of Hiawatha."
the American Civil War | Backdrop for Melville's Battle-Pieces, Howe's "Battle Hymn," Bierce's war stories, and plays on Lincoln's assassination.
lynching | Subject of poems by Claude McKay and Countee Cullen and stories by Richard Wright and James Baldwin ("Going to Meet the Man").
Scopes Monkey Trial | 1925 Tennessee evolution trial covered by H.L. Mencken, fictionalized in Lawrence and Lee's Inherit the Wind.
The Great Depression | Setting of Steinbeck's Of Mice and Men and The Grapes of Wrath, Odets's plays, and Kennedy's Ironweed.
Spanish Civil War | Conflict behind Hemingway's For Whom the Bell Tolls and Orwell's Homage to Catalonia; killed poet Federico García Lorca.
the Holocaust | Subject of Wiesel's memoir Night, Spiegelman's Maus, and Foer's Everything Is Illuminated.
Leon Trotsky | Assassinated Soviet revolutionary dramatized in David Ives's All in the Timing ("Variations on the Death of Trotsky").
September 11 attacks | Subject of DeLillo's Falling Man, Foer's Extremely Loud and Incredibly Close, and Pynchon's Bleeding Edge.
University of Iowa | Home of the Iowa Writers' Workshop, long directed by Paul Engle; faculty have included Vonnegut, Carver, and Robinson.

# Words, Names, and Things

Lillian | Bare first name for playwright Lillian Hellman (The Children's Hour, The Little Foxes), including her feud with Mary McCarthy.
Louise | Bare first name shared by poets Louise Bogan and Louise Glück (Nobel laureate, "Mock Orange," The Wild Iris).
Mary | Bare first name for novelist Mary McCarthy (The Group), story writer Mary Gaitskill (Bad Behavior), and O'Neill's Mary Tyrone.
Norman | Bare first name for New Journalism author Norman Mailer, of The Naked and the Dead and The Executioner's Song.
Quentin | Bare first name shared by Faulkner's doomed Quentin Compson (The Sound and the Fury) and the narrator of Miller's After the Fall.
Raymond | Bare first name for minimalist story writer Raymond Carver (Cathedral, What We Talk About When We Talk About Love).
Thomas | Bare first name for Thomas Dixon (The Clansman) and reclusive novelist Thomas Pynchon (Gravity's Rainbow, The Crying of Lot 49).
McCarthy | Surname spanning novelist Cormac McCarthy (Blood Meridian), Mary McCarthy (The Group), and Senator Joseph McCarthy's HUAC hearings.
Saroyan | Surname shared by Armenian-American playwright William Saroyan (The Time of Your Life) and his poet son Aram.
Smith | Surname spanning poets Tracy K. Smith (Life on Mars), Danez Smith, Patricia Smith, and novelist Zadie Smith (On Beauty).
Williams | Surname spanning poet William Carlos Williams, playwright Tennessee Williams, and novelists John A. Williams and John Williams (Stoner).
Wolfe | Surname spanning novelists Thomas Wolfe (Look Homeward, Angel), Tom Wolfe (The Right Stuff), and Tobias Wolff.
Mother | Addressee of Ocean Vuong's poetry, Emma Lazarus's "Mother of Exiles," and title figure of Marsha Norman's 'night, Mother.
mothers | Recurring figure in poems by Gwendolyn Brooks and Langston Hughes ("Mother to Son"), and Dudley Randall's "Ballad of Birmingham."
nun | Profession of characters in Shanley's Doubt, Durang's Sister Mary Ignatius Explains It All for You, and DeLillo's White Noise.
opera singer | Profession of Thea Kronborg in Cather's The Song of the Lark and Roxanne Coss in Patchett's Bel Canto.
painters | Subject of Frank O'Hara's "Why I Am Not a Painter" and poems by Ferlinghetti and Rukeyser invoking painters like Goya.
salesman = traveling salesman -> Traveling Salesman | Profession of Willy Loman (Death of a Salesman) and R.J. Bowman in Eudora Welty's stories.
spy | Profession of the narrators in Chang-rae Lee's Native Speaker and Viet Thanh Nguyen's The Sympathizer.
playing baseball | Activity in Malamud's The Natural (Roy Hobbs), Irving's A Prayer for Owen Meany, and Chaim Potok's The Chosen.
reading | Subject of Nabokov's Cornell lectures on literature and reader-response theories (Stanley Fish, Viktor Shklovsky, Coleridge).
swimming | Activity in Cheever's "The Swimmer" (Neddy Merrill), Yates's Revolutionary Road, and Knowles's A Separate Peace.
red | Color in John Logan's play Red (about Mark Rothko), Crane's The Red Badge of Courage, and Williams's "The Red Wheelbarrow."
yellow | Color in Charlotte Perkins Gilman's "The Yellow Wallpaper" and Stephen Crane's "The Bride Comes to Yellow Sky."
rose | Flower in Hawthorne's "Dr. Heidegger's Experiment," Stein's "Rose is a rose is a rose," and Williams's The Rose Tattoo.
roses | Recurring image in Stein's "Sacred Emily," Ocean Vuong's mother's chosen name Rose, and Tupac's "The Rose That Grew from Concrete."
masks | Prop in Eugene O'Neill's The Great God Brown and Mourning Becomes Electra, dramatizing divided consciousness.
memory | Genre term for Tennessee Williams's "memory play" The Glass Menagerie; also the classical mnemonic art, ars memoriae.
moon | Motif in poems by Amy Lowell, Li Bai ("Drinking Alone by Moonlight"), and Sidney's Astrophel and Stella.
night | Time-of-day motif in poems by Mark Strand and Li Bai; also titles Elie Wiesel's Holocaust memoir Night.
stars | Motif in Whitman's "When I Heard the Learn'd Astronomer" and "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd"; closing word of each Divine Comedy cantica.
the sea | Motif in Whitman's "Out of the Cradle Endlessly Rocking," H.D.'s "Oread," and Derek Walcott's Caribbean poetry.
tiger | Animal in Maxine Hong Kingston's The Woman Warrior and Amy Chua's Battle Hymn of the Tiger Mother.
tigers | Animal in Brautigan's In Watermelon Sugar, Adrienne Rich's "Aunt Jennifer's Tigers," and Blake's "The Tyger."
salt | Substance titling Highsmith's The Price of Salt (basis for the film Carol) and Monique Truong's The Book of Salt.
kings | Word in the titles of Bellow's Henderson the Rain King and Hijuelos's The Mambo Kings Play Songs of Love.
paradise | Word titling Toni Morrison's Paradise, Fitzgerald's This Side of Paradise, and Yanagihara's To Paradise.
theme parks | Setting of George Saunders's "Pastoralia" and CivilWarLand in Bad Decline, and Karen Russell's Swamplandia!.
trial | Courtroom setting of Inherit the Wind (the Scopes trial) and Reginald Rose's Twelve Angry Men.
weird | Genre descriptor for H.P. Lovecraft's Weird Tales stories and the "New Weird" movement (China Miéville, the VanderMeers).
women | Subject of feminist speculative fiction: Tiptree's "The Screwfly Solution," Piercy's Woman on the Edge of Time, Atwood's The Handmaid's Tale.
zoos | Setting of Edward Albee's The Zoo Story (and At Home at the Zoo) and Rajiv Joseph's Bengal Tiger at the Baghdad Zoo.

# Foreign Voices in American Literature Questions

Sappho = Sappho of Lesbos | Ancient Greek lyric poet of Lesbos ("Ode to Aphrodite"); Anne Carson's translations (If Not, Winter) recur in clues.
Sophocles | Ancient Greek tragedian of Oedipus Rex, Oedipus at Colonus, and Antigone.
Medea | Euripides' vengeful sorceress who kills her children; echoed in Cherríe Moraga's The Hungry Woman and Jesmyn Ward's Salvage the Bones.
Scheherazade | Arabian Nights storyteller who delays her execution with tales; referenced in Barth's Chimera and Murakami's stories.
Li Bai | Tang Dynasty Chinese poet of "Quiet Night Thoughts" and "Drinking Alone by Moonlight," rendered by Ezra Pound as "The River Merchant's Wife."
The Divine Comedy | Dante's 14th-century epic of Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise; inspired Longfellow's translation and sonnet "Mezzo Cammin."
King Arthur | British legendary king retold by Steinbeck in his unfinished The Acts of King Arthur and His Noble Knights.
The Tempest | Shakespeare's late romance of Prospero, Ariel, and Caliban; adapted postcolonially by Aimé Césaire, echoed in Hayden's "Middle Passage."
Samuel Johnson | 18th-century British lexicographer and essayist, subject of Boswell's biography, author of A Dictionary of the English Language.
Walter Scott | Scottish novelist of Waverley, Ivanhoe, and The Bride of Lammermoor; Twain blamed him for romanticizing the antebellum South.
Robert Browning | Victorian British poet of dramatic monologues ("Porphyria's Lover," "My Last Duchess"); influenced Ezra Pound's Personae.
The Hound of the Baskervilles | Arthur Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes novel of the phosphorus-doused hound haunting Dartmoor.
Saki | Pen name of British writer H.H. Munro, author of "The Open Window," "Sredni Vashtar," and the Reginald stories.
Rabindranath Tagore | Bengali Nobel laureate poet of Gitanjali; wrote India's and Bangladesh's national anthems, championed by Yeats.
Katherine Mansfield | New Zealand-born short story writer of "The Garden Party," "Bliss," and "Miss Brill."
Lu Xun | Chinese author of "Diary of a Madman" and The True Story of Ah Q, leading figure of the May Fourth Movement.
Louis-Ferdinand Celine -> Louis-Ferdinand Céline | French novelist of Journey to the End of the Night, notorious Vichy collaborator; alter ego Bardamu.
The Foundation Pit | Andrei Platonov's Soviet novel of workers digging a foundation for a "House of the Proletariat" amid collectivization.
Robert Graves | British poet-memoirist of Good-Bye to All That and I, Claudius; author of the mythography The White Goddess.
Samuel Beckett | Irish playwright of Waiting for Godot, Endgame, and Happy Days; wrote in French and English.
The Homecoming | Harold Pinter's play of Teddy bringing wife Ruth to his London family; also the first play of O'Neill's Mourning Becomes Electra.
Under Milk Wood | Dylan Thomas's Welsh radio play of the town Llareggub, narrated by Captain Cat and preacher-poet Eli Jenkins.
Watchmen | Alan Moore's graphic novel deconstructing superheroes (Rorschach, Dr. Manhattan, Ozymandias) amid Cold War paranoia.
Salman Rushdie | British-Indian novelist of Midnight's Children and The Satanic Verses, subject of a fatwa; memoir Joseph Anton.
The God of Small Things | Arundhati Roy's Booker-winning debut novel of twins Estha and Rahel in Kerala, India.
