# Southern Literature
> The South's Gothic decay and grotesque grace, from Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha and O'Connor's dark parables through the Fugitive-Agrarian critics to Capote, Percy, and Styron.

William Faulkner = William Cuthbert Faulkner | Nobel laureate of fictional Yoknapatawpha County: The Sound and the Fury, As I Lay Dying, Absalom, Absalom!, Light in August, and "A Rose for Emily."
- The Sound and the Fury | Compson family novel in four sections — Benjy's, Quentin's (ending in suicide at Harvard), Jason's, and Dilsey's; the title comes from Macbeth.
- Compson family | Yoknapatawpha family whose decline is chronicled across The Sound and the Fury's four narrators: Quentin, Caddy, Jason, and Benjy.
- Benjy Compson | Mentally disabled Compson sibling whose nonlinear, achronological section opens The Sound and the Fury.
- Caddy Compson | Compson sister whose muddy drawers and later promiscuity haunt her brothers Quentin and Benjy throughout The Sound and the Fury.
- Quentin Compson | Compson son who narrates a section of The Sound and the Fury before drowning himself, and reconstructs Sutpen's story in Absalom, Absalom!
- As I Lay Dying | Bundren family's multi-narrator journey to bury matriarch Addie in Jefferson; Darl burns a barn and is committed to an asylum.
- Sanctuary | Temple Drake is abducted and raped with a corncob by the impotent gangster Popeye, then perjures herself at Lee Goodwin's trial.
- Requiem for a Nun | Sequel to Sanctuary in which Temple Stevens's nurse Nancy Mannigoe kills her infant daughter to save the family.
- Light in August | Joe Christmas, a man of ambiguous race, is castrated and killed by Percy Grimm while Lena Grove searches for her child's father.
- Absalom, Absalom! | Thomas Sutpen's ruthless design to found a dynasty collapses when son Henry kills half-brother Charles Bon; reconstructed by Quentin and Shreve.
- Thomas Sutpen | Poor white arriviste whose dynastic "design" in Absalom, Absalom! unravels over his sons Henry and the part-Black Charles Bon.
- "Why do you hate the South?" | Shreve McCannon's question to Quentin Compson closing Absalom, Absalom!, answered by Quentin's repeated "I don't hate it."
- Go Down, Moses | Story collection following the McCaslin family across generations, including Ike McCaslin's repudiation of his inheritance and the hunt for Old Ben.
- "The Bear" | Ike McCaslin, guided by Sam Fathers and the dog Lion, hunts the legendary bear Old Ben; collected in Go Down, Moses.
- Intruder in the Dust | Lucas Beauchamp, falsely accused of murder, is saved from a lynch mob by teenager Chick Mallison's investigation.
- "A Rose for Emily" | Emily Grierson keeps the poisoned corpse of her lover Homer Barron in her bed for decades; narrated by the town's collective "we."
- "Barn Burning" | Young Sarty Snopes must decide whether to warn a family that his arsonist father Abner Snopes plans to burn their barn.
- "Dry September" | Miss Minnie Cooper's rumored assault leads a mob to lynch Will Mayes despite barber Henry Hawkshaw's attempt to stop it.

Flannery O'Connor | Georgia Catholic master of the grotesque: "A Good Man Is Hard to Find" with The Misfit, "Good Country People," Wise Blood, and The Violent Bear It Away.
- "A Good Man is Hard to Find" -> "A Good Man Is Hard to Find" | Title story of O'Connor's debut collection; a family's Florida road trip ends when the escaped convict Misfit murders them one by one.
- the grandmother | Unnamed protagonist of "A Good Man Is Hard to Find" whose lie about a hidden plantation causes the wreck that delivers her family to the Misfit.
- The Misfit | Escaped convict who muses on Jesus and grace before shooting the grandmother and her family in "A Good Man Is Hard to Find."
- "Everything That Rises Must Converge" = Everything that Rises Must Converge | Julian's racist mother collapses after a Black woman wearing her same hat strikes her at a bus stop; title from Teilhard de Chardin.
- "Revelation" | Ruby Turpin is called a "wart hog from hell" by the enraged Mary Grace in a doctor's waiting room, prompting a vision of ascending souls.
- "Parker's Back" | O.E. Parker covers his back with a Byzantine Christ tattoo to please his fundamentalist wife Sarah Ruth, who beats him for it.
- "The Life You Save May Be Your Own" | Drifter Tom T. Shiftlet marries, then abandons, the deaf-mute Lucynell Crater for a car.
- Wise Blood | Hazel Motes preaches a nihilistic "Church Without Christ" before blinding himself in penance.
- The Violent Bear It Away | Young Francis Marion Tarwater resists his prophetic calling to baptize his cousin Bishop, whom he ultimately drowns.

Carson McCullers | Georgia novelist of lonely misfits: The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter, The Member of the Wedding with Frankie Addams, and The Ballad of the Sad Café.
- The Heart is a Lonely Hunter -> The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter | McCullers's debut about deaf-mute John Singer, confidant to Mick Kelly, Dr. Copeland, Jake Blount, and Biff Brannon; Singer shoots himself after his friend Antonapoulos dies.
- The Ballad of the Sad Cafe -> The Ballad of the Sad Café | Love triangle in a rural Georgia café between the giant Miss Amelia Evans, hunchbacked Cousin Lymon, and her ex-husband Marvin Macy.
- The Member of the Wedding | Twelve-year-old Frankie Addams, renaming herself F. Jasmine, longs to join her brother's wedding, confiding in cook Berenice Sadie Brown.

Eudora Welty | Mississippi story writer: "A Worn Path" with Phoenix Jackson, "Why I Live at the P.O.," the Pulitzer-winning The Optimist's Daughter, and the memoir One Writer's Beginnings.
- "A Worn Path" | Elderly Phoenix Jackson walks a long path into Natchez to fetch throat medicine for her grandson.
- "Why I Live at the P.O." | Unnamed narrator's comic feud with her sister Stella-Rondo drives her to move into the small-town post office.
- The Optimist's Daughter | Laurel McKelva returns to Mississippi after her father Judge McKelva's death and clashes with his young second wife, Fay; won the Pulitzer.
- The Robber Bridegroom | Fairy-tale-style novella of planter's daughter Rosamond and the bandit Jamie Lockhart in frontier Natchez Trace Mississippi.

Robert Penn Warren | The only writer with Pulitzers in both fiction and poetry, and the first official U.S. Poet Laureate; All the King's Men and the Fugitive-Agrarian circle supply the clues.
- All the King's Men | Louisiana demagogue Willie Stark's rise and fall, narrated by aide Jack Burden, whose historical digging drives Judge Irwin to suicide; loosely based on Huey Long.
- Willie Stark | Huey Long-style Louisiana governor whose corrupt rise and assassination are narrated by aide Jack Burden in All the King's Men.

Allen Tate | Fugitive-Agrarian poet and critic married to Caroline Gordon; best known for his meditation on Confederate war dead outside a cemetery gate.
- "Ode to the Confederate Dead" | Speaker standing at a Confederate cemetery gate wrestles with modern disconnection from the heroic, mythologized Southern past.

John Crowe Ransom | Fugitive-Agrarian poet-critic at Vanderbilt whose textbook The New Criticism named that movement; mentored fellow Fugitives Allen Tate and Robert Penn Warren.

New Criticism | Mid-20th-century critical movement championing close reading of texts in isolation from biography; named by Ransom, joined by Cleanth Brooks and W.K. Wimsatt's "intentional fallacy."

Katherine Anne Porter | Texas-born writer of Pale Horse, Pale Rider, "The Jilting of Granny Weatherall," the Miranda stories, and the allegorical ocean-liner novel Ship of Fools.
- Pale Horse, Pale Rider | Newspaperwoman Miranda nearly dies of influenza during World War I while her soldier lover Adam Barclay dies of the same pandemic.

Truman Capote | Author of the "nonfiction novel" In Cold Blood, about the Clutter family murders, plus Breakfast at Tiffany's with Holly Golightly and Other Voices, Other Rooms.
- Breakfast at Tiffany's | Novella narrated by an unnamed writer about his glamorous, husband-hunting neighbor's escapades in 1940s New York.
- In Cold Blood | "Nonfiction novel" reconstructing killers Perry Smith and Dick Hickock's 1959 murder of the Clutter family in Holcomb, Kansas.
- Holly Golightly | Café-society "American geisha" who befriends the narrator of Breakfast at Tiffany's while chasing a rich husband.

A Confederacy of Dunces | John Kennedy Toole's posthumous Pulitzer-winning New Orleans comedy starring hot-dog vendor Ignatius J. Reilly, devotee of Boethius; published through Walker Percy's advocacy.

Walker Percy | Louisiana novelist of alienated searchers; The Moviegoer's Binx Bolling embodies his philosophical "malaise," and he championed A Confederacy of Dunces into print.
- The Moviegoer | New Orleans stockbroker Binx Bolling searches for authenticity amid his "malaise" and cousin Kate's suicidal despair; won the National Book Award.

Harper Lee | Alabama author of To Kill a Mockingbird; its sequel/first draft Go Set a Watchman controversially recast Atticus Finch as a segregationist.
- Go Set a Watchman | Adult Jean Louise "Scout" Finch returns to Maycomb and is disillusioned to find her father Atticus sympathetic to segregation.

Gone with the Wind | Margaret Mitchell's Pulitzer-winning Civil War epic of Scarlett O'Hara's survival at Tara plantation and her stormy romance with Rhett Butler.

Sophie's Choice | William Styron's Brooklyn-set novel narrated by Stingo; Auschwitz survivor Sophie Zawistowski's fatal wartime choice and her volatile lover Nathan Landau.

Nat Turner | Leader of an 1831 Virginia slave revolt whose "confessions" to lawyer Thomas Gray inspired William Styron's controversial novel The Confessions of Nat Turner.
