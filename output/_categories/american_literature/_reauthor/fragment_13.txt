# Contemporary Fiction (1980-present)
> American fiction since 1980 ranges from postmodern maximalism and Southern/Western violence through immigrant, Native, and autofictional voices reshaping the contemporary novel and short story.

David Foster Wallace | Postmodern maximalist of Infinite Jest, set partly at Enfield Tennis Academy; also wrote the essay "A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again" and the unfinished The Pale King.
- Infinite Jest | Wallace's doorstop novel of the Incandenza family, wheelchair-bound Quebecois separatists, and a lethally entertaining "samizdat" film cartridge.
Raymond Carver = Raymond Clevie Carver Jr. | Minimalist "dirty realist" story writer, heavily edited by Gordon Lish: "Cathedral," "Where I'm Calling From," and "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love."
- "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love" = What We Talk About When We Talk About Love | Title story: cardiologist Mel and Terri, plus Nick and Laura, drink gin and argue about love, recalling an old couple hurt in a crash.
- "Cathedral" | A blind man, Robert, guides the narrator's hand to draw a cathedral together on paper while high.
- "Where I'm Calling From" | Set at a drying-out facility; an alcoholic narrator listens to fellow patient J.P.'s life story.
Cormac McCarthy | Novelist of violent Souths and Southwests: Blood Meridian with Judge Holden, the Border Trilogy beginning with All the Pretty Horses, No Country for Old Men, and The Road.
- The Road | Post-apocalyptic Pulitzer winner following an unnamed father and son "carrying the fire" toward the coast, pushing a shopping cart through the ash.
- All the Pretty Horses | Border Trilogy opener: John Grady Cole rides into Mexico, falls for ranch owner's daughter Alejandra, and is imprisoned.
- Judge Holden | Hairless, giant, erudite member of Glanton's scalp-hunting gang; declares "war is god" and shoots the ex-priest Tobin.
- No Country For Old Men | Llewelyn Moss stumbles on a drug deal's cash, pursued by the coin-flipping killer Anton Chigurh; Sheriff Bell narrates.
- Blood Meridian | Ultraviolent Western following "the kid" through Glanton's scalp-hunting gang and its philosophizing judge across the Texas-Mexico borderlands.
Don DeLillo | Postmodernist of White Noise, Underworld — which opens at the "Shot Heard 'Round the World" baseball game — Libra, and Mao II.
- White Noise | National Book Award-winning satire of Hitler-studies professor Jack Gladney, the Airborne Toxic Event, and the fear-of-death drug Dylar.
- Underworld | Waste-management executive Nick Shay, haunted by wife Marian's affair, anchors a Cold War epic opening with Cotter Martin catching a home-run ball.
Jhumpa Lahiri | Chronicler of Bengali American life: the Pulitzer-winning Interpreter of Maladies, The Namesake with Gogol Ganguli, and later books written in Italian.
Joyce Carol Oates | Hyper-prolific novelist; "Where Are You Going, Where Have You Been?" with Arnold Friend, the National Book Award winner them, and Blonde are the usual clues.
- "Where Are You Going, Where Have You Been?" | Teenager Connie, alone at home, is menaced by the sinister, possibly supernatural stranger Arnold Friend.
Michael Chabon | Pulitzer winner for The Amazing Adventures of Kavalier & Clay, about Golden Age comic-book creators; The Yiddish Policemen's Union and The Mysteries of Pittsburgh also come up.
- The Amazing Adventures of Kavalier and Clay | Joe Kavalier escapes Prague for New York, teams with cousin Sam Clay to create the superhero comic The Escapist.
- The Yiddish Policemen's Union | Alternate history set in a Jewish settlement in Sitka, Alaska; detective Meyer Landsman investigates a murder amid looming resettlement.
Jonathan Franzen = Jonathan Earl Franzen | Novelist of Midwestern family sagas: The Corrections, about the Lambert family, and Freedom; his Oprah's Book Club snub is a recurring clue.
- Freedom | Novel of Patty and Walter Berglund's marriage and environmentalism; Patty gets a basketball scholarship to Minnesota.
- "Mr. Difficult" | Essay contrasting "Status" and "Contract" models of fiction while attacking William Gaddis's hard-to-finish novel J R.
Sherman Alexie | Spokane/Coeur d'Alene writer of The Lone Ranger and Tonto Fistfight in Heaven, the basis for the film Smoke Signals, and The Absolutely True Diary of a Part-Time Indian.
George Saunders | Satirical story writer of Tenth of December and CivilWarLand in Bad Decline; won the Booker Prize for Lincoln in the Bardo, narrated by graveyard ghosts.
Alice Munro = Alice Ann Munro | Canadian short story writer and 2013 Nobel laureate; "The Bear Came Over the Mountain," Lives of Girls and Women, and Dance of the Happy Shades recur as clues.
Amy Tan | Chinese American novelist best known for The Joy Luck Club; her fiction often traces immigrant mothers and American-born daughters across generations.
- The Joy Luck Club | Four Chinese immigrant mothers and their American-born daughters trade stories over mahjong; Jing-mei "June" Woo travels to China to meet her half-sisters.
Jamaica Kincaid | Antiguan American writer known for the prose poem "Girl" and the novel Annie John, drawing on a mother-daughter relationship in the Caribbean.
- "Girl" | A mother's stream of instructions to her daughter, from washing clothes to "the kind of woman the baker won't let near the bread."
John Irving = John Winslow Irving | Novelist of The World According to Garp, The Cider House Rules, and A Prayer for Owen Meany, often set in New England.
- The World According to Garp | T.S. Garp, son of feminist Jenny Fields, becomes a writer; the "Under Toad" and the Ellen Jamesians recur.
- The Cider House Rules | Orphan Homer Wells is trained in abortion medicine by Dr. Larch at a Maine orphanage, then works at an apple orchard.
- Owen Meany | Tiny-voiced Owen believes he is God's instrument and dies saving Vietnamese children; "keep passing the open windows" recurs.
Barbara Kingsolver | Novelist of The Poisonwood Bible, about the missionary Price family in the Congo, and the Pulitzer-winning Demon Copperhead, a Dickens-inspired Appalachian retelling.
Jennifer Egan | Novelist of A Visit from the Goon Squad, a Pulitzer-winning story cycle told partly through a slideshow chapter, following music-industry figures across decades.
- A Visit From the Goon Squad | Interlinked chapters follow kleptomaniac Sasha and record executive Bennie Salazar across decades; one chapter is a PowerPoint slideshow.
Jonathan Safran Foer | Novelist of Everything Is Illuminated and Extremely Loud and Incredibly Close; also writes food-politics nonfiction like Eating Animals.
- Extremely Loud and Incredibly Close | Nine-year-old Oskar Schell searches New York for a lock matching a key left by his father, who died on 9/11.
Louise Erdrich | Ojibwe novelist of Love Medicine and The Round House; her 2020 novel The Night Watchman draws on her grandfather's fight against Native termination policy.
- Love Medicine | Story cycle opening with June Kashpaw's death walking home in a snowstorm, following intertwined Ojibwe families on a North Dakota reservation.
Erdrich | Common-link surname spanning sisters Louise, the celebrated novelist, and Heid, an Ojibwe poet; both are tied to Minneapolis's Birchbark Books.
Margaret Atwood | Canadian novelist of The Handmaid's Tale and the speculative Oryx and Crake, often clued for dystopian and environmental themes.
- Oryx and Crake | Post-apocalyptic novel following Snowman/Jimmy, his genius friend Crake, and the genetically engineered Crakers after a bioengineered plague.
Sandra Cisneros | Chicana writer of The House on Mango Street, a vignette novel narrated by young Esperanza Cordero in a Chicago barrio.
- The House on Mango Street | Vignette novel narrated by Esperanza Cordero, a girl coming of age in a Chicago Latino neighborhood.
Annie Dillard | Nonfiction writer of Pilgrim at Tinker Creek, a Pulitzer-winning meditation on nature observed near her Virginia home.
Lydia Davis | Master of extremely short flash fiction, including "Samuel Johnson Is Indignant"; also translated Proust's Swann's Way and Flaubert's Madame Bovary.
Ocean Vuong | Vietnamese American poet and novelist of On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous, an epistolary novel addressed to the narrator's illiterate mother.
Paul Auster | Postmodern novelist of The New York Trilogy and 4 3 2 1, blending detective-story tropes with metafictional identity games.
- The New York Trilogy | Three linked detective novellas — City of Glass, Ghosts, and The Locked Room — that dissolve identity and authorship.
Tim O'Brien | Vietnam War novelist of The Things They Carried, blurring memoir and fiction; also wrote Going After Cacciato.
- The Things They Carried | Linked stories cataloging soldiers' physical and emotional burdens; Lieutenant Jimmy Cross blames himself for Kiowa's death in a sewage field.
Anne Carson | Canadian classicist and poet of the verse novel Autobiography of Red, following the winged monster Geryon; also wrote the brother-elegy Nox.
House of Leaves | Mark Z. Danielewski's typographically experimental horror novel about the Navidson family's house, which is larger inside than out.
Jane Smiley | Novelist of A Thousand Acres, a Pulitzer-winning King Lear retelling set on an Iowa farm.
Junot Diaz | Dominican American novelist of The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao, following a nerdy Dominican American and his family's curse, or fuku.
Kathy Acker | Punk postmodernist who used cut-up technique in Blood and Guts in High School and rewrote Don Quixote as an American woman.
Maxine Hong Kingston | Chinese American memoirist of The Woman Warrior, blending myth and autobiography around the legend of Fa Mu Lan.
- The Woman Warrior: Memoirs of a Girlhood Among Ghosts | Memoir blending the narrator's Chinese American girlhood with the legend of the woman warrior Fa Mu Lan and the fearsome aunt "No Name Woman."
Bless Me, Ultima | Rudolfo Anaya's Chicano coming-of-age novel: Antonio Marez is mentored by the curandera Ultima and learns of the sacred Golden Carp.
Denis Johnson | Story writer of Jesus' Son and the National Book Award-winning Vietnam epic Tree of Smoke.
- Jesus' Son | Linked stories narrated by an unnamed addict nicknamed "Fuckhead," including "Emergency" with the orderly Georgie.
Homegoing | Yaa Gyasi's debut novel tracing two half-sisters, Effia and Esi, and their descendants from the Gold Coast slave trade to America.
Lorrie Moore | Short story writer known for wordplay and dark comedy, including the collection Birds of America.
Patricia Lockwood | Poet-novelist of "Rape Joke" and the memoir Priestdaddy, about growing up with a married Catholic priest father; also wrote the internet-age novel No One Is Talking About This.
A Heartbreaking Work of Staggering Genius | Dave Eggers's metafictional memoir about raising his younger brother Toph after their parents' deaths.
Alison Bechdel | Graphic memoirist of Fun Home, about her closeted gay father and a family funeral home; the "Bechdel test" bears her name.
Art Spiegelman | Graphic novelist of Maus, depicting the Holocaust with Jews as mice and Nazis as cats through his father Vladek's story.
Ben Lerner | Poet-novelist of Leaving the Atocha Station and 10:04; The Topeka School follows his autofictional alter ego Adam Gordon.
Donna Tartt | Novelist of The Secret History, about classics students who murder a classmate, and the Pulitzer-winning The Goldfinch.
Emily St. John Mandel | Novelist of Station Eleven, following a Shakespearean traveling troupe after a flu pandemic collapses civilization.
James Welch | Blackfeet novelist of Winter in the Blood, set on the Fort Belknap Reservation, and the historical epic Fools Crow.
Jeffrey Eugenides | Novelist of The Virgin Suicides and the Pulitzer-winning Middlesex.
- Middlesex | Intersex narrator Cal/Calliope Stephanides traces a recessive gene through three generations of a Greek American family in Detroit.
Jonathan Lethem | Brooklyn novelist of Motherless Brooklyn, narrated by the Tourettic detective Lionel Essrog, and The Fortress of Solitude.
Marilynne Robinson | Novelist of Gilead, an epistolary novel from an aging Congregationalist minister to his young son.
- Gilead | Iowa minister John Ames writes a long letter to his young son, reflecting on his friendship with Boughton and his prodigal namesake.
Michael Cunningham | Novelist of The Hours, interweaving Virginia Woolf's writing of Mrs. Dalloway with two later women's lives.
Nathan Englander | Short story writer of For the Relief of Unbearable Urges and "What We Talk About When We Talk About Anne Frank."
Ottessa Moshfegh | Novelist of My Year of Rest and Relaxation, about a woman who sleeps for a year, and the unsettling Eileen.
Annie Proulx | Novelist of The Shipping News, set in Newfoundland, and the short story "Brokeback Mountain."
Richard Powers | Novelist of The Overstory, a Pulitzer-winning novel of interconnected lives entwined with trees.
Swamplandia! | Karen Russell's novel of the Bigtree family's failing alligator-wrestling theme park in the Florida Everglades.
Teju Cole | Nigerian American novelist of Open City, following psychiatric resident Julius wandering Manhattan; also coined the phrase "white savior industrial complex."
The Mezzanine | Nicholson Baker's debut novel of office worker Howie's digressive thoughts while riding an escalator during his lunch break.
Tobias Wolff | Memoirist of This Boy's Life, chronicling his turbulent Pacific Northwest childhood under an abusive stepfather.
Tommy Orange | Cheyenne/Arapaho novelist of There There, following twelve Native characters converging on a powwow in Oakland.
Bluets | Maggie Nelson's book of 240 numbered propositions on the color blue, named for a Joan Mitchell painting and recounting a breakup and a friend's paralysis.
