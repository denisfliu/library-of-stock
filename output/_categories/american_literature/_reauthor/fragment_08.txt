# African American Literature: Wheatley to the Harlem Renaissance
> African American letters from Phillis Wheatley's colonial-era poetry through slavery-era narratives to the 1920s Harlem Renaissance's flowering of poetry and fiction.

Jupiter Hammon | Enslaved poet from Long Island whose 1760 "An Evening Thought" was the first poem published by an African American; also wrote "An Address to Miss Phillis Wheatley."
Phillis Wheatley = Phyllis Wheatley | African-born enslaved poet of revolutionary Boston, first African American to publish a poetry book; wrote "On Being Brought from Africa to America."
Frederick Douglass | Escaped slave, orator, and editor of The North Star; his 1845 Narrative, the fight with slave-breaker Covey, and "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" are the clues.
slave narratives | Common-link genre spanning Douglass, Harriet Jacobs's Incidents, Olaudah Equiano, and Solomon Northup, often narrated or published under a pseudonym while still enslaved.
Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl | Harriet Jacobs's narrative, published as "Linda Brent," of seven years hiding in her grandmother's garret to escape the predatory Dr. Flint.
W. E. B. Du Bois | Sociologist-activist and NAACP co-founder; The Souls of Black Folk introduced "double consciousness" and "the Veil," and his fiction included the story "The Comet."
- The Souls of Black Folk | Du Bois's 1903 essay collection on "double consciousness," "the color line," and the "sorrow songs," including the story "Of the Coming of John."
Charles Chesnutt = Charles W. Chesnutt = Charles Waddell Chesnutt | Author of The Conjure Woman and The House Behind the Cedars, dramatizing Reconstruction-era racial passing and folk conjure tales.
- The House Behind the Cedars | Novel of Rena Walden, who passes as white to become George Tryon's "queen of love and beauty" until he learns her race and breaks their engagement.
Paul Laurence Dunbar | First nationally famous Black American poet; "We Wear the Mask" and "Sympathy" ("I know why the caged bird sings") anchor nearly every question.
- "We Wear the Mask" | Poem whose title object "grins and lies, hides our cheeks and shades our eyes" while masking the speaker's true suffering from the world.
- "Sympathy" | Poem of a caged bird who sings "a prayer... from his heart's deep core"; its refrain gave Maya Angelou her memoir title, I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings.
- Dunbar | Married surname shared by the poet and his first wife, Alice Dunbar-Nelson (born Alice Ruth Moore), author of Violets and Other Tales.
James Weldon Johnson | Writer of "Lift Every Voice and Sing," the novel The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man, and the sermon-poems of God's Trombones; also an NAACP leader and diplomat.
- the Ex-Colored Man | Unnamed narrator who passes as white after witnessing a lynching in Georgia, ultimately confessing he sold his "birthright for a mess of pottage."
Harlem Renaissance | The 1920s flowering of Black art centered in upper Manhattan: Hughes, Hurston, McKay, Countee Cullen, and Alain Locke's anthology The New Negro are the standard clues.
Langston Hughes | Poet laureate of the Harlem Renaissance: "The Negro Speaks of Rivers," "Harlem," "I, Too," "The Weary Blues," and the Jesse B. Semple ("Simple") stories.
- "The Weary Blues" | Poem of a pianist "swaying to and fro on his rickety stool," playing a "drowsy syncopated tune" on Lenox Avenue until he sleeps "like a rock."
- "Theme For English B" | Poem addressed to a white instructor; the speaker, the "only colored student" in his class, insists "that's American" of what the two share.
blues | Common-link musical genre clued through Hughes's poetry and Baldwin's story "Sonny's Blues," typically via a "drowsy syncopated tune" and the AAB stanza form.
Zora Neale Hurston | Anthropologist-novelist from Eatonville, Florida: Their Eyes Were Watching God, the folklore collection Mules and Men, and the essay "How It Feels to Be Colored Me."
- Their Eyes Were Watching God | Hurston's novel of Janie Crawford's three marriages; the pear tree, the mule of Eatonville, the hurricane, and the shooting of rabid Tea Cake are the standard clues.
- Sweat | Short story of Delia Jones, whose washing "sweat" pays for her house while husband Sykes dies from a rattlesnake he planted to kill her.
- "The Gilded Six-Bits" | Story set in Eatonville where Joe pays Missie May with a fake gold coin after catching her affair with ice-cream parlor owner Otis Slemmons.
- Eatonville, Florida | Hurston's all-Black hometown, setting of Their Eyes Were Watching God and "The Gilded Six-Bits" and subject of "How It Feels to Be Colored Me."
- Tea Cake | Janie's much-younger third husband in Their Eyes Were Watching God, shot by her in self-defense after a rabid dog bite drives him mad.
Claude McKay | Jamaican-born Harlem Renaissance poet of the defiant sonnet "If We Must Die" and "America" ("this cultured hell that tests my youth"), plus the novel Home to Harlem.
- Jamaica | McKay's island homeland, evoked in his dialect collection Songs of Jamaica and the novel Banana Bottom, set in its title village.
- "If We Must Die" | Sonnet urging "Kinsmen" to face "the murderous, cowardly pack," fighting back rather than dying "like hogs...in an inglorious spot."
Countee Cullen | Harlem Renaissance poet of the collection Color and its sonnet "Yet Do I Marvel"; "Incident" recalls a childhood racial slur suffered on a trip to Baltimore.
- "Yet Do I Marvel" | Sonnet on God's inscrutable ways, from Tantalus to the blind mole, before the "curious thing" of making "a poet black, and bid him sing."
Jean Toomer | Harlem Renaissance author of Cane, mixing poetry, prose, and drama; grandson of Louisiana governor P. B. S. Pinchback, later a disciple of George Gurdjieff.
- Cane | 1923 collection of vignettes, poems, and the closing play "Kabnis," opening with "Karintha" and including "Georgia Dusk" and "Blood-Burning Moon."
Nella Larsen | Harlem Renaissance novelist of Passing and Quicksand, exploring racial ambiguity and light-skinned Black women caught between white and Black worlds.
- passing -> Passing | Novel of Irene Redfield and the racially ambiguous Clare Kendry, who dies falling from a window after her white husband learns she is Black.
- Quicksand | Novel of Helga Crane, half-Danish and half-Black, who drifts from the Tuskegee-like school Naxos through Chicago, Harlem, and Copenhagen before marrying a rural minister.
