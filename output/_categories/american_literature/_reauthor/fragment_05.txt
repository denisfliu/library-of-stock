# Modernist Fiction and the Lost Generation
> World War One's disillusioned American generation, many expatriated to Paris, traded realist narrative for psychological interiority, formal experiment, and satire of small-town and consumer culture.

Ernest Hemingway = Ernest Miller Hemingway | Iceberg-theory stylist of spare prose: The Sun Also Rises, A Farewell to Arms, and For Whom the Bell Tolls.
- The Sun Also Rises | Novel of expatriate Jake Barnes, wounded and impotent, and Lady Brett Ashley's circle drifting from Paris cafes to the Pamplona bullfights.
- A Farewell to Arms | Novel of ambulance driver Frederic Henry's affair with nurse Catherine Barkley amid the WWI Italian retreat from Caporetto; she dies in childbirth.
- Frederic Henry | American lieutenant narrator of A Farewell to Arms who deserts the Italian army after the retreat from Caporetto.
- For Whom the Bell Tolls | Spanish Civil War novel of American dynamiter Robert Jordan's doomed love for Maria while blowing up a bridge with guerrillas.
- The Old Man and the Sea | Novella of Santiago's three-day struggle to land a giant marlin, only for sharks to strip it before he reaches shore.
- In Our Time | Hemingway's first major story collection, interleaving Nick Adams stories with terse italicized vignettes of war and violence.
- Nick Adams | Recurring Hemingway alter ego across linked short stories, from a wounded WWI veteran to a boy fishing alone in "Big Two-Hearted River."
- "Big Two-Hearted River" | Nick Adams returns from war to fish alone in the Michigan woods; understated prose masks unspoken postwar trauma.
- "Hills like White Elephants" -> "Hills Like White Elephants" | Story told almost entirely in dialogue; a couple at a Spanish train station debate an unnamed "operation," implicitly an abortion.
- "The Short Happy Life of Francis Macomber" | Story of a cowardly big-game hunter who briefly finds courage before being shot, ambiguously, by his wife Margot.
- "The Snows of Kilimanjaro" | Dying writer Harry reflects on wasted talent on an African safari as gangrene sets in; opens with an epigraph about a frozen leopard.
- A Moveable Feast | Posthumous Paris memoir recalling the "lost generation" circle — Gertrude Stein, Fitzgerald, Ezra Pound — during Hemingway's 1920s expatriate years.

Gertrude Stein | Expatriate Paris salon host who coined "you are all a lost generation"; wrote The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas and Tender Buttons.

F. Scott Fitzgerald = Francis Scott Key Fitzgerald | Jazz Age chronicler: The Great Gatsby, Tender Is the Night, This Side of Paradise, and stories like "Bernice Bobs Her Hair" and "Babylon Revisited."
- The Great Gatsby | Fitzgerald's novel narrated by Nick Carraway: the green light, the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, and Myrtle's death under Daisy's driving are giveaways.
- This Side of Paradise | Fitzgerald's debut novel following Princeton student Amory Blaine's romantic disappointments and coming-of-age; ends "I know myself, but that is all-"
- Tender is the Night -> Tender Is the Night | Novel of psychiatrist Dick Diver's marriage to wealthy patient Nicole, modeled on Zelda, unraveling amid the Riviera expatriate set.
- "The Diamond as Big as the Ritz" | Story of John Unger visiting classmate Percy Washington's family, whose Montana mountain conceals a diamond the size of the Ritz; Kismine is the love interest.
- "Winter Dreams" | Story of golf caddy Dexter Green's doomed pursuit of the wealthy, careless Judy Jones; an early sketch for Gatsby and Daisy.
- Bernice | Title character of "Bernice Bobs Her Hair," tricked by cousin Marjorie into a scandalous haircut before retaliating by shearing Marjorie's braids.
- Daisy Buchanan | The Great Gatsby's careless object of Gatsby's longing, married to the philandering Tom; her voice is famously "full of money."
- Zelda Fitzgerald | F. Scott's wife and model for Daisy Buchanan and Nicole Diver; wrote her own autobiographical novel, Save Me the Waltz, while institutionalized.

Fitzgerald | Common-link surname spanning F. Scott and Zelda: their marriage, mutual literary borrowings, and Zelda's own novel Save Me the Waltz.

Save Me the Waltz | Zelda Fitzgerald's autobiographical novel of aspiring ballerina Alabama Beggs; written at Johns Hopkins while she was treated for schizophrenia.

Thornton Wilder = Thornton Niven Wilder | Playwright-novelist of Our Town and The Skin of Our Teeth; also wrote The Matchmaker, source of Hello, Dolly!, and the novel The Bridge of San Luis Rey.
- The Bridge of San Luis Rey | Wilder's Pulitzer-winning novel investigating five deaths when a Peruvian rope bridge collapses, asking whether the victims were chosen by design.

John Steinbeck | Chronicler of Depression-era California: The Grapes of Wrath, Of Mice and Men, East of Eden, and Cannery Row.
- The Grapes of Wrath | Novel of the Joads' migration from the Oklahoma Dust Bowl to California; Tom Joad's "I'll be there" speech and Rose of Sharon's final gesture close it.
- East of Eden | Salinas Valley retelling of Cain and Abel across the Trask family; the Hebrew word timshel ("thou mayest") and villainous Cathy Ames anchor clues.
- Of Mice and Men | Novella of migrant workers George and the childlike, powerful Lennie, whose dream of a farm ends when George shoots him.
- Cannery Row | Episodic novel of Monterey's waterfront misfits, centered on marine biologist Doc and the vagrants of Mack and the boys.
- The Pearl | Parable of pearl diver Kino, whose discovery of a great pearl brings greed and tragedy to his family.

John Dos Passos = John Roderigo Dos Passos | Modernist of the U.S.A. trilogy, known for its Newsreel and Camera Eye collage sections; also wrote Manhattan Transfer and Three Soldiers.

Nathanael West | Darkly comic novelist of Miss Lonelyhearts, about an advice columnist, and The Day of the Locust, set amid a Hollywood mob riot.
- Miss Lonelyhearts | Novella of a male advice columnist drowning in readers' letters of misery, tormented by his editor Shrike and Shrike's wife.
- The Day of the Locust | Novel of Hollywood's fringe dreamers converging in an apocalyptic riot after a premiere; the painting "The Burning of Los Angeles" frames it.

Sinclair Lewis | First American Nobel literature laureate; satires of Midwestern conformity include Main Street, Babbitt, Arrowsmith, Elmer Gantry, and It Can't Happen Here.
- Babbitt | Satire of conformist realtor George Babbitt's midlife crisis and half-hearted rebellion against small-city Zenith's boosterism.
- Main Street | Satire of reformer Carol Kennicott's stifled ambitions in the small Minnesota town of Gopher Prairie.
- Arrowsmith | Novel following idealistic scientist Martin Arrowsmith's career from small-town doctor to bacteriologist battling a Caribbean plague.
- Elmer Gantry | Satire of a hypocritical, womanizing evangelist preacher; dedicated to Lewis's friend H. L. Mencken.
- It Can't Happen Here | Novel imagining a fascist demagogue, Buzz Windrip, elected U.S. president and installing an authoritarian regime.

H. L. Mencken | Baltimore journalist-critic and "Sage of Baltimore" who covered the Scopes trial, wrote The American Language, and was Elmer Gantry's dedicatee.

Sherwood Anderson | Story-cycle modernist best known for Winesburg, Ohio's portrait of small-town "grotesques."
- Winesburg, Ohio | Linked story cycle about small-town "grotesques," framed by "The Book of the Grotesque" and unified by young reporter George Willard; "Hands" opens it.
- George Willard | Young reporter who links Winesburg, Ohio's stories, observing the town's isolated "grotesques" before leaving for the city.
- Wing Biddlebaum | Winesburg, Ohio's twitchy-handed former teacher, driven from a Pennsylvania town by a false molestation accusation; his story opens the cycle ("Hands").

James Thurber = James Grover Thurber | New Yorker humorist and cartoonist best known for the daydreaming title character of "The Secret Life of Walter Mitty."
- Walter Mitty | Henpecked daydreamer of Thurber's story who imagines himself a Navy pilot, surgeon, and marksman amid mundane errands; catchphrase "pocketa-pocketa-pocketa."

Algonquin Round Table | Common-link 1920s New York "Vicious Circle" of wits lunching at the Algonquin Hotel: Dorothy Parker, Robert Benchley, Alexander Woollcott, Robert Sherwood, George S. Kaufman.

Edmund Wilson | Critic who edited Fitzgerald's The Crack-Up and unfinished The Last Tycoon; wrote Axel's Castle and Patriotic Gore, and was Mary McCarthy's second husband.

Henry Miller | Expatriate Paris novelist of explicit, autobiographical fiction; Tropic of Cancer, banned for decades in the U.S., made his name.
- Tropic of Cancer | Autobiographical novel of Miller's impoverished, sexually frank life among Paris expatriates; banned as obscene in the U.S. until 1961.

Anais Nin -> Anaïs Nin | Diarist and Henry Miller's lover, fictionalized as Tania in Tropic of Cancer; wrote erotica (Delta of Venus, Little Birds) and the novel sequence Cities of the Interior.

Djuna Barnes | Provincetown Players writer and Paris expatriate best known for Nightwood, a modernist novel of lesbian love and Greenwich Village decline.
- Nightwood | Barnes's novel of Robin Vote's relationships with Nora Flood and Jenny Petherbridge, and her marriage to Baron Volkbein; ends with Robin barking at a dog.

Ring Lardner | Humorist of baseball vernacular fiction (You Know Me Al) and the story "Haircut," about a small-town prankster's revenge killing.

Call It Sleep | Henry Roth's modernist novel of young immigrant David Schearl's Lower East Side childhood, ending with a near-electrocution on subway tracks.

stream-of-consciousness | Narrative technique rendering a character's unbroken flow of thought; associated with Faulkner's The Sound and the Fury among American modernists.

Let Us Now Praise Famous Men | James Agee's text, with Walker Evans's photographs, documenting Alabama sharecropper families (the pseudonymous Gudgers, Woods, and Ricketts) during the Depression.
