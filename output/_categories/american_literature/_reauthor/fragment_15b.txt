# Places in American Literature

Louisiana | Setting of Kate Chopin's The Awakening and "Désirée's Baby," and of Ignatius Reilly's New Orleans in A Confederacy of Dunces.
Mississippi River | Twain's Life on the Mississippi and the raft of Huckleberry Finn; also Faulkner's "Old Man" and Eliot's "The Dry Salvages."
Salem, Massachusetts | Hawthorne's "Young Goodman Brown" and The House of the Seven Gables; site of the 1692 witch trials dramatized in Miller's The Crucible.
Key West | Wallace Stevens's "The Idea of Order at Key West" and Elizabeth Bishop's island poems; longtime home of Hemingway.
Native American reservations | Setting of Erdrich's Love Medicine, Silko's Ceremony, and Alexie's The Lone Ranger and Tonto Fistfight in Heaven and The Absolutely True Diary of a Part-Time Indian.
plantations | Setting of Uncle Tom's Cabin and Chesnutt's Conjure Woman tales; also the lost estates of The Little Foxes and A Streetcar Named Desire.
Ohio | Sherwood Anderson's fictional Winesburg; also home state in Toni Morrison's Sula and The Bluest Eye and David Foster Wallace's The Broom of the System.
Illinois | Sandburg's "Chicago" and Masters's Spoon River Anthology; Chicago setting of A Raisin in the Sun and Shepard's Buried Child.
Oklahoma | Dust Bowl home of Steinbeck's Joad family; also Momaday's Kiowa homeland and the Lynn Riggs play behind the musical Oklahoma!
Philadelphia | Franklin's Autobiography and Charles Brockden Brown's Gothic novels; site of the yellow fever epidemic in Arthur Mervyn.
Washington, D.C. | Setting of Hellman's Watch on the Rhine, Edward P. Jones's Lost in the City, and Saunders's Lincoln in the Bardo.
Brooklyn Bridge | Title structure of Hart Crane's The Bridge; also lends its name to Arthur Miller's A View from the Bridge.
Los Angeles, California | Home of Nathanael West's Day of the Locust and Bukowski's Henry Chinaski; setting of Fitzgerald's The Last Tycoon.
Alaska | Setting of Chabon's alternate-history The Yiddish Policemen's Union and Krakauer's Into the Wild; homesteader poet John Haines's home state.
hospitals | Setting of One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest, Vonnegut's "2 B R 0 2 B," and hospital scenes in Angels in America.
Italy | Setting of Henry James's Daisy Miller and The Portrait of a Lady, Hawthorne's "Rappaccini's Daughter," and Hemingway's postwar stories.
Rome | Where Daisy Miller dies and Isabel Archer returns in James; also Wharton's "Roman Fever" and the "Eternal City" chapter of Catch-22.
Babylon | Title city of Fitzgerald's "Babylon Revisited" and Stephen Vincent Benét's "By the Waters of Babylon."
Mars | Setting of Bradbury's The Martian Chronicles and Tracy K. Smith's Pulitzer-winning Life on Mars.
Vietnam | Birth country of poet Ocean Vuong and novelist Viet Thanh Nguyen, whose The Sympathizer follows a Vietnamese narrator to Los Angeles.

# History and Culture through Literature

Jesus Christ | Common-link religious figure: nativity and Passion plays, Adrienne Kennedy's Funnyhouse of a Negro, Lew Wallace's Ben-Hur.
Euclid | Ancient Greek geometer invoked in Edna St. Vincent Millay's sonnet "Euclid alone has looked on Beauty bare" and a Vachel Lindsay poem about him.
Marilyn Monroe | Film icon and third wife of Arthur Miller; recurs as a biographical clue attached to his plays.
Rosenberg | Surname of executed Cold War spies Julius and Ethel; their ghosts haunt Roy Cohn in Angels in America, and their execution opens Plath's The Bell Jar.
Iraq War | Backdrop for veteran drama and fiction: Hudes's Water by the Spoonful, Joseph's Bengal Tiger at the Baghdad Zoo, Klay's Redeployment, Powers's The Yellow Birds.
slaves | Common link on enslavement: Jacobs-Jenkins's An Octoroon, Harris's Slave Play, August Wilson's Joe Turner's Come and Gone, Butler's Kindred.
Harvard University | Setting or alma mater across fiction and essay: Quentin Compson's college, Emerson's "American Scholar" address, poets Tracy K. Smith and Natasha Trethewey.
The Atlantic | Boston abolitionist magazine first edited by James Russell Lowell; published Howe's "Battle Hymn of the Republic" and Coates's "The Case for Reparations."
newspapers | Common-link industry: Ida B. Wells's and Nellie Bly's journalism, West's Miss Lonelyhearts advice column, Jefferson's "newspapers without government" line.
Helen Vendler | Influential American poetry critic; her readings of Dickinson, Plath, and other poets recur as biographical or critical clues.

# Words, Names, and Things

George | Common-link first name: George in Albee's Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf, husband of Martha, who taunts guests Nick and Honey with an invented dead son.
Martha | Common-link first name: George's wife in Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf; also war correspondent Martha Gellhorn, Hemingway's third wife.
Henry | Common-link first name: John Berryman's put-upon alter ego "Henry," addressed as "Mr. Bones," across The Dream Songs.
Richard | Common-link first name: poets Richard Wilbur, Richard Eberhart, and Richard Siken; also novelist Richard Brautigan of Trout Fishing in America.
Adam | Common-link first name: the Biblical first man of Whitman's "Children of Adam" section of Leaves of Grass.
Paul | Common-link first name: Cather's doomed aesthete of "Paul's Case," Morrison's Paul D in Beloved, poet Paul Laurence Dunbar.
Harriet | Common-link first name: Harriet Beecher Stowe (Uncle Tom's Cabin), Harriet Jacobs (Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl), and Harriet Wilson, the first Black novelist published in the US.
Johnson | Common-link surname: poets James Weldon and Georgia Douglas Johnson, story writers Denis and Adam Johnson.
names | Common-link motif of naming: DeLillo's novel The Names, Lahiri's The Namesake, Adam naming the animals in Genesis.
God | Common-link addressee/figure: Celie's letters in The Color Purple, Hurston's Their Eyes Were Watching God, O'Neill's The Great God Brown, Sexton's The Awful Rowing Toward God.
sunday | Common-link day: Wallace Stevens's "Sunday Morning" and Robert Hayden's "Those Winter Sundays," both meditating on mortality and a father's labor.
haiku | Japanese three-line form taken up by Richard Wright (This Other World) and Jack Kerouac, and adapted by Imagists like Ezra Pound.
sonnets | Fourteen-line form of Claude McKay's "If We Must Die," Countee Cullen's "Yet Do I Marvel," and Millay's Euclid sonnet.
villanelle | Nineteen-line French form used in Elizabeth Bishop's "One Art," Sylvia Plath's "Mad Girl's Love Song," and Theodore Roethke's "The Waking."
mystery plays | Medieval Biblical cycle drama (Wakefield, N-Town, Chester); invoked as source material and contrasted with miracle plays.
poets | Common-link profession: New York School figures like John Ashbery, Vietnam-era poets Levertov and Merwin, Bolaño's Visceral Realists.
English | Common-link language: Ngugi's rejection of it in Decolonizing the Mind, Ha Jin's adopted language, Cisneros's Chicana idiom in The House on Mango Street.
Russian | Common-link language: translators Pevear and Volokhonsky, Nabokov's switch away from it, Batuman's The Idiot and Saunders's A Swim in a Pond in the Rain.
Cuban-American | Ethnicity of playwrights Nilo Cruz (Anna in the Tropics) and Maria Irene Fornes (Fefu and Her Friends), and novelist Oscar Hijuelos.
Mexican-American | Chicano ethnicity of authors Sandra Cisneros, Gloria Anzaldúa, Rudolfo Anaya, and Gary Soto.
feminism | Movement traced through Gilman's "The Yellow Wallpaper," Friedan's The Feminine Mystique, and Adrienne Rich's poetry.
abortion | Common-link procedure in Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants," Irving's The Cider House Rules, and Hansberry's A Raisin in the Sun.
the poet's father | Common-link figure: Plath's "Daddy," Hayden's "Those Winter Sundays," Roethke's "My Papa's Waltz," Vuong's poems to his father.
time travel | Plot device of Butler's Kindred and Bradbury's "A Sound of Thunder," frequently compared to Wells's The Time Machine.
being buried alive | Common-link fate in Poe's "The Cask of Amontillado," "Berenice," and "The Premature Burial."
car crash | Common-link fate: George and Martha's fictional dead son in Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf, Denis Johnson's "Car Crash While Hitchhiking," Myrtle's death in The Great Gatsby.
drowning | Common-link fate: Edna Pontellier's suicide in The Awakening, Ophelia comparisons, Faulkner's Quentin Compson.
graphic novel | Medium of Spiegelman's Maus, Bechdel's Fun Home, and Ware's Jimmy Corrigan.
fly | Common-link animal: Emily Dickinson's "I heard a Fly buzz — when I died," and the flying men of Morrison's Song of Solomon.
horse | Common-link animal: Frost's "Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening," Plath's "Ariel," and Joy Harjo's horse poems.
horses | Common-link animal: the same Frost/Plath/Harjo cluster as "horse," plus Peter Shaffer's Equus and the stage adaptation of War Horse.
rabbits | Common-link animal: John Updike's Rabbit Angstrom tetralogy, Lennie's dream farm in Of Mice and Men, Mary Chase's Harvey, and the Br'er Rabbit trickster of An Octoroon.
butterfly | Common-link animal: Bradbury's "A Sound of Thunder," Hwang's M. Butterfly and Golden Child, Nabokov's lepidoptery.
bones | Common-link object: Jesmyn Ward's Salvage the Bones and Sing, Unburied, Sing, Hurston's Eatonville tales.
bridges | Common-link structure: Hart Crane's "For the Marriage of Faustus and Helen," Arthur Miller's A View from the Bridge, Bierce's Owl Creek Bridge.
ships | Common-link vessel: Melville's Pequod in Moby-Dick, Billy Budd, and Benito Cereno; Oliver Wendell Holmes's "Old Ironsides."
glass | Common-link material: Salinger's Glass family, Emily St. John Mandel's The Glass Hotel, the glass church of Peter Carey's Oscar and Lucinda.
machines | Common-link object: Sophie Treadwell's Machinal and Elmer Rice's The Adding Machine, both expressionist plays named for the title device.
ice | Common-link substance: Vonnegut's world-ending ice-nine in Cat's Cradle, Frost's "Fire and Ice."
cars | Common-link object: Tom Wolfe's The Kandy-Kolored Tangerine-Flake Streamline Baby, Nelson Angstrom's failing dealership in Rabbit at Rest.
tennis | Common-link sport: David Foster Wallace's tennis essays and the Enfield Tennis Academy of Infinite Jest.
basketball | Common-link sport: John Updike's Rabbit Angstrom, Sherman Alexie's reservation ball, Owen Meany's shot in A Prayer for Owen Meany.
fisherman | Common-link occupation: Hemingway's Santiago in The Old Man and the Sea, and Achille and Hector in Derek Walcott's Omeros.
fishing | Common-link activity: Santiago's marlin in The Old Man and the Sea, Hemingway's Nick Adams story "Big Two-Hearted River."
Icarus | Mythological figure of Auden's "Musée des Beaux Arts," William Carlos Williams's "Landscape with the Fall of Icarus," and poems by Sexton and Rukeyser.

# Foreign Voices in American Literature Questions

Sir Walter Scott | Scottish novelist of Ivanhoe and Waverley; his historical romances are a recurring point of comparison in American fiction.
Lord Byron | English Romantic poet of Childe Harold's Pilgrimage and "She Walks in Beauty"; appears as a character in Tennessee Williams's Camino Real.
Lewis Carroll | English mathematician-author of Alice's Adventures in Wonderland; his "Hunting of the Snark" inspired the title of Jack London's The Cruise of the Snark.
Robinson Crusoe | Daniel Defoe's castaway, subject of Elizabeth Bishop's poem "Crusoe in England" and J. M. Coetzee's Foe.
A Clockwork Orange | Anthony Burgess's dystopian novel of teen "droog" Alex and the invented slang Nadsat; Kubrick's film adaptation is frequently clued alongside it.
Finnegans Wake | James Joyce's final dream-narrative novel of HCE and Anna Livia Plurabelle; often compared to Thornton Wilder's The Skin of Our Teeth.
Henrik Ibsen | Norwegian father of modern realist drama (A Doll's House, Hedda Gabler); recurs as a dramaturgical touchstone.
Aesop | Ancient Greek fabulist, traditionally depicted as a freed slave; author of "The Tortoise and the Hare" and "The Fox and the Grapes."
Leo Tolstoy | Russian novelist of War and Peace and Anna Karenina; the latter is read aloud within Nilo Cruz's Cuban-American play Anna in the Tropics.
Isaac Bashevis Singer | Polish-born, Yiddish-language Nobel laureate naturalized in the US; wrote "Gimpel the Fool" and The Magician of Lublin.
Hungary | Home country of authors Imre Kertész, László Krasznahorkai, Péter Esterházy, and Sándor Márai, clued in translated-literature bonuses.
Milan Kundera | Czech-French novelist of The Unbearable Lightness of Being; recurs as a philosophical touchstone in discussions of fiction.
Michel Houellebecq | French novelist of Submission and The Elementary Particles, provocateur on religion, sex, and cloning.
Manuel Puig | Argentine novelist obsessed with classic Hollywood film; wrote Kiss of the Spider Woman and Betrayed by Rita Hayworth.
Omeros | Derek Walcott's epic poem reimagining the Iliad among Caribbean fishermen Achille and Hector; Walcott is a Saint Lucian Nobel laureate.
Haruki Murakami | Japanese novelist known for surreal, genre-blending fiction; recurs as a comparison figure for American postmodernists.
Cloud Atlas | David Mitchell's British novel of six nested narratives spanning 1850 to a post-apocalyptic future, including the replicant Sonmi-451.
Disgrace | J. M. Coetzee's Booker-winning novel of disgraced professor David Lurie, who moves in with his daughter Lucy after an affair with a student.
