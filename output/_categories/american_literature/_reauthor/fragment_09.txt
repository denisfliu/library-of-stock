# Postwar African American Literature
> From Richard Wright's naturalist protest fiction and the essays of Baldwin and Ellison through Toni Morrison's Nobel-winning novels to the poets, critics, and novelists carrying that tradition forward today.

Richard Wright | Naturalist of Black protest fiction: Native Son, the memoir Black Boy, and the story collection Uncle Tom's Children with "Big Boy Leaves Home."
- Native Son | Wright's novel of Bigger Thomas, who accidentally smothers Mary Dalton in Chicago and is defended by Boris Max; its three books are Fear, Flight, and Fate.
- Bigger Thomas | Fearful protagonist who kills Mary Dalton and his girlfriend Bessie Mears in Native Son; defended by lawyer Boris Max, sentenced to death.
- Black Boy | Wright's memoir of poverty and violence in Jim Crow Mississippi and Memphis; its long-suppressed second part, American Hunger, covers Chicago's Communist Party.
Ralph Ellison = Ralph Waldo Ellison | Author of Invisible Man and the essay collection Shadow and Act; the posthumous novel Juneteenth was assembled from decades of drafts.
- Invisible Man | 1952 novel of an unnamed narrator's journey from a Southern "battle royal" through the Brotherhood in Harlem, ending underground in a room lit by 1,369 bulbs.
James Baldwin = James Arthur Baldwin | Essayist-novelist of Go Tell It on the Mountain, Giovanni's Room, and The Fire Next Time; the story "Sonny's Blues" is a stock short-fiction clue.
- Go Tell It on the Mountain | Semi-autobiographical novel of John Grimes's religious awakening in a Harlem church on his fourteenth birthday; his abusive stepfather is preacher Gabriel.
- Giovanni's Room | Novel of the American David's Paris affair with the Italian bartender Giovanni, executed for murder, while his fiancee Hella is away in Spain.
- The Fire Next Time | Two-part essay collection: "My Dungeon Shook," a letter to Baldwin's nephew, and "Down at the Cross," recounting a dinner with Elijah Muhammad.
- "Sonny's Blues" | Story of a Harlem algebra teacher whose heroin-addicted jazz-pianist brother Sonny plays with Creole's band in a Greenwich Village club.
- "Notes of a Native Son" | Title essay of Baldwin's 1955 collection; recalls his father's funeral coinciding with the 1943 Harlem race riot and a jug of water thrown at a waitress.
Gwendolyn Brooks | First Black Pulitzer winner, for Annie Allen; "We Real Cool" ("We / Die soon") and the Chicago world of A Street in Bronzeville dominate clues.
- "We Real Cool" | Eight-line poem on "the pool players, Seven at the Golden Shovel," each line but the last ending in "We"; inspired Terrance Hayes's golden shovel form.
Robert Hayden | First Black poet to hold the Library of Congress poetry consultantship; "Those Winter Sundays" ("love's austere and lonely offices") and the slave-ship poem "Middle Passage" are the clues.
- "Those Winter Sundays" | Poem on a father who rose early to heat the house in "blueblack cold," his "love's austere and lonely offices" going unthanked.
Amiri Baraka | Black Arts Movement founder, formerly LeRoi Jones: the play Dutchman, in which Lula murders Clay on a subway, and the controversial poem "Somebody Blew Up America."
Ishmael Reed | Black Arts-adjacent postmodern satirist; coined "Neo-HooDooism" and the "neo-slave narrative" (Flight to Canada), with the houngan Papa LaBas recurring across several novels.
- Mumbo Jumbo | Novel of Papa LaBas battling the secret Wallflower Order to stop "Jes Grew," a virus spreading 1920s Black cultural and musical expression.
Toni Morrison | Nobel laureate whose Beloved, Song of Solomon, The Bluest Eye, Sula, and Jazz make her the most-asked postwar American novelist.
- Beloved | Morrison's novel of Sethe, haunted at 124 Bluestone Road by the daughter she killed rather than return to slavery; Paul D, Denver, and the Sweet Home plantation are stock clues.
- Sethe | Beloved's protagonist, modeled on Margaret Garner; kills her daughter with a saw rather than let the Schoolteacher return them to the Sweet Home plantation.
- 124 Bluestone Road | Beloved's haunted Cincinnati address, "spiteful" and "full of a baby's venom," which scares off Sethe's sons Howard and Buglar.
- Paul D | Beloved's former Sweet Home fellow slave who becomes Sethe's lover at 124 and drives out the house's ghost.
- Song of Solomon | Morrison's novel of Milkman Dead's journey south to trace the flying African Solomon; his aunt Pilate, born without a navel, and the insurance agent's opening leap are clues.
- milkman | Common-link nickname: Macon "Milkman" Dead III, who searches for Pilate's gold in Song of Solomon, and the title stalker of Anna Burns's Troubles-era Booker winner Milkman.
- The Bluest Eye | Morrison's first novel: Pecola Breedlove prays for blue eyes in Lorain, Ohio; partly narrated by Claudia MacTeer and framed by a distorted Dick-and-Jane primer.
- Sula | Novel of Sula Peace and Nel Wright's friendship in the Bottom; Shadrack's yearly "National Suicide Day" and the drowning of Chicken Little recur.
- Sula Peace | Title character who drowns Chicken Little as a child, sleeps with Nel's husband Jude, and returns to Medallion amid a "plague of robins."
Alice Walker | Author of The Color Purple; the story "Everyday Use," the essays of In Search of Our Mothers' Gardens, and her role reviving Hurston's reputation also appear.
- The Color Purple | Walker's epistolary Pulitzer winner: Celie's letters to God and from her missionary sister Nettie in Africa, with blues singer Shug Avery and the abusive Mister.
Audre Lorde | Self-described "Black, lesbian, mother, warrior, poet"; the essay "The Master's Tools Will Never Dismantle the Master's House," The Cancer Journals, and the "biomythography" Zami.
Maya Angelou | Memoirist-poet of I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings; "Still I Rise," "Phenomenal Woman," and reading "On the Pulse of Morning" at Clinton's inauguration recur.
- I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings | First of Angelou's seven memoirs: her Stamps, Arkansas childhood, rape by Mr. Freeman that leaves her mute, and becoming San Francisco's first Black streetcar conductor.
- "On the Pulse of Morning" | Inaugural poem urging listeners to "lift up your eyes" and "lift up your hearts," spoken by "A Rock, A River, A Tree"; read at Clinton's 1993 inauguration.
Nikki Giovanni | Black Arts poet of "Nikki-Rosa" and "Knoxville, Tennessee"; delivered the "We Are Virginia Tech" chant-poem after the 2007 campus shooting.
Terrance Hayes | Poet of Lighthead, winner of the 2010 National Book Award, and American Sonnets for My Past and Future Assassin; invented the golden shovel form from Brooks's "We Real Cool."
Claudia Rankine | Poet-essayist of the book-length "American Lyric" Citizen, addressing Serena Williams and Trayvon Martin in the second person; also wrote Just Us.
Octavia Butler | Afrofuturist author of Kindred and the Earthseed novels Parable of the Sower and Parable of the Talents; also wrote the novella "Bloodchild."
- Kindred | Neo-slave narrative in which writer Dana time-travels from 1970s Los Angeles to save her slaveholding ancestor Rufus Weylin on a Maryland plantation.
Colson Whitehead | Back-to-back Pulitzer winner for The Underground Railroad, which makes the railroad literal, and The Nickel Boys, based on the Dozier School; earlier wrote The Intuitionist and Zone One.
- The Intuitionist | Debut novel of Lila Mae Watson, the first Black female elevator inspector, whose intuitionist school of inspection rivals the rationalist Empiricists.
Jesmyn Ward | Mississippi novelist of Salvage the Bones and the National Book Award-winning Sing, Unburied, Sing; also edited the essay collection The Fire This Time.
- Salvage the Bones | Novel of pregnant teenager Esch Batiste and her brother Skeetah's prize pit bull China, set around Hurricane Katrina's landfall.
Percival Everett | Metafictional satirist of Erasure, whose academic Thelonious "Monk" Ellison pens the parody novel "My Pafology"; also wrote Dr. No and the Huck Finn retelling James.
James McBride | Author of the memoir The Color of Water, the John Brown novel The Good Lord Bird, and Deacon King Kong, set in a Brooklyn housing project.
Ta-Nehisi Coates | Atlantic writer of Between the World and Me, a letter to his son Samori, and the essay "The Case for Reparations"; also wrote the novel The Water Dancer.
The Sellout | Paul Beatty's 2016 Booker winner: the narrator tries reintroducing segregation to the disincorporated Los Angeles ghetto of Dickens and keeps Hominy as a slave.
A Lesson Before Dying | Ernest Gaines's novel of schoolteacher Grant Wiggins mentoring the wrongly condemned Jefferson, urged to die "like a man," not a "hog."
bell hooks | Feminist theorist, pen name lowercase to honor her great-grandmother; wrote Ain't I a Woman?, Teaching to Transgress, and All About Love.
Henry Louis Gates | Harvard critic of The Signifying Monkey, which theorizes Black "double-voiced" texts via the trickster figure Esu-Elegbara; co-edited the Norton Anthology of African-American Literature.
