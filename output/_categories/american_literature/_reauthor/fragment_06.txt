# Modernist Poetry
> The early twentieth century's break from Victorian verse, from Pound's Imagist manifesto and H.D.'s crystalline lyrics through the high modernism of Eliot, Stevens, and Moore to Frost's plainspoken New England voice.

Ezra Pound = Ezra Weston Loomis Pound | Imagist impresario who edited The Waste Land: "In a Station of the Metro," Hugh Selwyn Mauberley, and The Cantos; his fascist broadcasts and confinement at St. Elizabeths recur.
- The Cantos | Pound's sprawling, unfinished 116-plus-section epic, opening with a translation of Odysseus's descent to the underworld; includes the usura canto and the Pisan Cantos, written in a detention camp.
Imagism | Pound-launched movement demanding "direct treatment of the thing"; anthologized in Des Imagistes and later led by Amy Lowell ("Amygism"); H.D. and "In a Station of the Metro" are stock clues.
H.D. = H. D. | Pen name of Hilda Doolittle, signed "H.D. Imagiste" by Pound; crystalline lyrics like "Oread" and "Sea Rose" plus the later epics Trilogy and Helen in Egypt appear.
- asphodel | H.D.'s Madrigal-cycle autobiographical novel and its title flower, linked to Williams's "Asphodel, That Greeny Flower" and the underworld.
Amy Lowell | Boston Imagist poet-critic who succeeded Pound as movement leader ("Amygism"); pioneered free-verse "polyphonic prose."
- "Patterns" | Lowell poem of a woman in a formal garden mourning her fiancé's death in war; ends "Christ! What are patterns for?"

William Carlos Williams | Rutherford physician-poet of "The Red Wheelbarrow," "This Is Just to Say," Spring and All, and the epic Paterson; his slogan was "no ideas but in things."
- "Asphodel, That Greeny Flower" | Williams's late long poem addressed to his wife Flossie, recalling Helen of Troy and learning "there were flowers also in hell."
- Paterson | Williams's epic poem-in-prose of the New Jersey city and the Passaic Falls, embodying his credo "no ideas but in things."
- "no ideas but in things" | Williams's five-word poetic credo from Paterson's preface, repeated in the poem after the command "Say it!"
- "The Red Wheelbarrow" | Sixteen-word imagist poem: "so much depends / upon / a red wheel / barrow," glazed with rainwater beside white chickens.

Marianne Moore | Precise syllabic-verse modernist; "Poetry" ("imaginary gardens with real toads in them"), "The Fish," and her tricorn hat and Brooklyn Dodgers fandom are clues.
- "Poetry" = poetry | Moore's poem opening "I, too, dislike it"; her 1967 revision cut it to just three lines, ending on "a place for the genuine."
- "The Steeple Jack" -> "The Steeple-Jack" | Moore poem of a church spire re-gilded in a seaside town amid stranded whales; opens "Dürer would have seen a reason for living in a town like this."

Wallace Stevens | Hartford insurance executive; "The Emperor of Ice-Cream," "Thirteen Ways of Looking at a Blackbird," "Anecdote of the Jar," "Sunday Morning," and the collection Harmonium recur constantly.
- "Sunday Morning" = Sunday morning | Stevens poem in which a woman lounging with coffee and oranges skips church; ends with "casual flocks of pigeons" sinking "downward to darkness, on extended wings."
- Harmonium | Stevens's 1923 debut collection, containing "Sunday Morning," "The Emperor of Ice-Cream," and "Peter Quince at the Clavier."
- "The Emperor of Ice Cream" -> "The Emperor of Ice-Cream" | Stevens poem set at a wake, commanding "Let be be finale of seem" and declaring ice cream "the only emperor."
- "Peter Quince at the Clavier" | Stevens poem retelling Susanna and the Elders through a musician-narrator; declares "Music is feeling, then, not sound."
- "Thirteen Ways of Looking at a Blackbird" | Stevens's thirteen-stanza sequence of aphoristic vignettes, each a distinct perspective on the title bird.
- "The Auroras of Autumn" | Ten-canto title poem of Stevens's last original collection, opening "Farewell to an idea" and meditating on the Northern Lights.
- "The Idea of Order at Key West" | Stevens poem of a woman singing by the sea while the speaker asks Ramon Fernandez about the "maker's rage to order."

T. S. Eliot = T.S. Eliot | St. Louis-born, British-naturalized modernist of "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock," The Waste Land, and Four Quartets.
- "Preludes" | Eliot's four-part early poem of dingy urban imagery, opening at "six o'clock" on a winter evening; ends "gathering fuel in vacant lots."
- Sweeney | Eliot's recurring "apeneck" antihero of "Sweeney Erect," "Sweeney Among the Nightingales," and the unfinished verse drama Sweeney Agonistes.

Robert Frost = Robert Lee Frost | New England poet of "The Road Not Taken," "Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening," "Mending Wall," "Birches," and "Fire and Ice"; recited at Kennedy's inauguration.
- "The Death of the Hired Man" | Frost dialogue in which Warren and Mary debate taking back the dying hired man Silas; "home is the place where... they have to take you in."
- "Mending Wall" | Frost poem of neighbors annually repairing a stone wall; the speaker questions it, but the neighbor insists "good fences make good neighbors."
- "Birches" | Frost poem imagining a boy swinging on birch trees bent by ice storms; the speaker wishes to "get away from earth awhile."
- "Home Burial" | Frost poem of a husband and wife's icy dialogue after burying their dead child; ends with his threat "I'll follow and bring you back by force."
- "Out, Out-" | Frost poem, titled from Macbeth's "tomorrow and tomorrow" speech, of a boy who bleeds to death after a buzz saw severs his hand.
- "The Gift Outright" | Frost poem beginning "The land was ours before we were the land's"; recited from memory at JFK's 1961 inauguration.
- North of Boston | Frost's second collection (1914), containing "Mending Wall," "The Death of the Hired Man," and "Home Burial"; established his reputation.
- "Directive" | Late Frost poem guiding the reader back to a ruined farmhouse and playhouse; ends "Drink and be whole again beyond confusion."
- "Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening" | Frost poem of a traveler pausing by "the darkest evening of the year"; ends with "miles to go before I sleep."

Edwin Arlington Robinson = Edward Arlington Robinson | Poet of fictional Tilbury Town: "Richard Cory," who "went home and put a bullet through his head," plus "Miniver Cheevy" and "Mr. Flood's Party."
- "Eros Turannos" | Robinson poem of a woman's fear-driven, loveless marriage, its title Greek for "Love the Tyrant."
- "Luke Havergal" | Robinson poem in which a mysterious voice at "the western gate" summons the grieving title man toward his dead love.
- "Miniver Cheevy" | Robinson poem of a man who romanticizes the medieval past and Medici Florence, resents modernity, and "coughed, and called it fate."
- Tilbury Town | Robinson's fictional New England town, based on his native Gardiner, Maine; setting for "Miniver Cheevy," "Richard Cory," and other character portraits.

Edgar Lee Masters | Poet of the fictional Illinois town Spoon River, whose dead residents narrate their lives as verse epitaphs in Spoon River Anthology.
- Spoon River Anthology | Masters's collection of free-verse epitaphs spoken by Spoon River's dead, opening "All, all are sleeping on the hill."
- Spoon River | Masters's fictional Midwestern town, home to Anne Rutledge, Lucinda Matlock, and Jonathan Swift Somers's mock-epic within Spoon River Anthology.

Vachel Lindsay | Performer of chanted "singing poetry": "The Congo," "General William Booth Enters into Heaven," and "Abraham Lincoln Walks at Midnight"; he discovered Langston Hughes waiting tables.
Carl Sandburg | Chicago poet of "Chicago" ("City of the Big Shoulders") and "Fog" ("comes on little cat feet"); also wrote a multivolume Lincoln biography.
Stephen Vincent Benet | Author of the Pulitzer-winning Civil War verse epic John Brown's Body and the story "The Devil and Daniel Webster," in which Webster out-argues Mr. Scratch before a jury of the damned.
Archibald MacLeish | Poet-Librarian of Congress; "Ars Poetica" ("A poem should not mean / But be"), the Pulitzer epic Conquistador, and the Job-based verse play J.B.

Hart Crane = Harold Hart Crane | Visionary poet of The Bridge, addressed to the Brooklyn Bridge, and White Buildings; died jumping from the steamship Orizaba while returning from Mexico.
- The Bridge | Crane's epic poem addressed to the Brooklyn Bridge in its "Proem," with sections including "Powhatan's Daughter" and "Cape Hatteras."

E. E. Cummings = e e cummings = Edward Estlin Cummings | Lowercase typographical experimenter: "anyone lived in a pretty how town," "in Just-," "l(a," and the World War I internment memoir The Enormous Room.
- "i sing of olaf glad and big" | Cummings poem of a conscientious objector tortured by the army who declares "there is some shit I will not eat."
- capitalization | Typographical convention Cummings's poetry and penname largely eschew, alongside conventional punctuation and syntax.

Louis Zukofsky | Objectivist poet who edited Poetry's 1931 "Objectivists" issue; spent five decades on his book-length long poem "A."
- "A" | Zukofsky's book-length long poem written over five decades, its twenty-four sections corresponding to the hours of the day.

Robinson Jeffers | California poet who built Tor House and Hawk Tower near Carmel; his "inhumanist" narrative poems include "Roan Stallion."

Edna St. Vincent Millay | Sonneteer of Greenwich Village bohemia: "First Fig" ("My candle burns at both ends"), "Renascence," and the Pulitzer-winning The Ballad of the Harp-Weaver.
- "Renascence" | Millay's breakthrough long poem, written as a teenager, of a mystical near-burial vision that expands the speaker's sense of infinity.

Dorothy Parker | Algonquin Round Table wit; the story "Big Blonde," the poem "Résumé" ("you might as well live"), and her acid Constant Reader reviews fill questions.
Ogden Nash | Light-verse humorist of mismatched comic rhymes and couplets, such as "Candy / Is dandy / But liquor / Is quicker."

W. H. Auden = W.H. Auden = Wystan Hugh Auden | British-born poet who became a U.S. citizen and is asked as American literature: "Musée des Beaux Arts," "Funeral Blues," "The Unknown Citizen," and The Age of Anxiety.
- "September 1, 1939" | Auden's poem written "in one of the dives / on Fifty-second Street" at the outbreak of World War II; "We must love one another or die" is the famous line he later disowned.
