# Colonial and Early National Literature
> Colonial Puritan verse and early national prose forge an American voice, from Bradstreet's devotional poetry through Irving's and Cooper's frontier tales.

Anne Bradstreet | Puritan poet, the first published poet of the American colonies; questions cite The Tenth Muse Lately Sprung Up in America, "To My Dear and Loving Husband," and "Upon the Burning of Our House."
- Simon Bradstreet = Anne Bradstreet's husband | Her husband, addressed in "To My Dear and Loving Husband" and "A Letter to Her Husband, Absent upon Publick Employment"; subject of a John Berryman homage.
Edward Taylor | Puritan minister-poet unpublished until the 20th century; "Huswifery" ("Make me, O Lord, thy spinning wheel complete") and his Preparatory Meditations exemplify his knotty "American Baroque" style.
Philip Freneau | "Poet of the American Revolution"; wrote "The Wild Honey Suckle," the captivity account The British Prison-Ship, and the graveyard meditation "The Indian Burying Ground."
- "The Indian Burying Ground" | Poem on a Native burial site where the dead sit upright; "Reason's self shall bow the knee / To shadows and delusions" recurs in clues.
Joel Barlow | Early national poet-diplomat; wrote the mock-epic "The Hasty Pudding" praising corn and the more ambitious epic The Columbiad on Christopher Columbus.
Mercy Otis Warren | Revolutionary-era playwright and historian; satirized Massachusetts governor Thomas Hutchinson as "Rapatio" in The Adulateur, and wrote an early History of the American Revolution.
Benjamin Franklin | Wrote under pseudonyms like "Silence Dogood" and "Poor Richard"; Poor Richard's Almanack and his posthumously published Autobiography are his most-clued works.
Charles Brockden Brown | Early American Gothic novelist; in Wieland the biloquist Carwin dupes siblings Clara and Theodore, while Edgar Huntly and the yellow-fever novel Arthur Mervyn are also clued.
Gothic fiction | Common-link genre spanning Brockden Brown's Wieland, its European models Walpole and Radcliffe, and later American horror writers like Poe.
Washington Irving | Early national sketch writer behind "Rip Van Winkle" and "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow" from The Sketch Book; also wrote A History of New York as Diedrich Knickerbocker.
- "Rip Van Winkle" | Henpecked villager who drinks with Hendrick Hudson's ghostly crew and sleeps twenty years, waking after the American Revolution.
- "The Devil and Tom Walker" | Miser Tom Walker trades his soul to "Old Scratch" for Captain Kidd's buried treasure and becomes a ruthless moneylender.
James Fenimore Cooper | Author of the Leatherstocking Tales starring Natty Bumppo — The Last of the Mohicans, The Deerslayer, The Pioneers; Twain's essay on his "literary offenses" is a stock clue.
- The Last of the Mohicans | Second Leatherstocking Tale; Magua's massacre outside Fort William Henry and the clifftop deaths of Uncas and Cora close the novel.
- Natty Bumppo | Cooper's frontiersman hero, called Hawkeye, Deerslayer, or the trapper across the five Leatherstocking Tales; travels with Mohican companion Chingachgook.
- The Deerslayer | Earliest-set Leatherstocking Tale; young Bumppo earns his nickname at Glimmerglass Lake alongside Hurry Harry and the Hutter sisters, Hetty and Judith.
Susanna Rowson | First bestselling American novelist; her sentimental Charlotte Temple, about a schoolgirl seduced by John Montraville, was often called the country's first novel.
- Charlotte Temple | Title schoolgirl seduced by soldier John Montraville and abandoned in New York, dying after giving birth to her daughter Lucy.

# Transcendentalism and the American Renaissance
> Poe, Hawthorne, and Melville plumb sin and obsession in the Dark Romantic mode, while Emerson, Thoreau, and Margaret Fuller's Transcendentalist circle prizes self-reliant intuition.

Transcendentalism | Idealist literary movement centered on Emerson, Thoreau, and Fuller's Dial circle and the utopian Brook Farm community; stressed intuition and the individual soul over doctrine.
Ralph Waldo Emerson | Central Transcendentalist essayist; "Self-Reliance," Nature with its "transparent eyeball," "The American Scholar," and the Divinity School Address dominate clues.
- "Self-Reliance" | Emerson's essay urging nonconformity and self-trust ("Trust thyself: every heart vibrates to that iron string"); with Nature, his best-known work.
Henry David Thoreau | Transcendentalist who lived at Walden Pond; Walden and "Civil Disobedience" provide nearly all clues, often alongside his night in jail and Emerson's patronage.
- Walden | Thoreau's memoir of his two years, two months, and two days at Walden Pond, including the chapters "Economy" and "Brute Neighbors"; champions living "deliberately."
- Walden Pond | Concord pond, owned by Emerson, where Thoreau built his cabin and watched the "Battle of the Ants."
- "Civil Disobedience" | Essay defending refusal to pay taxes that support slavery and the Mexican War, inspired by Thoreau's night in jail.
Margaret Fuller | Transcendentalist first editor of The Dial; wrote the feminist Woman in the Nineteenth Century (expanded from "The Great Lawsuit") and the travelogue Summer on the Lakes.
Louisa May Alcott | Author of Little Women and its sequels; questions cite the four March sisters, Hospital Sketches, and the sensation fiction she published as A. M. Barnard.
- Little Women | Novel of the four March sisters' Civil War-era coming of age; Jo rejects Laurie's proposal and marries Professor Bhaer instead.
- Josephine March | Tomboyish second March sister who sells her hair for money, burns Amy's manuscript, and later runs the Plumfield school for boys.
- March family | Common-link family of sisters Meg, Jo, Beth, and Amy in Little Women; Beth dies of scarlet fever after delivering charity to the Hummels.
Edgar Allan Poe = Edgar Allen Poe | Master of Gothic tales and musical verse: "The Raven," "The Tell-Tale Heart," "The Fall of the House of Usher," and "The Cask of Amontillado," plus detective Auguste Dupin.
- "Ligeia" | Opium-addled narrator's dead first wife Ligeia returns from beyond, reincarnated in his second wife Rowena's body; recites the poem "The Conqueror Worm."
- "The Conqueror Worm" | Poem embedded in "Ligeia" describing angels who watch a "blood-red thing" devour actors in a theater; ends, "the hero [is] the Conqueror Worm."
- the house of Usher | Ancestral mansion of twins Roderick and Madeline in "The Fall of the House of Usher"; collapses into a tarn after Madeline's premature burial.
- "The Cask of Amontillado" | Montresor lures the boastful Fortunato into the catacombs with promised wine and walls him up alive in revenge.
- "The Pit and the Pendulum" | Inquisition prisoner narrowly escapes a swinging blade and closing walls before General Lasalle's army frees him from Toledo.
- "A Descent into the Maelstrom" | Fisherman narrator's hair turns white surviving a Norwegian whirlpool by lashing himself to a cask, unlike his drowned brother.
- "The Imp of the Perverse" | Essay-turned-story on the perverse urge to self-destruct; its narrator murders with a poisoned candle, then confesses despite getting away with it.
- "The Bells" | Onomatopoeic poem coining "tintinnabulation," cataloguing silver, golden, brazen, and iron bells across four stanzas of mounting terror.
- "The Raven" | Grieving narrator questions a talking raven perched on a bust of Pallas about his lost love Lenore; it answers only "Nevermore."
- C. Auguste Dupin | Poe's "ratiocination"-practicing detective of "The Murders in the Rue Morgue," "The Purloined Letter," and "The Mystery of Marie Rogêt."
- The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym of Nantucket | Poe's sole completed novel, ending abruptly as a shrouded white giant appears near the South Pole; Toni Morrison's Playing in the Dark reads its racial imagery.
- Arthur Gordon Pym | Poe's only novel's title character, a Nantucket stowaway; survives mutiny and cannibalism before the eerie white figure appears near the South Pole.
Nathaniel Hawthorne | Dark romancer of Puritan New England: The Scarlet Letter, The House of the Seven Gables, and tales like "Young Goodman Brown," "The Minister's Black Veil," and "Rappaccini's Daughter."
- The Scarlet Letter | Hawthorne's novel of Hester Prynne, Pearl, Dimmesdale, and Chillingworth in Puritan Boston; the three scaffold scenes and "The Custom-House" introduction recur in clues.
- Hester Prynne | Adulteress who embroiders the scarlet "A," raises daughter Pearl, and shields former lover Dimmesdale until his scaffold confession and death.
- "The Custom House" | Autobiographical preface to The Scarlet Letter; narrator finds Surveyor Jonathan Pue's manuscript and a scarlet "A" while working at the Salem customhouse.
- The House of the Seven Gables | Novel of the cursed Pyncheon family mansion, the aged Hepzibah, and her cousin Judge Pyncheon's mysterious death in his ancestral chair.
- "Rappaccini's Daughter" | Padua tale of Beatrice, poisoned by her father Dr. Rappaccini's toxic garden, who dies drinking a rival scientist's antidote.
- "Young Goodman Brown" | Puritan Goodman Brown leaves wife Faith for a satanic forest ritual, leaving him unable to trust his neighbors' piety afterward.
- "The Minister's Black Veil" | Reverend Hooper dons an unexplained black veil for life, unsettling his congregation and fiancée Elizabeth until his deathbed.
- "The Birth-Mark" | Scientist Aylmer, aided by servant Aminadab, kills his wife Georgiana trying to remove her "Crimson Hand" birthmark with an elixir.
- "The May-Pole of Merry Mount" | Puritan leader John Endicott cuts down the title Maypole and breaks up the wedding revels of Edgar and Edith.
- "Dr. Heidegger's Experiment" = Dr. Heidegger | Doctor gives elderly friends Fountain-of-Youth water, reviving a withered rose and their youth before both fade again; owns a "Forbear!"-uttering Hippocrates bust.
Herman Melville | Author of Moby-Dick, Billy Budd, "Bartleby, the Scrivener," and "Benito Cereno"; clues range from the early Polynesian novels Typee and Omoo to The Confidence-Man.
- Moby-Dick | Melville's whaling epic narrated by Ishmael; Ahab, Queequeg, Starbuck, the Pequod, and chapters like "The Whiteness of the Whale" are stock clues.
- Ishmael | Narrator of Moby-Dick ("Call me Ishmael"), a damp-November misanthrope who befriends Queequeg and survives the Pequod's sinking by clinging to a coffin.
- Captain Ahab | Monomaniacal, peg-legged Pequod captain hunting Moby Dick for biting off his leg; dies harpooning the whale, strangled by his own line.
- Queequeg | Tattooed Pacific islander harpooner and prince of Kokovoko who worships the idol Yojo; his coffin later saves Ishmael's life.
- Benito Cereno | Novella in which Amasa Delano boards the San Dominick without realizing captain Cereno has been deposed by Babo's slave revolt.
- Billy Budd = Billy Budd, Sailor | Handsome sailor impressed onto the Bellipotent who fatally strikes master-at-arms John Claggart after a false mutiny accusation, then hangs.
- John Claggart | Bellipotent master-at-arms who falsely accuses Billy Budd of mutiny and is killed by Budd's punch; Melville's narrator cites his "natural depravity."
- "Bartleby, the Scrivener: A Story of Wall Street" | Wall Street copyist who "would prefer not to" work, and eventually starves in the Tombs; the narrator recalls his rumored Dead Letter Office past.
- The Piazza Tales | Melville story collection including "Benito Cereno," "Bartleby, the Scrivener," "The Bell-Tower," and the Galapagos sketches "The Encantadas."
