# Postwar Fiction (1945-1980)
> Fiction's postwar boom: suburban short stories give way to Jewish-American social novels, postmodern maximalism, and Vietnam-era New Journalism blurring fact and fiction.

J. D. Salinger | Reclusive author of The Catcher in the Rye and the Glass family stories — Franny and Zooey and "A Perfect Day for Bananafish," ending in Seymour's suicide.
- "A Perfect Day for Bananafish" | Opening story of Nine Stories: Seymour Glass talks to young Sybil Carpenter about bananafish on a Florida beach, then shoots himself.
- "Uncle Wiggily in Connecticut" | Nine Stories entry in which Eloise Wengler, drinking with old college friend Mary Jane, mourns her wartime love Walt while daughter Ramona plays.
- Nine Stories | Collection opening with "A Perfect Day for Bananafish" and including "For Esme — with Love and Squalor" and "Uncle Wiggily in Connecticut."
- Seymour Glass | Eldest, spiritually precocious Glass sibling who describes the title creatures to Sybil Carpenter on the beach before shooting himself in "Bananafish."
- bananafish | Fictional fish that gorge on bananas in underwater holes until too fat to escape, described by Seymour Glass to Sybil Carpenter.
- Esme | Young English girl who befriends a traumatized American soldier before D-Day in "For Esme — with Love and Squalor."
- Franny Glass | Youngest Glass sibling whose breakdown over The Way of a Pilgrim, recited to boyfriend Lane Coutell, is chronicled in Franny and Zooey.
- Holden Caulfield | Alienated seventeen-year-old narrator of The Catcher in the Rye, disgusted by "phonies" during his two-day wander through New York City.
John Cheever | "Chekhov of the suburbs": stories like "The Swimmer," "The Enormous Radio," and "The Five-Forty-Eight," plus the Wapshot novels and prison novel Falconer.
- "The Swimmer" | Neddy Merrill swims home across the county through the "Lucinda River" of backyard pools, arriving at an empty, locked house as the seasons inexplicably turn.
- "The Enormous Radio" | Jim and Irene Westcott's new radio lets them eavesdrop on neighbors' secrets, curdling their own "satisfactory" marriage.
- Shady Hill | Cheever's fictional Westchester suburb, setting of "The Country Husband," "The Five-Forty-Eight," and "The Housebreaker of Shady Hill."
Saul Bellow | Nobel-winning novelist of Jewish Chicago: The Adventures of Augie March, Herzog with its unsent letters, Humboldt's Gift, Seize the Day, and Henderson the Rain King.
- Herzog | Divorced academic Moses Herzog writes unsent letters to Nietzsche, Eisenhower, and the dead after his wife Madeleine leaves him for his friend Valentine Gersbach.
- The Adventures of Augie March | Picaresque bildungsroman opening "I am an American, Chicago born"; the title character survives a torpedoed ship and trains an eagle in Mexico.
- Augie March | Bellow's picaresque title character, raised by Grandma Lausch and shaped by Einhorn; cites Heraclitus while declaring himself "an American, Chicago born."
- Eugene Henderson | Millionaire pig farmer who travels to Africa, blows up a cistern of frogs, and earns the title "Rain King" from the Wariri tribe.
- Henderson the Rain King | Novel following Eugene Henderson's African journey with guide Romilayu and King Dahfu, moving a statue of the goddess Mummah.
Philip Roth | Chronicler of Jewish Newark and alter ego Nathan Zuckerman: Portnoy's Complaint, American Pastoral, The Human Stain, Goodbye, Columbus, and The Plot Against America.
- Portnoy's Complaint | Roth's 1969 succes de scandale: Alexander Portnoy's unbroken monologue to psychoanalyst Dr. Spielvogel about his mother Sophie and his compulsions; the liver scene is infamous.
- American Pastoral | Pulitzer-winning novel in which Nathan Zuckerman recounts Seymour "Swede" Levov's shattered idyll after daughter Merry bombs a post office over Vietnam.
- The Human Stain | Classics professor Coleman Silk, fired over the word "spooks," is revealed by narrator Nathan Zuckerman to be a black man passing as white.
- Goodbye, Columbus | Title novella and story collection: Neil Klugman's summer romance with wealthy Brenda Patimkin ends after her parents discover her diaphragm.
- Nathan Zuckerman | Roth's recurring novelist narrator and alter ego, appearing from The Ghost Writer through American Pastoral, The Human Stain, and Exit Ghost.
Thomas Pynchon | Reclusive postmodernist: Gravity's Rainbow, The Crying of Lot 49, V., Mason & Dixon, and Inherent Vice; entropy and paranoia are his signature clue-words.
- Gravity's Rainbow | Pynchon's V-2 rocket epic opening "A screaming comes across the sky": Tyrone Slothrop's conditioned map of London, Rocket 00000, and the postwar Zone.
- The Crying of Lot 49 | Pynchon novella in which Oedipa Maas, executing Pierce Inverarity's estate, uncovers the Trystero and the muted post horn of the underground mail system W.A.S.T.E.
- Bleeding Edge | 2013 novel following fraud investigator Maxine Tarnow's probe of the security firm hashslingrz, set against the backdrop of 9/11.
- "Entropy" | Callisto nurses a dying bird in a hermetically sealed room while Meatball Mulligan's lease-breaking party rages downstairs; applies thermodynamics to culture and communication.
- Against the Day | Sprawling 2006 novel following the airship crew the Chums of Chance across the decades before World War I.
- Mason & Dixon | Novel narrated by Reverend Wicks Cherrycoke, recounting surveyors Mason and Dixon's line with cameos by Franklin and Washington.
- V-2 rockets | Weapons central to Gravity's Rainbow's Tyrone Slothrop plot, from "The White Visitation" research group to Rocket 00000; Pynchon once wrote for their manufacturer.
- V. | Debut novel in which Herbert Stencil searches for the mysterious title figure while Benny Profane drifts through the Whole Sick Crew.
Kurt Vonnegut | Satirist blending science fiction and dark humor: Billy Pilgrim comes "unstuck in time," recurring author-avatar Kilgore Trout, and the alien Tralfamadorians.
- Cat's Cradle | Vonnegut novel of ice-nine, which freezes the world's water; narrator John/Jonah, the invented religion of Bokononism, and the island of San Lorenzo are the clues.
- Slaughterhouse-Five, or The Children's Crusade: A Duty-Dance with Death = Slaughterhouse-Five -> Slaughterhouse-Five | Billy Pilgrim, "unstuck in time," survives the Dresden firebombing and is exhibited by Tralfamadorian aliens with Montana Wildhack.
- Kilgore Trout | Vonnegut's recurring hack science-fiction author, whose fan Eliot Rosewater and book Now It Can Be Told drive other characters' plots.
- Billy Pilgrim | Optometrist and Dresden survivor who comes "unstuck in time" and is abducted by the toilet-plunger-shaped Tralfamadorians.
- Welcome to the Monkey House | Story collection including "Harrison Bergeron" and the title story, in which "Nothinghead" Billy the Poet menaces an Ethical Suicide Parlor hostess.
- Bluebeard | "Autobiography" of Abstract Expressionist painter Rabo Karabekian, who guards his barn mural "Now It's the Women's Turn" from houseguest Circe Berman.
- Breakfast of Champions, or Goodbye Blue Monday = Breakfast of Champions -> Breakfast of Champions | Hack author Kilgore Trout's writing convinces car dealer Dwayne Hoover, in Midland City, that he alone has free will.
- Eliot Rosewater | Philanthropist millionaire and devoted Kilgore Trout fan who recurs across Vonnegut's novels, including his own God Bless You, Mr. Rosewater.
- Trout | Recurring Vonnegut surname: hack author Kilgore, his son Leon who narrates Galapagos, and other family across Vonnegut's fictional universe.
Norman Mailer | New Journalism brawler: The Naked and the Dead, the Pulitzer-winning The Armies of the Night, and The Executioner's Song, about Gary Gilmore's execution.
- "The White Negro: Superficial Reflections on the Hipster" | Essay casting the "hipster" as existentialist outsider fusing jazz, danger, and the language of Hip; collected in Advertisements for Myself.
- The Executioner's Song | Pulitzer-winning "true life novel" of Gary Gilmore, executed by firing squad in Utah after demanding his own death sentence be carried out.
Tom Wolfe | White-suited New Journalist: The Electric Kool-Aid Acid Test, The Right Stuff, and the novel The Bonfire of the Vanities with Sherman McCoy, self-styled "Master of the Universe."
- The Bonfire of the Vanities | Bond trader Sherman McCoy's hit-and-run of Henry Lamb in the Bronx, covered by journalist Peter Fallow, destroys his Wall Street status.
- I am Charlotte Simmons | Campus novel following a scholarship student's disillusionment at fictional Dupont University amid Saint Ray fraternity antics and "Fuck Patois."
Joan Didion | Essayist of California unraveling: Slouching Towards Bethlehem, The White Album, the novel Play It as It Lays, and the memoir The Year of Magical Thinking.
- "The White Album" = The White Album -> "The White Album" | Essay collection whose title essay recalls a Doors recording session and Manson witness Linda Kasabian; opens "We tell ourselves stories in order to live."
- Slouching Towards Bethlehem | Essay collection on 1960s California unraveling; title essay profiles Haight-Ashbury runaways Jeff and Debbie as "the center was not holding."
- The Year of Magical Thinking | Memoir of grief after husband John Gregory Dunne's sudden death; Didion keeps his shoes, unable to shake the belief he'll return.
