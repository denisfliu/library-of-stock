# Places in American Literature

Coney Island | Common-link Brooklyn amusement district: Ferlinghetti's *A Coney Island of the Mind*, Schwartz's "In Dreams Begin Responsibilities," Heller's memoir *Then and Now*.
Chinatown | Common-link ethnic enclave: Frank Chin's *The Year of the Dragon*, Charles Yu's screenplay-format novel, Polanski's neo-noir film of the same name.
Hollywood | Common-link film-industry setting: West's *The Day of the Locust*, Fitzgerald's unfinished *The Last Tycoon*, Didion's *Play It as It Lays*.
Indian reservation | Common-link Native setting: Sherman Alexie's Spokane-reservation stories, Erdrich's North Dakota novels, Momaday's *House Made of Dawn*.
Alexandria | Egyptian city common-linking Cavafy's poems, Mark Doty's AIDS-elegy collection *My Alexandria*, and Durrell's *Alexandria Quartet*.
Egypt | Common-link setting of H.D.'s *Helen in Egypt* and *Kora in Hell*, and of Naguib Mahfouz's Cairo-set novels.
Argentina | Common-link gaucho-literature homeland of Güiraldes's *Don Segundo Sombra*, Ernesto Sábato, and Manuel Puig.
Japan | Common-link home country of Mishima, Ōe, and Dazai, also invoked in WWII bombing poems by James Dickey and John Hersey's *Hiroshima*.

# History and Culture through Literature

The New York Times | Newspaper of record clued via its crossword, style magazine, and the viral 2021 article "Who Is the Bad Art Friend?"
The Paris Review | Literary magazine whose "Writers at Work"/"Art of Fiction" interview series and 2017 editorial scandal are common-link subjects.
assassination of Abraham Lincoln | Elegized in Whitman's "O Captain! My Captain!" and "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd"; dramatized in Parks's *Topdog/Underdog* and *Oh, Mary!*.
Captain William Kidd | Executed pirate whose buried-treasure legend inspires Irving's "The Devil and Tom Walker" and Poe's "The Gold-Bug."
The Vietnam War | Common-link conflict spanning O'Brien's *The Things They Carried*, Denis Johnson's "Emergency," Rabe's war-plays trilogy, and Lauro's *A Piece of My Heart*.
1980s | Common-link AIDS-crisis decade of Kushner's *Angels in America*, Kramer's *The Normal Heart*, Wasserstein's *The Heidi Chronicles*, Henley's *Crimes of the Heart*.
jazz | Common-link art form in Morrison's *Jazz*, Petry's *The Street*, Walker's *The Color Purple*, and Ellison's essay collection *Living with Music*.
Bob Dylan | Nobel Prize-winning singer-songwriter; clued via his Beat-influenced lyrics, "Brownsville Girl" co-write with Sam Shepard, and the 2016 Literature Prize controversy.
Frank Sinatra | Singer profiled without an interview in Gay Talese's "Frank Sinatra Has a Cold," a founding text of New Journalism.
Elaine Showalter | American feminist critic who coined "gynocriticism"; author of *A Literature of Their Own* and *The Female Malady*.

# Words, Names, and Things

Susan | Bare first name for Susan Glaspell, author of *Trifles* and *Alison's House*, co-founder of the Provincetown Players.
William | Bare first name clued mainly via William Faulkner (*As I Lay Dying*, *Sanctuary*); also Vachel Lindsay's "General William Booth Enters into Heaven."
Edward | Bare first name for Edward Albee, playwright of *Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?*, *The Goat*, and *The Zoo Story*.
Ernest | Bare first name for Ernest Hemingway, clued via *For Whom the Bell Tolls*, *To Have and Have Not*, and Gertrude Stein's "Lost Generation" quip.
Eudora | Bare first name for Eudora Welty, author of "Death of a Traveling Salesman," *The Optimist's Daughter*, and *The Eye of the Story*.
Gwendolyn | Bare first name for Gwendolyn Brooks ("We Real Cool," *Annie Allen*), sometimes conflated with Harlem Renaissance poet Gwendolyn Bennett.
Adrienne | Bare first name for Adrienne Rich, poet of "Diving into the Wreck" who theorized "compulsory heterosexuality."
W | Bare letter for W. H. Auden, poet of "September 1, 1939" and "Funeral Blues," clued via his obsessive line revisions.
J | Bare letter for J. D. Salinger ("A Perfect Day for Bananafish," *Nine Stories*); also clues J. M. Coetzee's autobiographical "John."
father | Common-link relation in Barthelme's *The Dead Father*, Strindberg's *The Father*, Roethke's "My Papa's Waltz," and Plath's "Daddy."
brothers | Common-link relation in Parks's *Topdog/Underdog*, Wilson's *Fences*, Shepard's *True West*, and Heaney's "Mid-Term Break."
children | Common-link figures titling E. Nesbit's and Gertrude Chandler Warner's series, clued via Plath's "The Night Dances" and "Edge."
doctors | Common-link profession of Heller's Daneeka, Lewis's Martin Arrowsmith, Fitzgerald's T. J. Eckleburg, and Nick Adams's father in Hemingway.
driving | Common-link action taught in Vogel's *How I Learned to Drive* and Uhry's *Driving Miss Daisy*.
time | Common-link concept meditated on in T. S. Eliot's *Four Quartets* and Prufrock's "time for a hundred visions and revisions."
witches | Common-link figures in Sexton's "Her Kind," Frost's dialogues, Updike's *The Witches of Eastwick*, and Miller's *The Crucible*.
Hell | Common-link locale in Sartre's *No Exit* ("hell is other people"), Rimbaud's *A Season in Hell*, and Williams's *Kora in Hell*.
Hinduism | Religion behind the Transcendentalists' "Boston Brahmin," clued via Emerson's "Hamatreya" and Thoreau's *Walden*.
German | Common-link language/ethnicity in Plath's "Daddy," Twain's "The Awful German Language," and the Nobel wins of Herta Müller and Günter Grass.
fairy tales | Common-link genre behind Ashbery's translations of D'Aulnoy, Angela Carter's *The Bloody Chamber*, and Glück's "Gretel in Darkness."
enjambment | Poetic technique in Marianne Moore's "The Fish" and Williams's "The Red Wheelbarrow," discussed by critics Culler and Agamben.
anecdote | First title-word of Wallace Stevens's "Anecdote of the Jar," from *Harmonium*.
film | Common-link medium in poems by Eileen Myles, Frank Bidart, and Danez Smith, and Fitzgerald's Hollywood novel *The Last Tycoon*.
films | Common-link medium in Danielewski's *House of Leaves* and Wallace's *Infinite Jest*, and adaptations like Polanski's *Death and the Maiden*.
graphic novels | Common-link medium of Bechdel's *Fun Home*, Yang's *American Born Chinese*, and Drnaso's *Sabrina*.
dictionary | Common-link reference-book genre: Samuel Johnson's, Ambrose Bierce's *Devil's Dictionary*, and Flaubert's *Dictionary of Received Ideas*.
funeral | Common-link event in Dickinson's "I felt a Funeral, in my Brain," Auden's "Funeral Blues," and Stevens's "The Emperor of Ice-Cream."
abortions | Common-link procedure in Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants," Irving's *The Cider House Rules*, and poems by Brooks and Sexton.
tattoos | Common-link body art in O'Connor's "Parker's Back," Bradbury's *The Illustrated Man*, and Karen Russell's "The New Veterans."
swimming pools | Common-link setting of Cheever's "The Swimmer," Salinger's "Teddy," and Wallace's "Forever Overhead."
houses | Common-link structures in Cisneros's *The House on Mango Street*, Machado's *In the Dream House*, and Woolf's "A Haunted House."
Job | Biblical figure reimagined as a banker in MacLeish's *J.B.* and as Mendel Singer's namesake in Joseph Roth's novel *Job*.
the Sun | Common-link object titling Ishiguro's *Klara and the Sun* and Ballard's *Empire of the Sun*, and clued in Shakespeare's Sonnet 130.
a camera | Common-link object that bludgeons Roberta in Dreiser's *An American Tragedy*; also wielded in a Cortázar story.
apples | Common-link fruit in Frost's "After Apple-Picking," Yeats's "The Song of Wandering Aengus," and Neruda's "Ode to the Apple."
bananas | Common-link fruit tied to Bashō's and Yoshimoto's pen names, and to Asturias's banana-trilogy novels.
daffodils | Common-link flower in Faulkner's *The Sound and the Fury* and Wordsworth's "I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud."
candy | Common-link foodstuff in Ogden Nash's light verse and Steinbeck's *Of Mice and Men* ("livin' off the fatta' the lan'").
books | Common-link objects in Bradbury's *Fahrenheit 451*, Hawthorne's "The Custom-House," and Anne Bradstreet's "The Author to Her Book."
bells | Common-link objects in Poe's "The Bells," Dickinson's "I felt a Funeral, in my Brain," and Ransom's "Bells for John Whiteside's Daughter."
bar | Common-link drinking establishment: O'Neill's *The Iceman Cometh*, Nottage's *Sweat* and *Ruined*, Gordone's *No Place to Be Somebody*.
cafe | Common-link setting of Hemingway's "A Clean, Well-Lighted Place" and "The Killers," and David Ives's *Sure Thing*.
beaches | Common-link setting of Whitman's "Out of the Cradle Endlessly Rocking" and "As I Ebb'd with the Ocean of Life."
a dog | Common-link animal in London's *The Call of the Wild*, Steinbeck's *Of Mice and Men*, Albee's *The Zoo Story*, Woolf's *Flush*.
bears | Common-link animal in Faulkner's "The Bear" and Steinbeck's *Of Mice and Men* (Lennie moves "like a creeping bear").
frogs | Common-link animal in Twain's "The Celebrated Jumping Frog of Calaveras County" and Joel Chandler Harris's Uncle Remus tales.

# Foreign Voices in American Literature Questions

Horace | Roman poet of the *Odes* and *Ars Poetica*, source of "carpe diem," clued via Andrew Marvell's Horatian ode and MacLeish's title borrowing.
John Milton | English poet of *Paradise Lost*, invoked via Stanley Fish's reader-response criticism of "how Milton works."
Alexander Pope | English poet of *The Rape of the Lock*, clued via his feud with fellow poet Anne Finch.
Henry Fielding | English novelist of *Tom Jones* and *Shamela*, a parody of Samuel Richardson.
John Ruskin | Victorian art critic of *Modern Painters* and *The Seven Lamps of Architecture*, coiner of "pathetic fallacy," idolized by Proust.
William Shakespeare | English playwright referenced via Eliot's essay "Hamlet and His Problems" and as the title-nickname joke in *The Glass Menagerie*.
- Hamlet | Shakespeare tragedy comically retyped by three monkeys in David Ives's *Words, Words, Words*.
William Butler Yeats | Irish poet of "Easter, 1916" and "The Second Coming"; also clued via "The Song of Wandering Aengus."
Virginia Woolf | British modernist clued via short stories "Kew Gardens," "The Legacy," and "A Mark on the Wall."
"Bliss" | Katherine Mansfield story of Bertha Young's dinner party, whose title feeling curdles on discovering husband Harry's affair with Pearl Fulton.
D. H. Lawrence = David Herbert Lawrence | English author of *Sons and Lovers*, *Women in Love*, and stories like "Odour of Chrysanthemums."
George Orwell | English author of *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, *Homage to Catalonia*, and "Shooting an Elephant."
Graham Greene | British author of stories involving Old Misery's demolished house and a turtle-soup matricide; wrote the introduction to Highsmith's story collection.
David Lodge | English novelist of the "Campus Trilogy" and critic whose *The Art of Fiction* discusses Salinger's prose style.
Equus | Peter Shaffer play in which psychiatrist Martin Dysart treats Alan Strang's obsession with horses.
Colm Toibin | Irish author of *Brooklyn* (Eilis Lacey's emigration) and *The Master*, a fictionalized life of Henry James.
Gustave Flaubert | French author of *Madame Bovary*, clued via Julian Barnes's *Flaubert's Parrot* and his lover Louise Colet.
Jean Genet | French playwright of *The Blacks* and novelist whose gender-nonconforming character inspired drag performer Divine's stage name.
Anna Akhmatova | Russian poet of "Requiem," mourning her arrested son Lev Gumilev; married to poet Nikolai Gumilev.
A Sportsman's Sketches | Turgenev's hunting-trip story collection ("Khor and Kalinych," "Bezhin Meadow") whose publication led to his house arrest.
"The Gentleman from San Francisco" | Ivan Bunin story of a rich, unnamed American who dies of a heart attack on Capri during a European vacation.
Giuseppe Ungaretti | Italian trench poet of "Nostalgia" and "Mattina," founder of the Hermetic school of poetry.
Bruno Schulz | Polish-Jewish author of *The Street of Crocodiles* and *Sanatorium Under the Sign of the Hourglass*, murdered by the Gestapo.
Alan Paton | South African anti-apartheid author of *Cry, the Beloved Country* and *Too Late the Phalarope*.
Gabriel Garcia Marquez | Colombian author of "A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings," *Chronicle of a Death Foretold*, and *Love in the Time of Cholera*.
Julio Cortazar | Argentine author of *Hopscotch* and the story "Axolotl."
Don Quixote | Cervantes's novel invoked as a comparison for Bellow's *Henderson the Rain King* and reworked in Borges's and Auster's metafiction.
Kahlil Gibran | Lebanese-American author of *The Prophet* (Almustafa's discourses at Orphalese), writing in English after emigrating to the U.S.
