# Postwar Poetry and the Beats
> Postwar American verse fractures into confessional self-exposure, Beat countercultural sprawl, and New York School wit, before opening into today's laureates and performance poetry.

Sylvia Plath | Confessional poet of Ariel — "Daddy," "Lady Lazarus" ("I eat men like air") — and The Colossus; The Bell Jar and her marriage to Ted Hughes appear constantly.
- The Bell Jar | Plath's autobiographical novel of Esther Greenwood's New York magazine internship, breakdown, and shock treatment; first published under the pseudonym Victoria Lucas.
- Esther Greenwood | Narrator of The Bell Jar; a magazine intern under Jay Cee who undergoes electroconvulsive therapy after a breakdown and suicide attempt.
- Ariel | Plath's posthumous collection, published by Hughes; its title poem is a fierce dawn horse ride "into the red eye, the cauldron of morning."
- "Daddy" | Plath poem addressing her dead father as a "man in black with a Meinkampf look"; opens "You do not do, you do not do."
- Sylvia Plath's father | Otto Plath, recalled as a "bag full of God" and "panzer-man" in "Daddy," and mourned in "Electra on Azalea Path" and "Little Fugue."
- "Lady Lazarus" | Plath poem of serial near-death and rebirth: "Out of the ash / I rise with my red hair / And I eat men like air."
- Ted Hughes | English poet and Plath's husband; his Birthday Letters answered her confessional poems decades later, and his Crow followed her 1963 suicide.
Elizabeth Bishop | Meticulous poet of "One Art" ("the art of losing isn't hard to master"), "The Fish," "In the Waiting Room," and "The Moose"; lived for years in Brazil with Lota de Macedo Soares.
- "One Art" | Villanelle listing escalating losses, from door keys to "a continent"; written after Lota de Macedo Soares's suicide, ending "(Write it!)".
- "The Armadillo" | Poem of the title animal fleeing Brazilian fire-balloons at a saint's-day festival; dedicated to Robert Lowell, who answered it with "Skunk Hour."
- "The Fish" | Poem of an old, battle-scarred fish held boatside and released once the speaker sees "rainbow, rainbow, rainbow" in the oil-slicked water; Marianne Moore wrote a same-titled poem.
Robert Lowell = Robert Traill Spence Lowell IV | Founder of confessional poetry with Life Studies; "Skunk Hour," "For the Union Dead," and "Memories of West Street and Lepke," from his conscientious-objector jail term, recur.
- "Skunk Hour" | Lowell poem set on Nautilus Island, ending as a skunk "jabs her wedge-head in a cup / of sour cream"; dedicated to Elizabeth Bishop, answering her "The Armadillo."
- "For the Union Dead" | Poem on the Robert Gould Shaw memorial, "a fishbone in the city's throat," set against a Boston parking garage under construction.
- Life Studies | Lowell's confession-launching 1959 collection; includes the prose memoir "91 Revere Street" and poems on his mental illness, ending with "Skunk Hour."
Anne Sexton | Confessional poet who began writing in therapy: To Bedlam and Part Way Back, the Pulitzer-winning Live or Die, and the fairy-tale revisions of Transformations.
John Berryman | Confessional poet of The Dream Songs, starring Henry and the minstrel-voiced "Mr. Bones," and Homage to Mistress Bradstreet; he jumped from a Minneapolis bridge.
- The Dream Songs | Berryman's 385-poem sequence for alter ego Henry and his blackface interlocutor "Mr. Bones"; twelve elegies were dedicated to Delmore Schwartz.
Confessional Poetry | Movement label for intensely autobiographical postwar verse; Lowell's Life Studies, Plath, Sexton, Berryman, and W. D. Snodgrass's Heart's Needle are the canonical clues.
Delmore Schwartz | New York poet-critic whose story "In Dreams Begin Responsibilities" and poem "In the Naked Bed, In Plato's Cave" made him the model for Saul Bellow's Humboldt's Gift.
- "In Dreams Begin Responsibilities" | Schwartz's title story: a man watches a film of his parents' courtship and disastrous marriage, shouting warnings at the screen.
Randall Jarrell | Poet-critic best known for the five-line "The Death of the Ball Turret Gunner," which ends "they washed me out of the turret with a hose"; also wrote Pictures from an Institution.
- "The Death of the Ball Turret Gunner" | Five-line WWII poem: "From my mother's sleep I fell into the State"; ends with the gunner "washed...out of the turret with a hose."
Theodore Roethke | Michigan-born poet of "My Papa's Waltz," the whiskey-drunk father who "beat time" on the speaker's head; also wrote "The Waking" and the greenhouse poem "Root Cellar."

John Ashbery | New York School poet whose Self-Portrait in a Convex Mirror, on Parmigianino's painting, swept the Pulitzer, National Book Award, and NBCC; famously difficult and prolific.
- "Self-Portrait in a Convex Mirror" | Ashbery's title poem on Parmigianino's self-portrait, "the right hand / Bigger than the head, thrust at the viewer"; won a rare Pulitzer/NBA/NBCC sweep.
Frank O'Hara | New York School poet and MoMA curator; Lunch Poems pieces like "The Day Lady Died," on Billie Holiday's death, and "Why I Am Not a Painter" recur.
- Lunch Poems | O'Hara's collection of quick, city-life poems written on lunch breaks; includes "The Day Lady Died" and "Lana Turner has collapsed!"
Kenneth Koch | New York School poet of "Fresh Air," mocking academic taste, and "Why I Am Not a Painter" 's foil; parodied William Carlos Williams's "This Is Just to Say."
The Changing Light at Sandover | James Merrill's Ouija-board epic in all capitals, voiced by spirits Ephraim and Mirabell to "DJ" and "JM"; set at his Stonington, Connecticut house.

Beat Generation | 1950s counterculture circle of Ginsberg, Kerouac, Burroughs, and Neal Cassady, centered on City Lights bookstore and the Six Gallery reading; Kerouac coined the name.
Allen Ginsberg | Beat poet of "Howl," "America," and "Kaddish," his elegy for his mother Naomi; "A Supermarket in California" imagines meeting Whitman among the groceries.
- "Howl" | Ginsberg's Beat epic beginning "I saw the best minds of my generation destroyed by madness"; addresses Carl Solomon ("I'm with you in Rockland"), rails against Moloch, and survived a 1957 obscenity trial.
- Rockland | Mental institution in "Howl" 's third section, repeatedly addressed to Carl Solomon: "I'm with you in Rockland where you're madder than I am."
- "Kaddish" | Ginsberg's elegy for his mother Naomi, opening "Strange now to think of you, gone without corsets & eyes"; titled for the Jewish mourning prayer.
Jack Kerouac | Beat novelist of On the Road, typed on a continuous scroll, with Sal Paradise and Dean Moriarty; The Dharma Bums and Big Sur also appear.
- On the Road | Novel of cross-country trips with Dean Moriarty (based on Neal Cassady); Sal Paradise narrates, encountering Old Bull Lee and jazz pianist George Shearing.
- Sal Paradise | Narrator of On the Road; steals a copy of Le Grand Meaulnes, picks cotton with Terry, and criss-crosses the U.S. with Dean Moriarty.
William S. Burroughs | Beat author of the cut-up novel Naked Lunch, plus Junky and Queer; he killed his wife Joan Vollmer in a drunken "William Tell" act in Mexico City.
- Naked Lunch | Cut-up novel of addict Bill Lee wandering Interzone; Dr. Benway runs a Reconditioning Center, and denizens eat the "Black Meat" of giant centipedes.
Gary Snyder | Beat poet and Zen practitioner who read "A Berry Feast" at the Six Gallery; wrote the environmentalist collection Turtle Island and inspired Japhy Ryder in Kerouac's The Dharma Bums.
- Turtle Island | Snyder's Pulitzer-winning collection titled with a Native American name for North America; includes "Smokey the Bear Sutra" and "The Bath."
Lawrence Ferlinghetti | Beat-era poet who co-founded San Francisco's City Lights bookstore and published Ginsberg's "Howl," triggering an obscenity trial; wrote A Coney Island of the Mind.
- A Coney Island of the Mind | Ferlinghetti's collection opening "In Goya's greatest scenes we seem to see"; includes "Junkman's Obbligato" and the tightrope-walking "Constantly Risking Absurdity."
Richard Brautigan | Countercultural novelist-poet of the post-apocalyptic commune iDEATH in In Watermelon Sugar; Trout Fishing in America gives its title to a person, a ballet, and a book.
- Trout Fishing in America | Brautigan's genre-blurring book whose title names a person, a hotel, and a ballet across its loosely linked vignettes.
City Lights Bookstore | San Francisco bookstore and press co-founded by Lawrence Ferlinghetti; published Ginsberg's Howl and Other Poems, sparking his 1957 obscenity trial.
Black Mountain poets | Avant-garde group centered at the North Carolina college, including Charles Olson, Robert Creeley, Robert Duncan, and Denise Levertov; Olson's "Projective Verse" was its manifesto.
Buddhism | Religion adopted by many Beat writers, including Gary Snyder (Japhy Ryder's model) and Ginsberg ("Sunflower Sutra," "Wichita Vortex Sutra"); Kerouac's The Dharma Bums follows its seekers.
Charles Bukowski | Skid-row poet-novelist of Los Angeles and his alter ego Henry Chinaski: Post Office, Ham on Rye, Factotum, and thousands of plainspoken poems.

Richard Wilbur | Formalist poet of "Love Calls Us to the Things of This World," where laundry-line angels fill "the morning air"; mentored the New Formalist poets.
Anthony Hecht | Pulitzer-winning poet of The Hard Hours; Holocaust poems "More Light! More Light!" and "The Book of Yolek" draw on his WWII service in Germany.
A. R. Ammons | Poet of colon-linked free verse; Garbage, inspired by a Florida landfill, won the National Book Award, and "Corsons Inlet" surveys a New Jersey beach walk.
James Dickey | Georgian poet-novelist of Deliverance, in which Ed Gentry survives a mountain-men attack; also wrote "Buckdancer's Choice" and read at Jimmy Carter's inauguration.
James Wright | Poet of "A Blessing," where two Indian ponies' "eyes / Darken with kindness," and "Lying in a Hammock..." ending "I have wasted my life."
Robert Hass | Berkeley poet and former U.S. Poet Laureate who translated neighbor Czeslaw Milosz; "Meditation at Lagunitas" repeats "blackberry, blackberry, blackberry."
Charles Simic | Serbian-American Pulitzer winner of The World Doesn't End; prose poems and "Watermelons" draw on his WWII Eastern European childhood, per "Eyes Fastened with Pins."
Thom Gunn | English-born American poet of The Man with Night Sweats, elegizing friends lost to AIDS; "On the Move" depicts a leather-clad motorcycle gang.
Muriel Rukeyser | Poet-activist of The Book of the Dead, on the Hawk's Nest mining disaster; "To Be a Jew in the Twentieth Century" calls it a "gift" one cannot refuse.

Adrienne Rich | Feminist poet of "Diving into the Wreck," "Aunt Jennifer's Tigers," and Snapshots of a Daughter-in-Law; she refused the National Medal of Arts in 1997.
Joy Harjo | Muscogee (Creek) poet and the first Native American U.S. Poet Laureate; "She Had Some Horses," An American Sunrise, and "Perhaps the World Ends Here" are common clues.
Louise Gluck -> Louise Glück | Nobel- and Pulitzer-winning poet of The Wild Iris, whose flowers "hate" like the speaker; retold the Odyssey in Meadowlands and the Iliad in The Triumph of Achilles.
Rita Dove | Former U.S. Poet Laureate of Thomas and Beulah; Sonata Mulattica follows biracial violinist George Bridgetower, and her Penguin anthology drew fire from Helen Vendler.
Natasha Trethewey | Mississippi-born former U.S. Poet Laureate of Native Guard, on a Black Civil War regiment and her mother's death; also wrote the Bellocq-inspired Bellocq's Ophelia.
Yusef Komunyakaa | African-American Vietnam veteran poet of Dien Cai Dau and "Facing It," on the Vietnam Veterans Memorial; won the Pulitzer for Neon Vernacular.
Ada Limon -> Ada Limón | Current U.S. Poet Laureate of Mexican-American descent; wrote "How to Triumph Like a Girl," "A New National Anthem," and the collections Bright Dead Things and The Carrying.
Billy Collins | Bestselling, accessible U.S. Poet Laureate of Picnic, Lightning and The Trouble with Poetry; wrote "The Best Cigarette" and "Dancing toward Bethlehem."
Mary Oliver | Nature poet of "Wild Geese" ("You do not have to be good") and "The Summer Day"; helped organize Edna St. Vincent Millay's papers at Steepletop.
Night Sky With Exit Wounds | Ocean Vuong's 2016 debut collection responding to Rilke ("Suppose you do change your life"); includes "Telemachus" and "Aubade with Burning City."

Shel Silverstein | Children's poet-illustrator of The Giving Tree and Where the Sidewalk Ends; also wrote Johnny Cash's "A Boy Named Sue" and the screenplay for Things Change.
slam poetry | Competitive spoken-word format invented by Chicago construction worker Marc Smith and popularized at the Nuyorican Poets Cafe; poems are judged by audience scorecards.
spoken word poetry | Broader performance genre of poets reading aloud to audience approval, from Louise Bennett-Coverley's dialect verse to Gil Scott-Heron; slam poetry is its competitive, judged form.
