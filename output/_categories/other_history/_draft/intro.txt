"Other History" gathers quizbowl questions on history outside the standalone United States, Europe, and world-history-survey buckets, and its distribution skews heavily toward the ancient world. Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece, and Rome together account for the largest share of answerlines, with recurring names such as Carthage, Sparta, Alexander the Great, Julius Caesar, and Hadrian appearing alongside cities like Babylon, Nineveh, and Troy and material-culture staples like cuneiform, bronze, and terracotta. Persian and Near Eastern material spans the Achaemenid kings Cyrus the Great and Darius I through the Sassanid Empire and the rise of Islam under Muhammad.

Beyond the Mediterranean world, East Asian history concentrates on the sequence of Chinese dynasties, from Shang oracle bones through the Qin, Han, and Ming, alongside Silk Road trade and twentieth-century figures like Deng Xiaoping. South Asian questions cluster around Ashoka, the Indus Valley Civilization, and Sri Lanka, while African answerlines range from ancient Nubia and the Garamantes to modern Nigeria and South Africa. Pre-Columbian and Latin American history is anchored by Mesoamerican and Andean civilizations -- the Olmecs, Aztec Empire, Teotihuacan, and Nazca -- alongside colonial and modern topics such as the Falklands War and Cuba.

A substantial cluster of questions concerns Australia, New Zealand, and the wider Pacific: Maori history and the Treaty of Waitangi, Aboriginal Australians, and the politics of modern Australia and New Zealand recur often, as do Canadian topics such as confederation-era figures and the peoples of the Arctic. Twentieth-century Europe is represented mainly through the World Wars, the Soviet Union, and Vladimir Lenin.

Finally, a distinct thematic cluster consists of common-link commodity and material-culture answerlines -- metals like tin, iron, and gold; crops like tea, rice, and coffee; and archaeological objects like urns, tombs, and shipwrecks -- which recur across many regions rather than belonging to any single civilization.
