# Classical & Neoclassical Foundations
> The classical and neoclassical economists and doctrines—from Ricardo and Malthus through Marshall, Jevons, Pareto, and Marx—that first formalized value, rent, and general-equilibrium theory.
Kenneth Arrow | Nobel economist known for the Arrow-Debreu general equilibrium model and Arrow's Impossibility Theorem on aggregating voter preferences.
Alfred Marshall | Neoclassical economist who formalized supply-and-demand curves and marginal analysis in Principles of Economics (1890).
Vilfredo Pareto | Italian economist-sociologist known for Pareto efficiency, the 80/20 Pareto principle, and elite-circulation theory in sociology.
William Stanley Jevons | Marginalist economist who co-founded marginal utility theory and wrote on the paradox of value and business cycles.
Thomas Malthus | Classical economist who argued population grows geometrically while food supply grows arithmetically, in An Essay on the Principle of Population.
economic rent | Payment to a factor of production above what is needed to keep it in its current use; central to Ricardian rent theory.
Karl Marx | German philosopher-economist whose Das Kapital critiqued capitalism via the labor theory of value and theory of exploitation.
socialism | Economic system of collective or state ownership of the means of production, contrasted with capitalism in Marxist and classical debate.
Thorstein Veblen | Institutional economist who coined conspicuous consumption in The Theory of the Leisure Class, critiquing status-driven spending.
An Essay on the Principle of Population | Thomas Malthus's 1798 work arguing population grows faster than food supply, leading to recurring subsistence crises.
barter system | Direct exchange of goods without money, the classical starting point for theories explaining money's origin as a medium of exchange.
economics | Social science studying how individuals and societies allocate scarce resources among competing uses, per Lionel Robbins's classic definition.
France | Home to French economists such as Cournot, Walras, and Piketty, and to the Physiocratic school preceding classical economics.
general equilibrium | Analysis of how supply and demand interact simultaneously across all markets in an economy; formalized by Walras and by Arrow and Debreu.
Gerard Debreu | Mathematical economist who, with Kenneth Arrow, proved the existence of general equilibrium; won the Nobel for Theory of Value.
Johann Heinrich von Thunen | German economist whose Isolated State (1826) modeled agricultural land use as concentric rings based on transport cost to a central market.
Soviet Union | Centrally planned command economy contrasted with market capitalism in classical and Cold War-era economic debate.
Thorstein Bunde Veblen | Full name of Thorstein Veblen, institutional economist who coined conspicuous consumption in The Theory of the Leisure Class.
water | Central to the classical paradox of value, where useful water is cheap while less-useful diamonds are costly, resolved by marginal utility.

# Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles
> Keynesian, monetarist, and new classical accounts of growth, inflation, unemployment, and the monetary and fiscal levers used to manage the business cycle.
John Maynard Keynes | British economist whose General Theory (1936) founded macroeconomics, advocating government spending to fight unemployment during recessions.
economic growth | Long-run increase in an economy's output, studied via Solow-Swan and endogenous growth models emphasizing capital, labor, and technology.
Milton Friedman | Chicago School monetarist who argued money-supply growth drives inflation and business cycles; wrote A Monetary History of the United States.
business cycles | Recurring fluctuations of expansion and recession in aggregate output, explained by Keynesian, monetarist, and real-business-cycle theories.
inflation | Sustained rise in the general price level, explained by monetarist money-supply growth, demand-pull, and cost-push theories.
money | Medium of exchange whose supply and velocity link to inflation and output in quantity-theory and Keynesian frameworks.
money supply | Central-bank-controlled stock of currency and deposits (M1/M2); monetarists tie its growth rate directly to inflation.
Interest rate | Price of borrowing money, set in part by central banks as a monetary-policy tool to influence investment and inflation.
Paul Samuelson | Founder of the neoclassical synthesis blending Keynesian and neoclassical economics; wrote the influential textbook Economics.
unemployment | Share of the labor force without work, decomposed into frictional, structural, and cyclical types tied to the business cycle.
Austrian School | Tradition of Menger, Mises, and Hayek emphasizing subjective value and an Austrian business-cycle theory blaming credit expansion for booms and busts.
consumption | Household spending component of GDP; Keynes's consumption function and Friedman's permanent-income hypothesis model its determinants.
Robert Lucas | New classical economist behind the Lucas critique and rational-expectations macroeconomics; won the Nobel Prize in 1995.
Friedrich August von Hayek | Austrian School economist who warned of central planning in The Road to Serfdom and developed Austrian business-cycle theory.
Simon Kuznets | Economist who developed national-income accounting for GDP and the Kuznets curve linking growth and inequality.
unemployment rate | Percentage of the labor force actively seeking work; tracked alongside inflation via the Phillips curve tradeoff.
debt | Borrowed funds owed by governments, firms, or households; public debt and deficits are central fiscal-policy concerns.
inflation rate | Percentage change in the price level over time; targeted by central banks via monetary policy.
Joseph Schumpeter | Economist who coined creative destruction to describe entrepreneurial innovation continually displacing incumbent firms and technologies.
real business cycle theory | New classical theory attributing business cycles to technology shocks rather than monetary or demand disturbances.
saving | Portion of income not consumed; determines capital accumulation in Solow-Swan growth models via the savings rate.
aggregate demand | Total planned spending in an economy across consumption, investment, government, and net exports; its shortfall Keynes blamed for recessions.
climate change | Global-warming economics, including carbon pricing and discounting future damages, associated with William Nordhaus's integrated assessment models.
creative destruction | Schumpeter's term for innovation-driven displacement of old firms, products, and jobs by new ones, fueling long-run growth.
interest rates | Cost of borrowing set partly by central-bank policy; influences investment, saving, and exchange rates.
James Tobin | Nobel economist known for Tobin's q investment theory and the proposed Tobin tax on international currency transactions.
rational expectations | New classical assumption that agents form forecasts using all available information, underlying Robert Lucas's critique of policy models.
Solow-Swan model | Neoclassical growth model where capital accumulation, labor, and exogenous technology determine long-run output per worker.
William Nordhaus | Nobel economist who integrated climate change into long-run growth models, pioneering environmental macroeconomics.
discount rate | Rate used to convert future values to present value, and the rate the Federal Reserve charges banks for direct loans.
Franco Modigliani | Nobel economist known for the life-cycle hypothesis of saving and, with Miller, the Modigliani-Miller theorem on capital structure.
frictional unemployment | Short-term joblessness from workers transitioning between jobs or entering the labor force, distinct from structural or cyclical unemployment.
GDP | Gross Domestic Product: total market value of goods and services produced in an economy, the standard measure of output.
government spending | Fiscal-policy component of aggregate demand; Keynesians advocate raising it during recessions to stimulate output and employment.
housing | Asset and market whose booms and busts, as in 2008, transmit through mortgage credit into broader business cycles.
monetary policy | Central-bank actions adjusting interest rates and the money supply to manage inflation and employment.
Paul Anthony Samuelson | Full name of Paul Samuelson, founder of the neoclassical synthesis and author of the textbook Economics.
Phillips curve | Empirical relationship suggesting a tradeoff between inflation and unemployment, later modified to include expectations.
accounting | National-income accounting framework, including GDP and GNP, used to measure an economy's aggregate output and flows.
Austria | Home of the Austrian School of economics, including Menger, Mises, Hayek, and Schumpeter, known for subjective value and business-cycle theory.
Federal Funds rate | Interest rate U.S. banks charge each other for overnight loans; the Federal Reserve's primary monetary-policy tool.
Friedrich Hayek | Austrian School economist known for The Road to Serfdom and Austrian business-cycle theory blaming credit expansion for booms and busts.
Greg Mankiw | Harvard economist and textbook author known for New Keynesian menu-cost models and popular Principles of Economics texts.
hyperinflation | Extremely rapid, out-of-control price increases, typically caused by excessive money creation to finance government deficits.
Janet Yellen | Economist who served as Federal Reserve Chair and later U.S. Treasury Secretary, known for labor-market and monetary-policy research.
Japan | Economy associated with the 1990s Lost Decade of stagnation and deflation following its asset-price bubble collapse.
menu costs | Costs of changing posted prices, used in New Keynesian models to explain short-run price stickiness and non-neutral monetary policy.
Modern Monetary Theory | Heterodox framework arguing sovereign currency issuers face no solvency constraint and should use fiscal policy to manage inflation.
neutrality of money | Classical proposition that changes in the money supply affect only nominal variables, not real output, in the long run.
OKUN'S LAW | Empirical relationship between GDP growth and changes in the unemployment rate, formulated by Arthur Okun.
Paul | 
quantitative easing | Unconventional monetary policy where a central bank buys long-term securities to lower long-term rates when short-term rates are near zero.
recessions | Periods of declining economic activity, typically defined as two consecutive quarters of falling GDP.
Robert Emerson Lucas, Jr. | New classical economist behind rational expectations and the Lucas critique; won the 1995 Nobel Prize.
Robert Solow | Nobel economist whose Solow growth model attributes long-run per-capita growth mainly to exogenous technological progress.
savings | Portion of income not spent on consumption; fuels investment and capital accumulation in growth models.
savings rate | Share of income saved rather than consumed; a key determinant of steady-state capital per worker in the Solow model.
Solow-Swan growth model | Neoclassical growth model, developed independently by Solow and Swan, where capital accumulation and exogenous technology drive long-run output.
sunspots | Term for self-fulfilling, extrinsic beliefs that can generate business-cycle fluctuations in models with multiple equilibria.
technology | Exogenous driver of long-run growth in the Solow model, whose contribution is captured by total factor productivity.
total factor productivity | Portion of output growth not explained by increases in capital or labor, typically attributed to technological progress.

# Consumer & Producer Theory
> The microeconomic building blocks of utility, demand, cost, and production that describe how households and firms make marginal choices.
utility | Measure of satisfaction from consumption underlying demand theory; cardinal and ordinal versions support indifference-curve analysis.
capital | Factor of production alongside land and labor, encompassing physical plant and equipment and its accumulation in growth models.
equilibrium | Point where quantity supplied equals quantity demanded, clearing a market at a stable price.
indifference curves | Curves showing consumption bundles yielding equal utility; their convex shape reflects diminishing marginal rate of substitution.
prices | Signals coordinating supply and demand decisions, set at the intersection of supply and demand curves under competition.
demand | Quantity of a good buyers wish to purchase at each price, graphed as a downward-sloping curve reflecting diminishing marginal utility.
marginal cost | Cost of producing one additional unit; profit-maximizing firms set output where marginal cost equals marginal revenue.
price | Amount paid for a good, determined by supply-and-demand equilibrium under perfect competition.
substitution | Consumer response to relative price changes, replacing a costlier good with a cheaper substitute; part of the Slutsky decomposition.
time | Intertemporal dimension of choice, underlying discounting, time preference, and the tradeoff between present and future consumption.
opportunity cost | Value of the next-best alternative forgone when making a choice; central to marginal decision-making in economics.
price elasticity of demand | Measure of how much quantity demanded changes in response to a price change, distinguishing elastic from inelastic goods.
rent | Payment for the use of a factor of production, notably land, distinguished from economic rent as surplus above opportunity cost.
Slutsky equation | Decomposes a price change's effect on demand into substitution and income effects; named for Eugen Slutsky.
elasticity | General measure of responsiveness of one variable to another, such as quantity demanded to price or income.
elasticity of substitution | Measure of how easily one input or good can be substituted for another as relative prices change.
production possibilities frontier | Curve showing maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce given fixed resources, illustrating opportunity cost.
utility functions | Mathematical representations of consumer preferences ranking bundles of goods, used to derive demand curves.
constant returns to scale | Production function property where output scales exactly proportionally with all inputs, so doubling capital and labor doubles output.
factors of production | Inputs to production, traditionally land, labor, and capital, sometimes entrepreneurship, combined via a production function to yield output.
Giffen goods | Rare goods for which quantity demanded rises as price rises, because a strong negative income effect outweighs the substitution effect.
income effect | Change in quantity demanded resulting from a change in real purchasing power caused by a price change, per the Slutsky decomposition.
increasing returns to scale | Production function property where output grows more than proportionally to input increases, often driving natural monopoly.
land | Classical factor of production encompassing natural resources; its fixed supply underlies Ricardian and Georgist rent theory.
leisure | Non-work time valued in utility, traded off against labor income in standard labor-supply models.
marginal | Prefix for concepts like marginal cost, marginal utility, and marginal product, describing the effect of one additional unit.
marginal utility | Additional satisfaction from consuming one more unit of a good; diminishes as consumption increases, per the law of diminishing marginal utility.
production | Process of combining inputs such as land, labor, and capital to create goods and services, modeled via production functions.
production functions | Mathematical relationships mapping input quantities like labor and capital to output, such as the Cobb-Douglas form.

# Market Structure & Industrial Organization
> How competition, market power, and strategic pricing vary across monopoly, oligopoly, monopolistic competition, and monopsony.
oligopoly | Market structure with few interdependent sellers, modeled via Cournot quantity competition or Bertrand price competition.
Harold Hotelling | Statistician-economist known for Hotelling's law of spatial competition and Hotelling's rule governing nonrenewable-resource extraction.
monopolistic competition | Market structure of many firms selling differentiated products with free entry, yielding downward-sloping demand and excess capacity.
monopoly | Market structure with a single seller facing the whole market demand curve, restricting output to raise price above marginal cost.
monopsony | Market structure with a single buyer, notably applied to labor markets where employers can suppress wages below marginal product.
advertising | Firm strategy to shift demand and signal quality under monopolistic competition and imperfect information.
economies of scale | Falling average cost as output rises, driving natural monopoly and industry concentration in some sectors.
firms | Business organizations whose boundaries Coase explained via transaction costs; theory of the firm studies their production decisions.
Joan Robinson | British economist who pioneered the theory of imperfect competition and introduced the term monopsony for single-buyer markets.
Antoine Augustin Cournot | 19th-century French mathematician whose Cournot model describes quantity-setting duopolists reaching a stable equilibrium.
market power | Ability of a firm to set prices above marginal cost, ranging from limited under monopolistic competition to unconstrained under monopoly.
network effects | Phenomenon where a good's value to users increases as more people use it, driving winner-take-all dynamics in platform markets.
perfect | Likely shorthand for perfect competition, the market structure of many price-taking firms with free entry and exit.
price discrimination | Charging different prices to different customers for the same good based on willingness to pay, requiring market power and no resale.

# Market Failure & Public Economics
> Externalities, public goods, taxation, and collective-action problems that cause markets to diverge from efficient outcomes, and the policies designed to correct them.
public goods | Non-excludable, non-rival goods like national defense that markets underprovide, motivating government provision and free-rider analysis.
Ronald Coase | Economist behind the Coase theorem and the transaction-cost theory of the firm, explaining why firms exist and how externalities can be resolved.
externalities | Uncompensated third-party costs or benefits from an economic activity, addressed via Pigouvian taxes or Coasean bargaining.
taxes | Government levies used to raise revenue, correct externalities via Pigouvian taxes, and redistribute income.
transaction costs | Costs of negotiating, monitoring, and enforcing exchanges; central to Coase's theory of the firm and property-rights bargaining.
voting | Public-choice topic covering majority rule and strategic voting, tied to Arrow's Impossibility Theorem for aggregating individual preferences.
cap-and-trade | Market-based environmental policy capping total emissions and letting firms trade pollution permits to reach efficient abatement.
commons | Shared resources vulnerable to overuse absent clear property rights, as in Garrett Hardin's tragedy-of-the-commons framing.
deadweight loss | Loss of economic efficiency when equilibrium is not achieved, caused by taxes, price controls, or monopoly pricing.
institutions | Formal and informal rules such as property rights, law, and norms shaping economic behavior, central to New Institutional Economics.
tragedy of the commons | Garrett Hardin's concept that shared, unregulated resources get overexploited because individual users bear only part of the cost.
Arthur Cecil Pigou | British economist who developed welfare economics and proposed Pigouvian taxes to correct negative externalities like pollution.
Coase theorem | Ronald Coase's proposition that with well-defined property rights and no transaction costs, parties bargain to an efficient outcome regardless of initial allocation.
free rider problem | Tendency for individuals to benefit from a public good without contributing to its cost, motivating collective-action analysis.
Henry George | 19th-century economist who proposed a single tax on land value in Progress and Poverty, founding the Georgist movement.
Mancur Olson | Economist whose The Logic of Collective Action explained why large groups struggle to organize for shared interests, due to free-riding.
median voter theorem | Public-choice model predicting that majority-rule voting outcomes converge to the preference of the median voter.
negative externalities | Uncompensated costs imposed on third parties, such as pollution, corrected via Pigouvian taxes or cap-and-trade schemes.
regulatory capture | Phenomenon where regulatory agencies come to serve the interests of the industries they regulate rather than the public, per George Stigler.
rent control | Price ceiling on rents intended to keep housing affordable, criticized by economists for reducing housing supply and quality.
Ronald Harry Coase | Full name of Ronald Coase, known for the Coase theorem and transaction-cost theory of the firm.

# Game Theory & Information Economics
> Strategic interaction, auctions, and the economics of asymmetric information, from Nash equilibrium to adverse selection and signaling.
information | Asymmetric or imperfect information distorts markets, studied via adverse selection, signaling, and screening models.
auctions | Mechanisms for allocating goods via bidding; English, Dutch, first-price sealed-bid, and Vickrey second-price formats are studied in auction theory.
Nash equilibrium | Strategy profile where no player benefits from unilaterally deviating; foundational solution concept named for John Nash.
prisoner's dilemma | Classic game where mutual defection is the dominant strategy despite cooperation being jointly better, illustrating collective-action failure.
Joseph Stiglitz | Nobel economist known for screening models and the economics of asymmetric information, alongside Akerlof and Spence.
game theory | Mathematical study of strategic decision-making among interdependent agents, formalized by von Neumann, Morgenstern, and Nash.
moral hazard | Tendency to take more risk once insured or protected from consequences, a key information-economics problem in insurance markets.
information asymmetry | Situation where one party has more or better information than another, causing adverse selection and moral hazard.
contracts | Agreements structuring economic exchange under incomplete information; contract theory studies incentives and principal-agent problems.
games | Formal strategic-interaction structures with players, strategies, and payoffs analyzed in game theory, from zero-sum to cooperative games.
mechanism design | Reverse game theory: designing rules or institutions, like auctions, so self-interested play yields a desired social outcome.
Tit-for-Tat | Simple, successful strategy in repeated prisoner's-dilemma tournaments: cooperate first, then mirror the opponent's previous move.
Battle of the Sexes | Coordination game where two players prefer to coordinate but disagree on which of two outcomes to coordinate on.
George Akerlof | Nobel economist whose Market for Lemons paper founded the study of adverse selection from information asymmetry.
George Stigler | Chicago School economist known for the economics of information, search theory, and the theory of regulatory capture.
insurance | Risk-pooling market plagued by adverse selection and moral hazard, a classic example in information economics.
Nash equilibria | Plural of Nash equilibrium: strategy profiles where no player can benefit by unilaterally changing strategy.
signaling | Costly action taken to credibly convey private information, as in Michael Spence's job-market signaling model using education.
ultimatum game | Bargaining game where one player proposes splitting a sum and the other can accept or reject, testing fairness against pure self-interest.
zero-sum games | Games where one player's gain exactly equals another's loss, contrasted with positive-sum or cooperative games.

# Behavioral Economics
> The psychology of economic decision-making, including bounded rationality, cognitive biases, and departures from the rational-actor model.
Prospect Theory | Kahneman and Tversky's model of decision-making under risk, featuring loss aversion and reference-dependent value functions.
rationality | Assumption that agents make consistent, self-interested choices; behavioral economics documents systematic departures from it.
behavioral economics | Field combining psychology and economics to study cognitive biases and bounded rationality, pioneered by Kahneman, Tversky, and Thaler.
bias | Systematic deviation from rational judgment, such as overconfidence or anchoring, studied extensively in behavioral economics.
rational | Standard neoclassical assumption of consistent, utility-maximizing behavior, challenged by behavioral economics' findings of systematic bias.
bounded rationality | Herbert Simon's concept that decision-makers face cognitive and informational limits, leading them to satisfice rather than optimize.
Daniel Kahneman | Psychologist who won the economics Nobel for Prospect Theory and cognitive-bias research with Amos Tversky; wrote Thinking, Fast and Slow.
Richard Thaler | Behavioral economist known for nudge theory, mental accounting, and the endowment effect; won the Nobel Prize in 2017.
satisficing | Herbert Simon's concept of choosing a good-enough option rather than the optimal one, under bounded rationality.
Thinking, Fast and Slow | Daniel Kahneman's 2011 book distinguishing fast, intuitive System 1 thinking from slow, deliberate System 2 reasoning.

# Financial Economics
> Asset pricing, risk, and the instruments and models—options, bonds, CAPM, Black-Scholes—that govern financial markets.
risk | Uncertainty over financial outcomes, measured via variance and volatility and priced through models like CAPM and the Sharpe ratio.
options | Derivative contracts giving the right, not obligation, to buy or sell an asset; priced using the Black-Scholes model.
arbitrage | Simultaneous buying and selling to exploit price discrepancies risklessly; underlies no-arbitrage asset-pricing models like Black-Scholes.
Irving Fisher | American economist known for the Fisher equation linking nominal and real interest rates, and the debt-deflation theory of depressions.
Capital Asset Pricing model | Model pricing an asset's expected return via its beta, or sensitivity to non-diversifiable market risk.
volatility | Statistical measure of price fluctuation used to gauge risk and as an input to option-pricing models like Black-Scholes.
Black-Scholes model | Formula for pricing European options based on volatility, strike price, and time to expiration, developed by Black, Scholes, and Merton.
risk aversion | Preference for certain outcomes over uncertain ones of equal expected value, formalized via concave utility functions in finance.
Sharpe ratio | Measure of risk-adjusted investment return, dividing excess return over the risk-free rate by volatility; devised by William Sharpe.
liquidity | Ease of converting an asset to cash without loss of value; liquidity crises can trigger bank runs and crashes.
bank runs | Situation where depositors withdraw funds en masse fearing insolvency, potentially causing a solvent bank to fail; motivates deposit insurance.
beta | Measure of an asset's sensitivity to overall market risk, used in the Capital Asset Pricing Model to determine expected return.
Black-Scholes-Merton model | Full name of the Black-Scholes option-pricing model, extended by Robert Merton, for which Scholes and Merton won the Nobel.
bonds | Fixed-income debt securities paying periodic interest, or coupons; prices move inversely to interest rates.
delta | Options-pricing sensitivity measuring how much an option's price changes per unit change in the underlying asset's price.
government bonds | Debt securities issued by national governments to finance spending, considered relatively low-risk fixed-income instruments.
mortgages | Loans secured by real estate; mortgage-backed securities and subprime lending were central to the 2008 financial crisis.
portfolios | Collections of financial assets held by an investor; diversification across a portfolio reduces idiosyncratic risk.
Robert Shiller | Nobel economist known for research on asset-price volatility, behavioral finance, and the Case-Shiller housing price index.
shareholders | Owners of a corporation's equity, whose interests corporate-finance theory, including Modigliani-Miller, studies relative to managers and creditors.
stocks | Equity shares representing partial ownership of a corporation, priced in markets partly per models like CAPM.

# Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics
> Labor markets, human capital, income distribution, and the empirical methods economists use to measure inequality and welfare.
human capital | Skills, education, and training embodied in workers that raise productivity and wages; theorized by Gary Becker and Jacob Mincer.
income | Flow of earnings to households from labor, capital, and transfers; its distribution is central to inequality measurement.
labor | Factor of production supplied by workers; its market clears through wages, subject to unions, minimum wages, and search frictions.
Amartya Sen | Indian welfare economist known for the capability approach, social choice theory, and work on famine and development.
minimum wage | Legal wage floor debated for effects on employment; David Card's natural-experiment studies challenged simple competitive predictions.
Pareto efficiency | Allocation where no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off; core welfare-economics benchmark.
econometrics | Statistical methods applying regression, instrumental variables, and natural experiments to test economic theories with real-world data.
Pareto optimality | Synonym for Pareto efficiency: no reallocation can improve one party without harming another; a central welfare criterion.
Thomas Piketty | French economist whose Capital in the Twenty-First Century argues returns on capital outpace growth, driving rising inequality.
difference-in-differences | Econometric technique comparing changes over time between treatment and control groups to estimate causal effects, as in minimum-wage studies.
income inequality | Unequal distribution of income across a population, measured by the Gini coefficient and analyzed by Piketty and Kuznets.
welfare economics | Branch studying how resource allocations affect social well-being, using Pareto efficiency and compensation criteria; Sen and Pigou are key figures.
David Card | Labor economist awarded the Nobel for natural-experiment studies of minimum wage and immigration, notably with Alan Krueger in New Jersey.
economic inequality | Disparities in income and wealth across individuals or groups, measured via the Gini coefficient and studied by Piketty.
Gary Becker | Chicago economist who extended economic analysis to human capital, discrimination, crime, and the family.
Gini coefficient | Statistical measure of income or wealth inequality ranging from 0, perfect equality, to 1, perfect inequality.
instrumental variables | Econometric technique using a variable correlated with treatment but not the outcome directly, to estimate causal effects.
Amartya | Likely shorthand for Amartya Sen, the Indian welfare economist known for the capability approach and famine studies.
Claudia Goldin | Nobel labor economist known for research on the gender pay gap and women's changing role in the U.S. labor force.
Gary Stanley Becker | Full name of Gary Becker, Chicago economist who applied economic analysis to human capital, discrimination, and crime.
height | Anthropometric measure used in economic history to proxy nutrition and living standards, and in labor economics to study wage premiums.
instrumental variable | Econometric tool for estimating causal effects when treatment is not randomly assigned, isolating variation uncorrelated with the outcome's error term.
labor supply | Amount of labor workers offer at each wage, modeled via the tradeoff between income from work and leisure.
labor unions | Worker organizations that bargain collectively over wages and conditions, studied for effects on wage compression and employment.
natural experiment | Empirical research design exploiting real-world events that mimic random assignment, as in David Card's minimum-wage studies.
poverty | State of lacking sufficient income or resources; measured via poverty lines and studied in development and welfare economics.
regression | Statistical technique estimating relationships between variables, foundational to empirical labor and applied microeconomics.

# International Trade & Development Economics
> Comparative advantage, exchange rates, and the theories explaining trade flows and economic development across nations.
David Ricardo | British classical economist who formulated comparative advantage, the labor theory of value, and a theory of land rent.
Paul Krugman | Nobel laureate known for New Trade Theory explaining intra-industry trade via economies of scale, and for economic geography.
international trade | Exchange of goods and services across borders, explained by comparative advantage, factor endowments, and increasing-returns trade models.
comparative advantage | Ricardian principle that countries gain from specializing in goods with lower opportunity cost, even without absolute advantage.
balance of trade | Difference between a country's exports and imports of goods and services; surpluses and deficits reflect trade competitiveness.
foreign exchange rate | Price of one currency in terms of another, determined by trade flows, interest-rate differentials, and purchasing power parity.
foreign exchange rates | Currency prices that adjust to trade and capital flows; fixed, floating, and managed regimes shape international monetary policy.
globalization | Increasing integration of world trade, capital, and labor markets, debated for effects on inequality and national sovereignty.
Jeffrey Sachs | Development economist known for shock-therapy transition advice to post-Soviet economies and work on ending extreme poverty.
exchange rates | Relative currency values that adjust to trade flows and interest-rate differentials, central to open-economy macroeconomics.
Heckscher-Ohlin model | Trade theory explaining that countries export goods intensive in their abundant factors of production, whether land, labor, or capital.
microfinance | Small loans to poor entrepreneurs lacking collateral, popularized by Muhammad Yunus's Grameen Bank as a development tool.
agriculture | Sector central to development economics, including farm subsidies, commodity markets, and rural poverty in developing economies.
Albert Otto Hirschman | Development economist known for exit, voice, and loyalty and the hiding hand concept of unplanned project benefits.
development | Subfield studying how poor countries grow, industrialize, and raise living standards, using tools from microfinance to aid-effectiveness studies.
foreign aid | Transfers of money or resources from richer to poorer nations intended to spur development, debated for effectiveness by Sachs and critics.
Hernando de Soto | Peruvian economist who argued that formalizing property rights for the poor unlocks dead capital and spurs development.
Marshall-Lerner condition | Condition stating a currency devaluation improves the trade balance only if export and import demand elasticities together exceed one.
PPP | Purchasing power parity: theory that exchange rates adjust so identical goods cost the same across countries.
trade | Exchange of goods and services across borders or markets, explained by comparative advantage and factor-endowment theories.
Wassily Leontief | Economist who developed input-output analysis of inter-industry production and identified the Leontief paradox in U.S. trade patterns.
