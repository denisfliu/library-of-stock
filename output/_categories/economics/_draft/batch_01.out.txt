John Maynard Keynes	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	British economist whose General Theory (1936) founded macroeconomics, advocating government spending to fight unemployment during recessions.
economic growth	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Long-run increase in an economy's output, studied via Solow-Swan and endogenous growth models emphasizing capital, labor, and technology.
Milton Friedman	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Chicago School monetarist who argued money-supply growth drives inflation and business cycles; wrote A Monetary History of the United States.
business cycles	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Recurring fluctuations of expansion and recession in aggregate output, explained by Keynesian, monetarist, and real-business-cycle theories.
risk	Financial Economics	Uncertainty over financial outcomes, measured via variance and volatility and priced through models like CAPM and the Sharpe ratio.
utility	Consumer & Producer Theory	Measure of satisfaction from consumption underlying demand theory; cardinal and ordinal versions support indifference-curve analysis.
David Ricardo	International Trade & Development Economics	British classical economist who formulated comparative advantage, the labor theory of value, and a theory of land rent.
oligopoly	Market Structure & Industrial Organization	Market structure with few interdependent sellers, modeled via Cournot quantity competition or Bertrand price competition.
capital	Consumer & Producer Theory	Factor of production alongside land and labor, encompassing physical plant and equipment and its accumulation in growth models.
information	Game Theory & Information Economics	Asymmetric or imperfect information distorts markets, studied via adverse selection, signaling, and screening models.
inflation	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Sustained rise in the general price level, explained by monetarist money-supply growth, demand-pull, and cost-push theories.
money	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Medium of exchange whose supply and velocity link to inflation and output in quantity-theory and Keynesian frameworks.
money supply	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Central-bank-controlled stock of currency and deposits (M1/M2); monetarists tie its growth rate directly to inflation.
auctions	Game Theory & Information Economics	Mechanisms for allocating goods via bidding; English, Dutch, first-price sealed-bid, and Vickrey second-price formats are studied in auction theory.
Interest rate	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Price of borrowing money, set in part by central banks as a monetary-policy tool to influence investment and inflation.
Nash equilibrium	Game Theory & Information Economics	Strategy profile where no player benefits from unilaterally deviating; foundational solution concept named for John Nash.
human capital	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	Skills, education, and training embodied in workers that raise productivity and wages; theorized by Gary Becker and Jacob Mincer.
public goods	Market Failure & Public Economics	Non-excludable, non-rival goods like national defense that markets underprovide, motivating government provision and free-rider analysis.
Kenneth Arrow	Classical & Neoclassical Foundations	Nobel economist known for the Arrow-Debreu general equilibrium model and Arrow's Impossibility Theorem on aggregating voter preferences.
Paul Krugman	International Trade & Development Economics	Nobel laureate known for New Trade Theory explaining intra-industry trade via economies of scale, and for economic geography.
Paul Samuelson	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Founder of the neoclassical synthesis blending Keynesian and neoclassical economics; wrote the influential textbook Economics.
Ronald Coase	Market Failure & Public Economics	Economist behind the Coase theorem and the transaction-cost theory of the firm, explaining why firms exist and how externalities can be resolved.
Alfred Marshall	Classical & Neoclassical Foundations	Neoclassical economist who formalized supply-and-demand curves and marginal analysis in Principles of Economics (1890).
externalities	Market Failure & Public Economics	Uncompensated third-party costs or benefits from an economic activity, addressed via Pigouvian taxes or Coasean bargaining.
Harold Hotelling	Market Structure & Industrial Organization	Statistician-economist known for Hotelling's law of spatial competition and Hotelling's rule governing nonrenewable-resource extraction.
income	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	Flow of earnings to households from labor, capital, and transfers; its distribution is central to inequality measurement.
international trade	International Trade & Development Economics	Exchange of goods and services across borders, explained by comparative advantage, factor endowments, and increasing-returns trade models.
labor	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	Factor of production supplied by workers; its market clears through wages, subject to unions, minimum wages, and search frictions.
monopolistic competition	Market Structure & Industrial Organization	Market structure of many firms selling differentiated products with free entry, yielding downward-sloping demand and excess capacity.
monopoly	Market Structure & Industrial Organization	Market structure with a single seller facing the whole market demand curve, restricting output to raise price above marginal cost.
prisoner's dilemma	Game Theory & Information Economics	Classic game where mutual defection is the dominant strategy despite cooperation being jointly better, illustrating collective-action failure.
taxes	Market Failure & Public Economics	Government levies used to raise revenue, correct externalities via Pigouvian taxes, and redistribute income.
transaction costs	Market Failure & Public Economics	Costs of negotiating, monitoring, and enforcing exchanges; central to Coase's theory of the firm and property-rights bargaining.
unemployment	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Share of the labor force without work, decomposed into frictional, structural, and cyclical types tied to the business cycle.
Vilfredo Pareto	Classical & Neoclassical Foundations	Italian economist-sociologist known for Pareto efficiency, the 80/20 Pareto principle, and elite-circulation theory in sociology.
voting	Market Failure & Public Economics	Public-choice topic covering majority rule and strategic voting, tied to Arrow's Impossibility Theorem for aggregating individual preferences.
Austrian School	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Tradition of Menger, Mises, and Hayek emphasizing subjective value and an Austrian business-cycle theory blaming credit expansion for booms and busts.
consumption	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Household spending component of GDP; Keynes's consumption function and Friedman's permanent-income hypothesis model its determinants.
equilibrium	Consumer & Producer Theory	Point where quantity supplied equals quantity demanded, clearing a market at a stable price.
indifference curves	Consumer & Producer Theory	Curves showing consumption bundles yielding equal utility; their convex shape reflects diminishing marginal rate of substitution.
Joseph Stiglitz	Game Theory & Information Economics	Nobel economist known for screening models and the economics of asymmetric information, alongside Akerlof and Spence.
monopsony	Market Structure & Industrial Organization	Market structure with a single buyer, notably applied to labor markets where employers can suppress wages below marginal product.
options	Financial Economics	Derivative contracts giving the right, not obligation, to buy or sell an asset; priced using the Black-Scholes model.
prices	Consumer & Producer Theory	Signals coordinating supply and demand decisions, set at the intersection of supply and demand curves under competition.
Robert Lucas	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	New classical economist behind the Lucas critique and rational-expectations macroeconomics; won the Nobel Prize in 1995.
Amartya Sen	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	Indian welfare economist known for the capability approach, social choice theory, and work on famine and development.
arbitrage	Financial Economics	Simultaneous buying and selling to exploit price discrepancies risklessly; underlies no-arbitrage asset-pricing models like Black-Scholes.
comparative advantage	International Trade & Development Economics	Ricardian principle that countries gain from specializing in goods with lower opportunity cost, even without absolute advantage.
demand	Consumer & Producer Theory	Quantity of a good buyers wish to purchase at each price, graphed as a downward-sloping curve reflecting diminishing marginal utility.
Friedrich August von Hayek	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Austrian School economist who warned of central planning in The Road to Serfdom and developed Austrian business-cycle theory.
game theory	Game Theory & Information Economics	Mathematical study of strategic decision-making among interdependent agents, formalized by von Neumann, Morgenstern, and Nash.
Irving Fisher	Financial Economics	American economist known for the Fisher equation linking nominal and real interest rates, and the debt-deflation theory of depressions.
marginal cost	Consumer & Producer Theory	Cost of producing one additional unit; profit-maximizing firms set output where marginal cost equals marginal revenue.
minimum wage	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	Legal wage floor debated for effects on employment; David Card's natural-experiment studies challenged simple competitive predictions.
Pareto efficiency	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	Allocation where no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off; core welfare-economics benchmark.
price	Consumer & Producer Theory	Amount paid for a good, determined by supply-and-demand equilibrium under perfect competition.
Simon Kuznets	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Economist who developed national-income accounting for GDP and the Kuznets curve linking growth and inequality.
unemployment rate	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Percentage of the labor force actively seeking work; tracked alongside inflation via the Phillips curve tradeoff.
William Stanley Jevons	Classical & Neoclassical Foundations	Marginalist economist who co-founded marginal utility theory and wrote on the paradox of value and business cycles.
advertising	Market Structure & Industrial Organization	Firm strategy to shift demand and signal quality under monopolistic competition and imperfect information.
cap-and-trade	Market Failure & Public Economics	Market-based environmental policy capping total emissions and letting firms trade pollution permits to reach efficient abatement.
Capital Asset Pricing model	Financial Economics	Model pricing an asset's expected return via its beta, or sensitivity to non-diversifiable market risk.
debt	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Borrowed funds owed by governments, firms, or households; public debt and deficits are central fiscal-policy concerns.
econometrics	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	Statistical methods applying regression, instrumental variables, and natural experiments to test economic theories with real-world data.
economies of scale	Market Structure & Industrial Organization	Falling average cost as output rises, driving natural monopoly and industry concentration in some sectors.
inflation rate	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Percentage change in the price level over time; targeted by central banks via monetary policy.
Joseph Schumpeter	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Economist who coined creative destruction to describe entrepreneurial innovation continually displacing incumbent firms and technologies.
moral hazard	Game Theory & Information Economics	Tendency to take more risk once insured or protected from consequences, a key information-economics problem in insurance markets.
Pareto optimality	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	Synonym for Pareto efficiency: no reallocation can improve one party without harming another; a central welfare criterion.
Prospect Theory	Behavioral Economics	Kahneman and Tversky's model of decision-making under risk, featuring loss aversion and reference-dependent value functions.
rationality	Behavioral Economics	Assumption that agents make consistent, self-interested choices; behavioral economics documents systematic departures from it.
real business cycle theory	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	New classical theory attributing business cycles to technology shocks rather than monetary or demand disturbances.
saving	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Portion of income not consumed; determines capital accumulation in Solow-Swan growth models via the savings rate.
substitution	Consumer & Producer Theory	Consumer response to relative price changes, replacing a costlier good with a cheaper substitute; part of the Slutsky decomposition.
Thomas Malthus	Classical & Neoclassical Foundations	Classical economist who argued population grows geometrically while food supply grows arithmetically, in An Essay on the Principle of Population.
Thomas Piketty	Labor, Welfare & Inequality Economics	French economist whose Capital in the Twenty-First Century argues returns on capital outpace growth, driving rising inequality.
time	Consumer & Producer Theory	Intertemporal dimension of choice, underlying discounting, time preference, and the tradeoff between present and future consumption.
volatility	Financial Economics	Statistical measure of price fluctuation used to gauge risk and as an input to option-pricing models like Black-Scholes.
aggregate demand	Macroeconomic Theory & Business Cycles	Total planned spending in an economy across consumption, investment, government, and net exports; its shortfall Keynes blamed for recessions.
balance of trade	International Trade & Development Economics	Difference between a country's exports and imports of goods and services; surpluses and deficits reflect trade competitiveness.
