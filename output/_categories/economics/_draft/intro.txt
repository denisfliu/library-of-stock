Economics questions center on macroeconomic theory and the economists who built it, with John Maynard Keynes, Milton Friedman, and David Ricardo among the most frequently asked answers. Business cycles, inflation, money supply, and unemployment recur constantly as both standalone answerlines and as the subject of theories attributed to named schools of thought—Keynesian, monetarist, Austrian, and new classical. Growth theory, centered on the Solow-Swan model, and the tools of monetary and fiscal policy (interest rates, quantitative easing, government spending) round out the macroeconomic core.

Microeconomic canon is built around utility, demand, and market structure: oligopoly, monopoly, monopolistic competition, and monopsony appear as a coherent family of market-structure answers, while indifference curves, marginal utility, and the Slutsky decomposition test consumer-theory fundamentals. Game theory contributes a dense cluster of its own—Nash equilibrium, the prisoner's dilemma, auctions, and mechanism design—overlapping with information economics (adverse selection, moral hazard, signaling) associated with Akerlof, Stiglitz, and Stigler.

A second major cluster covers named economists whose contributions double as concepts: Ronald Coase and the Coase theorem, Vilfredo Pareto and Pareto efficiency, Kenneth Arrow and general equilibrium, and Amartya Sen's welfare and development economics. Financial economics forms a distinct pocket—options, the Black-Scholes model, the Capital Asset Pricing Model, and risk/volatility—as does behavioral economics, anchored by Daniel Kahneman, Richard Thaler, and Prospect Theory. International trade and development (comparative advantage, exchange rates, the Heckscher-Ohlin model, microfinance) and labor/welfare economics (human capital, minimum wage, inequality measures like the Gini coefficient) each supply a further tier of recurring answers.

Beyond the highest-frequency answerlines, the canon has a long tail of specific terms, models, and applied econometric methods—instrumental variables, natural experiments, difference-in-differences—reflecting the field's empirical turn, alongside classical-era concepts (the water-diamond paradox, Malthusian population theory, Ricardian rent) that persist as identifiers even when asked only a handful of times.
