American playwright and screenwriter known for terse, profane dialogue. Best known for Glengarry Glen Ross and American Buffalo. Also wrote Oleanna, Speed-the-Plow, and screenplays including The Untouchables and Wag the Dog. Developed 'practical aesthetics' approach to acting with William H. Macy.
Glengarry Glen Ross

Mamet's most famous play, winner of the 1984 Pulitzer Prize. About down-and-out real estate salesmen in Chicago competing for 'leads.' The first act is set at the China Bowl Chinese restaurant where Ricky Roma delivers a rambling monologue to the timid James Lingk ('all train compartments smell vaguely of shit'). Aaronow and Moss mock people named Patel as deadbeats; these lines were edited for a 2004 revival but kept in the film. Shelley 'The Machine' Levene begs John Williamson for good leads (can't afford the $100 bribe); Dave Moss coerces Aaronow to steal leads between acts. Williamson inadvertently ruins Roma's sale to Lingk. Roma uses the 'Kenilworth trick' (pretends to need to catch a plane to Ray's wife's birthday). At the end, detective Baylen questions Levene after Williamson catches him in a lie (Williamson reveals the Nyborgs are mentally ill with no money). Roma demands his Cadillac and half of Levene's commissions. Unseen bosses Mitch and Murray run the contest. In the film adaptation, Alec Baldwin's added character Blake yells 'Coffee's for closers!' and shows off brass balls. Also inspired the play Bobby Gould in Hell (double-billed with Shel Silverstein).

American Buffalo

A play set at Don's Resale Shop. Don thinks he sold a rare American buffalo nickel for too little and plans with Teach and Bobby to rob the customer who bought it. Teach harbors a grudge against the lesbian couple Grace and Ruthie — Ruthie said 'Help yourself' after Teach stole a piece of toast from Grace's plate, and he accuses them of cheating at cards. Bobby was supposed to help but lies about the man leaving with a suitcase; Fletcher (the planned lookout) never arrives. Teach hits Bobby over the head with a 'dead pig sticker' after discovering Bobby was lying. Characters argue about whether the mugging victim Fletcher was taken to Columbus Hospital or Masonic Hospital. 'Action talks and bullshit walks.'

Oleanna

A two-person play inspired by the Clarence Thomas Supreme Court confirmation hearings. Title from a Norwegian folk song about a composer's failed utopian community. Carol accuses her professor John of sexual harassment and attempted rape after he offers to raise her grade to an 'A' if she keeps meeting with him. At the beginning John discusses 'term of art' and 'easement' (he's buying a house) with his wife on the phone. John has written a book about 'the virtual warehousing of the young.' John eventually violently beats Carol with a chair as his life unravels. Only two characters: the play ends with 'Yes. That's right.' The language of the professor and student switch in relative complexity over the course of three scenes.

Speed-the-Plow

A play satirizing the film industry. Title from the Middle English poem asking God to 'speed the plow' (also source of 'Piers Plowman'). Movie producer Bobby Gould green-lights a movie by Doug Brown instead of a movie about the apocalyptic effects of radiation, despite the efforts of the activist Karen.

General / Biographical

Mamet is known for terse, profane dialogue with characters talking over each other. He developed the 'practical aesthetics' approach to acting with William H. Macy at Goddard College. Joe Mantegna starred in his films Homicide and House of Games. He wrote screenplays for Wag the Dog (adapted from Barry Levinson film, adapted from American Hero), The Untouchables (Brian de Palma), and The Verdict. He adapted Chekhov's Uncle Vanya; Andre Gregory rehearsed it in abandoned spaces shown in Louis Malle's Vanya on 42nd Street. His play The Duck Variations is set on a park bench. His play The Cryptogram, inspired by the divorce of his parents, features Donny discovering her cheating husband has left. Boston Marriage is about two women in New England. The play November is about a president. His book Bambi vs Godzilla is about the movie business. He was a mentor to playwright Neil LaBute. Sexual Perversity in Chicago is another early play.

Summary of Facts

David Mamet is an American playwright and screenwriter known for terse, profane dialogue ('Mamet speak') with characters talking over each other. His most famous play is Glengarry Glen Ross (1984 Pulitzer Prize), which follows down-and-out real estate salesmen in Chicago competing for leads. The first act is set at the China Bowl Chinese restaurant, where Ricky Roma delivers a rambling monologue to James Lingk ('all train compartments smell vaguely of shit') and Aaronow and Moss mock people named Patel as deadbeats (controversial lines edited for 2004 revival but kept in the film). Shelley 'The Machine' Levene begs manager John Williamson for good leads but can't afford the bribe; Dave Moss coerces Aaronow to steal the leads between acts. Williamson inadvertently ruins Roma's sale to Lingk. Roma uses the 'Kenilworth trick' to avoid Lingk. At the end, detective Baylen questions Levene after Williamson catches him in a lie — Williamson reveals the Nyborgs are mentally ill and broke. Roma demands his Cadillac and half of Levene's commissions. The film adaptation added Alec Baldwin's character Blake who yells 'Coffee's for closers!' and shows off brass balls.

American Buffalo is set at Don's Resale Shop, where Don thinks he sold a rare buffalo nickel for too little and plans with Teach and Bobby to rob the customer. Teach harbors a grudge because Ruthie said 'Help yourself' after he stole Grace's toast, and accuses the couple of cheating at cards. Bobby was supposed to help but lies; Fletcher (the lookout) never arrives. Teach hits Bobby over the head with a 'dead pig sticker.' Characters argue whether Fletcher was taken to Columbus Hospital or Masonic Hospital.

Oleanna is a two-person play inspired by the Clarence Thomas confirmation hearings, titled after a Norwegian folk song about a failed utopian community. Carol accuses professor John of sexual harassment and attempted rape after he offers to raise her grade to an 'A' if she keeps meeting with him. The language of professor and student switch in complexity over three scenes. John violently beats Carol with a chair as his life unravels.

Speed-the-Plow, titled from the Middle English poem asking God to 'speed the plow,' satirizes the film industry: movie producer Bobby Gould green-lights a Doug Brown movie over a radiation apocalypse film despite activist Karen's efforts.

Mamet developed 'practical aesthetics' with William H. Macy at Goddard College. Screenwriting credits include Wag the Dog (Barry Levinson), The Untouchables (Brian de Palma), and The Verdict. He adapted Chekhov's Uncle Vanya; Andre Gregory rehearsed it in abandoned spaces, shown in Louis Malle's Vanya on 42nd Street. He is a mentor to playwright Neil LaBute. Other plays include Sexual Perversity in Chicago, The Duck Variations (set on a park bench), The Cryptogram, Boston Marriage, and November.
