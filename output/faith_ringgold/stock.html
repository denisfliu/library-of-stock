American artist best known for narrative story quilts combining painted imagery with text. African-American; born 1930 in Harlem, New York. Key works include Who's Afraid of Aunt Jemima?, Tar Beach, The French Collection (including Picasso's Studio and Dancing at the Louvre), and the American People Series of paintings. Also known for her Witch and Family of Women mask sculptures. Inspired to work in the quilt medium after visiting the Rijksmuseum and seeing Nepali thangkas.
Who's Afraid of Aunt Jemima? (1983)

Ringgold's first and most famous story quilt. 56 squares; a narrative about a Harlem restaurateur named Willa Marie Simone (reimagining the "Aunt Jemima" stereotype) and her husband Big Rufus. The outer ring of 26 portraits has brown/yellow backgrounds; inner ring has yellow. The story spans 7 of the 56 squares. Wikipedia

American People Series / Flag Paintings

A series of paintings created in response to the 1967 U.S. race riots. The 20th work, subtitled 'Die,' shows a bloody scene of white and Black figures in chaos, inspired by Picasso's Guernica. Other flag paintings include The Flag Is Bleeding (black man and white couple with Stars and Stripes) and a 'Die' flag painting that replaces stripes with the N-word (in the Black Light series). General / Biographical

Faith Ringgold is an African-American artist born 1930 in Harlem, New York. Best known for her narrative story quilts, she also created paintings and mask sculptures. Also illustrated a children's book based on Gwendolyn Brooks's poetry. Associated with second-wave feminism and Black arts movements. Her full name is Faith Willi Jones (married name Ringgold).

Summary of Facts

Faith Ringgold (born 1930, Harlem, New York; full name Faith Willi Jones) is an African-American artist best known for her narrative story quilts that combine painted imagery with written text. Her most famous work is Who's Afraid of Aunt Jemima? (1983), her first story quilt, which reimagines the 'Aunt Jemima' mammy stereotype as a successful Harlem restaurateur named Willa Marie Simone operating a restaurant with her husband Big Rufus. The quilt consists of 56 squares, with 26 brown-and-yellow-backgrounded portrait squares surrounding narrative text blocks; it was partially inspired by Ringgold's personal experience with overweight stereotypes. Ringgold was inspired to experiment with the quilt medium after visiting the Rijksmuseum and seeing Nepali thangkas there. Her Tar Beach story quilt depicts a girl named Cassie Louise Lightfoot flying over the George Washington Bridge while her family eats dinner on a Harlem rooftop; the work was inspired by African American folktales about flying (the same tradition that inspired Toni Morrison's Song of Solomon), and was also adapted into a children's book. Tar Beach #2 is the fabric quilt version. The French Collection, Part I is a series of story quilts set in Paris featuring recurring character Willa Marie Simone: Dancing at the Louvre shows the character and her daughters leaping in front of the Mona Lisa, while Picasso's Studio depicts Willa Marie posing as a model for Les Demoiselles d'Avignon. Ringgold's American People Series of paintings was created in response to the 1967 U.S. race riots; its 20th work, Die, depicts a bloody scene with a white boy and black girl at the center amid chaos, inspired by Picasso's Guernica. Her flag paintings include The Flag Is Bleeding (a black man and white couple interwoven with the Stars and Stripes, inspired by Jasper Johns), and a work in the Black Light series replacing the flag's stripes with a racial slur. She also created sculptural mask work: her Witch series uses raffia as hair (including Women's Liberation Talking Mask), and her Family of Woman Mask series includes a life-sized statue of Wilt Chamberlain. Ringgold illustrated a children's book based on Gwendolyn Brooks's A Street in Bronzeville poetry. Her work with the Street Story Quilt, a triptych with sections titled 'Accident,' 'Fire,' and 'Homecoming,' also appears in quizbowl clues.
