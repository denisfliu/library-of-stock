Summary of Facts

Charles Demuth (1883-1935) was an American Precisionist painter from Lancaster, Pennsylvania. Precisionism was an American art movement emphasizing hard edges, also including Charles Sheeler and Elsie Driggs. His works are housed at the Whitney Museum of American Art.

His most famous work is I Saw the Figure 5 in Gold (1928), a poster portrait inspired by William Carlos Williams's poem 'The Great Figure.' The number 5 appears three times, becoming smaller to evoke a speeding fire truck. The word 'BILL' appears at the top in billboard font, 'W.C.W.' initials are at the bottom, and 'Art Co.' is inscribed on the right edge. Two street lamps appear in the upper corners, black lines depict heavy rain, and criss-crossing diagonal shards represent motion. It depicts Williams's visit to Marsden Hartley's apartment. The artist incised graphite ink into Upsom wallboard material. Robert Indiana created five variations with 'EAT,' 'DIE,' 'HUG,' and 'ERR' superimposed on red stars, titled for the 'American Dream' of Demuth. Jasper Johns made a similar painting, Figure 7, with a Mona Lisa reproduction.

My Egypt depicts grain elevators (owned by John W. Eshelman & Sons) in Lancaster with diagonal light beams and two cylindrical structures, linking industrial architecture to Egyptian pyramids.

Demuth created eight 'poster portraits' of friends including Williams, Arthur Dove (showing a scythe and the word 'DOVE'), Georgia O'Keeffe, and Gertrude Stein. Love Love Love is an homage to Stein featuring a mask, numbers '1 2 3,' and the word 'Love' repeated three times. A poster portrait of 'female impersonator' Bert Savoy shows two calla lilies emerging from a shell, borrowed from Marsden Hartley as a symbol of homosexuality.

Other works include Distinguished Air, a watercolor depicting two gay sailors and a woman studying Brancusi's Princess X (inspired by a Robert McAlmon story), After Christopher Wren (painting of the Provincetown Heritage Museum), Turkish Bath with Self Portrait (portraying his homosexuality), and watercolor illustrations of Henry James stories including Boat Ride from Sorrento (inspired by The Beast in the Jungle). His work influenced Georgia O'Keeffe's skyscraper paintings.
