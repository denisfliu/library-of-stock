Contemporary American novelist known for The Corrections, Freedom, and Purity. Also known for the essays 'Mr. Difficult' (about William Gaddis) and 'Why Bother?' (originally 'Perchance to Dream'), collected in How to Be Alone. Currently writing the trilogy A Key to All Mythologies, whose first volume is Crossroads.
The Corrections

A novel set in St. Jude about the Lambert family. Enid Lambert wants the family to gather for 'one last Christmas.' Her husband Alfred, a retired railway engineer, suffers from Parkinson's disease and dementia and is treated with the drug Corecktall (correct-all). A character tries to steal an expensive salmon filet by hiding it in his pants and later goes to Lithuania with Gitanas. Franzen was criticized for elitism after saying his goal of 'actually reaching a male audience' made him uncomfortable about the novel's selection for Oprah's Book Club. Won the National Book Award.

Freedom

A 2010 novel about the Berglund family set in the first decade of the 21st century. Patty Berglund writes an autobiography as therapy (the second part of the novel). She got a basketball scholarship to the University of Minnesota and had an affair with the songwriter Richard Katz, whose album is Nameless Lake and whose band is the Traumatics. Katz resembles Muammar el-Qaddafi. Walter Berglund works for the Cerulean Mountain Trust to preserve warbler habitats in West Virginia, which is secretly an organization planning to strip mine coal. Walter has an affair with his Bengali assistant Lalitha after moving to D.C. Walter founds the anti-population growth group Free Space. Their son Joey goes through his poop to find his wedding ring on a trip to Argentina and attempts to get rich providing spare parts for supply trucks during the Iraq War. TIME called Franzen 'The Great American Novelist' in 2010.

Crossroads and A Key to All Mythologies Trilogy

The first volume (2021) of a planned trilogy called A Key to All Mythologies (sharing its name with the unfinished scholarly text by Edward Casaubon in George Eliot's Middlemarch). The trilogy is about 'the fundamentally irrational basis for everything we think and do and espouse.' Crossroads follows the Hildebrandt family in New Prospect, Illinois, as the associate minister Russ Hildebrandt's marriage to Marion deteriorates and he loses control of his church youth group. Frances Cottrell is flirted with by Russ.

"Mr. Difficult"

A 2002 New Yorker essay attacking William Gaddis and exploring the 'Problem of Hard-to-Read Books.' Differentiates between 'status' writers who ignore reader interests and 'contract' writers who care about pleasing readers. Includes a long anecdote about struggling to read Gaddis's The Recognitions (comparing it to being 'alone and unprepared on a steep-sided, frigid, airless, poorly mapped mountain') and inability to finish J R. Franzen notes he enjoys postmodern fiction for its characters, such as Oedipa Maas from Pynchon's The Crying of Lot 49. The essay was inspired by perceived difficulty of The Corrections and was collected in How to Be Alone.

"Why Bother?" and Other Essays

Originally published in Harper's as 'Perchance to Dream,' this essay cites Shirley Brice Heath's work on 'forced transition zones' and discusses promoting his first novel The Twenty-Seventh City (about St. Louis police chief S. Jammu). The essay discusses depictions of illogical life in Joseph Heller's Catch-22. Collected with 'Mr. Difficult' in How to Be Alone. He also wrote a New Yorker essay calling Edith Wharton a 'perverse, yearning' girl 'trapped inside all her best novels.'

Purity

A novel about a girl nicknamed 'Pip' with $130,000 in student loan debt. Andreas Wolf helps Pip kill her Stasi-informant stepfather. The novel was reviewed favorably by Ann Patchett, who compared Franzen to a performer juggling ten balls. Franzen also translated and modernized Frank Wedekind's Spring Awakening.

Summary of Facts

Jonathan Franzen is a contemporary American novelist and essayist. His breakthrough novel The Corrections is set in St. Jude and follows the Lambert family as Enid tries to gather them for 'one last Christmas.' Her husband Alfred, a retired railway engineer, suffers from Parkinson's disease and dementia and is treated with the drug Corecktall. A character steals a salmon filet in his pants and goes to Lithuania with Gitanas. Franzen was criticized for elitism after expressing discomfort about the novel's selection for Oprah's Book Club.

Freedom (2010) chronicles the Berglund family. Patty writes an autobiography as therapy, got a basketball scholarship to the University of Minnesota, and had an affair with Traumatics frontman Richard Katz (album Nameless Lake). Walter works for the Cerulean Mountain Trust preserving warblers in West Virginia while the organization secretly plans to strip mine coal. He founds the anti-population group Free Space and has an affair with Bengali assistant Lalitha. Their son Joey goes through his poop for his wedding ring in Argentina. TIME named Franzen 'The Great American Novelist' in 2010.

Franzen is currently writing the trilogy A Key to All Mythologies, whose first volume Crossroads (2021) follows the Hildebrandt family in New Prospect, Illinois, as minister Russ's marriage to Marion deteriorates. His novel Purity features protagonist 'Pip' with $130,000 in student debt and Andreas Wolf.

His essay 'Mr. Difficult' (New Yorker, 2002) attacks William Gaddis, distinguishing 'status' vs. 'contract' models of literature, with a long anecdote about struggling to read The Recognitions and J R. 'Why Bother?' (originally 'Perchance to Dream') discusses promoting The Twenty-Seventh City and cites Catch-22. Both were collected in How to Be Alone. He called Edith Wharton 'a perverse, yearning girl trapped inside all her best novels.'
