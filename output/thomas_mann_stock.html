General / Biographical

Mann is a German Nobel laureate. His brother Heinrich wrote Man of Straw/Professor Unrat; his daughter Erika married W.H. Auden for a passport; his son Klaus wrote Mephisto. Helen Tracy Lowe-Porter translated almost all his works. Mann prompted a debate over 'inner emigration.' He cited J.P. Jacobsen as his greatest stylistic influence. Colm Toibin fictionalized his life in The Magician. Mann acknowledged Wagnerian influences including leitmotifs.

Summary of Facts

Thomas Mann is a German Nobel Prize-winning novelist and short story writer best known for Death in Venice, Buddenbrooks, The Magic Mountain, and Doctor Faustus. He is a member of the famous Mann literary family, which includes his brother Heinrich Mann (author of Man of Straw/Professor Unrat), his daughter Erika Mann (who married W.H. Auden for a British passport and wrote School for Barbarians), and his son Klaus Mann (author of Mephisto).

Death in Venice is Mann's most frequently clued work. The writer Gustav von Aschenbach becomes obsessed with the classical beauty of the Polish boy Tadzio while vacationing in Venice, staying at the Hotel des Bains. A barber helps Aschenbach dye his hair and paint his face to look more youthful, and he imagines himself as Socrates lecturing Phaedrus on beauty. He dreams of a bacchanalia-like ritual dedicated to the 'Stranger God.' He contracts cholera after eating overripe strawberries and collapses after attempting to rise to meet Tadzio. A band of street musicians performs a song with rhythmic mocking Laughter. Aschenbach encounters a foppish man with hideous false teeth. Gustav Mahler partly inspired Aschenbach. The real-life inspiration for Tadzio was Baron Wladyslaw Moes, identified in Gilbert Adair's book The Real Tadzio. Tadzio first appears in a sailor costume and fights with an older boy named Jasiu. Patrick Suskind discussed Mann's obsession with a teenage waiter named Franzl.

Buddenbrooks depicts four generations of a declining Lubeck grain merchant family, based on Mann's own family. Tony (Antonie) has brief, loveless marriages to the bankrupt Grunlich and the alcoholic hops merchant Alois Permaneder. Tony Goes to a Baltic resort to escape Grunlich. Christian quarrels with Tom and ends up in an insane asylum. The last male member, Hanno, dies of typhoid. Tony's teacher is the hunchbacked crone 'Sesemi' (Sesame Weichbrodt). The opening scene has a grandfather helping granddaughter Tony recite the Lutheran catechism. Thomas Buddenbrook dies from a botched tooth extraction shortly after having an emotional reaction to a chapter of Schopenhauer's The World as Will and Representation. Hanno's breakfast cup of hot chocolate symbolizes decadence. Helen Tracy Lowe-Porter's translation was critical in introducing Mann to English-speaking audiences.

The Magic Mountain follows Hans Castorp's seven-year stay at the Berghof sanatorium in the Swiss Alps, where he visits his tubercular cousin Joachim Ziemssen. The humanist Italian Settembrini (a student of Carducci) spars intellectually with the terror-loving Jesuit Naphta (fictionalized from Gyorgy Lukacs). Naphta commits suicide during a duel with Settembrini. The Russian patient Clavdia Chauchat (with 'Kirghiz eyes') is Castorp's romantic interest, and he flirts with her in French on Walpurgis Night. A recurring leitmotif is Castorp borrowing a pencil. Characters begin saying 'we up here' rather than 'those up here.' Settembrini finds music 'politically suspect' and uses the phrase 'sine pecunia.' Mynheer Peeperkorn is another character attracted to Chauchat. Castorp hallucinates seeing two witches while skiing in a blizzard. He operates a gramophone playing La Traviata and The Tales of Hoffmann. Seven tables are used at meals. The second chapter, about a christening basin, recounts Hans visiting his grandfather. Mann acknowledged Wagnerian influences including leitmotifs. Lowe-Porter chose the word 'magic' though Mann preferred 'enchanted.'

Doctor Faustus follows the composer Adrian Leverkuhn, narrated by his friend Serenus Zeitblom. Leverkuhn voluntarily contracts syphilis to increase his artistic output and composes the Apocalypsis cum Figuris. He uses an 'H, E, A, E, E-flat' motif representing 'Hetaera Esmeralda.' Wendell Kretzchmar delivers music lectures. The doomed child Echo Nepomuk appears. Theodor Adorno's explanations of Schoenberg's twelve-tone technique aided conception of the novel, causing a feud with Schoenberg. The novel was partly based on the life of Nietzsche. Leverkuhn sings 'Weisengrund' as a hidden thank-you to Adorno. Adorno convinced Mann not to redeem Leverkuhn at the end.

Tonio Kroger is a semi-autobiographical novella about a tormented artist with half-Brazilian parentage (inspired by Mann's own) who tries to reconcile his Northern and Southern European sides. Tonio is embarrassed when called 'Fraulein' after making an incorrect dance move. He is infatuated with Ingeborg Holm and admires Hans Hansen. Lisaveta calls him a 'lost burgher.' He is arrested walking through Denmark to his hometown. The novella ends praising 'bourgeois love' for 'the happy, lovely, and commonplace.' Garcia Marquez referenced Tonio Kroger in his Nobel speech.

Mario and the Magician is an allegory of fascism about the hypnotist Cipolla. A tourist's daughter is reported for bathing naked. Cipolla cracks a whip to make a heckler stick out his tongue and gifts a lady a pin saying 'Pensez tres fort.' Cipolla points to his cheek to signal Mario to kiss it. Mario shoots Cipolla twice after being hypnotized. A crowd mobs a vendor after the shooting. The story 'Blood of the Walsungs' scandalized society with its depiction of incest between twins Siegmund and Sieglinde.

Other works include The Transposed Heads (Sita faces a dilemma after two men decapitate themselves before a statue of Kali and have their heads swapped), Tristan (Detlev Spinell and Gabriele Kloterjahn bond playing Wagner's second act on piano at a sanatorium; Gabriele relapses), The Confessions of Felix Krull (a confidence man who assumes the name 'Armand' and seduces Madame Houpfle and the Kuckuck family), Holy Sinner (about abandoned child Gregory who marries his mother Sibylla), Joseph and His Brothers (a four-part novel taking sixteen years, set in Egypt; the malicious dwarf Dudu; Joseph the Provider parallels FDR's policies), Lotte in Weimar (about Goethe's fictionalization of his romance with Charlotte), 'Tobias Mindernickel' (stabs his dog Esau to comfort it while dying), 'Little Lizzy' (a cuckolded husband performs in drag), and 'The Clown.' Mann cited J.P. Jacobsen as having the greatest influence on his style. He prompted a debate over 'inner emigration' with radio speeches.
