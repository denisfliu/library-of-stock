General / Biographical

Vittorio de Sica (1901–1974) was a pioneer of Italian neorealism. His frequent scriptwriter collaborator was Cesare Zavattini (Bicycle Thieves, Umberto D.). He used non-professional actors. Andre Bazin was his most important critical champion. He was also an actor — he played the diplomat Baron Donati in Max Ophuls's The Earrings of Madame de... (1953). He directed Marriage Italian Style (1964) and Divorce Italian Style-related films with Marcello Mastroianni. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Vittorio de Sica (1901–1974) was the central figure in Italian neorealism, alongside Roberto Rossellini. His most important collaborator was screenwriter Cesare Zavattini, with whom he made Bicycle Thieves and Umberto D., both featuring non-professional actors. Andre Bazin was his most important critical champion.

Bicycle Thieves (Ladri di biciclette, 1948) is his masterpiece. Antonio Ricci and his son Bruno wander Rome in search of Antonio's stolen bicycle — which he needs to hold a job putting up advertising posters (including one of Rita Hayworth). His wife Maria has pawned the family's bedsheets to retrieve the bicycle from a pawnshop. A famous restaurant scene cuts between a rich family and the poor father-son duo discussing death. The film ends with Bruno crying as Antonio, having himself attempted to steal a bicycle, walks into the crowd — a dynamic inversion foreshadowed by an early scene where one character imitates the other's morning routine. Bazin praised a scene showing the couple through a small pawnshop window. The film was adapted from Luigi Bartolini's novel and inspired both Satyajit Ray's Apu Trilogy and parodies by Maurizio Nichetti.

Umberto D. (1952) follows an elderly retired bureaucrat too proud to beg for rent money. He trains his dog Flike to beg (the dog sits on the street with a hat in its mouth). On returning from tonsillitis treatment, Umberto finds his landlady has knocked a giant hole in his room's wall, leading to eviction. He contemplates jumping in front of a train but is stopped by his dog whimpering. Bazin praised a scene in which a young maid makes coffee and stares out the window at a cat.

Shoeshine (Sciuscia, 1946) features two shoeshine-boy protagonists who rob a fortune teller to afford to buy a horse. It won the first honorary Oscar for Best International Feature Film.

De Sica was also a celebrated actor. He played Baron Donati — the diplomat who returns a pair of earrings from Constantinople to Paris — in Max Ophuls's The Earrings of Madame de... (1953).
