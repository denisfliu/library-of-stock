Plautus

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Early Roman comic playwright, known for farces drawing on Greek New Comedy. Most commonly identified by Miles Gloriosus (The Braggart Soldier), Menaechmi (which inspired Shakespeare's Comedy of Errors), The Pot of Gold (Aulularia), Pseudolus, and Amphitryon. Credited with developing stock characters like the clever slave (servus callidus) and the braggart soldier.

Summary of Facts

Plautus (Titus Maccius Plautus) was an early Roman comic playwright influenced by Menander's Greek New Comedy and credited with developing stock characters. His plays survive in varying states of completeness because the monk who produced the Ambrosian palimpsest got tired of scrubbing midway through the alphabet, so plays beginning with early letters are worse-preserved.

Miles Gloriosus (The Braggart Soldier) centers on the arrogant soldier Pyrgopolynices (whose name means 'Conqueror of Many Towers'), a stock alazon character. He has a comically large shield carried by several servants and is constantly flattered by Artotrogus. The clever slave Palaestrio tricks his master by inventing the character 'Honoria' and helps Pleusicles rescue his lover Philocomasium from Pyrgopolynices. Pyrgopolynices is beaten at the end. He inspired the commedia dell'arte figure Il Capitano and Nicholas Udall's Ralph Roister Doister.

Menaechmi (The Brothers Menaechmus) is about separated twins reunited after comic confusion involving the prostitute Erotium and dogsbody Peniculus in Epidamnus. Sosicles changes his name after his twin is kidnapped. A wife's mantle is given to Erotium causing confusion. This play inspired Shakespeare's Comedy of Errors, which adds twin servants named Dromio (taken from Amphitryon) and relocates to Ephesus.

The Pot of Gold (Aulularia) is about the miser Euclio who guards a pot of gold. The chef Congrio complains about beatings, and Phaedria and Lyconides receive the gold as dowry. Molière drew on Euclio for The Miser.

Pseudolus centers on the clever slave who rescues Calidorus's lover Phoenicium from the pimp Ballio by impersonating the messenger Harpax. Amphitryon is Plautus's only mythological play, featuring the first reference to 'tragicomedy' — Zeus seduces Alcumena disguised as her husband and she gives birth to Hercules. Rudens features the star Arcturus foretelling the shipwreck of the pimp Labrax.

Plautus used a low proportion of iambic verse (more dialogue was sung than in Terence), compounded words for neologisms, and his corpus is the oldest extant source for 'homo homini lupus.' His Poenulus preserves the only Latin text in Carthaginian Punic.
