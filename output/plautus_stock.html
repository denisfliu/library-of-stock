Early Roman comic playwright (c. 254-184 BC), known for farces drawing on Greek New Comedy. Most commonly identified by Miles Gloriosus (arrogant soldier Pyrgopolynices), Menaechmi (twin confusion that inspired Shakespeare's Comedy of Errors), The Pot of Gold / Aulularia (miser Euclio, basis for Moliere's The Miser), Pseudolus (clever slave rescues lover from pimp Ballio; loosely adapted by Sondheim's A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum), and Amphitryon (Zeus seduces Alcumena; first use of 'tragicomedy'; basis for Moliere, Dryden, and Kleist adaptations). Credited with developing stock characters: the servus callidus (clever slave), the miles gloriosus (braggart soldier), and the senex amator (lustful old man). His Poenulus preserves the only extant Latin text in Carthaginian Punic. Oldest extant source for 'homo homini lupus.'

Summary of Facts

Plautus (Titus Maccius Plautus, c. 254-184 BC) was an early Roman comic playwright influenced by Menander's Greek New Comedy and credited with developing stock characters including the servus callidus (clever slave), the miles gloriosus (braggart soldier), and the senex amator (lustful old man, exemplified by Lysidamus in Casina). His plays survive in varying states of completeness because the monk who produced the Ambrosian palimpsest got tired of scrubbing midway through the alphabet. He used a low proportion of iambic verse (more dialogue was sung than in Terence), compounded words for neologisms, and his corpus preserves the oldest extant source for 'homo homini lupus' and the most uses of the exclamations ecastor and hercle. Eduard Fraenkel analyzed his use of comparative idioms.

Miles Gloriosus (The Braggart Soldier) centers on the arrogant soldier Pyrgopolynices ('Conqueror of Many Towers'), a stock alazon character. He has a comically large shield carried by several servants, claims to have killed 7,000 men 'at one fell blow,' and is constantly flattered by the parasitus Artotrogus. The clever slave Palaestrio invents the character 'Honoria,' using a hole in a partition wall to trick the dim-witted Sceledrus (who is caught peeking through a skylight and claims to be chasing a monkey), and helps Pleusicles rescue his lover Philocomasium. The courtesan Acroteleutium is paid to pretend to love Pyrgopolynices, who is lured into Periplectomenus's house and beaten. He inspired the commedia dell'arte figure Il Capitano and Nicholas Udall's Ralph Roister Doister.

Menaechmi (The Brothers Menaechmus) is about separated twins — sons of Moschus — reunited after comic confusion involving the prostitute Erotium and dogsbody Peniculus in Epidamnus. Sosicles changes his name after his twin is kidnapped. A wife's mantle given to Erotium causes confusion. This play inspired Shakespeare's Comedy of Errors, which adds twin servants named Dromio (taken from Amphitryon), relocates to Ephesus, and includes a scene comparing a kitchen maid's body to a 'globe' with France on her forehead, Spain on her breath, and Ireland's bogs in her buttocks.

The Pot of Gold (Aulularia) is about The Miser Euclio who guards a pot of gold past the point of paranoia, even killing a rooster scratching near it. Opens with a prologue by the household deity (lares familiaris). The chef Congrio complains about beatings, and Phaedria and Lyconides receive the gold as dowry. Moliere drew on Euclio for his The Miser (Harpagon). Ariano Suassuna retold it as The Saint and the Sow, set in Northeast Brazil.

Pseudolus centers on a wine-loving clever slave who rescues Calidorus's lover Phoenicium from the pimp Ballio (introduced with a monologue demanding a lavish birthday dinner) through impersonations and 2,000 drachma bets. Loosely adapted by Sondheim's A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum along with Curculio, Miles Gloriosus, and Mostellaria.

Amphitryon is Plautus's only mythological play, containing the first reference to 'tragicomedy.' Zeus seduces Alcumena disguised as her husband while Mercury impersonates the servant Sosia — the French word 'sosie' (look-alike) derives from this character. Alcumena gives birth to Hercules. Moliere based his own Amphitryon on this play (later adapted by Dryden and Heinrich von Kleist), producing the French proverb 'the real Amphitryon is the one who gives you dinner.'

Rudens (The Rope) features the star Arcturus foretelling the shipwreck of the pimp Labrax. A chest dragged from the ocean contains childhood toys proving Palaestra's identity as Daemones's long-lost daughter. Palaestra takes shelter in the temple of Venus. A delusional fisherman plans to found a city in his own name after catching a large trunk.

Other notable plays: Poenulus preserves the only extant Latin text in Carthaginian Punic, with Milphio comically 'translating' Hanno's words into nonsense about African mice. Truculentus features a prostitute who swindles the title character. Captivi inspired Ben Jonson's The Case is Altered. Mostellaria (The Haunted House) features a man jumping onto an altar. Machiavelli's Clizia was based on a lost Plautus play. Tom Stoppard's Arcadia features a tortoise named Plautus.
