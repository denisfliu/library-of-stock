Antonín Dvořák (1841–1904) was a Czech Romantic composer best known for his Symphony No. 9 'From the New World,' written during his time as director of the National Conservatory of Music in New York. His major works include the Cello Concerto in B minor, the 'American' String Quartet, the Slavonic Dances, and a series of symphonic poems based on Czech folk tales from Karel Erben's Kytice. His student and son-in-law was Josef Suk, who wrote the Asrael Symphony in Dvořák's memory.
Symphony No. 9 in E minor, 'From the New World'

Dvořák's final symphony, subtitled 'From the New World' (Z nového světa), is the most clued of his works. Written during his time in America (1892–1895), it was premiered by Anton Seidl at Carnegie Hall to thunderous applause. The symphony is in E minor and opens with a 4/8 Adagio slow introduction before transitioning to an Allegro molto. Its famous second movement (Largo) is in D-flat major after opening brass chords modulate from E major; an English horn solo introduces the pentatonic main theme, later adapted as the spiritual-style song 'Goin' Home.' The scherzo begins with the rhythmic motif of two eighth notes followed by a half note, paralleling Beethoven's Ninth. The fourth movement finale is marked Allegro con fuoco and uses a single cymbal note. Dvořák was inspired by African-American spirituals heard from Harry Burleigh (a maintenance man at the Conservatory who sang for him) and by The Song of Hiawatha. Dvořák denied quoting actual Native American tunes. Because Dvořák's music was published out of order, the symphony was formerly numbered as No. 8 and then No. 5. A recording was taken to the Moon by Neil Armstrong.

Cello Concerto in B minor, Op. 104

Dvořák's Cello Concerto in B minor (1895) is among the most beloved cello concertos in the repertoire. It was inspired by Victor Herbert's Second Cello Concerto, which Dvořák heard during his time at the National Conservatory. The intended dedicatee and cellist Hanuš Wihán wrote a 59-bar cadenza for the finale, which Dvořák refused to include; after Wihán was unable to make the premiere, the gift of two rare pigeons helped Leo Stern perform the premiere in 1895. The soloist enters in the first movement with the notes B, (short) C-sharp, (short) D-sharp, B; the descending second theme is 'long high F-sharp, E D B, D A.' The third movement quotes Dvořák's song 'Leave Me Alone' as tribute to his sister-in-law Josefína Čermáková. The concerto begins with an 86-bar orchestral introduction before the soloist enters. Herbert von Karajan's canonical 1969 Deutsche Grammophon recording paired it with Tchaikovsky's Variations on a Rococo Theme; Yo-Yo Ma recorded it twice.

String Quartet No. 12 in F major, 'American'

Dvořák's 'American' String Quartet (Op. 96) was written during his summer vacation in Spillville, Iowa in 1893. The quartet opens with two violins oscillating on an F-major triad before the viola enters with a folksy, syncopated pentatonic melody. The third movement includes a transcription of the sound of a red-eyed vireo (sometimes erroneously identified as a scarlet tanager) that pestered the composer. All main melodies use some combination of the notes D, F, G, A, and C. The quartet was composed immediately before the String Quintet No. 3 in E-flat major. It was written at the same time as the New World Symphony and contemporaneously with the Cello Concerto.

Slavonic Dances

The sixteen Slavonic Dances were originally written for piano four-hands and later orchestrated, achieving great commercial success. Publisher Fritz Simrock (encouraged by Brahms) commissioned the set and insisted Dvořák write lighter music. The dances are modeled on Brahms's Hungarian Dances. Despite their name, they do not directly use actual folk music material. They draw on dance forms such as the sousedská, skočná, and furiant. The first set's Furiant final movement opens with a descending G minor arpeggio over a hemiola. Dvořák also used horizontal hemiola — a measure of two beats followed by a measure of three — in these dances.

Symphonic Poems (after Erben's Kytice)

Dvořák composed four tone poems inspired by Karel Erben's Czech folklore collection Kytice: The Water Goblin (Vodník), The Noon Witch (Polednice), The Golden Spinning Wheel, and The Wild Dove. A fifth tone poem, A Hero's Song, is the only one not based on Kytice. The Water Goblin's motif is three repeated F-sharps; near the end, the goblin throws a dead baby against a door. The Noon Witch depicts the Slavic demon Lady Midday who comes to steal a misbehaving child; the mother faints over the child and smothers him. The Golden Spinning Wheel involves a spinning wheel that reveals the murder of Dornička. All belong to the genre invented by Franz Liszt.

Dumky Trio (Piano Trio No. 4)

Dvořák's Piano Trio No. 4 in E minor (Op. 90), nicknamed 'Dumky,' is a six-movement work based on the Ukrainian folk-ballad form dumka. Each movement alternates between a solemn, slow section and a fast, bright major-key section. The first three movements are played without pause. The opening begins with slow quarter notes C, E, A on cello intertwined with descending sixths in triplet rhythm from the piano. The first movement is in 4/8 time, switching between E minor and E major before ending in C-sharp minor. Dvořák incorporated the dumka form after it was popularized by Ukrainian composer Mykola Lysenko. The work starts in E minor but ends in C major.

General / Biographical

Dvořák was a Czech composer (1841–1904) who served as director of the National Conservatory of Music in New York from 1892 to 1895, recruited by Jeanette Thurber with a promise of a $15,000 annual salary (payments became irregular due to the Panic of 1893). His first symphony is nicknamed 'The Bells of Zlonice' — the manuscript was lost after he submitted it to a competition and was found in a Leipzig bookstore 18 years later. His son-in-law and former pupil Josef Suk composed the Asrael Symphony after Dvořák's death. Dvořák's Sixth Symphony is often compared to Brahms's Second Symphony (same tempo, key, structure). His Eighth Symphony in G major is sometimes called the 'English' symphony (published by London's Novello & Co.) and its finale opens with a trumpet fanfare. He wrote a trilogy of overtures: In Nature's Realm, Carnival, and Othello. His most famous piano piece is the G-flat major Humoresque (seventh of eight). He arranged 'Silent Woods' (Klid) for cello and orchestra from a piano duet. His opera The Devil and Kate features Kate and Jirka journeying to Hell.

Summary of Facts

Antonín Dvořák (1841–1904) was the preeminent Czech composer of the Romantic era and one of the most frequently clued composers in quizbowl. He served as director of the National Conservatory of Music in New York from 1892 to 1895, recruited by Jeanette Thurber for a salary of $15,000 per year (which became irregular after the Panic of 1893). During this American period he wrote his most celebrated works, spending summers in Spillville, Iowa.

His Symphony No. 9 in E minor 'From the New World' (1893) is the most commonly clued of his works. It was premiered by Anton Seidl at Carnegie Hall. The symphony's famous Largo second movement opens with brass chords modulating from E major to D-flat major before the English horn introduces a pentatonic theme that was later adapted as the song 'Goin' Home.' Dvořák was inspired by African-American spirituals heard from Harry Burleigh, a Black singer who worked as a maintenance man at the Conservatory to pay his tuition. Dvořák denied quoting actual Native American tunes. Due to out-of-order publication, the symphony was formerly numbered No. 8 and then No. 5. A recording was taken to the Moon by Neil Armstrong.

The Cello Concerto in B minor (Op. 104, 1895) was inspired by Victor Herbert's Second Cello Concerto. The soloist enters after an 86-bar orchestral introduction with the notes B–C-sharp–D-sharp–B. The third movement quotes Dvořák's song 'Leave Me Alone' as a tribute to his sister-in-law Josefína. The dedicatee Hanuš Wihán wrote a cadenza for the finale that Dvořák refused to include; Leo Stern performed the premiere after being given a gift of two rare pigeons. Herbert von Karajan's 1969 DG recording paired it with Tchaikovsky's Rococo Variations; Yo-Yo Ma recorded it twice.

The 'American' String Quartet (Op. 96) was also written in Spillville. It opens with oscillating violins on an F-major triad followed by a pentatonic viola melody. Its third movement transcribes the call of a red-eyed vireo (sometimes misidentified as a scarlet tanager) that pestered the composer. All main melodies use the pentatonic subset D, F, G, A, C.

The sixteen Slavonic Dances, originally for piano four-hands and then orchestrated, brought Dvořák his first international fame. They were commissioned by publisher Fritz Simrock at Brahms's urging and modeled on Brahms's Hungarian Dances. They draw on Czech dance forms including the furiant (notable for horizontal hemiola) and sousedská.

His four symphonic poems based on Karel Erben's folklore collection Kytice include The Water Goblin (motif of three repeated F-sharps; the goblin throws a dead baby against a door), The Noon Witch (Lady Midday steals a misbehaving child; his mother faints and smothers him), The Golden Spinning Wheel (a wheel reveals Dornička's murder), and The Wild Dove. A Hero's Song is a fifth tone poem not based on Kytice.

The Dumky Piano Trio (Op. 90) is a six-movement work based on the Ukrainian folk-ballad form dumka, with each movement alternating between solemn slow sections and fast bright sections. The first three movements play without pause. Dvořák also wrote a set of eight Humoresques for piano, of which the seventh in G-flat major is his most famous piano piece.

Biographically: Dvořák's first symphony 'The Bells of Zlonice' was lost after being submitted to a competition and recovered in a Leipzig bookstore 18 years later. His son-in-law and pupil Josef Suk wrote the Asrael Symphony after Dvořák's death. Dvořák's Sixth Symphony is often compared to Brahms's Second Symphony (same key, tempo, structure); his Eighth Symphony in G major (the 'English' symphony) was published by Novello & Co. after his usual publisher offered only 1,000 marks, and its finale opens with a trumpet fanfare. He also wrote the overture trilogy In Nature's Realm, Carnival, and Othello.
