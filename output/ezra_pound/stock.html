Ezra Pound is one of the most frequently clued American modernist poets in quizbowl. Questions center heavily on The Cantos (especially the Pisan section and the usura canto), his iconic two-line Imagist poem 'In a Station of the Metro,' and the long poem 'Hugh Selwyn Mauberley.' His role as founder of Imagism and co-founder of Vorticism, his literary criticism, his fascist sympathies and treason charges, and his relationships with T.S. Eliot, H.D., and Wyndham Lewis all feature prominently.
The Cantos

The Cantos is Pound's unfinished modernist epic poem and by far his most clued work. The poem begins with 'And then went down to the ship,' an adaptation of the nekuia from the Odyssey. Its second canto famously addresses Robert Browning with 'Hang it all, there can be but the one Sordello.' Canto XLV repeats the phrase 'with usura' over twenty times, denouncing usury as something that 'rusteth the chisel' and ensures no man has 'a house of good stone.' The poem includes sections named for China, John Adams, 'Rock-Drill,' and 'Thrones,' and in its later sections incorporates Chinese characters. The line 'what thou lovest well remains / the rest is dross' and the dictum 'Make it new' appear in the work. The Cantos controversially won the first Bollingen Prize, prompting the Library of Congress to withdraw its involvement. A Brazilian group of concrete poets called Noigandres took their name from a passage in the poem. H.D. wrote her poem Helen in Egypt partly as a response to The Cantos.

The Pisan Cantos

The Pisan Cantos are a subsection of The Cantos written while Pound was imprisoned in a cage near Pisa after being captured for broadcasting fascist propaganda in Italy. Clues frequently reference the circumstances of composition: Pound wrote portions on toilet paper while detained in an exposed cage in Tuscany. The 81st canto contains the line 'pull down thy vanity,' and the 80th ends with 'God knows what else is left of London, our London.' The first Pisan Canto adopts the persona of 'OY TIS' and blends into other personas. Pound shared a prison cell with Louis Till (Emmett Till's father), which inspired a segment. Elizabeth Bishop's poem 'Visits to St. Elizabeths' references Pound's subsequent incarceration at St. Elizabeths Hospital.

In a Station of the Metro

This iconic two-line Imagist poem is one of the most frequently clued individual poems in quizbowl. It reads in its entirety: 'The apparition of these faces in the crowd: / Petals on a wet, black bough.' Pound reportedly reduced the poem from thirty lines to just fourteen words. It is sometimes called the first American haiku. In a 1914 essay about Vorticism, Pound described how the scene that inspired the poem made him feel that if he had been a painter, he would have founded a new school based on arrangements of color. He also connected the poem's inspiration to Kandinsky's Concerning the Spiritual in Art.

Hugh Selwyn Mauberley

This long poem is one of Pound's most frequently referenced works after The Cantos and 'In a Station of the Metro.' The title character 'strove to resuscitate the dead art of poetry,' and his 'true Penelope was Flaubert.' The poem laments a 'botched civilization' and 'an old bitch gone in the teeth,' while also stating 'the age demanded an image of its accelerated grimace.' It contains named sections including 'Yeux Glauques,' 'Brennbaum,' and 'Mr. Nixon,' in which Mr. Nixon tells the speaker to 'give up verse, my boy.' The poem ends with a section called 'Medallion' and includes an 'Envoi' that mimics Edmund Waller's 'Go Lovely Rose' by addressing a 'dumb-born book.' In the third section, the speaker says 'We see toh kalon / decreed in the marketplace.'

Cathay

Cathay is Pound's collection of loosely translated Chinese poems, primarily from the work of Li Bai (Li Po). The most frequently referenced poem from the collection is 'The River Merchant's Wife: A Letter,' in which the speaker promises 'And I will come out to meet you / As far as Cho-Fu-Sa' and recalls when 'my hair was still cut straight across my forehead.' The collection also includes 'Jewel Stairs' Grievance.' Pound's translations were based on the notes of Ernest Fenollosa rather than direct knowledge of Chinese.

Imagism

Pound was the central founding figure of Imagism, the early 20th-century poetry movement advocating for 'direct treatment of the thing' and 'no superfluous words.' He published the movement's key early manifesto 'A Few Don'ts by an Imagiste' and the essay 'A Retrospect,' which codified three principles of Imagism that he developed with H.D. (Hilda Doolittle). Pound suggested H.D. publish under her initials, and other members of the movement included Amy Lowell. The Deep Image movement is described as a spin-off of Imagism. Pound's study of Chinese characters influenced his development of Imagist principles.

Vorticism and BLAST →

Pound co-founded the Vorticist art and literary movement with Wyndham Lewis, and coined the term 'Vorticism.' The movement's manifesto was published in the magazine BLAST, which listed things to be 'blasted' and 'blessed.' Pound defined the vortex as 'the point of maximum energy.' In a 1914 article about Vorticism, he discussed the inspiration for 'In a Station of the Metro' and called Picasso and Kandinsky the 'father and mother' of the Vorticist movement. His wife Dorothy Shakespear published paintings in BLAST. Pound also coined the term for the movement in the short-lived magazine.

Literary Criticism

Pound's critical writings are frequently clued. His ABC of Reading defined great literature as 'language charged with meaning to the utmost possible degree' and famously defined literature as 'news that STAYS news.' It opens with the 'parable of the sunfish,' in which Louis Agassiz repeatedly forces a student to describe a sunfish. The essay 'I Gather the Limbs of Osiris' propounded his theory of the 'luminous detail' and compared the poet's task to that of the goddess Isis. 'A Few Don'ts by an Imagiste' warns 'don't chop your stuff into separate iambs' and 'don't be viewy.' The Spirit of Romance discusses troubadour poetry. His book How to Read was later distilled into the ABC of Reading. Pound also described his theory of each soul having an element called 'virtu.'

Other Works and Collaborations

Several minor works and collaborations appear in clues. 'Homage to Sextus Propertius' is a loose translation from the Latin elegist. 'Ancient Music' parodies the medieval song 'Sumer is icumen in' with 'Winter is icumen in, Lhude sing Goddamm.' The poem 'The Return' describes 'pallid' and 'half-awakened' gods. 'Sestina: Altaforte' exclaims 'You whoreson dog, Papiols, come!' Pound's only opera, Le Testament de Villon, contains the aria 'Dictes Moy.' His Treatise on Harmony championed George Antheil's music and introduced theories about 'Absolute Rhythm.' Pound wrote music reviews under the pseudonym 'William Atheling.' With his lover, the violinist Olga Rudge, he helped repopularize Vivaldi's music. 'Ballad of the Goodly Fere' includes Simon Zealot claiming 'If they think they ha' slain our Goodly Fere they are fools eternally.' He also translated 'The Seafarer' from Old English.

General / Biographical

Pound's biography is heavily clued, especially his fascism and treason. He broadcast fascist propaganda from Italy during World War II, was captured and detained in a cage near Pisa, and was subsequently charged with treason. He pleaded insanity and was confined to St. Elizabeths Hospital for over a decade, where he was visited by Elizabeth Bishop (who wrote 'Visits to St. Elizabeths'), Charles Olson, and John Berryman. T.S. Eliot dedicated The Waste Land to Pound as 'il miglior fabbro' ('the better craftsman'), a phrase Dante originally applied to troubadour Arnaut Daniel. Pound significantly edited The Waste Land, convincing Eliot to cut sections from the poem originally titled 'He Do the Police in Different Voices.' He organized the 'Bel Esprit' crowdfunding initiative to support Eliot. Pound and Eliot gave each other animal nicknames from Uncle Remus stories (Rabbit and Possum). Bob Dylan's 'Desolation Row' has calypso singers laughing at Pound and Eliot fighting in the captain's tower. Pound was a lover of H.D. and was married to Dorothy Shakespear. Robert Lowell wrote a sonnet about Pound in which Pound looks at his 'blotched, bent hands' and refers to Olga Rudge. Daniel Bell led opposition to awarding Pound the Emerson-Thoreau Medal. A Gonzalo Rojas poem calls Pound an 'incredible copier.'

Summary of Facts

Ezra Pound is a staple of quizbowl Literature, appearing across all difficulty levels from novice through Chicago Open. The Cantos dominates as his most clued work, with the Pisan section (written in an exposed cage near Pisa) and the usura Canto XLV being the most recognizable elements. Nearly every tossup ends with some combination of The Cantos, 'In a Station of the Metro,' and 'Hugh Selwyn Mauberley' as giveaway clues. 'In a Station of the Metro' is the single most recognizable individual poem, with its full text ('The apparition of these faces in the crowd / Petals on a wet, black bough') serving as a near-universal giveaway. 'Hugh Selwyn Mauberley' is identified by its title character who 'strove to resuscitate the dead art of poetry' and whose 'true Penelope was Flaubert,' along with named sections like 'Yeux Glauques' and 'Mr. Nixon.'

Pound's role as founder of Imagism and co-founder of Vorticism (with Wyndham Lewis) provides substantial power-level cluing material. His literary criticism, particularly the ABC of Reading (literature as 'news that STAYS news,' the sunfish parable) and 'I Gather the Limbs of Osiris,' appears at higher difficulties. Cathay and its Li Bai translations surface regularly. His biography is inseparable from his poetry in quizbowl: his fascist propaganda broadcasts, treason charges, detention in Pisa, and confinement at St. Elizabeths Hospital all appear frequently. His relationship with T.S. Eliot (editing The Waste Land, the 'il miglior fabbro' dedication, Bel Esprit) is another major clue axis. At the highest difficulties, clues reference his opera Le Testament de Villon, his music criticism as 'William Atheling,' Olga Rudge, Dorothy Shakespear, the Robert Lowell sonnet, and the Daniel Bell controversy over the Emerson-Thoreau Medal.
