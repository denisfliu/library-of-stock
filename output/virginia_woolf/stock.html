British modernist novelist, essayist, and publisher. Central figure of the Bloomsbury Group. Best known for Mrs Dalloway, To the Lighthouse, Orlando, A Room of One's Own, and The Waves. Co-founded the Hogarth Press with husband Leonard Woolf. Committed suicide in 1941 by filling her pockets with stones and walking into the River Ouse. Her name partly titles Edward Albee's play Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?
Mrs Dalloway

Woolf's most frequently clued novel. Opens with the title character's decision to 'buy the flowers herself' for a party. Clarissa Dalloway reminisces about her youth at Bourton, where she kissed Sally Seton ('the most exquisite moment of her whole life'). She reflects on her old crush Peter Walsh and dislikes Miss Kilman, who teaches her daughter Elizabeth. She feels 'far out to sea and alone' while watching taxi cabs. A beggar woman sings 'ee um fah um so / foo swee too eem oo' in Regent's Park. A crowd sees a plane spelling 'toffee.' The shell-shocked Septimus Warren Smith commits suicide by jumping out of a window, and Clarissa hears the 'leaden circles dissolve in the air' at her party. Michael Cunningham's novel The Hours was inspired by the novel. The character originally appeared in The Voyage Out and a story 'in Bond Street.' Woolf recalled reading Ulysses with 'spasms of wonder' and drew on its 'Wandering Rocks' section. Septimus hears sparrow song as 'piercing Greek words.'

To the Lighthouse

A novel about the Ramsay family at their summer home, framed by a visit to the Isle of Skye. The first section 'The Window' climaxes with a dinner centered on the dish Boeuf en Daube, declared 'a triumph' by William Bankes. Paul searches for Minta's lost brooch on the beach. Minta Doyle is late to dinner after losing her grandmother's brooch. The middle section 'Time Passes' uses brackets to set off terse plot points (including that Prue died in childbirth) from long imagistic passages. Mrs. Ramsay's son James cuts shapes from a magazine while hating his tyrannical father (based on Woolf's father, the academic Leslie Stephen). Mr. Ramsay is a philosopher who laments reaching Q but never R. The novel ends as the painter Lily Briscoe finishes her painting and the family sails to the title location. Woolf called it an 'elegy' to exorcise memories of her parents.

Orlando

A novel inspired by Woolf's affair with Vita Sackville-West, called 'the longest and most charming love letter in literature.' The title character is an Elizabethan noble who lives for over 300 years and changes gender from male to female. Orlando first appears as a boy of sixteen. He/she writes the poem 'The Oak Tree' over several centuries (the only surviving work after burning 57 manuscripts following a satirical review by Nick/Nicholas Greene). Orlando is gifted an emerald frog by Archduke Harry, shouts 'It's the goose!' at the end, and sees a frost fair with Sasha. Orlando's lover Vita Sackville-West wrote about Knole, the mansion with 365 rooms and 52 staircases. Sally Potter's film cast Tilda Swinton; the cancelled 2020 Met Gala was themed around it.

A Room of One's Own

Woolf's famous feminist essay arguing that 'a woman must have money and a room of her own if she is to write fiction.' It invents Judith Shakespeare, William's equally talented sister who runs away from marriage, becomes pregnant by the actor-manager Nick Greene, and commits suicide. Woolf examines the sentence 'Chloe liked Olivia' from the fictional novel Life's Adventure by Mary Carmichael. She contrasts a sumptuous lunch (sole in cream sauce) at a men's college with a dull dinner ('plain gravy soup') at a women's college, concluding 'one cannot think well, love well, sleep well, if one has not dined well.' She says 'Anon, who wrote so many poems without signing them, was often a woman' and calls Frances Burney the 'mother of English fiction.' She uses Mary Beton, Mary Seton, Mary Carmichael as placeholder names. Adapted from lectures given at women's colleges.

The Waves

A novel Woolf described as a 'play-poem,' consisting of nine soliloquies delivered by six friends: Bernard, Rhoda, Neville, and three others. They mourn the death of Percival, who dies in a riding accident in India after a dinner party. Bernard observes a 'seven-sided' flower at the farewell dinner. Bernard's final monologue declares 'Against you I will fling myself, unvanquished and unyielding, O Death!' The penultimate section is set in a restaurant at Hampton Court Palace, where the middle-aged characters walk along an avenue of trees.

Between the Acts

Woolf's last novel, published posthumously after her 1941 suicide. Miss La Trobe directs a 'pageant of England' (history pageant) in the garden of Bartholomew Oliver. The pageant includes a mock Restoration comedy (Lady Harpy Harraden loves Sir Spaniel Lilyliver), a Victorian scene, and ends with actors holding mirrors up to the audience while a gramophone repeats 'dispersed are we.' Mr. Streatfield, the rector, misinterprets the play, 'excruciating' Miss La Trobe. The Oliver family's heirlooms include a watch that stopped a bullet at Waterloo.

Short Stories

Woolf's short fiction includes 'Kew Gardens' (alternates between four couples' comments and a snail's progress through a flowerbed), 'The Mark on the Wall' (narrator muses whether a mark is a nail or rose petal; interrupted by 'I'm going out to buy a newspaper'; the mark turns out to be a snail), 'A Haunted House' ('Safe, safe, safe'; ghostly couple walks around), 'The Legacy' (Gilbert Clandon reads wife Angela's diary, discovering her affair and suicide), and 'Street Haunting' (a flaneur's quest to buy a pencil; a dwarf lady shows her perfectly proportioned feet). The only collection published during her lifetime was Monday or Tuesday. 'The Mark on the Wall' was published in Two Stories alongside Leonard's 'Three Jews,' the first Hogarth Press publication.

Essays (Modern Fiction, Mr. Bennett and Mrs. Brown, Death of the Moth, On Not Knowing Greek)

Woolf's critical essays are frequently clued. 'Modern Fiction' attacks H.G. Wells, John Galsworthy, and Arnold Bennett ('materialists'), praising Chekhov's 'Gusev'; declares 'life is a luminous halo' not 'a series of gig-lamps.' 'Mr. Bennett and Mrs. Brown' says human character changed 'on or about December 1910' and attacks Bennett's characterization. 'Death of the Moth' describes an insect as 'a tiny bead of pure life.' She divided novelists into 'preachers and teachers' (Tolstoy, Dickens) and 'pure artists' (Austen, Turgenev), with E.M. Forster straddling both. 'On Not Knowing Greek' discusses Sophocles' Electra. She also wrote 'The Cinema' (1926) about The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari and a biography of Roger Fry.

General / Biographical

Virginia Woolf (born Adeline Virginia Stephen) was a central figure of the Bloomsbury Group. She co-founded the Hogarth Press with husband Leonard Woolf (who wrote The Village in the Jungle and 'Three Jews'). She committed suicide in 1941 by filling her pockets with stones and walking into the River Ouse, which is the subject of The Hours's prologue. Before her death she wrote 'I don't think two people could have been happier than we have been.' She was the sister of painter Vanessa Bell. Her niece Angelica was unaware Duncan Grant was her biological father. In 1910, she participated in the Dreadnought hoax (dressing as Abyssinian ambassadors). She ran a house magazine called Hyde Park Gate News as a child. Her father was the academic Leslie Stephen. Her memoir Moments of Being includes the essay 'Am I a Snob?' She also wrote a biography of Roger Fry. Edward Albee's play Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf? uses her name in a nursery-rhyme substitution.

Summary of Facts

Virginia Woolf (born Adeline Virginia Stephen, 1882-1941) was a British modernist novelist, essayist, and publisher who is one of the most heavily clued literary figures in quizbowl. She was a central member of the Bloomsbury Group and co-founded the Hogarth Press with her husband Leonard Woolf.

Her most frequently clued novel is Mrs Dalloway, which opens with Clarissa's decision to 'buy the flowers herself.' Clarissa remembers kissing Sally Seton at Bourton ('the most exquisite moment of her whole life'), ruminates about Peter Walsh, and learns of the shell-shocked Septimus Smith's suicide at her party, hearing 'leaden circles dissolve in the air.' The novel inspired Michael Cunningham's The Hours.

To the Lighthouse follows the Ramsay family at their summer home on the Isle of Skye. The first section 'The Window' culminates in a dinner of Boeuf en Daube. The middle section 'Time Passes' reveals in brackets that Prue died in childbirth. The painter Lily Briscoe finishes her painting at the end. Mr. Ramsay is a tyrannical philosopher (based on Woolf's father Leslie Stephen) who laments reaching Q but never R.

Orlando, inspired by her affair with Vita Sackville-West, follows a gender-shifting Elizabethan noble who lives 300+ years and writes the poem 'The Oak Tree.' The Waves, which Woolf called a 'play-poem,' consists of nine soliloquies by six friends mourning Percival, who dies in India. Between the Acts is her final novel, featuring Miss La Trobe's history pageant with actors holding mirrors to the audience.

A Room of One's Own argues women need money and space to write, inventing Judith Shakespeare as an example. 'Modern Fiction' attacks Bennett, Wells, and Galsworthy while declaring 'life is a luminous halo.' 'Mr. Bennett and Mrs. Brown' claims human character changed 'on or about December 1910.'

Woolf's short stories include 'Kew Gardens' (snail in a flowerbed), 'The Mark on the Wall' (mark is a snail), 'A Haunted House,' and 'Street Haunting.' She committed suicide in 1941 by filling her pockets with stones and walking into the River Ouse. Her name partly titles Edward Albee's play Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?, in which George and Martha's dysfunctional marriage is staged with games like 'Get the Guests.'
