John Locke

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
English Enlightenment philosopher, often called the 'father of liberalism.' Author of An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (tabula rasa, primary and secondary qualities, personal identity) and Two Treatises of Government (labor theory of property, refutation of Filmer's Patriarcha, natural rights of life, liberty, and estate). Also wrote Some Thoughts Concerning Education and A Letter Concerning Toleration. Secretary to Anthony Ashley Cooper, Earl of Shaftesbury.
An Essay Concerning Human Understanding

Locke's major epistemological work argues against innate ideas in favor of the mind as a tabula rasa (blank slate) at birth. It distinguishes between simple ideas (from sensation) and complex ideas (formed by reflection), and between primary qualities (intrinsic to objects, like extension) and secondary qualities (subjective, like color and sound). The chapter 'Of Identity and Diversity' introduces the memory theory of personal identity with the prince-and-cobbler thought experiment. Thomas Reid's 'brave officer' objection exposes a transitivity problem. Other key features include the concept of 'sortals,' the 'Abuse of Words' chapter criticizing metaphysics, and Leibniz's chapter-by-chapter refutation in New Essays.

Two Treatises of Government

Locke's major political work. The First Treatise refutes Robert Filmer's Patriarcha and its doctrine of divine right of kings. The Second Treatise argues for natural rights of 'life, liberty, and estate,' the labor theory of property (mixing labor with nature creates ownership), the social contract, and the right to revolution when the state destroys the people's property. Key concepts include the 'Lockean proviso' (acquisition must leave 'enough and as good' for others, coined by Robert Nozick), 'express' vs. 'tacit' consent, and 'in the beginning, all the world was America.' C. B. Macpherson criticized Locke's 'possessive individualism.'

Other Works and General / Biographical

Locke's Some Thoughts Concerning Education builds on the tabula rasa idea and grew from letters advising Mary and Edward Clarke about their son. A Letter Concerning Toleration argues that religious tolerance is beneficial but excludes atheists. Locke was secretary to Anthony Ashley Cooper, Earl of Shaftesbury. He was attacked as Socinian for The Reasonableness of Christianity. Berkeley and Butler both critiqued his Essay. Peter Laslett's 1960 edition of Two Treatises redated the work.

Summary of Facts

John Locke (1632-1704) is one of the most frequently clued philosophers in quizbowl, with questions spanning epistemology, political philosophy, and personal identity. The two central giveaways are the tabula rasa and Two Treatises of Government.

An Essay Concerning Human Understanding argues against innate ideas in favor of the mind as a blank slate at birth. It distinguishes simple ideas (from sensation) from complex ideas (from reflection) and primary qualities (intrinsic) from secondary qualities (subjective). The prince-and-cobbler thought experiment illustrates the memory theory of personal identity, which Thomas Reid attacked with the 'brave officer' who forgets his flogging. The 'Abuse of Words' chapter criticizes metaphysics. Locke coined the term 'sortal,' defined persons as 'thinking intelligent beings' (a 'forensic' term), and used the Day-Man/Night-Man experiment. Leibniz refuted the Essay chapter-by-chapter in New Essays; Catharine Trotter Cockburn wrote a Defence.

Two Treatises of Government refutes Filmer's Patriarcha (First Treatise) and argues for natural rights of life, liberty, and property through the labor theory (Second Treatise). The 'Lockean proviso' requires acquisition leave 'enough and as good' for others. Nozick satirized the labor theory with tomato juice poured into the ocean. Macpherson attacked Locke's 'possessive individualism.' The work distinguishes 'express' and 'tacit' consent and declares 'in the beginning, all the world was America.'

Other works include Some Thoughts Concerning Education (builds on tabula rasa, from letters to the Clarke family) and A Letter Concerning Toleration (tolerates religious minorities but not atheists). Locke was secretary to Shaftesbury and was attacked as Socinian for The Reasonableness of Christianity.
