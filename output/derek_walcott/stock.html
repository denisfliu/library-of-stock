Saint Lucian poet and playwright who won the Nobel Prize in Literature. Best known for the epic poem Omeros (an Iliad-inspired retelling featuring fishermen Achille and Hector fighting over Helen on St. Lucia) and the poem 'A Far Cry from Africa' (ending 'How can I turn from Africa and live?'). Also wrote 'Sea Grapes' ('The classics can console. But not enough'), 'Ruins of a Great House', the play Dream on Monkey Mountain (about the charcoal-burner Makak), and Ti-Jean and His Brothers. Had a public feud with V.S. Naipaul. Collaborated with Paul Simon on the musical The Capeman.
Omeros

An Iliad- and Odyssey-inspired epic poem set on St. Lucia, featuring the fishermen Achille and Hector who fight over Helen. The blind poet Seven Seas mentors the narrator and frequents Ma Kilman's No Pain Cafe. Major Plunkett tries to write a history of the 'Battle of the Saints' and his wife is Maud. Philoctete has a wound that Ma Kilman heals. The poem uses a three-line form reminiscent of Dante's terza rima. Achille paints 'In God we Troust' on his boat and sails to Africa. The narrator mockingly declares 'sic transit taxi, sport.' Helen sings the Beatles' 'Yesterday' while strolling on the beach. Seven Seas mentions being a member of Ghost Dance and traveling the Trail of Tears. The poem divides the narrative between the characters and the author's own voice so it has no single protagonist. A Far Cry from Africa

One of Walcott's most frequently clued short poems. Opens with 'a wind ruffling the tawny pelt' of Africa. Contains the famous line 'the gorilla wrestles with the superman.' The speaker is 'divided to the vein' and 'poisoned with the blood of both.' References 'a white child hacked in bed' contrasted with 'savages, expendable as Jews.' Mentions 'Kikuyu, quick as flies.' Ends with 'How can I face such slaughter and be cool? / How can I turn from Africa and live?' References the Mau Mau Uprising. States that 'upright man seeks his divinity by inflicting pain.' Dream on Monkey Mountain

A play about the charcoal-burner Makak (Felix Hobain), who is imprisoned by Corporal Lestrade with the criminals Tigre and Souris. In his dream/vision, a white goddess urges him to return to Africa. He beheads the goddess and awakens. Moustique is beaten to death by a mob for pretending to be a healer. Makak heals a man's snakebite by pressing a coal into his palm (Josephus). Florence Nightingale, Al Jolson, and Horatio Nelson are sentenced to hang for being white. A policeman gets a vendor to agree his melon is actually a pawpaw. Opens with an epigraph from Sartre's prologue to The Wretched of the Earth. Won an Obie Award. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Walcott was from Saint Lucia and won the Nobel Prize in Literature. His twin brother was the playwright Roderick Walcott. He had a famous feud with V.S. Naipaul. He collaborated with Paul Simon on the musical The Capeman. Ruth Padel resigned as Oxford Professor of Poetry after alerting journalists to harassment charges against Walcott. His Nobel lecture was subtitled 'Fragments of Epic Memory.' He co-founded the Trinidad Theatre Workshop with his brother Roderick. He also wrote the play Beef, No Chicken and The Sea at Dauphin.

Summary of Facts

Derek Walcott is a Saint Lucian poet and playwright who won the Nobel Prize in Literature. He is by far most frequently clued for Omeros, an Iliad- and Odyssey-inspired epic poem set on St. Lucia featuring the fishermen Achille and Hector fighting over Helen. Nearly every tossup ends with Omeros and St. Lucia as the giveaway. Key characters in Omeros include the blind poet Seven Seas, who frequents Ma Kilman's No Pain Cafe; Major Plunkett, who tries to write a history of the island and whose wife Maud dies; and Philoctete, whose wound is healed by Ma Kilman. Achille paints 'In God we Troust' on his boat. The poem uses terza rima-like three-line stanzas and has no single protagonist.

Walcott's short poems are equally central to his quizbowl profile. 'A Far Cry from Africa' is his most clued short poem, ending with 'How can I face such slaughter and be cool? / How can I turn from Africa and live?' The poem contains 'the gorilla wrestles with the superman,' 'divided to the vein,' 'Kikuyu, quick as flies,' and the tension between Africa and 'the English tongue I love.' 'Sea Grapes' is almost as frequently clued, always identified by its ending: 'The classics can console. But not enough.' It describes 'the ancient war between obsession and responsibility.' 'Ruins of a Great House' depicts 'disjecta membra' and 'the leprosy of empire,' with 'a smell of dead limes' and references to 'Hawkins, Walter Raleigh, Drake, ancestral murderers and poets.'

Dream on Monkey Mountain is his most important play, about the charcoal-burner Makak (Felix Hobain), imprisoned by Corporal Lestrade with the criminals Tigre and Souris. A white goddess urges Makak to return to Africa; he beheads her before waking. Moustique is beaten to death for pretending to be a healer. Ti-Jean and His Brothers features the ghost of an aborted fetus Bolom challenging three brothers.

Other notable poems include 'Love After Love' ('you will love again the stranger who was your self'), 'The Swamp' (which provided the title for Ngugi wa Thiong'o's Petals of Blood), 'The Schooner Flight' (Shabine's 'either I'm nobody, or I'm a nation'), 'The Star-Apple Kingdom,' and 'The Mongoose' (mocking V.S. Naipaul). Walcott's collection Tiepolo's Hound includes his own watercolors and a narrative about Camille Pissarro. His autobiographical long poem Another Life begins with 'verandahs, where the pages of the sea / are a book left open by an absent master.'

Biographically, Walcott had a famous feud with V.S. Naipaul, the only other Caribbean Nobel laureate. He collaborated with Paul Simon on the musical The Capeman. His twin brother Roderick was also a playwright. Ruth Padel resigned as Oxford Professor of Poetry for leaking harassment charges against him.
