An argument for the existence of God first presented by Saint Anselm in the Proslogion, which argues that a 'being than which nothing greater can be conceived' must exist because existence is more perfect than non-existence. Has been reformulated by Descartes, Plantinga (modal version), and others, and criticized by Gaunilo (Lost Island), Kant (existence is not a predicate), and others.
Anselm's Original Formulation (Proslogion)

The ontological argument first appeared in Saint Anselm's Proslogion, imagining a 'supremely perfect' or 'maximally perfect' entity — a 'being than which nothing greater can be conceived.' The argument holds that such a being would not be the greatest if it existed only in the mind, so it must also exist in reality. It relies on the premise that existence in reality is greater than existence in the mind alone. The Proslogion uses the Fool from the Psalms in a reductio ad absurdum. Norman Malcolm noted that Anselm actually presented two versions of the argument, the first being susceptible to Kant's reply about existence.

Gaunilo's Lost Island Refutation

The first published refutation of the ontological argument came from Gaunilo of Marmoutiers in the text In Behalf of the Fool (In Defense of the Fool). He used a reductio ad absurdum showing that the same logic would entail the existence of a 'lost island' — the greatest conceivable island must exist by the same reasoning. This demonstrates the argument proves too much.

Kant's Criticism (Existence Is Not a Predicate)

Immanuel Kant criticized the ontological argument by arguing that existence is not a real predicate. Therefore, a 'supremely perfect being' need not necessarily exist just because we can conceive of it. Kant pointed out that the argument's flaw lies in assuming perfection is predicated by existence. Frege also argued that existence is only a property of the concept of God, not part of God's meaning.

Plantinga's Modal Ontological Argument

Alvin Plantinga created a 'victorious' modal version of the ontological argument in his book The Nature of Necessity. It uses modal logic (axiom S5: anything possibly necessary is necessary) to argue that considering the argument in one possible world automatically extends it to every possible world. Plantinga builds on Norman Malcolm's version and defines 'maximum excellence.' Charles Hartshorne also updated the argument in The Logic of Perfection using dipolar attributes and a 'cumulative case' strategy.

Summary of Facts

The ontological argument is an argument for the existence of God first presented by Saint Anselm in his Proslogion. It imagines a 'being than which nothing greater can be conceived' and argues that such a being must exist in reality, because existence in reality is greater than existence in the mind alone. The argument uses the Fool from the Psalms in a reductio ad absurdum. It is named for ontology, the philosophical study of being.

The first published refutation came from Gaunilo of Marmoutiers in In Behalf of the Fool (also called In Defense of the Fool). Gaunilo used a 'Lost Island' rebuttal: by the same reasoning, the greatest conceivable island must also exist, showing the argument proves too much.

Immanuel Kant delivered the most famous criticism by arguing that existence is not a real predicate. A 'supremely perfect being' need not necessarily exist just because perfection is predicated of it. Norman Malcolm noted that Anselm actually presented two versions of the argument, with the first being susceptible to Kant's reply.

Descartes extended the argument in the fifth section of Meditations on First Philosophy. Leibniz defined perfection as 'a simple quality which is positive and absolute' in his own version. Duns Scotus's arguments for the univocity of being were based on Anselm. Mulla Sadra from the school of Isfahan also produced a version, collected in The Four Journeys.

Alvin Plantinga created the most discussed modern version: a 'victorious' modal ontological argument in The Nature of Necessity. Using modal logic (axiom S5, where anything possibly necessary is necessary), Plantinga showed that considering the argument in one possible world automatically extends it to every possible world. He builds on Norman Malcolm's version and defines 'maximum excellence.' Charles Hartshorne also updated the argument in The Logic of Perfection using dipolar attributes and a 'cumulative case' strategy.

Other notable contributions include Robert Maydole's MPA and 'temporal-contingency' arguments, Graham Oppy's text dividing the argument into six subtypes, and Matthews and Baker's 2010 simplification using 'unmediated causal powers.' Bertrand Russell famously said he was certain the argument was wrong but could not find a satisfying reason why. Michael Dummett concluded it was invalid in 'Metaphysical Necessity.'
