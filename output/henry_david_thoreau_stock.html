Summary of Facts

Henry David Thoreau is an American Transcendentalist writer best known for Walden, an account of two years, two months, and two days spent living in a cabin near Walden Pond, built on Ralph Waldo Emerson's land. The book's author "wished to live deliberately, to front only the essential facts of life." Its section titled "Economy" provides an account of the cost of building the cabin, and "Brute Neighbors" contains the famous "Battle of the Ants" between "red imperialists" and "black republicans." The book was inspired by Emerson's call for "solitude" in his essay "Nature." Annie Dillard's Pilgrim at Tinker Creek is often thematically compared to Walden, and Dillard's MA thesis was on the book.

"Civil Disobedience" (originally "Resistance to Civil Government") describes Thoreau's night in jail for refusing to pay the poll-tax, cut short by an anonymous relative's intervention. The essay opens by accepting the motto "the best government is that which governs least" (borrowed from O'Sullivan's Democratic Review). The experience makes him realize his neighbors' "friendship was for summer weather only" and leads him to call the State "timid as a lone woman with her silver spoons." The essay harangues "merchants and farmers" about slavery and Mexico. It was fictionalized by Robert E. Lee and Jerome Lawrence in The Night Thoreau Spent in Jail.

Thoreau was profoundly affected by the death of his brother John from tetanus and recalled their boat trip in A Week on the Concord and Merrimack Rivers. He spent three years as a tutor to Emerson's children and met Emerson at Harvard in 1837. Emerson's eulogy lamented that "instead of engineering for his entire country, he was the captain of a huckleberry-party." Thoreau defended John Brown, writing "A Plea for Captain John Brown" and describing Brown's career as "meteor-like." Robert Richardson wrote biographies of both Thoreau (subtitled "A Life of the Mind") and Emerson.
