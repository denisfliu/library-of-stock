Summary of Facts

Umberto Menotti Maria Giordano (1867–1948) was an Italian opera composer and one of the principal figures of the verismo movement in opera, alongside Pietro Mascagni (Cavalleria Rusticana) and Ruggero Leoncavallo (I Pagliacci). His operas focus on realistic, emotionally intense subjects and were staples of the late 19th- and early 20th-century Italian operatic repertoire.

Giordano's most celebrated work is Andrea Chénier (1896), with a libretto by Luigi Illica (who also wrote Puccini's La Bohème, Tosca, and Madama Butterfly in collaboration with Giacosa). The opera is set during the French Revolution and Reign of Terror. The title character is a real historical French poet who is eventually executed by guillotine alongside his lover Maddalena de Coigny. The opera opens with a scene in which a servant's son (Gerard) watches his elderly father struggle to move furniture and adjusts cushions on a blue sofa — described as an apostrophe to the sofa. In Act II, the spy l'Incrédibile sees through the maid Bersi's disguise at the Café Hottot while following the title poet. A Cyrano-esque scene has Maddalena admit to sending anonymous love letters to the title character; they pledge their love in 'Ora soave.' The baritone Gerard, a jealous revolutionary, makes accusations against the title character but eventually retracts them; the Revolutionary Tribunal nonetheless sentences Andrea to death. The most famous aria is 'La mamma morta,' a soprano aria sung by Maddalena to Gerard, in which she recalls how Love encouraged her to keep living after her home was burned down and her mother killed — she then offers her body ('A dying body is my body / So take it. / I am already dead matter') to save her poet lover. The Maria Callas recording of this aria was featured in the 1993 film Philadelphia. The title character's aria 'Un dì all'azzurro spazio' (nicknamed 'L'Improvviso') has him describe the beauty of nature against the suffering of the poor; it is called the 'Improvviso' because it follows an argument that true poetry cannot be produced on command. Gerard's baritone aria 'Nemico della Patria' has him question his own motives. Notably, celebrity sopranos (Elena Obraztsova, Rosalind Plowright, Fedora Barbieri) have made cameo appearances in productions playing the small role of old blind Madelon.

Giordano's second most-clued opera is Fedora (1898), based on a play by Victorien Sardou (who refused Giordano twice before granting permission). The opera features the Russian princess Fedora accusing the nihilist Loris Ipanov of murdering her fiancé Vladimir. Enrico Caruso achieved fame through his performance in the premiere as Loris, who sings the aria 'Amor ti vieta.' Giordano also composed Madame Sans-Gêne (1915), in which the title character reminds Napoleon Bonaparte that he owes her sixty francs for laundry.
