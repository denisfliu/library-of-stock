British author and caricaturist (1872-1956), best known for his satirical novel Zuleika Dobson, his short story collection Seven Men (which includes 'Enoch Soames' and 'Savonarola Brown'), his book of parodies A Christmas Garland, and his caricature collection Rossetti and His Circle. He was the half-brother of the actor Herbert Beerbohm Tree.

General / Biographical

Max Beerbohm (Sir Henry Maximilian Beerbohm, 1872-1956) was a British author, critic, and caricaturist. He was the half-brother of the actor and theatrical manager Herbert Beerbohm Tree (born Herbert Draper Beerbohm), who managed the Haymarket Theatre and built Her Majesty's Theatre, where he staged lavishly decorated Shakespeare productions; Herbert Tree premiered the role of Henry Higgins in Shaw's Pygmalion and the role of Lord Illingworth in Wilde's A Woman of No Importance. Max claimed Herbert chose his adopted surname (Beerbohm) because it was easy for fans to shout. Beerbohm established his reputation as a caricaturist with the book The Poets' Corner, which depicted 'twenty-five gentlemen' including Aubrey Beardsley. He also wrote an essay on Lytton Strachey. He chose to adopt the surname 'Beerbohm' — the same name famously associated with his half-brother Herbert Beerbohm Tree.

Summary of Facts

Max Beerbohm (Sir Henry Maximilian Beerbohm) was a British author and caricaturist whose work spans satirical fiction, parody, and visual art. In quizbowl, he is most strongly identified by two works: his short story collection Seven Men (also published as Seven Men and Two Others) and his satirical novel Zuleika Dobson.

The collection Seven Men is Beerbohm's most frequently clued prose work, particularly through its story 'Enoch Soames.' The title character of that story is a self-described 'Catholic Diabolist' who wrote the poorly-received poetry collection Fungoids and the volume Negations. Soames is memorably described in a future encyclopedia entry as 'a thurd-rait poit hoo beleevz imself a grate jeneus' — rendered in phonetic spelling. He makes a deal with a Mephistophelean devil to travel 100 years into the future, arriving at the Reading Room of the British Museum on June 3, 1997, only to discover that he is remembered only as the fictional protagonist ('immajnari karrakter in Inglish Littracher') of a Max Beerbohm short story. In the story, Soames dismisses Percy Bysshe Shelley as 'second-rate' and accuses him of 'a deadly evenness.' Seven Men also contains 'Savonarola Brown,' in which the narrator tries to complete a bizarre verse drama by the title playwright, with the Guelfs and Ghibellines fighting and Michelangelo, Benvenuto Cellini, and Pippa all appearing. The collection further includes 'A.V. Laider,' whose title palmist foresees his own injury and the deaths of his companions in a train accident, but refuses to act to prevent it; the narrator of that story imagines a conversation between a very young envelope and a very old envelope. 'Hilary Maltby and Stephen Braxton' is another story in Seven Men, featuring a character startled by a doppelganger of his rival who causes him to cut himself shaving and spill borscht at the Duchess of Hertfordshire's estate at Keeb.

Zuleika Dobson is Beerbohm's most famous long work, a satirical novel set at Oxford University and subtitled 'An Oxford Love Story.' The title character is a femme fatale and bumbling magician whose arrival at Oxford causes busts of twelve Roman emperors to sweat 'great beads of perspiration.' The Duke of Dorset is the first undergraduate to fall in love with Zuleika and drown himself in the River Isis, his doom foretold by two black owls perching on the battlements of Tankerton Hall. All the other undergraduates follow suit in mass suicide. Peripheral details include the Junta dining club (which proposes toasts to Nellie O'Mora), Noaks (who falls in love without ever meeting Zuleika), and one character discovering his studs have turned the colors of Zuleika's earrings.

Beyond fiction, Beerbohm was a celebrated caricaturist. His collection Rossetti and His Circle comprises 23 caricatures lampooning Dante Gabriel Rossetti and the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. His book The Poets' Corner, depicting 'twenty-five gentlemen' including Aubrey Beardsley, established his reputation as a caricaturist. He also wrote the parody anthology A Christmas Garland, which parodied 17 or more contemporary authors, including a mock Rudyard Kipling poem about Santa Claus being arrested; he also scrawled a private parody of A.E. Housman's A Shropshire Lad in his personal copy, beginning 'and now, lad, all is over, / 'twixt you, your love, and the clover.' His story 'The Happy Hypocrite,' published in The Yellow Book, features Lord George Hell, who is rejected by Jenny Mere for not having 'the faint of a saint' and changes his surname from Hell to Heaven.

Biographically, Max Beerbohm was the half-brother of the actor and theater manager Herbert Beerbohm Tree (born Herbert Draper Beerbohm), who managed the Haymarket Theatre, built Her Majesty's Theatre, and staged lavishly decorated Shakespeare productions. Herbert Tree premiered the roles of Lord Illingworth in Oscar Wilde's A Woman of No Importance and Henry Higgins in George Bernard Shaw's Pygmalion (opposite Mrs. Patrick Campbell as Eliza Doolittle); Shaw was so angered by Tree's liberties with the ending that he appended the essay 'What Happened Afterwards' to printed editions. Max Beerbohm also wrote an essay on Lytton Strachey, author of Eminent Victorians.
