An American philosophical tradition founded by C.S. Peirce and William James. Emphasizes practical consequences of ideas as the measure of their truth. Key works include James's Pragmatism: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking. Later figures include John Dewey and Richard Rorty (neopragmatism).
Pragmatism: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking (William James)

William James's 1907 lecture series presenting pragmatism as a philosophical method. The book distinguishes between 'tender-minded' rationalists and 'tough-minded' empiricists, introduces the idea that truth has 'cash value in experiential terms,' and uses the famous squirrel-around-a-tree anecdote to show that the pragmatic method resolves metaphysical disputes by asking what 'practically means.' It also discusses Walt Whitman's 'To You' in monistic vs. pluralistic readings, offers 'melioristic theism' as a middle ground, and examines eight ways the world could be 'unified.'

C.S. Peirce & the Founding of Pragmatism

Charles Sanders Peirce is the 'father of pragmatism' who wrote the foundational essays 'How to Make Our Ideas Clear' and 'The Fixation of Belief' for Popular Science Monthly. He formulated the pragmatic maxim (consider the practical effects of your thinking), introduced the methods of tenacity and authority for settling opinion, advocated for the scientific method, and developed abductive reasoning and triadic semiotics. William James credited Peirce when first publishing the term 'pragmatism' in 1898.

Richard Rorty & Neopragmatism

Richard Rorty revived pragmatism in its 'neo' form, combining it with analytic philosophy's focus on language. His Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature (1979) promotes 'epistemological behaviorism' and attacks representational theories of knowledge. Contingency, Irony, and Solidarity defines 'ironists' who doubt their 'final vocabulary.' Achieving Our Country favorably contrasts the progressive Left with the 'spectatorial' academic Left. Susan Haack has argued Rorty did not truly belong to pragmatism.

Louis Menand & The Metaphysical Club

Louis Menand's book The Metaphysical Club traces the origins of pragmatism. The 'Metaphysical Club' was a discussion group at Harvard that included Peirce, James, and Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. Susan Haack and the Pittsburgh School (Robert Brandom, Wilfrid Sellars, John McDowell) are modern pragmatists.

Summary of Facts

Pragmatism is the dominant American philosophical tradition, founded by Charles Sanders Peirce and developed by William James and John Dewey. It holds that the meaning and truth of ideas should be judged by their practical consequences. Peirce formulated the pragmatic maxim in 'How to Make Our Ideas Clear' and advocated the scientific method over the methods of tenacity and authority in 'The Fixation of Belief.'

William James's 1907 lecture series Pragmatism: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking is the most heavily clued work on pragmatism. It distinguishes between 'tender-minded' rationalists and 'tough-minded' empiricists, introduces the idea that truth has 'cash value,' and uses the famous squirrel-around-a-tree anecdote to show that the pragmatic method resolves metaphysical disputes. The book also discusses carving constellations from the flux of reality, Walt Whitman's 'To You' in pluralistic and monistic readings, and proposes 'melioristic theism.'

Richard Rorty revived pragmatism in its 'neo' form, combining it with analytic philosophy in Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature. His Achieving Our Country praises the progressive Left and criticizes the spectatorial academic Left. Susan Haack has argued Rorty did not truly belong to pragmatism. The Pittsburgh School (Robert Brandom, Wilfrid Sellars, John McDowell) represents modern analytic pragmatism. Louis Menand traced the origins of pragmatism in The Metaphysical Club. Pragmatism also influenced thinkers abroad: Nishida and the Kyoto School adopted James's concept of 'pure experience,' and B.R. Ambedkar studied under Dewey at Columbia.
