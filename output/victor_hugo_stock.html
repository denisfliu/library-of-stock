Victor Hugo

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
French Romantic author and poet, one of the most significant literary figures of 19th-century France. Known for his monumental novels Les Miserables and The Hunchback of Notre Dame, as well as influential plays like Hernani and Cromwell (whose preface became a manifesto of Romanticism). Also a major poet whose collection Les Contemplations includes the famous elegy 'Tomorrow, at Dawn.' Spent 15 years exiled on the island of Guernsey for criticizing Napoleon III. Rejected the classical unities championed by Racine and the Academie Francaise. Coined the term 'Comprachico.' Also known for over 4,000 drawings.
Les Miserables

Hugo's massive historical novel following the ex-convict Jean Valjean, pursued by Inspector Javert across post-revolutionary France. Features Fantine, Cosette, Marius Pontmercy, the Thenardiers, the street urchin Gavroche, and the revolutionary group the Friends of the ABC led by Enjolras. Contains famous digressions on the Battle of Waterloo, the Paris sewers, and argot (street slang). Jean Valjean is given candlesticks by Bishop Myriel after stealing his silverware, later becomes a factory owner under the name Monsieur Madeleine, carries Marius through the sewers, and is pursued throughout by Javert, who eventually drowns himself in the Seine. The Hunchback of Notre Dame

Novel set in medieval Paris featuring the bell-ringer Quasimodo, the gypsy Esmeralda, and the villainous Archdeacon Claude Frollo. Esmeralda owns a trained goat named Djali and is loved by the poet Pierre Gringoire. She is framed for the attempted murder of Captain Phoebus (actually stabbed by Frollo) and hanged. Her death causes a character to exclaim 'Oh! All that I have ever loved!' Esmeralda is reunited with her mother (a cellar-dwelling hermitess) through a red baby shoe. Frollo predicts that 'print will kill' architecture. Hugo argues architecture enjoyed greater freedom of expression than literature. Wikipedia

Les Contemplations / 'Tomorrow, at Dawn'

Poetry collection divided into six books with sections titled 'Once' and 'Today.' The fourth book, Pauca Meae (titled from a phrase in Virgil's Eclogues), contains the famous poem 'Tomorrow, at Dawn' ('Demain, des l'aube'), an elegy mourning the drowning of Hugo's daughter Leopoldine. The speaker resolves to place 'a bouquet of green holly and flowering heather' on her grave. The poem 'In Villequier' (named for the city where Leopoldine drowned in the Seine) implores God to 'Let me cry!' Written in alexandrins ternaires. Wikipedia

Hernani

A play about Charles V (Don Carlos), Don Ruy Gomez, and John of Aragon (disguised as the title bandit), all of whom love Dona Sol. Its premiere occasioned a famous fistfight/riot between literary radicals (Romantics) and conservatives (Classicists), known as the 'Battle of Hernani.' Supporters dressed in outrageous costumes to foil censors. Alexandre Dumas preceded it with his own Romantic play Henry III and His Court. Wikipedia

Preface to Cromwell

Hugo's theoretical manifesto for Romanticism, written as the preface to his almost-unperformable play Cromwell (400+ pages, 77 characters, iambic pentameter). Calls the stage an 'optical point' where all humanity should be reflected by the 'wand of art.' Attacks the 'worm-eaten timber' of the classical unities and calls for drama that allows 'grotesque with the sublime' as done by Shakespeare. The play itself was Hugo's first but was never staged during his life. Wikipedia

The Toilers of the Sea

Novel dedicated to the island of Guernsey, where Hugo was exiled. The fisherman Gilliatt seeks to rescue the steam engine of the ship Durande from the ocean. Gilliatt's employer, the merchant Mess Lethierry, offers his daughter Deruchette's hand to anyone who can recover it. Gilliatt fights an octopus underwater (popularizing the French term 'pieuvre'). Sieur Clubin tries to steal his employer's money but drowns when attacked by an octopus (whose eyes Gilliatt gouges out). Gilliatt rescues Ebenezer Caudray from a rock, but after learning of Caudray's interest in Deruchette, he resigns himself to drown on the Gild-Holm-'Ur seat as the Cashmere sails away carrying the newlyweds. General / Biographical

French Romantic author and poet. Spent 15 years exiled on the island of Guernsey for criticisms of Napoleon III's government. His daughter Leopoldine drowned in the Seine. His wife was Adele Foucher; critic Sainte-Beuve had an affair with her. His daughter Adele suffered from schizophrenia and obsessed over British officer Albert Pinson (sometimes gives her name to Love Fever Syndrome). Received a royal pension for his early poems. Part of the French Generation of 1820 with Lamartine and Comte de Vigny. Told abolitionist Victor Schoelcher 'God offers Africa to Europe. Take it.' Created over 4,000 drawings, some anticipating Surrealist automatic writing. Worshipped by Cao Dai.

Summary of Facts

Victor Hugo is one of the most frequently asked-about figures in quizbowl Literature, with clues spanning his major novels, plays, poetry, and biography. His most commonly referenced work is Les Miserables, which generates clues about Jean Valjean's pursuit by Inspector Javert, Bishop Myriel's gift of candlesticks, Fantine's degradation and her daughter Cosette, Marius Pontmercy and his escape through the Paris sewers, the street urchin Gavroche living in the Elephant of the Bastille, the Friends of the ABC led by Enjolras, and the novel's famous digressions on the Battle of Waterloo, the Paris sewers ('The Intestine of the Leviathan'), and argot. Javert's backstory as the son of a convict and fortune teller, and his suicide by drowning in the Seine, also appear.

The Hunchback of Notre Dame generates clues about Quasimodo the bell-ringer, the gypsy Esmeralda (who owns a goat named Djali), and the villainous Archdeacon Claude Frollo. Esmeralda is framed for the stabbing of Captain Phoebus (actually done by Frollo) and hanged. She is reunited with her hermitess mother through a red baby shoe. Frollo's prediction that 'print will kill architecture' is a notable power clue.

Hugo's poetry is anchored by Les Contemplations, particularly the elegy 'Tomorrow, at Dawn,' which mourns the drowning of his daughter Leopoldine. The collection is divided into 'Once' and 'Today,' with the fourth book titled Pauca Meae (from Virgil's Eclogues). The poem's imagery of the speaker walking 'alone, unknown, my back curved' and placing 'a bouquet of green holly and flowering heather' on the grave recurs frequently. The companion poem 'In Villequier' also appears. Hugo popularized the alexandrin ternaire (three-part alexandrine).

Hernani is central to Hugo's dramatic legacy. Clues focus on the 'Battle of Hernani' — the riots between Romantics and Classicists at its premiere — and the love triangle involving Charles V, Don Ruy Gomez, and the disguised bandit John of Aragon pursuing Dona Sol. The Preface to Cromwell functions as Hugo's manifesto for Romanticism, attacking the classical unities championed by Racine and calling for drama that mixes 'the grotesque with the sublime.' Cromwell itself was never staged due to its 400+ pages and 77 characters.

The Toilers of the Sea is set on Guernsey and features the fisherman Gilliatt's famous underwater battle with an octopus (which popularized the French word 'pieuvre'). Gilliatt seeks to recover the steam engine of the Durande, and the novel ends with him drowning on the Gild-Holm-'Ur seat as the woman he loves sails away with another man. Ninety-Three, Hugo's last novel, depicts the Chouannerie revolt in the Vendee. The Last Day of a Condemned Man reflects Hugo's opposition to the death penalty. The Man Who Laughs features the disfigured Gwynplaine traveling with Ursus and the wolf Homo, and introduces the term 'Comprachico.'

Biographically, Hugo was exiled for 15 years on Guernsey for criticizing Napoleon III. His daughter Leopoldine's drowning is central to his poetry. He was part of the French Generation of 1820 alongside Lamartine and Vigny. Critic Sainte-Beuve had an affair with Hugo's wife Adele Foucher. Hugo is also a figure of worship in the Vietnamese syncretic religion Cao Dai, and created over 4,000 drawings that anticipated Surrealist automatic writing.
