American silent film director (1875–1948), widely credited with pioneering modern film language. Best known for The Birth of a Nation (1915), a technically innovative but deeply racist pro-KKK film, and its follow-up Intolerance (1916). Frequent actress Lillian Gish starred in many of his films. Jean-Luc Godard paraphrased his supposed quote that all you need to make a film is 'a girl and a gun.'
The Birth of a Nation (1915)

The first film screened at the White House. Based on Thomas Dixon's novel The Clansman. Follows the Cameron and Stoneman families through the Civil War and Reconstruction. African-Americans are 'heroically' stopped from voting. Women are saved from a black militia by the Confederate army. 'The Ride of the Valkyries' plays during the climax as men race to save Lillian Gish's character (Elsie Stoneman) from the doctor Silas Lynch. Raoul Walsh (later a director) played John Wilkes Booth in this film. Widely credited with restarting/reinvigorating the KKK. Wikipedia

Intolerance (1916)

A 'film fugue' (Theodore Huff's term) consisting of four interwoven storylines set in Babylon, Judea (France: St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre), and America, bridged by shots of a woman rocking a cradle (representing Eternal Motherhood). The Babylonian section: Griffith used a 100-foot crane to film women moving down a massive staircase below elephant-topped pillars; a king is delivered a white rose by dove-pulled chariot. The American section: a peon of the 'Musketeer of the Slums' is sentenced to death with the recurring line 'an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth.' The film closes with 'Dare we dream of a golden day when the bestial War shall rule no more.' Made in response to criticism of Birth of a Nation. Jean-Luc Godard paraphrased Griffith's quote ('a girl and a gun') when discussing this director. Wikipedia

Broken Blossoms (1919)

A film about a Chinese missionary known as 'The Yellow Man' who falls in love with Lucy, whose boxer father Battling kills her. In the most famous scene, a girl (Lucy) spins in frantic horror hiding in a closet from her axe-wielding father; she is then beaten to death while The Yellow Man is killed by a Chinese Buddhist missionary. Summary of Facts

D.W. Griffith (1875–1948) is widely regarded as a pioneer of classical film language, credited with establishing techniques like cross-cutting, the close-up, and the tracking shot. Jean-Luc Godard paraphrased his supposed quote that 'all you need to make a film is a girl and a gun.' His frequent actress was Lillian Gish (the 'First Lady of the Silent Screen'), who appeared in Birth of a Nation, Broken Blossoms, Way Down East, and Orphans of the Storm.

The Birth of a Nation (1915), based on Thomas Dixon's novel The Clansman, is his most famous — and most notorious — work. It was the first film screened at the White House, and follows the Cameron and Stoneman families through the Civil War and Reconstruction. It ends with African-Americans being 'heroically' prevented from voting. 'The Ride of the Valkyries' plays during the climax as men race to save Lillian Gish's character Elsie Stoneman from Silas Lynch. The film is widely credited with restarting and glamorizing the KKK. Raoul Walsh (later a director) played John Wilkes Booth in the film.

Intolerance (1916) was made in response to the controversy of Birth of a Nation. Theodore Huff called it a 'film fugue': four interwoven stories set in Babylon, Judea, France (the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre), and America, bridged throughout by shots of a woman rocking a cradle (representing Eternal Motherhood). For the Babylonian section, Griffith used a 100-foot crane to film women moving down a massive staircase beneath colossal pillars topped with elephant statues; a king is delivered a white rose in a dove-pulled chariot. The American section features the recurring line 'an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth.' The film ends with 'Dare we dream of a golden day when the bestial War shall rule no more.'

Broken Blossoms (1919) features a Chinese missionary ('The Yellow Man') who falls in love with Lucy, whose father Battling beats her to death. The most famous scene depicts Lucy spinning in frantic horror hiding in a closet from her axe-wielding father.

The Musketeers of Pig Alley (1912) is sometimes called the first gangster film and believed to be the first to use follow focus.
