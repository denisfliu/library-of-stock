On Liberty & the Harm Principle

Mill's most famous political philosophy work (1859), co-written with his wife Harriet Taylor. It introduces the 'harm principle' — that power can only be rightfully exercised over an individual to prevent harm to others. The work warns against the 'tyranny of the majority' (quoting Tocqueville), uses Marcus Aurelius as an example of a well-intentioned persecutor, and discusses a Muslim country banning pork to show how morals can influence decisions. It contrasts 'Christian self-denial' with 'Pagan self-assertion.'

Utilitarianism (the essay) →

Mill's 1861 essay defending and refining the greatest happiness principle. It distinguishes between higher and lower pleasures ('better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied'), rebutting the charge that Utilitarianism is a 'doctrine worthy only of swine.' The essay contains a notorious 'proof' comparing visibility to desirability, which G.E. Moore cited as a case of the naturalistic fallacy. It credits the term 'utilitarian' to John Galt's Annals of the Parish.

The Subjection of Women & Feminism

Mill's 1869 essay on gender equality, co-written with his wife Harriet Taylor. It argues for female education and suffrage, claims 'there remain no legal slaves, save the mistress of every house,' and attacks marriage laws. Harriet Taylor also wrote The Enfranchisement of Women independently. Mill served in Parliament and sponsored the 1868 Married Women's Property Bill.

A System of Logic & Theory of Names

Mill's 1843 work on scientific methodology and the philosophy of language. It presents five methods of induction (agreement, difference, concomitant variations, residues, and joint method of agreement and difference) and Mill's direct reference theory of names (Millianism), which holds that a name's meaning is only its referent. The Dartmouth example shows a name can persist even when its original descriptive connection is severed. Saul Kripke rejected Mill's theory of general terms but resurrected his theory of proper names. The sixth book, 'On the Logic of the Moral Sciences,' was influential in establishing social science.

Principles of Political Economy

Mill's widely used economics textbook, largely replaced by Alfred Marshall's Principles of Economics at the turn of the 20th century. It advocates worker cooperatives as the 'nearest approach to social justice,' discusses the 'Stationary State' and warns against unlimited increases in wealth and population. It reflects Mill's utilitarian views and includes a chapter 'On the Probable Futurity of the Working Classes.'

General / Biographical

Mill was raised in near-total isolation from other children and taught Greek from age three by his father James Mill, who wrote Elements of Political Economy and The History of British India. He suffered a mental breakdown at age twenty. He paired Bentham with Coleridge as the 'seminal minds' of their age. He debated Thomas Carlyle over slavery (Carlyle's 'Occasional Discourse on the Negro Question' coined the term 'dismal science'). Mill justified colonialism by not applying the harm principle to 'uncivilized' communities. He defended the death penalty in Parliament as the 'least cruel mode of punishment.'

Summary of Facts

John Stuart Mill is one of the most heavily clued philosophers in quizbowl, with major works spanning ethics, political philosophy, logic, economics, and feminism. His most famous work, On Liberty (1859), co-written with his wife Harriet Taylor, introduces the 'harm principle' — the idea that power can only be rightfully exercised over an individual to prevent harm to others. The work warns against the 'tyranny of the majority' (quoting Tocqueville), uses Marcus Aurelius as an example of well-intentioned persecution, and discusses a Muslim country banning pork to illustrate how morals influence decisions.

Mill's 1861 essay Utilitarianism refines Bentham's greatest happiness principle by distinguishing between higher and lower pleasures, famously claiming 'better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied.' The essay contains a notorious 'proof' comparing visibility to desirability, which G.E. Moore cited as a case of the naturalistic fallacy. It also credits the term 'utilitarian' to John Galt's novel Annals of the Parish.

The Subjection of Women (1869), co-written with Harriet Taylor, argues for female education and suffrage, claiming 'there remain no legal slaves, save the mistress of every house.' Harriet Taylor independently wrote The Enfranchisement of Women. Mill served in Parliament and sponsored the 1868 Married Women's Property Bill.

A System of Logic (1843) presents five methods of induction (agreement, difference, concomitant variations, residues, and joint method) and Mill's direct reference theory of names (Millianism), which holds that a name's meaning is only its referent — illustrated by the Dartmouth example. Saul Kripke rejected Mill's theory of general terms but resurrected his theory of proper names. The sixth book, 'On the Logic of the Moral Sciences,' was influential for social science methodology.

Mill's Principles of Political Economy was a widely used economics textbook that advocated worker cooperatives and was eventually replaced by Alfred Marshall's work. Mill was raised in near-total isolation from other children, taught Greek from age three by his father James Mill, and suffered a mental breakdown at age twenty. He debated Thomas Carlyle over slavery, justified colonialism by not applying the harm principle to 'barbarous' peoples, and defended the death penalty in Parliament.
