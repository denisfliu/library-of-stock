Gabriel Garcia Marquez

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Colombian novelist, journalist, and Nobel laureate (1982), central figure of the Latin American Boom and magical realism. Best known for One Hundred Years of Solitude (the Buendia family in Macondo) and Love in the Time of Cholera. Also wrote Chronicle of a Death Foretold, The Autumn of the Patriarch, and numerous short stories including 'A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings' and 'The Handsomest Drowned Man in the World.' His journalism includes The Story of a Shipwrecked Sailor and News of a Kidnapping. His Nobel speech 'The Solitude of Latin America' quotes Faulkner. Pedro Paramo by Juan Rulfo was a major influence. Punched by Mario Vargas Llosa in 1976.
One Hundred Years of Solitude

Novel about seven generations of the Buendia family in the fictional town of Macondo. Jose Arcadio Buendia founds Macondo and goes mad, babbling in Latin, and is tied to a chestnut tree. Colonel Aureliano Buendia starts 32 failed uprisings, fathers 17 sons who share his name (all but one assassinated), makes miniature golden fish and melts them down repeatedly, and faces a firing squad in the famous opening line. Melquiades visits with trinkets and ice; his manuscript predicts the destruction of Macondo. Ursula fears incestuous relationships will produce a child with a pig's tail; the last Buendia child is born with one and eaten alive by ants. Remedios the Beauty ascends into the sky while folding laundry/sheets. Pilar Ternera and Petra Cotes are lovers of various Buendias. Mauricio Babilonia is surrounded by yellow butterflies. A massacre of banana workers occurs. The inhabitants put up a sign 'God exists' during an insomnia plague. Innocent Erendira also appears. Smoked 60 cigarettes a day while writing it in a Mexico City apartment where he read Pedro Paramo. Translated by Gregory Rabassa. Wikipedia

"A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings"

A short story subtitled 'A Tale for Children.' Pelayo and Elisenda discover a mute old man with large, dirty wings on a beach during a rainstorm (crabs are killed). He is imprisoned in a chicken coop. Father Gonzaga questions him in Latin. A girl transformed into a giant spider/tarantula for disobedience replaces him as an attraction. Women tie 'junk relics' and jars of holy water to him. The old man eventually regains strength, sings chanteys, and flies away. The Church suggests he is merely a Norwegian. Wikipedia

Love in the Time of Cholera

Novel in which Florentino Ariza waits 53 years to be reunited with his teenage sweetheart Fermina Daza. Opens when Dr. Juvenal Urbino smells bitter almonds and deduces that photographer Jeremiah de Saint-Amour committed suicide using gold cyanide. Urbino falls to his death from a ladder while trying to retrieve his parrot from a mango tree. Florentino cannot remain a clerk at the River Company of the Caribbean because his letters are too poetic; he hires Leona Cassiani after mistaking her for a prostitute. Translated by Edith Grossman; discussed by Oprah's Book Club in 2007. Wikipedia

The Autumn of the Patriarch

Dictator novel inspired by Francisco Franco. The 200+ year-old General/patriarch rules a Caribbean island. He has a massive testicle that constantly emits sad whistling. He is obsessed with beauty queen Leticia Nazareno. He buys all the water in the Caribbean and transports it to the Arizona desert. He serves his Defense Minister at a banquet. Fathers many children born two months premature. A delivery initially mistaken for coconuts consists of skulls sent by the protagonist's torturer. Eaten by vultures after death. Six stream-of-consciousness sections, each beginning with a version of his death. Opens with a chapter from the perspective of 'the People.' Wikipedia

"The Handsomest Drowned Man in the World"

A short story subtitled 'A Tale for Children.' A shockingly tall, beautiful corpse washes up in a fishing village. When a handkerchief is removed from his face, he is revealed to be Esteban. He weighs almost as much as a horse; a shirt is made from bridal linen. He has 'so much authority that he could have drawn fish out of the sea simply by calling their names.' The village is described as 'cold' or 'Wednesday meat.' The villagers bury him and make his memory 'eternal' by painting their housefronts in 'gay colors,' planting flowers on cliffs, and expanding their village. Wikipedia

Nobel Speech / "The Solitude of Latin America"

Garcia Marquez's 1982 Nobel Prize acceptance speech. Quotes William Faulkner: 'I decline to accept the end of man.' Opens by discussing Antonio Pigafetta's fanciful account of the New World during Magellan's voyage. Defines the 'crux' of Latin American solitude as 'a lack of conventional means to render our lives believable.' Claims 'Latin America neither wants, nor has any reason to be, a pawn without a will of its own.' Calls for literature to create an 'opposite utopia.' Does not want to embody Tonio Kroger's dream of 'uniting a chaste north to a passionate south' (referencing Thomas Mann). Links colonial accounts to the 'outsized reality' of the continent. Journalism

Garcia Marquez worked as a journalist for El Espectador. The Story of a Shipwrecked Sailor: 14 newspaper articles about the sole survivor of a ship that sank from the weight of its contraband (Luis Alejandro Velasco). This contradicted the government's report, forcing Gustavo Rojas Pinilla to shut down the newspaper. News of a Kidnapping: account of ten prisoners of the 'Extraditables' based on interviews with Alberto Villamizar and Maruja Pachon; records the failed rescue of Diana Turbay from the Medellin Cartel. Clandestine in Chile: Miguel Littin disguises himself as a Uruguayan businessman to return to Chile; burned by Pinochet. The Scandal of the Century: posthumous 2018 collection titled from pieces about Wilma Montesi's death. He purportedly saw a skull with copper hair tumble from a crypt being destroyed for a hotel.

General / Biographical

Colombian author. Nobel Prize 1982. Punched in the face by Mario Vargas Llosa at a 1976 Mexico City movie premiere ('That's for what you did to Patricia'). Close friendship with Fidel Castro; Reinaldo Arenas asked if he was 'a henchman or an ass.' Pedro Paramo was a major influence (memorized much of it, bought dozens of copies for friends). Translated by Gregory Rabassa and Edith Grossman. Son Rodrigo Garcia is a filmmaker. Died in 2014. Studied screenwriting with Jose Rivera at Sundance.

Summary of Facts

Gabriel Garcia Marquez is one of the most heavily clued authors in quizbowl Literature. His masterwork One Hundred Years of Solitude dominates, generating clues about the Buendia family across seven generations in the fictional town of Macondo. The most recognizable clues include: Colonel Aureliano Buendia facing a firing squad in the opening line and recalling his first time seeing ice; Jose Arcadio Buendia founding Macondo, going mad babbling in Latin, and being tied to a chestnut tree; Remedios the Beauty ascending into the sky while folding laundry; Melquiades visiting with trinkets and ice, his manuscript predicting Macondo's destruction; the last Buendia born with a pig's tail and eaten by ants (fulfilling Ursula's fear about incest); Colonel Aureliano's 32 failed uprisings, 17 sons, and miniature golden fish; Mauricio Babilonia surrounded by yellow butterflies; and the banana worker massacre.

'A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings' is his most frequently clued short story, featuring a mute old man imprisoned in a chicken coop by Pelayo and Elisenda, questioned in Latin by Father Gonzaga, overshadowed by a girl transformed into a tarantula. Love in the Time of Cholera centers on Florentino Ariza waiting 53 years for Fermina Daza, with Dr. Juvenal Urbino deducing a gold cyanide suicide by bitter almonds before dying himself falling from a ladder retrieving his parrot.

Chronicle of a Death Foretold features the Vicario twins stabbing Santiago Nasar to avenge their sister Angela. The Autumn of the Patriarch is a dictator novel inspired by Franco, featuring a 200+ year-old General with a whistling testicle. 'The Handsomest Drowned Man in the World' describes the beautiful corpse Esteban inspiring villagers to paint their houses in bright colors.

His Nobel speech 'The Solitude of Latin America' quotes Faulkner's 'I decline to accept the end of man' and opens with Pigafetta's account of the New World. His journalism includes The Story of a Shipwrecked Sailor (which forced El Espectador's closure) and News of a Kidnapping. He was famously punched by Mario Vargas Llosa in 1976 and was deeply influenced by Juan Rulfo's Pedro Paramo.
