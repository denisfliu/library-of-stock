Reception and Critical Legacy

Pauline Kael wrote an approximately 9000-word (sources also say 8000-word) review championing the film's 'violence without sadism,' which catapulted her to notoriety and led William Shawn to hire her at The New Yorker. Bosley Crowther of the New York Times repeatedly savaged the film's violence; he was fired (or pressured to resign) and replaced by Renata Adler. The BFI Film Classic book on the film by Lester Friedman identifies a generational divide: Crowther and Page Cook were negative; Kael and Kathleen Carroll were positive. A review by Charles Samuels called it 'a bunch of decaying cabbage leaves smeared with catsup.' Along with Easy Rider and The Graduate, the film is considered the start of New American Cinema (New Hollywood). A still from the film appears on the cover of Mark Harris's book Pictures at a Revolution.

Iconic Scenes and Style

The film opens with B&W credits over 32 sepia-tinted photographs; the credits initially appear in white text that turns blood red; then an extreme close-up of a woman's lips as she applies lipstick. At their first meeting, Bonnie strokes Clyde's gun suggestively along his thigh, daring him to 'use it.' The title characters go on a crime spree with C.W. Moss. A car chase uses Earl Scruggs's bluegrass banjo track 'Foggy Mountain Breakdown,' popularizing it. Gene Wilder's big-screen debut is in this film as Eugene, kidnapped along with his girlfriend Velma. The camera tracks two newspapers fluttering in the wind after the couple have sex. A hazy filter is used as Bonnie and Clyde visit one of their mothers in a wheat field. The film ends in an ambush — Texas Rangers led by Frank Hamer riddle them with bullets by a fake flat tire; the climax uses several quick cuts between their faces.

Costumes and Fashion

Theadora van Runkle designed the costumes. For Bonnie, the design included a mid-calf skirt and blouse combination with an angled (rakish) beret and a colorful kerchief. The costume design inspired a real-world fashion fad.

Summary of Facts

Bonnie and Clyde (1967) is a landmark New Hollywood film directed by Arthur Penn, starring Warren Beatty as Clyde Barrow and Faye Dunaway as Bonnie Parker. The film is considered alongside Easy Rider and The Graduate as the start of the New American Cinema. The screenplay was written by David Newman and Robert Benton, untested Esquire magazine writers inspired by Godard's Breathless; it was first offered to Francois Truffaut. Jean-Luc Godard sent a telegram — 'I'm talking cinema, you're talking weather. Au revoir' — ending his involvement. The film was edited by Dede Allen; Robert Towne served as a script doctor before going on to write Chinatown.

The film opens with B&W credits over 32 sepia-tinted photographs; the credits initially appear in white text that turns blood red, then an extreme close-up of a woman's lips applying lipstick. At their first meeting, Bonnie strokes Clyde's gun suggestively along his thigh, daring him to 'use it.' They go on a crime spree with C.W. Moss. Car chase scenes popularized Earl Scruggs's bluegrass banjo piece 'Foggy Mountain Breakdown.' Gene Wilder made his big-screen debut as Eugene, kidnapped with his girlfriend Velma. The film ends with Texas Rangers led by Frank Hamer ambushing and killing the title characters in a hail of bullets by a fake flat tire; several quick cuts between their faces precede the ambush.

Theadora van Runkle designed Bonnie's iconic costume: an angled beret, mid-calf skirt and blouse with a colorful kerchief — inspiring a real-world fashion fad. Pauline Kael's approximately 9000-word review championing the film's 'violence without sadism' catapulted her to notoriety; William Shawn hired her at The New Yorker on its strength. Bosley Crowther of the NYT repeatedly attacked the film and was subsequently fired, replaced by Renata Adler. A still from the film appears on the cover of Mark Harris's book Pictures at a Revolution. In honor of the film's 50th anniversary, Beatty and Dunaway announced the 2017 Best Picture winner — the La La Land/Moonlight Oscar mix-up. Serge Gainsbourg and Brigitte Bardot recorded a song called 'Bonnie and Clyde.'
