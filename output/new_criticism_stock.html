American school of literary criticism (1940s-60s) that championed close reading of self-contained texts. Name coined by John Crowe Ransom. Key figures include Cleanth Brooks, W.K. The Movement

New Criticism was a formalist school of literary criticism that emphasized close reading of poetry as self-contained texts, rejecting biographical and historical context.

Summary of Facts

New Criticism is an American school of literary criticism that emerged in the 1940s-60s, championing the close reading of self-contained texts. The movement's name was coined by John Crowe Ransom in his textbook The New Criticism, and it originated among the Southern Agrarian students of Ransom.

The movement's theoretical foundations were laid out in essays by W.K. Wimsatt and Monroe Beardsley, who critiqued the "intentional fallacy" (assuming authorial intent determines meaning) and the "affective fallacy" (judging a work by its emotional effects on readers). The Well-Wrought Urn by Cleanth Brooks, which begins with an analysis of John Donne's "The Canonization," was another foundational text.

T.S. Eliot's essays, including "Tradition and the Individual Talent" and "Hamlet and His Problems" (which introduced the "objective correlative"), were a major influence on the New Critics. I.A. Richards introduced the term "stock response" to the movement in his essay "Badness in Poetry," which criticized works by Ella Wheeler Wilcox. The New Critics were known for critiquing the poetry of Sidney Lanier and Joyce Kilmer.

Frank Lentricchia later criticized the movement's "subtle denial of history" in his book After the New Criticism. The movement's formalist emphasis on "the words on the page" is often compared with F.R. Leavis's approach in the British journal Scrutiny.
