William Blake

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
English Romantic poet, painter, and printmaker. Known for Songs of Innocence and of Experience (containing 'The Tyger,' 'The Lamb,' 'The Chimney Sweeper,' 'The Sick Rose,' 'Jerusalem'), The Marriage of Heaven and Hell, and visual works including The Ancient of Days, Newton, the Great Red Dragon series, The Ghost of a Flea, and Pity. Created his own mythology involving Urizen, the Four Zoas, and Albion. Used relief etching/illuminated printing.

Summary of Facts

William Blake was an English Romantic poet, painter, and printmaker who created a vast personal mythology and illustrated his own poetry using relief etching (illuminated printing) with copper plates and acid. His poetry collection Songs of Innocence and of Experience contains his most famous poems: 'The Tyger' ('What immortal hand or eye / Could frame thy fearful symmetry,' 'burning bright in the forests of the night'), 'The Lamb' ('softest clothing woolly bright,' 'Dost thou know who made thee?'), 'The Chimney Sweeper' (two versions — the Innocence version with Tom Dacre and an angel with a bright key, the Experience version with 'a little black thing among the snow'), 'The Sick Rose' ('invisible worm / That flies in the night'), 'Infant Sorrow,' 'The Little Black Boy,' 'Holy Thursday,' 'The Garden of Love,' 'Earth's Answer,' 'The Human Abstract,' 'London' ('marks of weakness, marks of woe' near 'the chartered Thames'), and 'The Echoing Green.'

His poem 'Jerusalem' ('And did those feet in ancient time') was set to music by Hubert Parry and became a patriotic hymn. It denounces 'dark Satanic Mills' and calls for a 'bow of burning gold,' 'arrows of desire,' and 'chariot of fire.' It serves as the preface to his epic Milton: A Poem. Jez Butterworth named his play Jerusalem after this poem. The Marriage of Heaven and Hell contains the 'Proverbs of Hell' (including 'Exuberance is beauty') and begins with the roaring of Rintrah; Aldous Huxley took the title The Doors of Perception from it, and Olga Tokarczuk borrowed a title from its proverbs.

Blake's prophetic mythology centers on Urizen (god of reason holding architect's tools, inspiring Ginsberg's Moloch in 'Howl'), the Four Zoas (Urthona, Tharmas, Luvah, Urizen as emanations of the primordial giant Albion), and works including Jerusalem: The Emanation of the Giant Albion (city of Golgonooza), Milton: A Poem (Milton as a falling star entering the poet's foot; Ololon the skylark), Visions of the Daughters of Albion, Europe a Prophecy, Book of Thel, and Books of Los and Ahania.

As a visual artist, his most iconic work is The Ancient of Days — the frontispiece for Europe a Prophecy, showing bearded Urizen crouching from the sun and holding a compass. Newton (1795) depicts a naked Newton on a mossy rock drawing with a compass, the basis for Eduardo Paolozzi's bronze sculpture outside the British Library. The Great Red Dragon watercolor series illustrates Revelation, featuring the Woman Clothed with the Sun and The Beast from the Sea. The Ghost of a Flea shows a muscular creature on a star-studded stage extending its tongue toward a bowl of blood, part of the Visionary Heads series commissioned by astrologer John Varley. Pity illustrates a Macbeth simile with a woman on a white flying horse snatching a newborn babe. Nebuchadnezzar depicts a mad king crawling with talon-like toenails. His twelve 'large color prints' (which he called 'frescoes') include Pity, Nebuchadnezzar, Newton, and The House of Death. His last commission was illustrations of Dante's Divine Comedy. Northrop Frye's study Fearful Symmetry is named after 'The Tyger.' Allen Ginsberg had a vision of Blake, and T.S. Eliot wrote about him in The Sacred Wood.
