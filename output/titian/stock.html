Venetian Renaissance painter (c. 1488–1576), considered the greatest colorist of the Italian Renaissance. Known for his 'poesie' mythological series for Philip II of Spain (including Rape of Europa, Danaë, Diana and Actaeon), Venus of Urbino, Sacred and Profane Love, Bacchus and Ariadne, Pesaro Madonna (Frari Basilica), Assumption of the Virgin (also Frari), Equestrian Portrait of Charles V, The Flaying of Marsyas (late masterpiece), and Man with a Quilted Sleeve. Pupil of Giorgione; collaborated on Pastoral Concert.

Summary of Facts

Titian (Tiziano Vecellio, c. 1488–1576) was the leading Venetian Renaissance painter and the greatest colorist of the Italian Renaissance. Along with Giorgione, his teacher, he pioneered the colorito (colorist) style characteristic of the Venetian school; both were pupils of Giovanni Bellini. He also briefly taught Tintoretto, who was thrown out of his studio after three days.

**Venus of Urbino (1538)**: His most frequently clued work. A reclining nude holds a posy of roses; a sleeping dog curls at her feet; a maid in white rummages through a cassone marriage chest; a potted myrtle plant is visible above. The model likely also posed for Girl in a Fur and La Bella. Guidobaldo II della Rovere may have commissioned it for his wife; it is linked to the 'il toccamano' bridal ritual. Art historian Jozef Grabski argued it subtly shows the Colonna family coat of arms; Rona Goffen's book argues it depicts a post-coital scene. Featured in Johan Zoffany's Tribuna of the Uffizi; discussed in T.J. Clark's Painting of Modern Life. Manet's Olympia (modeled by Victorine Meurent) adopted its pose. Inspired by Giorgione's Sleeping Venus. Now in the Uffizi alongside Titian's portrait of Eleonora Gonzaga, both containing the same sleeping dog.

**'Poesie' Paintings for Philip II**: A series of six mythological works based on Ovid's Metamorphoses, featured in the 2021 Gardner exhibit 'Women, Myth, and Power.' Rape of Europa (c. 1560–62): The original is in the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum. A putto rides a fish while Europa waves a pink cloth and grabs the horn of a white bull; nymphs appear on land in the left background; a monstrous brown sea creature opens its mouth at her. Rubens made a copy with gloomier colors during a trip to Spain. Velázquez's Las Hilanderas contains a tapestry copy in its background. Danaë: First version for a Farnese, later version for Philip II; a maid watches a gold cloud approach the reclining nude. Diana and Actaeon: A two-part depiction of the myth.

**Sacred and Profane Love (c. 1514)**: Commissioned for Niccolò Aurelio's wedding to Laura Bagarotto. Shows a clothed and a nude woman (the nude holds a jar of burning incense) flanking a water-filled sarcophagus into which Cupid dips his hand. Men hunt a hare in the background, with sheep and a distant castle. Friedlaender suggested it may have been inspired by Hypnerotomachia Poliphili; features an inner painting possibly depicting Cain slaying Abel. Wischnitzer-Bernstein and Friedlaender debated whether a horse in it represented virginity. Inspired Rubens's Feast of Venus.

**Bacchus and Ariadne (1520–23)**: Bacchus leaps from a chariot drawn by two cheetahs toward Ariadne, who wears a blue mantle and reaches toward the horizon. A man wrapped with a snake and a woman clashing cymbals march in procession. Corona Borealis depicted in the ultramarine sky; a small ship sails away. Sources are Ovid and Catullus. The artist's signature appears on a gold cup near a King Charles Spaniel. Velázquez depicted a similar Triumph of Bacchus.

**Pesaro Madonna**: In the Frari Basilica, Venice, where it stands near Titian's Assumption of the Virgin. Two offset columns in the center are partially obscured by black clouds with two Cupids frolicking on top. St. Peter's keys (and a blue-and-yellow-clad saint) rest on marble steps next to a turbaned prisoner of war. A papal coat of arms appears on an orange flag with an olive branch. Commissioned by Jacopo Pesaro.

**Assumption of the Virgin**: Also in the Frari Basilica. The Virgin ascends in a twisted, spiral pose on clouds surrounded by cherubim; a man in red with his back turned reaches upward; a sarcophagus is partly visible at the bottom. Germano Casale complained it lacked 'decorum' (poor fishermen used as apostle models); Lodovico Dolce defended it against 'foolish masses.'

**Equestrian Portrait of Charles V (1548)**: Unrealistically depicts the gouty emperor in full armor, carrying a spear, on horseback at the Battle of Mühlberg.

**The Flaying of Marsyas**: Late work in the Kromeriz National Museum, inspired by the death of Marcantonio Bragadin. A dog laps blood while a figure holds a pail beneath the upside-down Marsyas tied to a tree. Contains a self-portrait as Midas watching Apollo draw blood with his knife. A woman plays the violin sadly on the left. Iris Murdoch's favorite painting; Tom Phillips set his Iris Murdoch portrait against its backdrop.

**Portrait of a Man with a Blue Sleeve / Man with a Quilted Sleeve**: Subject leans his blue quilted sleeve over a parapet, looking sidelong at the viewer. Vasari incorrectly claimed it depicted Ariosto. Quoted by Rembrandt's Self-Portrait at the Age of 40.

**Other notable works**: Allegory of Prudence — three animal heads (dog, lion, wolf) under three human heads, thought to be Titian, his son Orazio, and cousin Marco. Portrait of Paul III and His Grandsons — the Farnese pope sits clutching an armrest flanked by two grandsons. Portrait of Isabella d'Este — depicted the 62-year-old as a baby-faced young woman in a balzo headdress and ermine zibellino. Tarquin and Lucretia (late) — Tarquin in red brandishes a knife at the nude Lucretia. Venus with a Mirror — Venus covers her left breast while putti hold a mirror. La Schiavona — subject leans on a relief of her own head. Flora — woman in loose white camicia.

**Connections**: Van Dyck kept 19 Titian works in a 'Cabinet de Titien.' Artists from Titian's studio made woodcuts for Vesalius's De Humani Corporis Fabrica. He parodied the Laocoon by replacing the figures with apes. He painted Suleiman the Magnificent's portrait (headgear compared to an onion). The Carracci were called 'the scum of Titian' by John Ruskin.
