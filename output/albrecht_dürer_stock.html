Albrecht Dürer

View source questions · Make cards
German Renaissance engraver and painter. Known for three 'master prints' (Melencolia I, St. Jerome in His Study, Knight Death and the Devil), the Rhinoceros woodcut, nature studies (Young Hare, Great Piece of Turf), and a Christ-like self-portrait at age 28. Also created The Four Apostles and Feast of the Rosary.

Summary of Facts

Albrecht Dürer (1471-1528) was the preeminent German Renaissance engraver and painter. In quizbowl, he is most commonly identified by his three 'master prints' (Meisterstiche): Melencolia I (1514), featuring a brooding winged figure with a magic square, hourglass, and polyhedron; Saint Jerome in His Study (1514), showing the haloed saint writing the Vulgate with a lion and dog sleeping on the floor and a skull on the windowsill; and Knight, Death, and the Devil (1513), depicting an armored knight riding past skeletal Death and a horned Devil.

His Self-Portrait at Twenty-Eight (1500) is one of the most frequently clued works — a frontal portrait with Christlike iconography, flowing hair, fur-trimmed coat, and his monogram with the date 1500. The Latin inscription was composed by Conrad Celtes's secretary. Robert Mapplethorpe's photograph of Patti Smith imitates this self-portrait.

His Rhinoceros (1515) is a famous woodcut based on accounts from Lisbon — Dürer never saw the animal, whose body resembles plate armor. It was the first image of a rhinoceros in Europe since the 3rd century. His nature studies include the highly naturalistic watercolor Young Hare (in the Albertina museum), a stag beetle, and The Great Piece of Turf. The Four Apostles is a diptych with passages from Martin Luther's Bible translation, housed at the Alte Pinakothek.

Biographically, he was apprenticed to Michael Wolgemut and contributed illustrations to Hartmann Schedel's Nuremberg Chronicle. He married Agnes Frey (called 'the old crow') and sent a lost self-portrait in gouache on cambric to Raphael.
