Italian-American film director from New York City, born 1942. Best known for Taxi Driver, Goodfellas, Raging Bull, and Mean Streets. Longtime collaborator with Robert De Niro, editor Thelma Schoonmaker, and screenwriter Paul Schrader. Also a major film preservationist through his World Cinema Project. Known for rock music soundtracks, tracking shots, and slow-motion cinematography.

General / Biographical

Martin Scorsese is an Italian-American director from New York City's Little Italy (Elizabeth Street). His frequent collaborators include screenwriter Paul Schrader (Taxi Driver, Raging Bull), editor Thelma Schoonmaker (tied for most Oscar nominations in her category), and actor Robert De Niro. Scorsese had a cameo as Van Gogh in Kurosawa's Dreams. He directed the pilot of Boardwalk Empire. He denounced Marvel franchise films as lacking in art. Summary of Facts

Martin Scorsese is an Italian-American film director born in 1942 in New York City's Little Italy neighborhood. His most frequently clued film is Taxi Driver (1976), scripted by Paul Schrader, in which Robert De Niro plays Travis Bickle, a disturbed Vietnam vet who becomes obsessed with rescuing underage prostitute Iris (played by 12-year-old Jodie Foster) and plans to assassinate Senator Charles Palatine. The famous 'You talkin' to me?' mirror monologue was improvised by De Niro. Scorsese himself cameos as a man planning to kill his wife with a .44 magnum. Travis gives himself a Mohawk when planning to kill the senator — John Hinckley emulated this before shooting Ronald Reagan. Jodie Foster wore red wedge shoes as Iris. Scorsese used a 'priest's-eye view' shot for the brothel massacre aftermath. Camera work was influenced by Hitchcock's The Wrong Man. The film's title comes from Raymond Chandler's phrase about a hero going down 'mean streets.'

Goodfellas (1990) stars Ray Liotta as Henry Hill, who opens with 'As far back as I can remember, I always wanted to be a gangster.' Joe Pesci plays Tommy DeVito, murdered after being tricked into thinking he's a made man. One character is called 'Jimmy Two-Times' for repeating himself. The film is famous for its Copacabana long-take tracking shot following Henry Hill and his girlfriend. Raging Bull (1980) is a black-and-white film about boxer Jake LaMotta (De Niro), opening with slow-motion boxing footage set to Mascagni's 'Cavalleria Rusticana: Intermezzo.' LaMotta ends the film quoting Brando's 'I coulda been a contender' monologue from On the Waterfront (a Kazan film). Mean Streets (1973) was Scorsese's first collaboration with De Niro and Harvey Keitel; Charlie covers for debt-ridden Johnny Boy and has an affair with his epileptic cousin Teresa. It ends with Cream's 'Steppin' Out' playing as Teresa's car hits a fire hydrant. Cassavetes told Scorsese his previous Boxcar Bertha was 'a piece of shit,' spurring Mean Streets; the film was itself inspired by Fellini's I Vitelloni.

The Departed (2006) adapted Hong Kong's Infernal Affairs to a Boston setting, starring Jack Nicholson and Matt Damon — it gave Scorsese his sole Best Director Academy Award. The Irishman (2019) based on I Heard You Paint Houses (Charles Brandt) follows Frank Sheeran among the Philadelphia mob and Teamsters; de-aging CGI was used without motion tracking markers; Al Pacino's first Scorsese collaboration. Hugo (2011) stars Asa Butterfield as a French orphan whose father repaired an automaton; the boy befriends Isabelle, daughter of Méliès. Killers of the Flower Moon (2023) depicts the Osage murders; Lily Gladstone translated dialogue and helped restructure the film away from a white-savior narrative.

Scorsese's documentary Italianamerican — about his parents Catherine and Charles at their Elizabeth Street apartment — is his own self-identified 'best film'; the credits include a meatball recipe. The Last Waltz (1978) is his concert film of The Band's final show, often called the greatest concert film ever made; a 'traveling booger matte' was invented to obscure cocaine in Neil Young's nose. His 1995 BBC documentary A Personal Journey through American Movies classifies directors as storytellers, illusionists, smugglers, and iconoclasts. Other documentaries cover Bob Dylan (No Direction Home), the Rolling Stones (Shine a Light), and George Harrison (Living in the Material World, 3.5 hours).

Scorsese founded the World Cinema Project to restore non-Western films, including Ousmane Sembene's Black Girl, Lino Brocka's Manila in the Claws of Light, Iran's Chess Game of the Wind, Leone's Once Upon a Time in America, Bengali film The Cloud-Capped Star, and others. Key collaborators: screenwriter Paul Schrader (Taxi Driver, Raging Bull), editor Thelma Schoonmaker (tied with Michael Kahn for most Oscar editing nominations), Robert De Niro, and (later) Leonardo DiCaprio. Scorsese had a cameo as Van Gogh in Kurosawa's Dreams. He directed the pilot of Boardwalk Empire. He famously said Marvel films lack artistic value. Saul Bass designed title sequences for his films, and he uses circular iris shots as a trademark device.
