1942 Hollywood film directed by Michael Curtiz, starring Humphrey Bogart as American bar owner Rick Blaine and Ingrid Bergman as his ex-lover Ilsa Lund. Set in Vichy-occupied Morocco during WWII. Rick helps Ilsa and her husband Victor Laszlo (Czech Resistance leader) escape using letters of transit. Famous for the song 'As Time Goes By,' iconic quotes, and major supporting performances by Claude Rains and Conrad Veidt. Scored by Max Steiner.
Characters and Plot

Rick Blaine (Humphrey Bogart) owns Rick's Café Américain in Vichy-occupied Casablanca. His ex-lover Ilsa Lund (Ingrid Bergman) arrives with her husband Victor Laszlo, a Czech Resistance leader. Rick possesses letters of transit (imaginary documents) that Ilsa and Victor need to escape to Portugal (Lisbon). At the climax, Rick convinces Ilsa to board a plane with Victor, telling her their problems 'don't amount to a hill of beans.' Nazi Major Strasser (Conrad Veidt) is killed. Captain Renault (Claude Rains) tells his men to 'round up the usual suspects.'

Summary of Facts

Casablanca (1942) is a Hollywood film directed by Michael Curtiz and starring Humphrey Bogart as Rick Blaine, an American expatriate who owns Rick's Café Américain in Vichy-occupied Casablanca, Morocco. The film's central conflict involves Rick's ex-lover Ilsa Lund (Ingrid Bergman, Swedish actress) arriving with her husband Victor Laszlo, a Czech Resistance leader. Rick possesses letters of transit — imaginary documents needed for Ilsa and Victor to flee to Lisbon, Portugal. At the climax, Rick convinces Ilsa to board the plane with Victor, telling her their problems 'don't amount to a hill of beans.' After Rick kills Nazi Major Strasser (played by Conrad Veidt, who also played Cesare in The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari), Captain Renault (Claude Rains) tells his men to 'round up the usual suspects' — a line that later gave its title to the 1995 film The Usual Suspects. The film ends with Rick declaring 'Louis, I think this is the beginning of a beautiful friendship.'

Rick's Café is famous for multiple iconic scenes: a chorus of 'Die Wacht am Rhein' by Nazi officers is countered by the band playing 'La Marseillaise'; an official is shocked to discover gambling before being handed his winnings (Captain Renault); and Rick directs a Bulgarian couple to try roulette number 22. The café scenes also include Rick's retorts — 'If I gave you any thought, I probably would' (to 'You despise me?') and 'I stick my neck out for nobody.' A practical film-making detail: dwarf extras were used with fog to hide the cardboard plane model on the runway. The film is based on the play 'Everybody Comes to Rick's' by schoolteacher Murray Burnett and Joan Alison (dubbed 'sophisticated hokum').

The character Sam, the Black café pianist played by Dooley Wilson, is iconic for the line 'Play it, Sam. Play As Time Goes By' — though Sam is never actually told 'Play it again, Sam.' Sam pretends not to remember the melody when Rick requests it; Ilsa is the one who first asks Sam to play it. Dooley Wilson's actual piano playing was dubbed by Jean Plummer (not Elliot Carpenter, as was long believed). 'As Time Goes By' (lyrics: 'You must remember this, a kiss is still a kiss, a sigh is just a sigh') ranks second on AFI's 100 Years...100 Songs list, behind only 'Somewhere Over the Rainbow.' Producer Hal B. Wallis had considered changing Sam's sex to cast Hazel Scott or Lena Horne; the Epstein twin screenwriters and story editor Irene Diamond intervened.

The film was scored by Max Steiner, the Austrian 'father of film music' who also scored King Kong and Gone with the Wind; Steiner disliked 'As Time Goes By' but was required to use it. Roger Ebert criticized Ted Turner for colorizing Casablanca and other films. The Umberto Eco essay 'The Clichés are Having a Ball' argues that Casablanca became a cult film through its 'orgy of sacrificial archetypes' / 'intertextual archetypes,' citing the casting of Conrad Veidt and Peter Lorre. Ingrid Bergman later starred in Spellbound, Gaslight, and Joan of Arc before leaving Hollywood for over a decade to work with future husband Roberto Rossellini. Claude Rains and Bergman also reunited for Hitchcock's Notorious (1946).
