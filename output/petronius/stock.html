Summary of Facts

Petronius (Gaius Petronius Arbiter) was a Roman author best known as the writer of the Satyricon, considered the first Roman novel. He served as 'Arbiter of Elegance' (arbiter elegantiae) at the court of the emperor Nero, essentially acting as Nero's fashion and taste adviser. According to Tacitus, he committed suicide by opening his veins while having poetry recited around him. His life at Nero's court was fictionalized in Henryk Sienkiewicz's 1896 novel Quo Vadis, where he tells Nero to 'commit murder, but write no verses.'

The Satyricon is a satirical novel following the picaresque adventures of Encolpius, his lover Giton, and The Poet Eumolpus. Its most famous section is the Feast (Cena) of Trimalchio, a lavish banquet given by a vulgar wealthy freedman that satirizes Roman society. Trimalchio's house features a 'Cave Canem' (Beware the Dog) mosaic, and at the feast, stories of witches and a werewolf are told. Trimalchio also recounts meeting the Cumaean Sibyl, alluding to Virgil's Aeneid. A recurring subplot involves Encolpius's impotence, which he tries to cure by various means including killing a sacred goose and seeking help from the priestess Oenothea. The novel also contains the famous embedded story of the Widow of Ephesus, in which a mourning widow is seduced by a soldier and offers her husband's corpse to replace a stolen one. Eumolpus narrates 'Milesian tales' including one about seducing his employer's son while working as a tutor in Pergamum. Quartilla forces Giton and Encolpius into an orgy. The three main characters survive a shipwreck, after which Eumolpus proposes posing as a childless rich old man.
