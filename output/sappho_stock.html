Summary of Facts

Sappho is an ancient Greek lyric poet from the island of Lesbos, widely known as the 'Tenth Muse.' She is one of the nine canonical Greek lyric Poets and its sole female member. Her works survive almost entirely in fragments, with the 'Hymn to Aphrodite' (Fragment 1) being her only complete surviving poem. Anne Carson's If Not, Winter is the standard modern translation, notable for its liberal use of square brackets to indicate lacunae in the original text.

The 'Hymn to Aphrodite' (Fragment 1) opens with the phrase 'Deathless Aphrodite of the spangled mind' and describes a chariot drawn by sparrows (or doves, depending on translation). The speaker addresses the goddess as 'iridescent-throned,' 'child of Zeus,' and asks her to 'be my ally' and not to 'break with hard pains my heart.' Aphrodite asks 'Whom this time should I persuade to lead you back again to her love?'

Fragment 31 (also called 'Ode to Anactoria' or 'phainetai moi') opens with 'He seems to me equal to the gods,' describing a man who sits opposite a woman listening to her 'sweet speaking.' The speaker experiences physical symptoms of desire: tongue breaks, 'thin fire racing under skin,' feeling 'greener than grass' (or 'paler than grass,' depending on translation), cold sweat, and seems 'nearly to have died.' The poem was quoted by Longinus in On the Sublime as an example of literary intensity. It was adapted by Catullus as Poem 51. The poem abruptly ends mid-sentence with 'But all is to be dared, because even a person of poverty.'

The 'Brothers Poem' was rediscovered in 2014 by a team led by Dirk Obbink. It centers on Sappho's brothers Charaxos and Larichos, with the speaker wishing that one brother would 'raise up his head and become a man.'

Fragment 58 (the 'Tithonus poem') recalls the mythological story of Tithonus and describes skin that was 'once supple and smooth.' Carson translated it as 'The Beat Goes On' after additional lines were found at the University of Cologne. The poem states 'a human being without old age is not a possibility.'

The 'midnight poem,' long attributed to Sappho, describes the speaker sleeping alone when 'the moon and the Pleiades have set' and 'the time is passing.' A.E. Housman wrote several adaptations of it.

Sappho also wrote a poem about a wedding that repeats 'O Hymenaeus!' and proclaims 'Lift high the roofbeam, carpenters.' She wrote 'The Marriage of Hektor and Andromache' and a poem in which a girl declares 'Mother dear, I can't finish my weaving.' She predicted 'not one girl I think / who looks on the light of the sun / will ever / have wisdom / like this' and boasted 'Although they are only breath, words which I command are immortal.' In a poem sometimes titled 'To an army wife in Sardis,' the speaker argues that the 'most beautiful thing on the black earth' is not an army of horses or ships but 'what you love,' citing Helen leaving her child and parents for Troy.

Sappho wrote in the Aeolic dialect and lends her name to the Sapphic stanza form, consisting of three hendecasyllable lines capped by a five-syllable Adonic line. She was a contemporary of Alcaeus of Mytilene, who praised her as having a 'honey-sweet smile.' Her name on the island of Lesbos is the origin of the term 'lesbian.'

Biographically, legend holds that Sappho threw herself from the Leucadian cliffs due to unrequited love for the ferryman Phaon. This story first appeared in a play by Menander. She was exiled in Sicily according to the Marmor Parium. Her purported daughter Cleis is sometimes interpreted as a homosexual lover instead. Ulrich von Wilamowitz-Moellendorff theorized she was head of a girls' boarding School. Franz Grillparzer wrote a tragedy in which Sappho believes her slave Melitta has seduced the youth she loves. Algernon Swinburne wrote the dramatic monologue 'Anactoria' and a sadomasochistic poem in Poems and Ballads from Sappho's perspective. Renee Vivien published a translation of Sappho in 1903. Judy Chicago referenced Sappho as 'the flower of the Graces' in The Dinner Party. Giacomo Leopardi wrote 'The Last Song of Sappho.' Anne Carson's book Eros the Bittersweet claims Sappho first called eros 'bittersweet.'
