Opera in four acts by Georges Bizet, premiered in 1875, set in Seville, Spain. The title character is a Roma mezzo-soprano who works in a cigar factory. She seduces corporal Don José, who abandons his duty and girlfriend Micaëla for her, but she eventually falls in love with the bullfighter Escamillo. The opera ends with Don José stabbing Carmen to death outside the bullring. Based on a Prosper Mérimée novella. Famous for the Habanera, the Toreador Song, and the Flower Song.
Carmen

Four-act French opera by Georges Bizet, premiered at the Opéra-Comique in Paris in 1875. Based on a novella by Prosper Mérimée. Set in Seville, Spain, the opera follows Carmen, a Roma cigar-factory worker, and her fatal entanglement with soldier Don José and bullfighter Escamillo. It ends at a bullfight where Don José murders Carmen. Despite its initial mixed reception, it became one of the most frequently performed operas in the repertoire. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Carmen is a four-act opera by the French composer Georges Bizet, based on the 1845 novella of the same name by Prosper Mérimée. It is set in Seville, Spain, though sung in French — a distinction clued several times. The opera premiered at the Paris Opéra-Comique in 1875 and, despite a mixed initial reception, became one of the world's most performed operas. Johannes Brahms was famously so taken with it after its Vienna premiere that he saw the opera over twenty times.

The plot centers on Carmen, a Roma cigar-factory worker and mezzo-soprano, who seduces corporal Don José in Act I by singing the Seguidilla and persuading him to free her hands, defying his superior Zuniga. Don José's Micaëla subplot — she arrives with a letter from his mother encouraging him to return home — provides a contrasting domestic alternative. In Act II, set at Lillas Pastia's tavern, Don José sings the Flower Song ('La fleur que tu m'avais jetée'), revealing he kept the flower Carmen once threw him while he was in prison. By Act II's end, he joins the smugglers Le Dancaire and Le Remendado. The opera ends at a bullfight where Carmen, now in love with the toreador Escamillo, rejects Don José; she defiantly throws his ring back at him during the closing duet 'C'est toi? C'est moi!' before he stabs her.

The opera's signature numbers are well-clued. The Habanera ('L'amour est un oiseau rebelle') opens with a descending chromatic scale from D and was based — without Bizet's knowledge — on a song by Sebastián Iradier rather than an authentic folk tune. The Toreador Song ('Votre toast, je peux vous le rendre') is Escamillo's Act II showpiece, reprised as Carmen is murdered. The fate motif — long D, C-sharp, B-flat, C-sharp, A — appears over tremolo D minor triads near the end of the prelude, followed by two soft timpani strokes. Carmen's Act III card-reading aria 'En vain pour éviter' depicts her foretelling her own death. The Act III entr'acte begins with harp arpeggios under a flute melody passed to clarinet and English horn.

The opera's famous derivatives include Pablo de Sarasate's Carmen Fantasy for violin (which quotes the Aragonaise and other dances), Rodion Shchedrin's ballet arrangement scored for strings and percussion, and Vladimir Horowitz's virtuosic piano variations on the Danse Bohème (beginning with the motif grace note A-sharp, B). Franz Waxman's Carmen Fantasy, premiered at Jascha Heifetz's behest and featured in the film Humoresque, is another major offshoot. Saint-Saëns drew the opening violin motif of his Havanaise from Carmen's habanera.

Legend holds that Célestine Galli-Marié, the creator of the title role, fainted during a performance on the very night Bizet died. Carmen Jones (1943), Oscar Hammerstein II's all-Black adaptation updating the story to a World War II parachute factory, was filmed in 1954 with Harry Belafonte and Dorothy Dandridge. Susan McClary's only single-work monograph is devoted to this opera. Charles Gounod, Bizet's mentor, allegedly said after the premiere that Bizet deserved credit only for 'the sauce that masks the fish.'
