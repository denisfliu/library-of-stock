Democracy and Education

Dewey's magnum opus (1916) on educational philosophy, arguing that democracy depends on how education conditions people to be part of the community. It opens by noting that 'unlike inanimate things, living things maintain themselves through renewal.' The work argues that schools should be part of civic life, groups geography with history, defines communication as 'sharing experience until it becomes a common possession,' and synthesizes the educational philosophies of Plato and Rousseau's Emile.

The Public and Its Problems

Dewey's 1927 response to Walter Lippmann's The Phantom Public. While Lippmann argued that the public is an illusion incapable of meaningful democratic participation, Dewey countered that the public is not a 'phantom' but comes into being when threatened by negative external forces, and that widespread communication could form a 'Great Community.' Dewey argued that technology distracts the public from political issues, creating an 'eclipse' of the public.

Art as Experience

Dewey's major work on aesthetics (1934), delivered as the first William James Lectures at Harvard. It argues that artistic objects should not be separated from 'conditions of origin and operation in experience' and that there is a continuity between aesthetics and everyday life. The 'live creature' is a central concept — a being situated in and interacting with its environment. The work challenges the 'museum' conception of art, citing Asian and African artifacts as objects with both aesthetic and instrumental functions.

The Reflex Arc Concept in Psychology

Dewey's influential 1896 paper in Psychological Review that challenged the sequential stimulus-response model of behavior. This paper established Dewey's scientific credentials and contributed to the 'perception-action cycle' that influenced Donald Hebb's neuroscience and the later Head Start program.

General / Biographical & Political Activity

Dewey founded the University of Chicago Laboratory Schools to promote experiential, progressive learning. He called his philosophy 'instrumentalism' and wrote Knowing and the Known with Arthur Bentley. He led a commission that sought to exonerate Leon Trotsky during the Moscow Trials. He spent two years in China lecturing, with his lectures back-translated from Chinese. Bertrand Russell warned Dewey's philosophy would contribute to 'cosmic impiety.' Richard Rorty praised Dewey as embodying the American spirit. Sidney Hook was known as 'Dewey's bulldog.' Dewey's 'My Pedagogic Creed' is a five-article creed.

Summary of Facts

John Dewey is the American pragmatist philosopher best known for his work in education, aesthetics, and political philosophy. His magnum opus, Democracy and Education (1916), argues that democratic society depends on education conditioning people for community participation. It synthesizes Plato and Rousseau's educational thought and defines communication as 'sharing experience until it becomes a common possession.'

The Public and Its Problems (1927) is Dewey's response to Walter Lippmann's The Phantom Public. While Lippmann argued the public is an illusion, Dewey countered that the public is in 'eclipse' rather than phantom, and that widespread communication could form a 'Great Community.' Dewey argued technology distracts the public from political engagement.

Art as Experience (1934), delivered as the first William James Lectures at Harvard, argues for continuity between aesthetics and everyday life, centered on the concept of the 'live creature' interacting with its environment. It challenges the 'museum' conception of art by citing Asian and African artifacts that serve both aesthetic and instrumental functions.

Dewey's 1896 paper 'The Reflex Arc Concept in Psychology' challenged the stimulus-response model and influenced Donald Hebb's neuroscience. He founded the University of Chicago Laboratory Schools to promote experiential learning and called his philosophy 'instrumentalism.' He led a commission to exonerate Leon Trotsky during the Moscow Trials, spent two years lecturing in China, and wrote Knowing and the Known with Arthur Bentley. Richard Rorty praised Dewey as embodying the American spirit in Achieving Our Country, and Sidney Hook was known as 'Dewey's bulldog.' Cornel West discussed Dewey's reframing of 'givens' as 'takens' in The American Evasion of Philosophy, presaging Wilfrid Sellars's 'Myth of the Given.'
