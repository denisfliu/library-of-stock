Summary of Facts

Charles Baudelaire (Charles Pierre Baudelaire) was a French Symbolist poet, essayist, and art critic, overwhelmingly identified through his poetry collection Les Fleurs du Mal (The Flowers of Evil). That collection is dedicated to Theophile Gautier, whom Baudelaire called a "perfect magician of French letters" and "impeccable poet." It opens with the poem "To the Reader," which begins with "Folly, error, sin, avarice" and addresses the "hypocrite reader" as "my double, my brother" (or "my likeness, my twin"). The poem describes Ennui as a "refined monster" who "dreams of scaffolds and smokes his hookah pipe." T.S. Eliot alluded to Baudelaire with the phrase "Unreal City" and quoted him in the final line of The Waste Land's first section. The collection's sections include "Spleen and Ideal," "Wine," "Revolt," "Death," and the later-added "Tableaux Parisiens" ("Parisian Scenes"), appended during Paris's Haussmann renovation.

"The Albatross" compares The Poet to the title "prince of the skies" whose wings drag like oars; the men of the crew "lay hold" of the "comic" and "ugly" bird with large wings. It is dedicated to Victor Hugo. Aime Cesaire quoted the word "COMICAL AND UGLY" from it in Notebook of a Return to My Native Land.

"The Swan" opens with "Andromache, I think of you!" and laments that a city's face "changes faster than a human heart." The bird escapes its cage and cries "homesick for his fair native lake: Rain, when will you fall? Thunder, when will you roll?" The poem is dedicated to Victor Hugo and appears in the "Parisian Scenes" section.

"Correspondences" describes nature as a "temple" with "living pillars" and a "forest of symbols." Perfumes are described as "cool as the flesh of children / Sweet as oboes, green as meadows." The Symbolist movement took its name from this poem.

"The Litanies of Satan" beseeches the "Wisest of Angels" to "take pity on my long despair" (or "misery"), with a repeating prayer after every couplet. Satan is addressed as one who teaches "the taste for Heaven / To the cursed pariah, even to the leper."

"Invitation to the Voyage" begins "My child, my sister, think of the rapture of living together there!" and envisions "ornate ceilings" and "limpid mirrors" with "oriental splendor." Each stanza imagines a place where all is "order" and "beauty," ending with the word "volupte."

"The Seven Old Men" depicts a sickly "old man with tattered yellow clothes" and leaves the speaker comparing his soul to "a ship without a mast on a monstrous, borderless sea" after encountering identical hobbled men. Baudelaire called yellow the "color of death."

Six poems were censored from the first edition, including "The Damned Women," "Lesbos," "The Jewels," "Lethe," "The Vampire's Metamorphoses," and "To She Who Is Too Gay." These were later republished in a collection titled Les Epaves ("scraps" or "wrecks"). In "Lesbos," the "virile Sappho, paramour and poet" is said to have died on the day of her "blasphemy" of having heterosexual relations. In "The Damned Women," a prophetess curses Hippolyta.

Other notable poems include "The Beacons" (eight quatrains about Rubens, Da Vinci, Rembrandt, Michelangelo), "The Fountain of Blood" (the speaker's blood "gushing out in rhythmical sobs"), "The Soul of Wine" (a bottle sings a "song full of brotherhood and light"), "The Giantess" (the speaker imagines living like "a voluptuous cat at the feet of a queen"), "The Voyage" (travelers discover "an oasis of horror in a desert of boredom"), "The Mendicant Redhead," "A Carcass" (sexualizes a corpse "blossoming like a flower"), "The Joyous Corpse," "The Little Old Ladies," and "Les Chats."

Paris Spleen collects prose poems including "Be Drunk" (telling the addressee not to be "the martyred slave of time" and to reach drunkenness via "poetry or virtue"), "Let's Beat Up the Poor!" (the narrator fights a beggar to test his worthiness), "The Desire to Paint," and a poem about a "Shoddy" glazier on whom the narrator drops a flowerpot. Baudelaire was inspired to write prose poems by his obsessive reading of Aloysius Bertrand's Gaspard de la Nuit.

"The Painter of Modern Life" is Baudelaire's most important critical essay, examining illustrator Constantin Guys ("Monsieur G") as an "eternal convalescent" and "passionate spectator" for whom "the crowd is his element" and who is "everywhere in possession of his incognito." The essay examines Poe's "The Man in the Crowd" and distinguishes "ladies" from "fallen women" in a section on "Women and Prostitutes." Baudelaire coined the term "modernity" and originated the archetype of the flaneur. Walter Benjamin extensively discussed Baudelaire's flaneur in the Arcades Project, calling Baudelaire a "secret agent" of bourgeois discontent and theorizing a "commodity-soul." Benjamin's study Charles Baudelaire: A Lyric Poet in the Era of High Capitalism took Baudelaire's line "Everything for me becomes allegory" as its starting point. Baudelaire's idea of "botanizing on the asphalt" is cited in books by Rebecca Solnit and Lauren Elkin. Griselda Pollock expanded Baudelaire's distinctions in "Modernity and the Spaces of Femininity." T.J. Clark's book on Manet is titled after a Baudelaire essay.

Baudelaire also wrote "Richard Wagner and Tannhauser in Paris" (a review despite having little musical training), "On the Essence of Laughter" (about a Pierrot robbing a cleaning woman in pantomime), and "On Photography" ("It is useless and tedious to represent what exists, because nothing that exists satisfies me"). He spent the last twenty years of his life translating Edgar Allan Poe into French, and supposedly learned English for that express purpose, popularizing Poe throughout Europe. He was a close friend of Edouard Manet and posed for Courbet's The Artist's Studio. His half-Haitian mistress was Jeanne Duval, fictionalized in Nalo Hopkinson's The Salt Roads. Arthur Rimbaud called him "the king of Poets, a true God." Algernon Swinburne eulogized him in "Ave Atque Vale" (titled in reference to Catullus). Mallarme wrote a tomb sonnet for him. Georges Bataille discussed him in Literature and Evil. Richard Brautigan imagined him as the "Galilee Hitch-Hiker." Lemony Snicket's orphans took their surname from him. Alejandra Pizarnik drew inspiration from his "suffering." Pablo Neruda mentioned seeing a "surviving" copy of his works.
