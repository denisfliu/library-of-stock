17th-century French comic playwright, born Jean-Baptiste Poquelin. Co-founder of the Illustrious Theater Company. Master of the comedy of manners. Frequently played the recurring character Sganarelle in his own productions. Died of a tubercular coughing fit hours after performing as the hypochondriac Argan in The Imaginary Invalid. His plays were often satirical, targeting religious hypocrisy (Tartuffe), misanthropy (The Misanthrope), social climbing (The Bourgeois Gentleman), and pretentiousness (The Affected Young Ladies). His works drew on classical sources (Plautus, Terence). Richard Wilbur translated several of his plays into English. His troupe created the genre of the comedie-ballet. After his death, his acting company evolved into the Comedie-Francaise.
Tartuffe

Moliere's most frequently referenced play. The title character is a scheming religious hypocrite who insinuates himself into Orgon's household. Orgon hides under a table and witnesses Tartuffe attempting to seduce his wife. The play ends with a deus ex machina in which the king's officer arrests Tartuffe by royal decree. Its premiere at the multi-day festival Pleasures of the Enchanted Island was scandalous. Richard Wilbur's English translation preserves the rhyming couplets. Valere tries to win the heart of Orgon's daughter Mariane. The maid Dorine tries to mend the relationship between the two lovers, which was damaged by an arranged marriage. Colley Cibber adapted it as The Non-Juror. Wikipedia

The Imaginary Invalid

Also known as The Hypochondriac (Le malade imaginaire). Argan, the title character, believes he is chronically ill and wants his daughter Angelique to marry a doctor so he can use the medical services. The maid Toinette disguises herself as a doctor to fool Argan; she also has Argan play dead, revealing that his wife Beline does not truly love him. Angelique marries her lover Cleante (disguised as a music master, they sing a duet about shepherds). The final scene is a mock-Latin ballet ceremony ending with 'Vivat, vivat, vivat, vivat.' The 'dormitive principle' logical fallacy was coined in reference to a scene where characters try to explain how opium works. The quack doctor Thomas Diafoirus is in love with Angelique. Moliere collapsed and died hours after performing as Argan. Wikipedia

The School for Wives

Arnolphe (who assumes the name 'Monsieur de la Souche') tries to raise his ward Agnes in ignorance to make her his ideal wife, giving her eleven maxims of marriage. Moliere himself would have played Arnolphe. The play provoked such criticism that Moliere wrote the Critique of The School for Wives in response. Wycherley's The Country Wife draws on this play. The Bourgeois Gentleman

Also known as The Would-Be Gentleman (Le Bourgeois gentilhomme). Monsieur Jourdain, the son of a cloth merchant, aspires to join the aristocracy. The Music Master and Dancing Master stage ballet scenes for him. In a famous exchange, Jourdain is surprised to learn he has been speaking prose his entire life. At the end, Cleonte disguises himself as a Turkish prince to marry Jourdain's daughter Lucille, speaking Sabir rather than Turkish. The play includes musical interludes and is a comedie-ballet. General / Biographical

Moliere (born Jean-Baptiste Poquelin) was a 17th-century French comic playwright, actor, and co-founder of the Illustrious Theater Company. His troupe created the genre of the comedie-ballet. He frequently played the recurring character Sganarelle, who appears across many of his plays including as Don Juan's servant in The Feast of Stone. Moliere's troupe made a permanent home in the Hotel du Petit-Bourbon after a performance of Corneille's Nicomede before Louis XIV. Key members of his troupe included La Grange and Madeleine Bejart. Racine's first play La Thebaide was premiered by Moliere's troupe. Racine later quarreled with Moliere over the leading lady Therese Du Parc. Moliere collaborated with Corneille and Quinault on the comedy Psyche. His company eventually evolved into the Comedie-Francaise. French theatre awards are named for him (won by Yasmina Reza). He angered the Catholic Church but was often in favor with the Royal Court.

Summary of Facts

Moliere (born Jean-Baptiste Poquelin, 1622) is the most frequently asked-about French playwright in quizbowl, known primarily as a master of the comedy of manners. His most commonly clued play is Tartuffe, in which the scheming religious hypocrite Tartuffe insinuates himself into Orgon's household. The iconic scene of Orgon hiding under a table while Tartuffe seduces his wife appears in nearly every tossup. The play ends with a deus ex machina in which a royal officer arrests Tartuffe. Richard Wilbur's English translation, which preserves rhyming couplets, is also frequently mentioned. The Imaginary Invalid (Le malade imaginaire) is similarly prominent, primarily because Moliere himself died of a tubercular coughing fit hours after performing as the hypochondriac Argan — one of the most commonly clued facts about any literary figure. The maid Toinette's disguise as a doctor and the trick of having Argan play dead to expose his wife Beline's lack of love are recurring clues, as is the mock-Latin ballet ceremony that closes the play. The 'dormitive principle' logical fallacy originates from a scene in this play. The Misanthrope centers on Alceste, who insults Oronte's sonnet and is sued, while attempting to woo Celimene despite his hatred for humanity. The School for Wives features Arnolphe's misguided attempt to raise his ward Agnes in ignorance to make her an ideal wife; Moliere responded to criticism of this play by writing the Critique of The School for Wives. The Bourgeois Gentleman depicts Monsieur Jourdain, a cloth merchant's son who aspires to aristocracy and is famously surprised to learn he has been speaking prose all his life; Cleonte disguises himself as a Turkish prince to marry Jourdain's daughter Lucille. The Doctor in Spite of Himself features Sganarelle as a woodcutter beaten into becoming a doctor, who sardonically notes that doctors can 'butcher whomever they want' without consequences. The Affected Young Ladies satirizes the precieuses literary movement. Biographically, Moliere co-founded the Illustrious Theater Company, whose troupe created the comedie-ballet genre. He frequently played Sganarelle, a recurring character who appears across many of his plays. His troupe gained a permanent home at the Hotel du Petit-Bourbon after performing Corneille's Nicomede before Louis XIV. The troupe eventually became the Comedie-Francaise. He drew extensively on classical sources: The Miser from Plautus's Aulularia, The School for Husbands from Terence's Adelphoe, and Amphitryon from Plautus. He collaborated with Corneille and Quinault on Psyche. His death is said to have originated the theatrical superstition against wearing green onstage.
