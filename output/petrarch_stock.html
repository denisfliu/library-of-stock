← Home · View source questions · Make cards
14th-century Italian poet and humanist, called the 'father of Humanism.' Most commonly identified by Il Canzoniere (Songbook / Rime Sparse), a collection of 366 poems (mostly sonnets) addressed to Laura, whom he met in Avignon on Good Friday 1327. Also wrote the epic poem Africa (about Scipio Africanus), the Triumphs, Secretum (dialogues with Augustine), and the famous letter 'Ascent of Mont Ventoux.' Namesake of the Petrarchan sonnet (octave + sestet).

Summary of Facts

Petrarch (Francesco Petrarca) was a 14th-century Italian poet and humanist, called the 'father of Humanism' and one of the 'three crowns' of Italian literature alongside Dante and Boccaccio. He is the namesake of the Petrarchan sonnet (octave + sestet with a volta or 'turn').

His most famous work is Il Canzoniere (Songbook / Rime Sparse), a collection of 366 poems, mostly sonnets, addressed to Laura, whom he met in Avignon on Good Friday 1327. It opens 'You who hear the sound, in scattered rhymes' and is divided into 'In Life' and 'In Death' sections. The speaker declares 'Now I have become an old tale amongst all these people' and 'shame is the fruit of my vanities.' Love found him 'all weaponless' on 'that day when the sun's ray was darkened.' Other poems describe 'Clear, sweet fresh water,' a ship 'packed with forgetfulness' sailing between Charybdis and Scylla, and flowers. One poem features five stanzas where each ends with a quotation from a previous poet (Arnaut Daniel, Cavalcanti, Dante, Cino da Pistoia, then himself). His work was spread in England through translations by Thomas Wyatt and Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey ('Love, that doth reign and live within my thought'). Clément Marot translated six of his works. Pietro Bembo published the 1501 Aldine edition.

His famous letter 'The Ascent of Mont Ventoux' (from the Epistolae Familiares) describes climbing the mountain for pleasure, ignoring a shepherd's warnings, and having an epiphany when reading Augustine at the summit ('men wonder at the heights of mountains... but they consider themselves not'). His letter collections (Familiares and Seniles) were inspired by his rediscovery of Cicero's ad Atticum. His 'Letter to Posterity,' the last work in the Seniles, is hailed as the first Western autobiography since Augustine's Confessions. Another letter to Agapito Colonna is credited with coining the concept of the 'Dark Ages.' The Liber sine nomine (Book Without a Name) harshly criticized the Avignon Papacy.

Africa is an unfinished epic in Latin hexameter about Scipio Africanus in the Second Punic War, inspired by Livy's third decade. It contains 'Scipio's Dream' and was based on research for On Illustrious Men. Robert of Naples examined it before granting Petrarch the title of Poet Laureate of Rome. The Triumphs (Trionfi) is a collection of six allegorical poems depicting the conquering of Love, Chastity, Death, Fame, Time, and Eternity. Secretum contains imagined dialogues with Augustine.
