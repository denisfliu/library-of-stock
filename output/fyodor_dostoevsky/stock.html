Summary of Facts

Fyodor Dostoevsky (Fyodor Mikhailovich Dostoevsky) was a Russian novelist and proto-existentialist author. He was imprisoned after a mock execution for membership in the Fourierist Petrashevsky Circle and spent four years in a Siberian prison camp, which he fictionalized in The House of the Dead.

The Brothers Karamazov is Dostoevsky's final and most philosophically dense novel. The title brothers are Dmitri, Ivan, Alyosha (Alexei), and the illegitimate Smerdyakov. Ivan tells the parable of the Grand Inquisitor to Alyosha, in which Christ returns to 16th-century Seville and is imprisoned by the Inquisitor, who argues the Church follows the Devil's principles. Ivan declares "if God is dead, everything is permitted" and tells his brother "it's not God that I don't accept... only I most respectfully return him the ticket." In the chapter "Rebellion," Ivan describes a baby ripped apart by hunting dogs. Dmitri is tried for the murder of their father Fyodor Pavlovich, but the actual perpetrator is Smerdyakov. Evidence includes a letter on a tavern bill and 3,000 rubles from an envelope addressed to Grushenka. The Father's Adam's apple and hooked nose provoke Dmitri as he stares through a garden window clutching a brass pestle. Alyosha's mentor is the Elder Father Zosima. Grushenka tells the parable of the onion to Alyosha (a woman's one good deed of giving an onion). Alyosha dreams of Zosima in heaven during a passage about the Wedding at Cana. Father Paissy reads over Zosima's coffin. Freud's essay "Dostoevsky and Parricide" blamed a desire to commit patricide for Dostoevsky's epilepsy. Mikhail Bakhtin used Menippean satire to analyze the Grand Inquisitor in Problems of Dostoevsky's Poetics. The father compares an atheist to von Sohn and ponders "iron hooks" in hell.

Crime and Punishment follows Raskolnikov, who murders a pawnbroker and her sister with an axe. Foreshadowed by: a student in a bar denouncing the old woman as a parasite; Raskolnikov's dream of a mare being beaten to death by Mikolka. The inspector Porfiry questions him about his article "On Crime," which argues "extraordinary" people have the right to "transgress the law." Porfiry asks if he believes in the raising of Lazarus. Raskolnikov forces the prostitute Sonia to read him the Lazarus story. Sonia's father Marmeladov is killed by a carriage. Svidrigailov shoots himself with Dunya's revolver after telling a soldier he's headed for "America." Raskolnikov is exiled to Siberia; Sonia follows him. Dostoevsky's extensive reading of Dickens during his Siberian imprisonment inspired the character Nellie.

Notes from Underground opens "I am a sick man... I am a spiteful man." The unnamed narrator laments not being able to become an insect and describes the pleasure of a toothache. He ridicules Chernyshevsky's utopian Crystal Palace. In "Apropos of the Wet Snow," he behaves poorly at Zverkov's party, challenges Ferfichkin to a duel, and ridicules the prostitute Liza. He fantasizes about transferring Lake Como to Rome and hosting parties at the Villa Borghese after forcing the Pope to retire to Brazil. Bakhtin analyzed the "dialogized self-consciousness" of this novella. Rene Girard's study examines the irony of "I am alone and they are everyone."

The Idiot features the epileptic Prince Myshkin, who may have experienced Stendhal syndrome viewing Holbein's Dead Christ. During an anti-Catholic tirade at the Yepanchin estate, he gesticulates wildly and destroys a Chinese vase. Nastasya Filippovna throws 100,000 rubles into her fireplace to mock Ganya's avarice. Rogozhin murders Nastasya. Myshkin returns to a sanatorium in Switzerland. Aglaya is a love interest.

Demons (also translated as The Possessed or The Devils) is an allegory about nihilists led by Stavrogin, inspired by Sergey Nechayev. A censored chapter ("At Tikhon's") has Stavrogin confessing to a monk about raping an 11-year-old girl. Kirillov wants to kill himself as an act of self-determination. Pyotr Verkhovensky manipulates the radicals. Stavrogin hangs himself. Liza Tushin is killed by a mob during a fire. Virginia Woolf helped translate the censored chapter.

Other works include: Poor Folk (pauper Makar writes letters to his second-cousin), The Double (Golyadkin is thrown out of a party and carted to an asylum), White Nights (a lonely narrator develops feelings for Nastenka in St. Petersburg), "The Dream of a Ridiculous Man" (pulled from his grave and shown an Earth-like planet), "A Gentle Creature" (a husband drives his 16-year-old wife to suicide), "An Honest Thief" (riding breeches stolen by the drunken Yemelyan), "Bobok" (dead people in a cemetery discuss philosophy), "The Beggar Boy at Christ's Christmas Tree," and the unfinished Netochka Nezvanova.

Dostoevsky suffered from epilepsy (Geschwind syndrome is sometimes named for him). Nabokov disliked him, parodying him as Hermann Karlovich in Despair. Walter Kaufmann's anthology Existentialism from Dostoevsky to Sartre uses him as the starting point. Camus called him an "absurd creator" in The Myth of Sisyphus. Simone de Beauvoir aimed to disprove his statement about God. Bakhtin introduced "polyphony" and "unfinalizability" through his study of Dostoevsky. J.M. Coetzee's The Master of Petersburg fictionalizes him. Leonid Tsypkin's Summer in Baden-Baden recounts his wife Anna copying a novel about his roulette addiction. He called the concept "the man in man" (what social sciences cannot explain). Ironic process theory (the white bear problem) comes from his travel notes. Raymond Carver wrote a screenplay about him. Ursula K. Le Guin said "forgetting" him inspired Omelas.
