American modernist poet, best known for the poem "Poetry" which begins "I, too, dislike it" and contains the phrase "imaginary gardens with real toads in them." Poetry

Moore's most famous poem, frequently cited in quizbowl. Begins with the declaration "I, too, dislike it." Contains the famous phrase "imaginary gardens with real toads in them" and invokes "literalists of the imagination." Lists "the baseball fan" and "the statistician" among "things that are important beyond all this fiddle." Refuses to discriminate against "business documents and school-books." Notoriously cut from 29 lines to just 3 lines in her 1967 Complete Poems, prefaced by the epigraph "Omissions are not accidents." Also references "hands that can grasp, eyes that can dilate."

Marriage

A long poem collected in Observations. Describes marriage as "an institution... requiring public promises of one's intention." Includes a conversation where a woman says "I should like to be alone" and a man replies "why not be alone together?" Criticizes men as "monopolists of stars, garters, buttons, and other shining baubles / unfit to be the guardians of another person's happiness." Ends with "the book on the writing table / the hand in the breast-pocket" and quotes Daniel Webster about "enterprise."

General / Biographical

Moore was a major American modernist poet associated with Imagism and syllabic verse. She mentored Elizabeth Bishop and edited The Dial magazine in the 1920s. Known as a baseball fan (Brooklyn Dodgers) who compared baseball to writing. Collaborated with Muhammad Ali on a poem about Ernie Terrell. Proposed car names like "Mongoose Civique" and "Utopian Turtletop" for Ford. Translated La Fontaine's Fables. Used unaccented syllabic verse. Also wrote "To a Snail," "To a Steam Roller," "To a Chameleon," "The Mind Is an Enchanting Thing," "Nevertheless," "A Grave," "England," "O to Be a Dragon," and "Baseball and Writing."

Summary of Facts

Marianne Moore is one of the most frequently clued American modernist poets in quizbowl. Her signature poem is "Poetry," which begins with the iconic declaration "I, too, dislike it" and contains the famous phrase "imaginary gardens with real toads in them." The poem invokes "literalists of the imagination" and lists "the baseball fan," "the immovable critic," and "the statistician" among phenomena we do not admire because we do not understand them. Moore controversially cut the poem from 29 lines to just 3 in her 1967 Complete Poems, which opens with the epigraph "Omissions are not accidents."

Moore's animal poems are another major focus of clues. "The Fish" describes creatures that "wade through black jade" at a cliffside described as a "defiant edifice," and shares its title with a famous Elizabeth Bishop poem. "The Pangolin" praises the title "armored animal" as "a near artichoke with head and legs" and compares it to "Leonardo da Vinci's replica." "An Octopus" was inspired by a trip to Mount Rainier and begins "of ice. Deceptively reserved and flat," using the glacier as its central metaphor.

Moore's "The Steeple-Jack" is set in a coastal town with "eight stranded whales" where C. J. Poole puts up a "Danger" sign and gilds a "solid-pointed star, which stands for hope." The poem notes that "Durer would have seen a reason for living" there. Her long poem "Marriage" describes the institution as requiring "public promises of one's intention" and criticizes men as "monopolists of stars, garters, buttons."

Biographically, Moore mentored Elizabeth Bishop and edited The Dial magazine in the 1920s, where she rejected Hart Crane's work. She was a devoted baseball fan (Brooklyn Dodgers) and wrote "Baseball and Writing." She collaborated with Muhammad Ali on a poem about Ernie Terrell and proposed unusual car names for Ford. She was a master of unaccented syllabic verse and translated La Fontaine's Fables.
