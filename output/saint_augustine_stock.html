Early Christian philosopher and Church Father from Hippo, associated with Confessions, The City of God, and On Christian Doctrine. Known for his views on original sin, grace, predestination, just war theory, and his conversion from Manichaeism to Christianity.
Confessions (Autobiography)

Augustine's spiritual autobiography recounting his conversion from Manichaeism to Christianity, heavily featuring his mother Monica. The text is one of the most frequently clued works in philosophy and literature, appearing across both categories. It describes his youthful sins, his intellectual journey through skepticism and Neoplatonism, and his dramatic conversion in a garden.

The City of God

Augustine's major theological and political work, written in response to the sack of Rome by Alaric in 410 AD. It frames human history as a conflict between the sinful 'earthly city' and the heavenly 'City of God,' and is the origin of the term 'just war.' The work also engages with pagan philosophy, including a polemic against Varro.

Original Sin, Grace, and Pelagius

Augustine's theology of original sin and divine grace was developed largely in opposition to Pelagius, who denied heritable original sin and believed humans could free themselves from sin through their own efforts. Augustine's views on predestination, concupiscence, and irresistible grace profoundly influenced Western Christian theology, including Calvinism and Jansenism.

On Christian Doctrine

A four-book work on how to teach and interpret scripture. It divides the world into 'signs' and 'things,' outlines teaching styles (subdued, moderate, grand) inspired by Cicero, and quotes Ticonius's seven rules of interpretation. The work has been cited in discussions of rhetoric, religion as discipline, and semiotics.

Summary of Facts

Saint Augustine of Hippo is one of the most frequently clued philosophers and theologians in quizbowl. He is primarily identified as a 5th-century Church Father, Bishop of Hippo, and author of the Confessions and The City of God.

The Confessions is Augustine's spiritual autobiography, recounting his journey from Manichaeism to Christianity. The most iconic scene is his conversion in a garden, prompted by a child's voice chanting 'take up and read,' which leads him to read Romans 13. Other major episodes include stealing pears as a youth (a symbol of his sinfulness), his relationship with his mother Monica (who arranges a marriage for him and whose death he mourns), his plea 'make me chaste, but not yet,' and his friendship with Alypius. The Confessions has significant literary afterlife: T.S. Eliot's The Waste Land quotes 'to Carthage then I came' and 'thou pluckest me out'; Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations opens with a passage about language acquisition from the Confessions; and Petrarch read it during his ascent of Mont Ventoux, finding the passage about 'mighty waves of the sea' to be an epiphany. Augustine also appears as a character in Petrarch's Secretum dialogues.

The City of God, written in response to Alaric's sack of Rome in 410, frames human history as a conflict between the sinful earthly city and the heavenly City of God. It originated the concept of 'just war,' which was later expanded by Thomas Aquinas. The work contains a polemic against Varro, argues against Platonic natural theology, and includes the early cogito-like argument 'if I am mistaken, I exist.'

Augustine's theology of original sin and divine grace was developed in opposition to Pelagius, who denied heritable original sin. Augustine argued that the human capacity to avoid sin was lost after the Fall of Adam, that original sin is transmitted through concupiscence, and that divine grace (distinguished into 'prevenient' and 'operative' forms) is necessary for salvation. These views influenced Calvinism (especially the doctrine of irresistible grace) and Jansenism (Cornelius Jansen's major work was a treatise on Augustine's thought).

Augustine introduced to Christian philosophy the Neoplatonic idea that evil is the 'privation of good.' He also wrote On Christian Doctrine, a four-book work on scriptural interpretation that divides the world into 'signs' and 'things' and outlines teaching styles influenced by Cicero. Additional clued facts include: he pioneered the argument that God created the world instantaneously, he used the Delphic maxim 'know thyself' to argue for the immateriality of the mind, he was of probable Berber heritage, he was baptized alongside his son Adeodatus, and he died during a Vandal siege of Hippo. The Augustinian hypothesis regarding Gospel priority (Matthew first) is also occasionally clued.
