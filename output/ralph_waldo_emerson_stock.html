Ralph Waldo Emerson

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
American Transcendentalist essayist and poet. Best known for the essays "Self-Reliance" ("a foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds"), "Nature" ("transparent eye-ball" metaphor), "The Over-Soul," "The American Scholar" ("Man Thinking"), and the "Divinity School Address" (attacking Unitarianism). Major poems include "Brahma," "The Rhodora," "Hamatreya," and "Concord Hymn" ("shot heard round the world"). Close friendship with Thoreau.

Summary of Facts

Ralph Waldo Emerson is an American Transcendentalist essayist and poet, the central figure of the Transcendentalist movement based in Concord, Massachusetts. His most frequently clued essay is "Self-Reliance," which argues "Whoso would be a man must be a nonconformist," calls "a foolish consistency the hobgoblin of little minds," and proposes "to be great is to be misunderstood." His 1836 essay "Nature" lays the foundation for Transcendentalism with its "transparent eye-ball" metaphor ("I am nothing; I see all") and the claim "Nature always wears the colors of the spirit."

His poems include "Brahma" (inspired by the Bhagavad Gita, declaring "I am the doubter and the doubt" and instructing the "meek lover of the good" to "find me, and turn thy back on heaven"), "Hamatreya" (drawn from the Vishnu Purana, containing the "Earth-Song" asking "How am I theirs, / If they cannot hold me?"), "Concord Hymn" (set "by the rude bridge that arched the flood" where "embattled farmers... fired the shot heard round the world"), and "The Rhodora" ("beauty is its own excuse for Being").

The "Divinity School Address" (1838) attacked Unitarianism at Harvard, arguing that "Historical Christianity" fails to teach the "doctrine of the soul." "The American Scholar" proposes the "Man Thinking" ideal over the "mere thinker." "The Poet" defines genius as "the activity which repairs the decay of things" and inspired Whitman to write Leaves of Grass. "Experience" opens with grief at the death of his son Waldo. Representative Men contains essays on Shakespeare, Goethe, and other "great men."

Emerson provided land for Thoreau's Walden cabin and wrote a eulogy lamenting that "instead of engineering for his entire country, he was the captain of a huckleberry-party." His letter to Whitman called Leaves of Grass "the most extraordinary piece of wit and wisdom that America has yet contributed." He was introduced to Vedic thought by his aunt Mary Moody Emerson, and his concept of "The Over-Soul" was inspired by the Hindu paramatman. Charles Ives's Concord Sonata was inspired by Emerson and fellow Transcendentalists. Stanley Cavell named a form of "perfectionism" after him.
