German opera composer (1714–1787), best known for his 'reform operas' which rejected Baroque convention in favor of drama and simplicity. Collaborated with librettist Ranieri de' Calzabigi on Orfeo ed Euridice (1762), Alceste (1767), and Paride ed Elena. The preface to Alceste laid out reform principles (no da capo arias, no secco recitatives, syllabic text-setting). Had a famous Paris rivalry with the Neapolitan Niccolò Piccinni. Was the music teacher of Marie Antoinette and the main operatic model for Berlioz.
General / Biographical

German opera composer who led the 18th-century 'reform opera' movement, working to prioritize drama over vocal display. Collaborated with librettist Ranieri de' Calzabigi. Music teacher of Marie Antoinette. Wagner cited him as a predecessor of the Gesamtkunstwerk. Main operatic model for Hector Berlioz. Summary of Facts

Christoph Willibald Gluck (1714–1787) was a German opera composer best known for leading the 18th-century 'reform opera' movement. Working with librettist Ranieri de' Calzabigi, he sought to make opera more dramatic and natural by eliminating the artificial conventions of Baroque opera seria: da capo arias, secco recitatives (speech-like passages with only keyboard accompaniment), and elaborate melismatic vocal display. His reform principles were set out in the preface to Alceste (1767), probably co-authored with Calzabigi, which decried 'tiresome ritornellos' and argued that an overture should 'apprise the spectator of the action to be represented.' Wagner later cited Gluck as a predecessor of his own idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk.

Gluck was the music teacher of Marie Antoinette and composed Iphigénie en Aulide for the French court. Early in his career, Handel insulted him by saying his cook knew more counterpoint. He became the principal operatic model for Hector Berlioz, whose Treatise on Instrumentation draws primarily on Gluck's operas. Berlioz rearranged Orfeo ed Euridice for an 1859 production with the mezzo-soprano Pauline Viardot in the lead male role. Gluck suffered a stroke later in life and handed the commission for Les Danaïdes to his protégé Antonio Salieri. The poor reception of Echo et Narcisse caused him to retire to Vienna.

Orfeo ed Euridice (1762) was his first 'reform opera.' The lead male role was sung by a castrato in the Vienna premiere; the revised French version (Orphée et Eurydice, 1774) added the 'Dance of the Blessed Spirits.' The most famous aria, 'Che farò senza Euridice,' is a rondo sung when Orfeo thinks Euridice is lost; the god Amore (Cupid) eventually brings her back to life, after first talking Orfeo out of suicide in the aria 'Tendre amour.' In Act II, a dancer removes a scarf cover to reveal Elysium; the aria 'Che puro ciel' is sung by the title character on arriving there.

Alceste (1767) centers on the Queen of Thessaly, who sings 'Divinités du Styx' in Act I, resolving to sacrifice herself to save her husband Admète. Berlioz argued that Mozart plagiarized the Commendatore's music in Don Giovanni from this opera. Jean-Jacques Rousseau criticized the Italian version as monotonous (putting all best arias in Act I); the French version addressed this. Mozart wrote an insert aria 'Popoli di Tessaglia!' for Alceste for the singer Aloysia Weber.

Iphigénie en Tauride (1779) was Gluck's most developed reform opera. It opens without a formal overture — instead, a short calm passage leads directly into a storm. In one scene, the orchestral accompaniment reveals that a character is lying. The Paris Opera commissioned both Gluck and Niccolò Piccinni to write rival settings of the same libretto (the rivalry was fueled by a political split between the king's mistress and the dauphine), dividing Parisian operagoers into 'Gluckists' and Piccinni fans. The opera features a 'Dance of the Scythians' and includes the role of the Scythian king Thoas. Gluck also wrote a German version for Grand Duke Paul's visit.

Paride ed Elena (the third reform opera with Calzabigi) features the aria 'O del mio dolce ardor,' an unusual minor-key love song long misattributed to Alessandro Stradella, and the plot twist that Erasto is Cupid in disguise. It was the vehicle for Janet Baker's farewell to the operatic stage.
