Learning from Las Vegas (1972) →

Co-written with husband Robert Venturi and Steven Izenour (1972). Grew out of a Yale studio studying A&P parking lots and the Las Vegas commercial Strip. Introduces the typology of 'duck' (building whose form is symbolic of its purpose — named for the Big Duck on Long Island) versus 'decorated shed' (functionally built structure using vivid signage). Contrasts these with reference to billboards along the Strip, examined as a means of attracting consumer attention. Uses the La Tourette Monastery as an example and traces its influences on both Neiman Marcus and Levittowns; contains a section called 'Space as God.' Celebrates roadside decor and vernacular architecture against modernist orthodoxy — Venturi's quip 'less is a bore' countered Mies van der Rohe's 'less is more.' Led to the emergence of two architectural factions: the 'Grays' (Venturi/Scott Brown postmodernists) and the 'Whites' (pure modernists like Richard Meier and Peter Eisenman); these colorful names were popularized by Robert Stern. Michael Graves defected from the Whites to the Grays and became a leading postmodernist; Philip Johnson's defection from Whites to Grays was the highest-profile, marked by the ornamental top on his AT&T Building. Aron Vinegar's critical work 'I am a Monument' is an examination of the book. Scott Brown's own photographs appear in the book.

'Room at the Top?' / Pritzker Prize controversy

Scott Brown published the essay 'Room at the Top? Sexism and the Star System in Architecture,' which examines sexism in the profession and blames the 'starchitect' phenomenon (a portmanteau for celebrity architects like Frank Gehry) for obscuring female collaborators' contributions. The essay credits Budd Schulberg's idea of 'Star Quality' and compares Venturi to Howard Roark in the context of the star system. She accused the system of crediting her husband Robert Venturi for coining 'duck building' even though she was equally responsible. The essay noted that 'QP' (a colleague) invited only male friends to the Century Club. In 1991, when Venturi alone was awarded the Pritzker Architecture Prize, Scott Brown boycotted the ceremony in protest; she never received the award.

Summary of Facts

Denise Scott Brown (b. 1931, South Africa) is an American architect and urban theorist who has worked closely with her husband Robert Venturi as part of the firm Venturi, Scott Brown and Associates. She is best known for co-authoring Learning from Las Vegas (1972) with Venturi and Steven Izenour, one of the defining texts of postmodern architecture. The book grew out of a Yale studio that studied A&P parking lots and the commercial Strip of Las Vegas. It introduced the typology of 'duck' (a building whose form represents its purpose — named for the Big Duck on Long Island) versus 'decorated shed' (a functionally built structure that uses vivid signage), and celebrated roadside vernacular architecture against modernist orthodoxy. Billboards along the Strip were examined as a means of attracting consumer attention. The La Tourette Monastery was used as a comparative example and the book traced its influences on both Neiman Marcus and Levittowns; a section is called 'Space as God.' Scott Brown's own photographs appear in the book. The book's publication triggered the 'Whites vs. Grays' debate in architecture — names popularized by Robert Stern — with the Grays (led by Venturi and Scott Brown) championing postmodern ornamentation and the Whites (Peter Eisenman, Richard Meier) maintaining pure modernism. Michael Graves defected from the Whites to the Grays. The highest-profile defection was Philip Johnson, whose AT&T Building featured a postmodernist ornamental top. Venturi's quip that 'less is a bore' — directed at Mies van der Rohe's 'less is more' — was central to the book's polemic. Aron Vinegar's 'I am a Monument' is a critical examination of the book.

Scott Brown is also known for her essay 'Room at the Top? Sexism and the Star System in Architecture,' which examines how the 'starchitect' phenomenon (a portmanteau for popular architects like Frank Gehry) obscures the contributions of female collaborators. The essay cites Budd Schulberg's idea of 'Star Quality' and compares Venturi to Howard Roark in the context of the star system. It notes that a colleague referred to as 'QP' invited only male friends to the Century Club, and critiques how Venturi was credited for coining the term 'duck building.' The controversy reached its peak in 1991 when the Pritzker Architecture Prize — the highest honor in architecture — was awarded solely to Venturi; Scott Brown boycotted the ceremony in protest and has never received the prize.

In the 1960s, Scott Brown ran studios approaching urban architecture as a combination of form, forces, and function. Her built work includes the Sainsbury Wing of the National Gallery in London and the Seattle Art Museum, both realized in collaboration with Venturi. She designed campus plans for Williams College, Bryn Mawr College, and the University of Michigan. Both she and Venturi were students of Louis Kahn in Philadelphia, and helped revive the reputation of Victorian architect Frank Furness.
