General / Biographical

Frederick Law Olmsted (1822–1903) was a landscape architect, author, conservationist, and public servant. Before his design career he was a merchant, apprentice seaman, experimental farmer, and goldmine manager. He was co-founder of The Nation magazine. His mentor Andrew Jackson Downing introduced him to partner Calvert Vaux. He served as chairman of the first Yosemite Commission. He authored The Cotton Kingdom (a.k.a. Journeys and Explorations in the Cotton Kingdom), an account of antebellum Southern poverty arguing that slavery made the South economically inefficient. Near the end of his life he was committed to McLean Hospital in Waverly, Massachusetts—whose grounds he had himself designed.

Summary of Facts

Frederick Law Olmsted (1822–1903) was the founding figure of American landscape architecture, best known for co-designing Central Park in Manhattan with his English-born partner Calvert Vaux. Their winning submission, the Greensward Plan, beat out a design by engineer Ebert Ludovicus Viele. The park's landmarks include the Bethesda Fountain, the Sheep Meadow, the Bow Bridge, the Great Lawn, and a zoo. It is the setting of Edward Albee's play The Zoo Story, and Robert Smithson's essay 'Dialectical Landscape' centers on Olmsted's Greensward Plan. Olmsted and Vaux also co-designed Prospect Park in Brooklyn.

Olmsted's Boston work produced the Emerald Necklace, a chain of parks and parkways linking several green spaces across the city. For the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago, he designed the grounds including the Midway Plaisance (an entertainment corridor) and a Wooded Island at its center, though he originally wanted to cut a canal through the Midway. He also designed the campus of Stanford University, the University of Chicago campus, the U.S. Capitol Grounds, the planned city of Riverside, Illinois, and the Buffalo park system and the grounds of the Buffalo State Hospital alongside his friend H.H. Richardson.

Olmsted designed the landscape for the Biltmore Estate in North Carolina, the buildings of which were by Richard Morris Hunt. His care for the estate's surrounding forests inadvertently helped lay the groundwork for American forestry, and the nation's first forest school was located there.

Before his design career, Olmsted had been a merchant, apprentice seaman, experimental farmer, and goldmine manager. He was co-founder of The Nation magazine and chairman of the first Yosemite Commission. His mentor, Andrew Jackson Downing (who died in a steamboat explosion on the Hudson River), introduced him to Calvert Vaux. Olmsted also wrote The Cotton Kingdom (formally Journeys and Explorations in the Cotton Kingdom), arguing that slavery made the antebellum South economically inefficient. Near the end of his life he was committed to McLean Hospital in Waverly, Massachusetts—whose grounds he had himself designed.
