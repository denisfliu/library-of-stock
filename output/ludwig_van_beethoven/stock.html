German composer (1770–1827) who spanned and defined the transition from the Classical to Romantic era. Best known for his nine symphonies (especially the 5th, 6th 'Pastoral,' and 9th 'Choral'), five piano concertos (the 5th is the 'Emperor'), thirty-two piano sonatas (including 'Moonlight,' 'Pathetique,' 'Appassionata,' 'Hammerklavier'), the Violin Concerto, the opera Fidelio, the War Requiem, String Quartets (including the Grosse Fuge and the 'Razumovsky' quartets), and a Missa solemnis. Wrote the song cycle An die ferne Geliebte. Famous for composing while deaf. The Beethoven Frieze by Gustav Klimt is in the Vienna Secession Building.
General / Biographical

German composer (1770–1827), born in Bonn, worked in Vienna. Studied with Haydn among others. Composed while progressively losing hearing; wrote his 9th Symphony while deaf. Famous for tearing up his third symphony's dedication page to Napoleon. Originally dedicated the Kreutzer Sonata to George Bridgetower (a biracial British violinist who premiered it), then re-dedicated it to Rodolphe Kreutzer after Bridgetower insulted a friend. His Opus 1 consists of three piano trios dedicated to Prince Lichnowsky. He was an early adopter of the metronome (built by Johann Nepomuk Maelzel), leading to confusingly high tempo markings. Analysis of his hair revealed elevated lead levels (likely from lead acetate in cheap wine) and Hepatitis B infection. The Beethoven Frieze by Gustav Klimt (covering three walls in the Vienna Secession Building) was inspired by his music; its central section features the giant Typhoeus and the Gorgon sisters.

Symphony No. 3 (Eroica)

Symphony No. 3 in E-flat major, Op. 55. Often cited as the beginning of Beethoven's middle period. Originally dedicated to Napoleon; the dedication page was torn up. Opens with two isolated E-flat major forte chords; cellos then play eight notes outlining an E-flat major triad. Violins enter in bar 7 with syncopated G's floating above a sustained C-sharp resolving to D. The second movement is an unusually long funeral march in C minor. The finale is a set of variations on a theme from Beethoven's The Creatures of Prometheus. At rehearsal, Ferdinand Ries mistakenly berated a horn player for starting the recapitulation early. Tan Dun wrote an 'Internet Symphony' inspired by it using hubcaps and brake drums. Donald Tovey describes a 'vision of dry bones' in the finale. Wikipedia

Symphony No. 5 in C minor

Symphony No. 5 in C minor, Op. 67. First movement (Allegro con brio) opens with the famous four-note 'fate' motif: 'G, G, G, long E-flat.' The first oboe plays an unexpected adagio cadenza in the recapitulation at a half cadence ending on G. C-minor scherzo includes a Mannheim rocket in low strings (pianissimo); a reprise of the scherzo interrupts the joyous C-major finale (E.M. Forster described this as a return of 'the goblins'). The first movement motif inspired Tchaikovsky's 4th Symphony. Winds and strings trade pianissimo B-diminished-seventh chords just before the retransition. Peter Schickele and Gifford Nielsen commentated over the first movement as if it were a football game in 'New Horizons in Music Appreciation.' Wikipedia

Symphony No. 6 (Pastoral)

Symphony No. 6 in F major, Op. 68, nicknamed 'Pastoral.' Five movements, with programmatic titles. A flute, oboe, and two clarinets imitate bird calls in the 'Scene by the brook.' The fourth movement depicts a storm: cellos and basses sustain a low tremolo; the piccolo and timpani appear for the first time in the work. The storm gives way to a shepherd's Thanksgiving hymn. Symphony No. 9 (Choral)

Symphony No. 9 in D minor, Op. 125 ('Choral'). Beethoven was deaf at the premiere and had to be turned to face the audience's applause. Finale features the 'Ode to Joy' theme adapted from Friedrich Schiller's poem praising the 'daughter of Elysium.' The finale opens with a 'terror fanfare' followed by a cello/bass recitative that 'tries out' and rejects previous themes. A 100-bar section imitates janissary bands (Turkish March), with prominent piccolo, triangle, and tenor solo. Third movement modulates up a fifth into 12/8 after a French horn solo. Opening: winds enter one-by-one as violins play descending fifths and fourths. Influenced Bruckner's unfinished symphony in D minor. Premiered alongside the Missa solemnis. The Choral Fantasy (Op. 80) is a precursor. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Ludwig van Beethoven (1770–1827) was a German composer who spanned the transition from the Classical to Romantic era, working primarily in Vienna. He composed nine symphonies, five piano concertos, thirty-two piano sonatas, a Violin Concerto, seventeen String Quartets, one opera (Fidelio), and a Missa solemnis, among many other works. He famously composed while progressively losing his hearing, writing his Ninth Symphony while completely deaf — he had to be turned to face the audience's applause at the premiere.

His Third Symphony ('Eroica,' E-flat major, Op. 55) is often cited as the beginning of his middle period. Originally dedicated to Napoleon, the dedication was furiously torn up. It opens with two isolated E-flat forte chords; in the seventh bar, violins enter with syncopated G's above a sustained C-sharp resolving to D. The second movement is an unusually long C-minor funeral march, and the finale is a set of variations on a theme from his ballet The Creatures of Prometheus. Symphony No. 5 (C minor, Op. 67) opens with the famous four-note 'fate knocks at the door' motif (G, G, G, long E-flat). At the recapitulation, the first oboe plays an unexpected adagio cadenza on a half cadence. The C-minor scherzo opens with a pianissimo Mannheim rocket; a quiet pizzicato reprise of the scherzo interrupts the joyous C-major finale — E.M. Forster described this as a return of 'the goblins.' The motif inspired Tchaikovsky's Fourth Symphony. Symphony No. 6 ('Pastoral,' F major, Op. 68) has five programmatic movements, including bird calls from flute, oboe, and clarinets in the 'Scene by the brook,' and a storm movement (cellos and basses sustain a low tremolo; piccolo and timpani appear for the first time) giving way to a Thanksgiving hymn. Symphony No. 7 (A major, Op. 92) was called 'the apotheosis of dance' by Wagner; its second movement (marked Allegretto but the symphony's slowest) features a famous long-short-short-long-long ostinato in A minor; it borrows from Beethoven's 12 Irish Songs. Symphony No. 9 (D minor, Op. 125, 'Choral') has a finale featuring the 'Ode to Joy' from Schiller's poem. The finale opens with a 'terror fanfare' followed by a cello/bass recitative that rejects previous themes; a 100-bar Turkish March section imitates janissary bands with piccolo, triangle, and a tenor solo.

Key piano sonatas: the Moonlight Sonata (No. 14, C-sharp minor, Op. 27 No. 2) opens with constant triplet arpeggios; the instruction requires the pedal to be held down constantly; dedicated to student Julie Guicciardi; nicknamed by Ludwig Rellstab. The Pathetique Sonata (No. 8, C minor, Op. 13) features a slow Grave introduction interrupted again in the development section; its Adagio cantabile in A-flat inspired Elgar's 'Nimrod.' The Appassionata (No. 23, F minor) begins with arpeggios on the tonic and G-flat Neapolitan chord. The Hammerklavier (No. 29, B-flat major) is titled for the German name of the piano and ends with a three-voice Allegro risoluto fugue. His final sonata (No. 32, C minor) ends with an arietta with variations in 9/16 time.

The Violin Concerto (D major, Op. 61) opens with five timpani taps and was dedicated to Franz Clement; characterized as 'the greatest, most uncompromising' by Joachim. The Emperor Concerto (Piano Concerto No. 5, E-flat major) opens with a tutti chord immediately followed by arpeggios from the soloist. His String Quartets include the Grosse Fuge (originally the finale of String Quartet No. 13, Op. 130) and three Razumovsky quartets; the final string quartet (No. 16, F major, Op. 135) contains 'The Difficult Decision' movement with the inscription 'Must it be? It must be!'

Beethoven originally dedicated the Kreutzer Sonata to the biracial British violinist George Bridgetower, who premiered it, then re-dedicated it to Rodolphe Kreutzer after a quarrel. His Opus 1 consists of three piano trios for Prince Lichnowsky; the Archduke Trio was for Archduke Rudolf. He wrote An die ferne Geliebte (often called the first song cycle, six poems by Alois Isidor Jetteles), quoted in Schumann's Fantasy in C. The Beethoven Frieze by Gustav Klimt covers three walls of the Vienna Secession Building. His Missa solemnis in D major was dedicated to Archduke Rudolf and premiered at the same concert as the Ninth Symphony. Analysis of his hair revealed elevated lead levels (likely from lead acetate in cheap wine) and Hepatitis B.
