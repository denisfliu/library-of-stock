Summary of Facts

Nikolai Gogol was a Ukrainian-born Russian author known for satirical plays, supernatural folk tales, and stories set in St. Petersburg. He burned the second volume of his only novel, Dead Souls, shortly before dying of self-starvation after a priest prescribed an ascetic lifestyle to rid him of 'inner filth.' Vissarion Belinsky coined the term 'Russian soul' after reading Dead Souls, and later wrote a harsh letter denouncing Gogol's conservative mysticism in Selected Passages from Correspondence with My Friends. Fyodor Dostoevsky quipped 'We all come out from' Gogol's 'The Overcoat,' and Belinsky called Dostoevsky the 'new Gogol.' Simon Karlinsky wrote The Sexual Labyrinth of Nikolai Gogol, analyzing his repressed sexuality. Alexander Pushkin praised Gogol as 'so unusual...I am unable to get over it.' He is considered possibly gay. Boris Eikhenbaum coined the concept of 'skaz' (Russian oral narrative form) in an essay about 'The Overcoat.'

'The Overcoat' is Gogol's most frequently clued story. The 'certain clerk' Akaky Akakievich obsessively saves money and uses his Christmas bonus to have the one-eyed tailor Petrovich make a new overcoat. After being robbed of it, he is berated by a 'Person of Consequence' whose system is 'Strictness, strictness, and always strictness!' He develops a fatal fever and dies. His ghost then haunts the streets of St. Petersburg, stealing coats from passersby, including from the 'Person of Consequence' at the Kalinkin Bridge. A watchman sees a phantom with an 'enormous mustache' disappear across a bridge. The story begins by claiming 'There is nothing more irritable than departments.' Eikhenbaum called its narrator 'a performer, almost a comedian.'

The Inspector General (Revizor) is Gogol's most famous play. Members of a provincial town offer bribes to the naive clerk Khlestakov, whom they mistake for an incognito government inspector. The servant Osip helps trick the Governor (Mayor Anton). The comic squires Bobchinsky and Dobchinsky appear. Khlestakov flirts with Anton's wife and daughter, and claims to be the author of The Marriage of Figaro. A Postmaster reads aloud a letter brutally mocking everyone. The play ends with a 'silent scene': all characters freeze for a minute and a half after a gendarme announces the real inspector's arrival. The angry man shouts 'What are you laughing about? You are laughing at yourselves!' Dostoevsky played the postmaster in a charity performance. Gogol's dialogue 'Leaving the Theater After the Presentation of a New Comedy' depicts the audience at its premiere. Vsevolod Meyerhold directed a landmark 1926 production where actors emerged from 11 coffin-like doors during a bribe scene.

Dead Souls features the trickster Chichikov traveling the countryside collecting the title items (rights to deceased serfs) to sell for money. He gambles with the cheating Nozdryov. The novel's first part ends with an address to 'O Rus'' comparing its 'headlong motion' to a troika. The second volume (which Gogol burned) ends mid-sentence with an anti-corruption speech. Chichikov is arrested for attempting to forge the will of Khlobuyev's aunt. Chichikov entices landowners with tax relief.

Evenings on a Farm Near Dikanka is Gogol's first collection, narrated by the beekeeper Rudy Panko and drawn from his Ukrainian upbringing in Poltava. Its preface mentions a man who pretends to be cultured but can't Remember the word for 'rake.' Stories include 'St. John's Eve' (a witch instructs Petro to decapitate a boy to obtain treasure beneath a blooming red fern), 'Christmas Eve' (the blacksmith Vakula woos the vain Oksana by getting the Tsarina's slippers; the devil hides in coal sacks), 'The Fair at Sorochyntsi' (a cursed jacket heralded by a pig's head), 'May Night' (Levko identifies a witch from a group of drowned maidens), and 'The Terrible Vengeance' (a bard sings of a knight who cursed his brother's descendants).

'Diary of a Madman' chronicles the civil servant Poprishchin's descent into insanity. He overhears the dogs Meggy and Fidel gossiping, reads about Isabella II's accession to Spain, and declares himself King of Spain (Ferdinand VII). Lu Xun admired Gogol and wrote a similarly titled story; Lu Xun translated Dead Souls into Chinese.

'The Nose' features Major Kovalyov, whose nose is found in a loaf of bread by the barber Ivan Yakovlevich. The nose is seen wearing a gold-braided uniform and heading to Riga on a stagecoach. It is thrown off St. Isaac's Bridge wrapped in cloth.

'Nevsky Prospect' contrasts the painter Piskarev, who takes opium and slits his throat after learning the woman he followed was a prostitute, with Lieutenant Pirogov, who followed a blonde married woman. The cobbler Hoffmann helps beat up Pirogov for dancing with the wife of the tinsmith Schiller.

'Viy' is a horror story in which the philosophy student Khoma is forced to chant prayers for three nights over the coffin of a witch (a Cossack chief's daughter). The title creature is a gnome-like being with elongated eyelids that touch the floor.

'The Portrait' features a painter who buys opium from a Persian, abandons his style after finding money in a cursed portrait of a moneylender, and later becomes a monk painting religious scenes with demonic eyes.

Taras Bulba is a Cossack novella in which the title character tells his son Andriy 'I gave you life, I will also kill you!' Andriy is killed for bringing bread to a Polish girl. Taras is tied to a tree and burned alive. Gogol revised the 1842 edition to remove Ukrainian themes. It inspired a symphonic rhapsody by Janacek and an opera by Lysenko.

Marriage features Kochkaryov's incompetent matchmaking; Podkolyosin jumps out a window to avoid the title event. A man named Yaichnitsa (meaning 'omelette') courts Agafya. In The Gamblers, a deck of cards is called 'Adelaida Ivanovna.' Tommaso Landolfi wrote the bizarre story 'Gogol's Wife,' in which a fictional Gogol marries a balloon named Caracas. Laughable Loves by Kundera was not related.

Gogol's works inspired the concept of skaz (oral narrative form), coined by Eikhenbaum. His last letter to his priest asked if they could 'exchange overcoats.'
