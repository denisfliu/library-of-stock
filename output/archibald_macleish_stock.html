"Ars Poetica"

MacLeish's most frequently clued poem, appearing in virtually every tossup. Inspired by Horace, it famously declares 'A poem should not mean / But be.' Other key lines include 'palpable and mute / as a globed fruit,' 'motionless in time / As the moon climbs,' 'silent as the sleeve-worn stone / Of casement ledges,' 'wordless / as the flight of birds,' 'equal to, not true,' and 'Dumb / as old medallions to the thumb.' The poem is often clued alongside Marianne Moore's 'Poetry' in questions about poetry as a concept.

J.B.

A verse drama retelling the biblical tale of Job. Nickles and Mr. Zuss put on masks representing Satan and God and make a bet about how the title banker will react when they ruin his life. The title character J.B. is a New York banker. His children Jonathan and Mary die when their driver crashes into a bridge abutment. An explosion destroys his bank. Eliphaz, Bildad, and Zophar appear as three comforters. J.B. rejects the comfort of History, Science, and Religion. The play's premiere gave Orson Welles his first leading role in America in MacLeish's earlier play Panic.

"You, Andrew Marvell"

A poem that responds to Marvell's 'To His Coy Mistress,' describing the 'always coming on / the always rising of the night' as it sweeps across world cities. It lists Ecbatan, Kermanshah, Baghdad, Palmyra, Lebanon, Crete, and Spain. Addressed to a figure 'face down beneath the sun,' the poem ends with 'the shadow of the night comes on.' It asserts 'For all the history of grief,' there is 'an empty doorway and a maple leaf.'

Other Works and Biographical

MacLeish was appointed Librarian of Congress by Franklin Roosevelt. He wrote 'Voyage to the Moon' commissioned by the New York Times for the Apollo 11 front page. His play The Fall of the City features a woman who rises from the dead to prophesy 'masterless men will take a master,' and the Conqueror is an empty suit of armor. 'The End of the World' describes Vasserot the armless ambidextrian 'lighting / A match between his great and second toe.' 'Invocation to the Social Muse' declares 'There is nothing worse for our trade than to be in style.' The long poem The Happy Marriage contains 'beauty is that Medusa's head.' His play Panic (set during the 1933 bank panic) gave Orson Welles his first American leading role. He judged the Yale Series of Younger Poets between Stephen Vincent Benet and W. H. Auden.

Summary of Facts

Archibald MacLeish is an American poet best known in quizbowl for his poem 'Ars Poetica,' which appears in virtually every tossup. The poem declares 'A poem should not mean / But be' and compares poetry to being 'palpable and mute / as a globed fruit,' 'motionless in time / As the moon climbs,' and 'silent as the sleeve-worn stone / Of casement ledges.' It is frequently clued alongside Marianne Moore's 'Poetry' in questions about the nature of poetry.

His verse drama J.B. is the second most important work, retelling the biblical story of Job. Nickles and Mr. Zuss represent Satan and God, and the title banker J.B.'s life is systematically destroyed. His children die in a car crash and his bank is blown up. The three comforters Eliphaz, Bildad, and Zophar appear.

'You, Andrew Marvell' responds to Marvell's 'To His Coy Mistress,' describing night sweeping across the globe through cities like Ecbatan, Kermanshah, Baghdad, and Palmyra. It addresses a figure 'face down beneath the sun.'

Biographically, MacLeish was appointed Librarian of Congress by FDR and wrote 'Voyage to the Moon' for the New York Times to accompany the Apollo 11 landing story. His play The Fall of the City features a prophecy about 'masterless men' and a Conqueror who is an empty suit of armor. 'The End of the World' describes circus performer Vasserot. He judged the Yale Series of Younger Poets between Benet and Auden.
