Ancient Athenian philosopher, known as the teacher of Plato. Associated with the Socratic method (elenchus), the claim to know nothing, the daimonion (inner divine voice), and his trial and execution for impiety and corrupting the youth. Featured as the central figure in Plato's dialogues including the Apology, Phaedo, Crito, Symposium, and Theaetetus.
The Apology and Trial of Socrates

The Apology is by far the most frequently clued work featuring Socrates, depicting his defense speech at trial. Clues cover his charges of impiety and corrupting the youth brought by Meletus, Anytus, and Lycon. Key moments include the oracle of Delphi declaring no man wiser than Socrates, his comparison of himself to a gadfly stinging Athens, his elenchus of Meletus using the horse-trainer analogy, and his claim that the unexamined life is not worth living. The trial resulted in his death sentence by hemlock. The Crito and Phaedo are set after the trial.

The Daimonion (Divine Sign)

Socrates's mysterious inner voice or divine sign, called the daimonion, is a frequently clued concept. It only forbids actions, never commands. Nietzsche in The Birth of Tragedy called it a 'monstrous defect' where 'instinct becomes the critic and consciousness becomes the creator.' Meletus referred to it as a 'new divinity' among the charges. Kierkegaard's master's thesis also discusses the difficulty of interpreting this entity. Apuleius wrote a treatise about it that Augustine rebutted in The City of God.

Kierkegaard and The Concept of Irony →

Kierkegaard's 1841 master's thesis, On the Concept of Irony with Continual Reference to Socrates, is one of the most frequently clued connections to Socrates. Kierkegaard defined Socratic irony as 'infinite absolute negativity' and argued Socrates facilitated the birth of subjectivity. Kierkegaard called Socrates 'the unity of the comic and the tragic' and argued against Hegel's view that Socrates was merely a 'founder of morality,' instead describing him as a 'magnificent pause in the course of history.'

Nietzsche on Socrates

Nietzsche is one of the most frequently clued critics of Socrates. In The Birth of Tragedy, Nietzsche argues that a 'Socratic' mindset displaced both the Apollonian and Dionysian, ending the golden age of Greek tragedy. In Twilight of the Idols, the section 'The Problem of Socrates' discusses Socrates as a 'symptom of decline' who made 'a tyrant out of reason.' Nietzsche cites Socrates's 'ugliness' and claims the wisest men believe life is no good.

The Socratic Method (Elenchus)

The Socratic method, also called elenchus, involves asking a series of questions to clarify the subject of discussion, often leading to aporia (impasse or puzzlement). Socrates likens himself to a midwife because he 'cannot himself give birth to wisdom' but helps others arrive at truth. The method appears across dialogues like the Theaetetus, Meno, and Gorgias. Gregory Vlastos discussed the constructivist interpretation; Peter Geach criticized the assumption that one cannot know instances without providing a definition.

Symposium and Diotima

In Plato's Symposium, Socrates recounts teachings from Diotima of Mantinea about love as a 'ladder' ascending through stages of beauty toward the Form of Beauty. Alcibiades arrives drunkenly and compares Socrates to busts of Silenus and Marsyas, containing 'divine, golden images.' Socrates compares trading beauty to Diomedes trading bronze for gold. The dialogue features speeches praising Eros, including Aristophanes's myth of original double humans.

Phaedo and Crito

The Phaedo depicts Socrates's last hours in prison, discussing the immortality of the soul with Cebes and Simmias before drinking hemlock. The dialogue features the affinity argument and the argument from recollection. In the Crito, Socrates refuses Crito's plan to escape prison, arguing he has been 'too thoroughly nurtured by his city's laws to defy its judgment.' The Laws of Athens are personified and argue against escape. Xanthippe, Socrates's wife, cries in the prison.

General / Biographical and Other Dialogues

General identification clues for Socrates include: teacher of Plato, Athenian philosopher who left no writings of his own, used as an example in syllogisms ('Socrates is a man'), and classified in Porphyry's Isagoge as an individual with peculiarities like being 'the son of Sophroniscus.' Karl Jaspers listed him as one of four 'paradigmatic individuals' alongside Buddha, Confucius, and Jesus. Cicero said Socrates 'brought philosophy down from heaven.' Hegel's earliest works claimed Socrates was superior to Jesus for embracing the 'democratic spirit.' Other dialogues: in the Gorgias, Socrates cannot get Callicles to concede doing evil is worse than suffering it. Socrates is uniquely absent from the Laws.

Summary of Facts

Socrates is the foundational figure of Western philosophy, known entirely through the writings of others, particularly his student Plato. According to the clues, Socrates is most frequently identified as the central character of Plato's Apology, in which he defends himself against charges of impiety and corrupting the youth brought by Meletus, Anytus, and Lycon. Key moments from the Apology include his famous gadfly metaphor (comparing himself to a fly stinging the sluggish horse of Athens), the oracle of Delphi declaring no man wiser through Chaerephon, his elenchus of Meletus using the horse-trainer analogy, the attribution of Anaxagoras's theories to him, and his claim that the unexamined life is not worth living. The trial ended with his death sentence in 399 BCE.

Socrates's daimonion, or divine inner voice, is a major clue topic. This mysterious sign only forbade actions, never commanded them. Nietzsche in The Birth of Tragedy called it a 'monstrous defect' where 'instinct becomes the critic and consciousness becomes the creator.' Meletus cited it as evidence of Socrates introducing 'new divinities,' and Socrates himself said it prevented him from entering politics. Apuleius wrote a treatise about it that Augustine attacked in The City of God.

The relationship between Socrates and later philosophers is heavily clued. Kierkegaard's 1841 master's thesis, On the Concept of Irony with Continual Reference to Socrates, defined Socratic irony as 'infinite absolute negativity' and called Socrates a 'magnificent pause in the course of history.' Nietzsche appears frequently as Socrates's critic: The Birth of Tragedy argues that the Socratic mindset displaced the Apollonian and Dionysian, ending Greek tragedy, while Twilight of the Idols presents 'The Problem of Socrates' as a symptom of decline. Gregory Vlastos discussed Socrates as a moral philosopher, and Robert Talisse argued he teaches 'what democracy is.'

The Socratic method (elenchus) involves systematic questioning that often leads to aporia. Socrates compares himself to a midwife who helps others give birth to wisdom. In the Symposium, Socrates recounts Diotima's teachings on love as an ascending ladder of beauty, and Alcibiades drunkenly compares him to busts of Silenus. In the Phaedo, set in Socrates's prison cell, he discusses the immortality of the soul with Cebes and Simmias before drinking hemlock. In the Crito, personified Laws of Athens persuade him not to escape. Socrates is also clued through his use in logic (the classic syllogism), his classification in Porphyry's Isagoge, Karl Jaspers listing him as one of four paradigmatic individuals, and Aristophanes's parody in The Clouds.
