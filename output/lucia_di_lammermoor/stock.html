Summary of Facts

Lucia di Lammermoor is a three-act opera composed by Gaetano Donizetti, premiered in 1835 and based on Walter Scott's novel The Bride of Lammermoor. Set in Scotland, the opera depicts a bitter feud between the noble Ashton and Ravenswood families. The title character, Lucia Ashton, is deeply in love with Edgardo di Ravenswood, her family's enemy. The opera is one of the pillars of the bel canto repertoire, and the role of Lucia is a coloratura soprano showpiece.

In Act I, Lucia sings the aria 'Regnava nel silenzio' to her maid Alisa, recounting her vision of a ghostly maiden — killed by an ancestor at a fountain — whom she takes as an omen of her own doom. This aria reaches its lowest note, a C-natural, on the phrase 'l'ombre mostrarsi.' Act II culminates in the famous sextet 'Chi mi frena,' which erupts when Edgardo arrives just as Lucia has been manipulated into signing a marriage contract with the nobleman Arturo Bucklaw. After the sextet, Edgardo removes the ring from Lucia's finger in a gesture of repudiation.

The opera's crown jewel is the Act III Mad Scene, centered on the aria 'Il dolce suono.' After murdering Arturo on their wedding night, Lucia stumbles into the Great Hall in a disheveled white dress soaked with blood, in a state of delirium. She hallucinates her wedding with Edgardo, singing themes from their Act I duet 'Verranno a te sull'aure' as a response to a 'celestial harmony.' The scene is famous for its sparse orchestration — a solo flute (often substituting for the originally specified glass harmonica) accompanies the soprano's extended vocal line. The Mad Scene is capped by the cabaletta 'Spargi d'amaro pianto,' whose seven-note opening melody is a recognized signature. The aria was originally written in F major for soprano Fanny Tacchinardi Persiani, but is customarily transposed down to E-flat; the most popular cadenza was composed by Mathilde Marchesi. The opera ends with Edgardo's suicide at his ancestors' tomb, following a duel challenge by Lucia's brother Enrico.

The opera's performance history is inseparable from soprano Joan Sutherland, who made her signature role debut as Lucia in a 1959 Covent Garden production directed by Franco Zeffirelli. Her Met debut in the role was greeted with a 12-minute ovation. A 2016 Covent Garden production by director Katie Mitchell used a split-screen design by Vicki Mortimer to show the usually unseen murder of Arturo. Adelina Patti also made her professional debut in the title role.
