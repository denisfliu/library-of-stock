German Enlightenment philosopher of transcendental idealism. Author of the three Critiques (Pure Reason, Practical Reason, Judgment). Developed the categorical imperative, the thing-in-itself, and synthetic a priori judgments. Never left Königsberg.
The Categorical Imperative & Moral Philosophy

Kant's central ethical framework, developed primarily in the Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals (1785). The categorical imperative demands that one act only according to maxims that could be universalized — leading to the Kingdom of Ends, where all rational beings treat each other as ends, not means. This deontological system judges actions by duty rather than consequences, producing controversial conclusions like the prohibition on lying even to a murderer at one's door. Key interlocutors include Christine Korsgaard, Schopenhauer (who attacked Kant's ethics as egoistic), and John Rawls (Kantian constructivism).

Critique of Pure Reason / Transcendental Idealism

Kant's magnum opus and the foundation of transcendental idealism. Argues that we only know the world through appearances, not the thing-in-itself (Ding an sich), and that space and time are pure intuitions — forms of experience, not properties of external reality. The book defends synthetic a priori truths (with '7 + 5 = 12' as the go-to example), develops the categories of understanding, and frames the entire project as a 'Copernican Revolution' in philosophy. Hume woke Kant from his 'dogmatic slumber,' and the Prolegomena elaborates the response. Structurally, the Transcendental Aesthetic, Transcendental Deduction, antinomies, and schematism are all frequently clued.

Aesthetics (Critique of Judgment)

Kant's third Critique, dealing with beauty and the sublime. Beauty is analyzed through four 'moments' (including pleasure and universality), and Kant distinguishes free beauty from adherent beauty (attached to a concept). The sublime is split into noble, splendid, and terrifying varieties. Other key concepts include genius ('nature giving the rule to art'), the sensus communis (community of taste), and the four categories of subjective experience: the agreeable, the beautiful, the good, and the sublime. Hannah Arendt located an 'unwritten political philosophy' in these aesthetic writings.

Race and Anthropology

An increasingly studied dimension of Kant's work. He posited four races descending from a common ancestor, adapted a Galenic four-humor theory into a racial hierarchy (with Native Americans at the bottom), and debated race with Georg Forster. Emmanuel Eze ignited modern scholarship on Kant's racism in the 1990s. Achille Mbembe titled Critique of Black Reason and Gayatri Spivak titled Critique of Postcolonial Reason in deliberate reference to Kant. His Anthropology from a Pragmatic Point of View argued only white men possess reason and dignity. He also lectured more on geography than any other subject.

Perpetual Peace & Political Philosophy

Kant's proposal for lasting international peace, written as a mock treaty with three definitive and six preliminary articles. The first definitive article requires republican constitutions; preliminary articles ban assassins and standing armies. Kant advocates 'universal hospitality' for citizens of the world and condemns colonial 'inhospitable behavior.' The essay presages democratic peace theory. Related: the 'crooked timber of humanity' (from 'Idea of a Universal History') — 'no straight thing was ever made' — which Isaiah Berlin used as a book title.

The Murderer at the Door (Lying Debate)

Kant's controversial position that lying is always morally wrong — even if a murderer asks whether your friend is hiding in your house. Benjamin Constant first challenged this implication; Kant replied in 'On a Supposed Right to Tell Lies from Benevolent Motives.' Helga Varden later added a Nazi officer to the thought experiment. Rae Langton's 'Duty and Desolation' contrasts 'sane' and 'severe' readings of Kant's position.

Neo-Kantianism & Reception

How later thinkers received and rebuilt Kant's philosophy. The Marburg school (Hermann Cohen, Ernst Cassirer) and Southwest school (Wilhelm Windelband) both rejected Kant's distinction between intuitions and concepts. The Fischer-Trendelenburg Debate introduced the 'Neglected Alternative' about Kant's theory of space. Schopenhauer both praised Kant ('a cataract operation on a blind person') and criticized him, identifying the thing-in-itself with 'will.' Modern Kantians include Korsgaard, Rawls, and Onora O'Neill. Bakhtin, Mach, and Einstein were also influenced.

Summary of Facts

Immanuel Kant (1724-1804) was a German Enlightenment philosopher who never left Königsberg, where his daily walks were said to set the city's clocks. He is most identified by his three Critiques and the categorical imperative.

His moral philosophy, developed in the Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals, centers on the categorical imperative: act only according to maxims that can be universalized. He derived it from four formulas (humanity, autonomy, etc.) and imagined a Kingdom of Ends where rational beings treat each other as ends, not means. His deontological ethics judges actions by duty, not consequences. The famous 'murderer at the door' thought experiment — where Kant argues lying is always wrong, even to save a friend — was challenged by Benjamin Constant, with later additions by Helga Varden (Nazi officer variant) and analysis by Rae Langton and Christine Korsgaard. Other moral concepts include radical evil (Religion Within the Bounds of Reason Alone), the shopkeeper example, and his controversial views on sex outside marriage.

The Critique of Pure Reason, his magnum opus, develops transcendental idealism: we only know appearances, not the thing-in-itself (Ding an sich). Space and time are pure intuitions (forms of experience), not properties of external reality. He argued for synthetic a priori truths (using '7 + 5 = 12' as example), proposed the categories of understanding, and framed his project as a 'Copernican Revolution' in philosophy. Hume woke him from his 'dogmatic slumber,' and the Prolegomena elaborates his response. Key structural elements include the Transcendental Aesthetic, Transcendental Deduction (which Strawson called his most important contribution), the schematism chapter, and the antinomies. The A/B edition convention began with Dilthey's Academy edition.

The Critique of Judgment covers aesthetics: beauty analyzed in four moments (pleasure, universality); free vs. adherent beauty; the sublime (noble, splendid, terrifying); taste and sensus communis; genius as 'nature giving the rule to art.' Hannah Arendt found an 'unwritten political philosophy' in Kant's aesthetics.

Kant's racial theories are increasingly studied: he posited four races from a common ancestor, adapted Galenic humors into a hierarchy, and debated Georg Forster. Emmanuel Eze ignited this scholarship in the 1990s; Achille Mbembe (Critique of Black Reason) and Gayatri Spivak (Critique of Postcolonial Reason) titled books in reference to Kant. His Anthropology from a Pragmatic Point of View argued only white men can reason. Adrian Piper's performance Food for the Spirit centered on reading Kant.

Toward Perpetual Peace proposes conditions for international peace: republican constitutions, abolition of standing armies and assassins, universal hospitality, and condemnation of colonial 'inhospitable behavior.' It presages democratic peace theory. 'What is Enlightenment?' defines it as 'man's emergence from self-incurred immaturity,' with the motto 'Sapere aude!' It distinguishes public reason (as scholar) from private reason (in one's vocation) and lost its competition to Moses Mendelssohn.

Neo-Kantianism flourished through the Marburg school (Cohen, Cassirer) and Southwest school, both rejecting Kant's intuition/concept distinction. The Fischer-Trendelenburg Debate concerned the 'Neglected Alternative.' Schopenhauer both praised Kant ('cataract operation on a blind person') and criticized him, identifying the thing-in-itself with 'will.' Modern Kantians include Korsgaard (Creating the Kingdom of Ends, Fellow Creatures), Rawls (Kantian constructivism), and Onora O'Neill (Constructions of Reason).
