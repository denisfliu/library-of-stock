Sir Gawain and the Green Knight

A 14th-century Arthurian romance in alliterative verse, using a 'bob and wheel' metrical device (a pair of stressed syllables followed by a rhyming four-line stanza). Sir Gawain beheads the Green Knight and accepts a challenge to be beheaded himself in a year and a day. Lady Bertilak tests Gawain by pretending to seduce him. Gawain turns down her offer of a ring but accepts a silken girdle/sash/belt that supposedly grants invulnerability. He breaks the rules of a game by keeping the girdle rather than trading it (animal carcasses are traded for kisses). He receives a nick on the neck rather than full beheading, as punishment. The confrontation was planned by Morgan le Fay. Lord Bertilak is the Green Knight. Wikipedia

Pearl

An allegorical poem about a father grieving the loss of his daughter, who is named for the title object (a pearl). The narrator falls asleep in a 'garden green' on a mound covered in herbs and dreams of seeing a maiden (his daughter) across a river representing death, who lies out of reach. The maiden teaches him about sin and grace and shows him the Heavenly Jerusalem / New Jerusalem. She is part of a procession of virgins in heaven. The first five stanzas end with the phrase 'without a spot.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

The Pearl Poet (also called the Gawain Poet) is an anonymous 14th-century Middle English author. All four of the attributed poems — Pearl, Sir Gawain and the Green Knight, Cleanness (or Purity), and Patience — are found in the Cotton Nero A.x manuscript. The poet is grouped with the Alliterative Revival, alongside William Langland's Piers Plowman.

Sir Gawain and the Green Knight is an Arthurian romance in alliterative verse that uses a 'bob and wheel' metrical device — a short half-line of two stressed syllables followed by a rhyming four-line stanza. Sir Gawain beheads the Green Knight and accepts a challenge to be beheaded in return in a year and a day. Lady Bertilak tests Gawain by pretending to seduce him. Gawain turns down her offer of a ring but accepts a silken girdle (green belt/sash) that supposedly grants invulnerability. Under the rules of a game, animal carcasses are to be traded for kisses, but Gawain breaks the rules by secretly keeping the girdle. He receives only a nick on the neck rather than a full beheading. The confrontation was planned by Morgan le Fay. Lord Bertilak is revealed to be the Green Knight.

Pearl is an allegorical poem about a father grieving his lost daughter, identified with the title object (a pearl). After falling asleep in a 'garden green' on a mound covered in herbs, the narrator dreams of a different garden where his daughter appears as a maiden across a river representing death. The first five stanzas end with the phrase 'without a spot.' The maiden teaches him about sin and grace and shows him the Heavenly Jerusalem / New Jerusalem. She is part of a procession of virgins in heaven.

Cleanness (or Purity) is an alliterative poem urging mankind to follow the title virtue, using episodes of Noah's ark and the Flood, Abraham and Lot, and the writing on the wall at Belshazzar's Feast. The description of the Dead Sea and vessels at Belshazzar's Feast are drawn from Mandeville's Travels.

Patience identifies the title virtue with poverty and describes its role in the story of Jonah, mocking Jonah for not possessing patience, which leads to his being swallowed by a whale.

The Pearl Poet is also sometimes credited with a 352-line alliterative poem about St. Erkenwald, the late-seventh-century bishop of London. In one attributed poem, a saint sheds a tear on a reanimated corpse which then dissolves into dust.
