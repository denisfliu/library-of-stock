German Romantic composer (1809–1847), grandson of philosopher Moses Mendelssohn and sibling of composer Fanny Mendelssohn. He revived Bach's St. Matthew Passion in 1829, wrote the incidental music to A Midsummer Night's Dream (including the famous Wedding March), and composed the String Octet at age 16. Known for the Italian, Scottish, and Reformation symphonies; the E minor Violin Concerto; the oratorios Elijah and St. Paul; and Songs Without Words.
General / Biographical

Felix Mendelssohn (1809–1847) was a German Romantic composer and child prodigy. His grandfather was philosopher Moses Mendelssohn; his sister Fanny was also a composer. He founded the Leipzig Conservatory and directed the Gewandhaus Orchestra. He wrote his first string symphony at age 15 for Fanny's birthday and his celebrated String Octet at age 16. He revived interest in J.S. Bach by conducting the 1829 revival of the St. Matthew Passion. He had an unrequited obsession with soprano Jenny Lind, for whom he wrote the solo 'Hear Ye Israel' in Elijah. He wrote 'Variations sérieuses' in D minor (with 17 variations) to fund a Beethoven statue in Bonn. He had Jewish heritage, which became a source of anti-Semitic criticism. Arthur Sullivan was the first recipient of the 'Mendelssohn Scholarship.'

Incidental Music to A Midsummer Night's Dream (Op. 61) – The Work as a Whole

Mendelssohn wrote the concert overture to A Midsummer Night's Dream (Op. 21) at age 17, then wrote a full set of incidental music (Op. 61) years later to complete a suite. The famous Wedding March is used at weddings. The overture imitates the braying of Bottom (turned into a donkey). The scherzo was inspired by the Walpurgis Night section of Goethe's Faust. The suite includes vocal numbers 'Through this house give glimmering light' and 'Ye Spotted Snakes,' and a nocturne with bassoon accompaniment of the horn melody following 'what hempen homespuns.' The suite opens with woodwind chords in E major. The bergamask (Dance of Clowns) uses a descending major ninth motif. Trumpets play a fanfare followed by 'C, B, short F#-A.' Saint-Saëns parodied the scherzo in Carnival of the Animals. Wikipedia

Violin Concerto in E minor, Op. 64 →

Mendelssohn's Violin Concerto (1844/premiered 1845) is innovative in several ways: the soloist enters in the second bar (no orchestral tutti), the cadenza is fully written out (not improvised), and all three movements are played attacca. The first movement features 'three B's' as the opening theme (long B, B, long B, G, E, long E, B). The cadenza ends with accelerating arch-shaped spiccato arpeggios as the orchestra re-enters. Ferdinand David (the dedicatee) removed counterpoint from the cadenza in early performances. Joseph Joachim called it 'the heart's jewel.' The 1948 Milstein/NY Philharmonic recording was pressed on the first commercial 12-inch LP. Wikipedia

String Octet in E-flat, Op. 20

Written at age 16 (for a double quartet — 8 strings). The scherzo is inspired by the Walpurgis Night section of Goethe's Faust and must always be played 'pianissimo and staccato.' The finale quotes Handel's Hallelujah Chorus ('And he shall reign forever') in a fast 8-part fugue. Mendelssohn later orchestrated the scherzo to replace the minuet movement of his Symphony No. 1. Composer Conrad Wilson called it a demonstration of 'youthful verve.' Mendelssohn's E-flat major Op. 20 essentially invented the string octet as a genre. Wikipedia

Symphony No. 4 in A Major ('Italian') →

Mendelssohn's most popular symphony, nicknamed 'Italian.' He described it as 'blue sky in A major.' It is the first symphony in the standard repertoire to start in major but end in a minor key. The second movement (Andante con moto) is nicknamed the 'Pilgrim's March' after Ignaz Moscheles claimed its melody came from a Czech pilgrims' song; solo clarinet evokes a chanting priest. The finale combines saltarello and tarantella themes. Written after a Grand Tour of Europe that also inspired the Scottish Symphony and the Hebrides Overture. Wikipedia

Symphony No. 3 in A Minor ('Scottish')

Mendelssohn's 'Scottish' Symphony, inspired by a trip to the British Isles. The first bars were possibly sketched at Holyrood Chapel. The first movement's opening theme uses A, C-sharp, A, A, C-sharp, A in the horns. The second movement scherzo in 2/4 imitates a 'snap dance' and uses the characteristic 'Lombard' or 'Scotch snap' short-long rhythm. The finale was originally marked 'Allegro guerriero' to imitate a chorus of male warriors. The coda shifts from A minor to A major, with a melody reminiscent of the composer's Ave Maria setting. Otto Klemperer disliked the coda and sometimes substituted his own. Written alongside the Italian Symphony, both inspired by the same Grand Tour. Wikipedia

Symphony No. 5 ('Reformation')

Mendelssohn's 'Reformation' Symphony, written for the tricentennial of the Augsburg Confession (1830, though published as his fifth). It quotes the 'Dresden Amen' (a rising motif) in the first movement; the low strings and bassoons open with a six-note motif Wagner later used as his 'Grail theme' in Parsifal. The finale opens with a solo flute playing Luther's hymn 'A Mighty Fortress Is Our God' (Ein feste Burg) in a woodwind chorale. A slow third movement ends in the dominant key of G major before an attacca finale. Written before the Italian and Scottish symphonies despite its higher number. Wikipedia

Oratorios (Elijah and St. Paul)

Mendelssohn wrote two major oratorios: St. Paul and Elijah. Elijah was his final oratorio; premiered at the Birmingham Triennial Music Festival and performed there every year. Mendelssohn wrote the solo 'Hear Ye Israel' for Jenny Lind. The chorus sings 'And then shall your light break forth' at the end of Elijah. St. Paul features the chorus 'Happy and blest are they' at the end of the 'Stoning of Stephen' scene. His Lobgesang (Symphony No. 2 in B-flat) is a choral symphony-cantata. He also wrote an anthem 'Hear My Prayer' which includes the soprano solo 'O for the Wings of a Dove.' Wikipedia

Songs Without Words (Lieder ohne Worte)

Eight books of lyrical solo piano pieces published between 1829 and 1845, six pieces per book. Fanny Mendelssohn may have inspired Felix to write the collection and likely wrote some entries. The 'Spring Song' (Frühlingslied, Book 5, last piece; also nicknamed 'Camberwell Green') opens with the ascending theme 'long C#, D D# E, long A.' Book 6 includes the 'Spinner's Song' (nicknamed 'Bee's Wedding'). Each book concludes with a 'Venetian Boat Song.' A posthumously published cello work is sometimes grouped as part of this collection. Fanny published similar pieces as 'Songs for Piano.' Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Felix Mendelssohn (1809–1847) was a German Romantic composer, child prodigy, and grandson of philosopher Moses Mendelssohn. His sister Fanny was also a composer. He wrote his first string symphony at age 15 (for Fanny's birthday) and the celebrated String Octet in E-flat (Op. 20) at age 16. He founded the Leipzig Conservatory and directed the Gewandhaus Orchestra. His 1829 revival of Bach's St. Matthew Passion (at the Singakademie, in partnership with Eduard Devrient and Carl Friedrich Zelter) is credited with starting the Bach Revival; his grandmother had given him the score as a Christmas gift. He had an unrequited obsession with soprano Jenny Lind, for whom he wrote the solo 'Hear Ye Israel' in Elijah (his final oratorio); Lind worried her rejection of him precipitated his death in 1847.

Mendelssohn's best-known work is the incidental music to A Midsummer Night's Dream. The Concert Overture (Op. 21), written at 17, imitates Bottom's braying as 'hee-hawing' in the strings. The full suite (Op. 61) opens with E-major woodwind chords that expand in range. The Wedding March is universally used at weddings (usually on the organ). The scherzo was inspired by the Walpurgis Night section of Goethe's Faust and must be played pianissimo and staccato. A nocturne follows 'what hempen homespuns' and features bassoons accompanying the horn melody. Saint-Saëns parodied the scherzo in Carnival of the Animals.

The String Octet (E-flat, Op. 20) — written for a double quartet — has a scherzo (Allegro leggierissimo, Faust-inspired) and a finale quoting Handel's Hallelujah Chorus in an 8-part fugue. Mendelssohn later orchestrated its scherzo for use as an alternative third movement in his Symphony No. 1.

The Violin Concerto in E minor (1844) is famous for: the soloist entering in bar 2 (subverting double-exposition form), the cadenza being fully written out (ending with spiccato arpeggios as the orchestra re-enters), and all three movements played attacca. Joseph Joachim called it 'the heart's jewel.' The 1948 Milstein/NY Philharmonic recording was the first commercial 12-inch LP.

Of the four mature symphonies: the 'Italian' (No. 4) is 'blue sky in A major,' the first standard-repertoire symphony to start in major but end in minor; its second movement is called the 'Pilgrim's March'; the finale combines saltarello and tarantella. The 'Scottish' (No. 3) uses the 'Scotch snap' (Lombard rhythm) in the scherzo; its first bars were sketched at Holyrood Chapel. The 'Reformation' (No. 5, written before the other two despite its number) quotes the 'Dresden Amen' and opens its finale with solo flute playing 'A Mighty Fortress Is Our God'; the opening six-note motif was later used by Wagner as the Parsifal Grail theme.

The oratorios St. Paul and Elijah are major choral works. Elijah premiered at the Birmingham Triennial Festival. The anthem 'Hear My Prayer' features the soprano solo 'O for the Wings of a Dove,' recorded by 15-year-old Ernest Lough as EMI's first million-selling classical record. Songs Without Words (eight volumes, 6 pieces each, 1829–1845) includes the 'Spring Song' (Camberwell Green) and the 'Spinner's Song' (Bee's Wedding). A melody by Mendelssohn became 'Hark! the Herald Angels Sing.' Arthur Sullivan was the first recipient of the Mendelssohn Scholarship.
