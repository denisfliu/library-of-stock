Summary of Facts

Thomas Chatterton is clued as an 18th-century Bristol poet who forged medieval poems attributed to the fictional 15th-century monk Thomas Rowley. He committed suicide at age 17, reportedly by arsenic poisoning, and became a major icon for the Romantics. His death scene was painted by Henry Wallis using George Meredith as a model; the painting hangs in the Tate. Peter Ackroyd wrote a novel about him. Wordsworth called him a "marvellous Boy" in "Resolution and Independence," and Keats dedicated Endymion to him. Shelley names him alongside Sidney and Lucan as "inheritors of unfulfill'd renown" in Adonais. Dante Gabriel Rossetti attributed "Shakespeare's manhood at a boy's wild heart" to Chatterton. His forged works include Aella, a Tragical Interlude, "An Excellent Ballad of Charitie," and "The Parliament of Sprites." Before leaving Bristol, he wrote a satirical "Last Will and Testament." Robert Browning adopted the term "slug-horn" from Chatterton's pseudo-medieval language.
