Summary of Facts

Georges de la Tour (1593-1652) was a 17th-century French Baroque painter based in Luneville, patronized by both Louis XIII and Louis XIV. He is best known for his candle-lit interior chiaroscuro scenes, heavily influenced by Caravaggio and the Utrecht Caravaggisti, especially Gerrit van Honthorst and Hendrick ter Brugghen. His works were largely forgotten until a 1915 article by German art historian Herman Voss.

His most iconic painting, The Penitent Magdalene, shows a woman with a skull on her lap staring into a mirror reflecting candlelight. A variant, Magdalene with the Smoking Flame, appeared in The Little Mermaid (in Ariel's grotto). The 1640 version depicts the Magdalene as extremely emaciated.

The Cheat exists in two versions (Ace of Diamonds and Ace of Clubs), showing three people colluding against a foppish youth as a card sharp retrieves a card from his belt, with a shifty woman in a feathered (sometimes red-feathered) cap. The Ace of Clubs version is at the Kimbell Art Museum.

De la Tour painted multiple hurdy-gurdy players, including blind players. Some of these have been attributed to the mysterious 'Hurdy-Gurdy Master.' The Hurdy-Gurdy Player features a man with a vulgarly open mouth. In The Musician's Brawl (or Quarrel), hurdy-gurdy players fight, one squeezing lemon juice into a blind beggar's eye, and the leftmost fighter defends himself with a hurdy-gurdy.

St. Sebastian Tended by St. Irene shows St. Irene holding a large torch. The Fortune Teller shows a boy having his purse and watch stolen while his fortune is read, and was at the center of a forgery controversy at the Met. Other works include Joseph the Carpenter (also painted as The Dream of Saint Joseph, which Anton Raphael Mengs also painted), The Newborn Christ, and The Flea Catcher.
