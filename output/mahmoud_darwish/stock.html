Palestinian national poet, widely considered the most famous contemporary Arabic-language poet. Best known for the poem 'Identity Card' with its refrain 'Write down! I am an Arab.' Also known for the prose poem Memory for Forgetfulness (set during the 1982 Siege of Beirut on Hiroshima Day), the poem 'In Jerusalem,' and collections such as Mural, State of Siege, and The Butterfly's Burden. Admired Yehuda Amichai but described him as 'a challenge to me.' Wrote the Palestinian Declaration of Independence. Called the 'Essential Breath' of his people by Naomi Shihab Nye. Edward Said wrote about his poetry.
"Identity Card" ("Bitaqat huwiyya")

Darwish's most famous poem by far and his near-universal giveaway in quizbowl. The speaker addresses an Israeli official with the refrain 'Write down! / I am an Arab,' cataloguing his distinguishing features across multiple stanzas. The speaker mentions having eight children (with a ninth on the way), that his identity card number is 'fifty thousand,' that he has 'a name without a title,' and asks 'Will you be angry?' The poem ends with the warning 'Beware / Of my hunger / And my anger,' preceded by 'I do not hate people / Nor do I encroach / But if I become hungry / The usurper's flesh will be my food.' Israel's defense minister Avigdor Lieberman controversially likened this poem to Mein Kampf in 2016. The poem also states 'Nothing is left for us and my grandchildren / Except these rocks.' Memory for Forgetfulness

A long prose poem (also subtitled 'The Time: Beirut / The Place: August') set on Hiroshima Day (August 6) during the 1982 Israeli siege of Beirut. The narrator frequently yearns for the 'aroma of coffee,' declaring 'Coffee is geography.' The poem opens 'Out of one dream, another dream is born' and asks 'Does it happen much, that you are awakened from one dream by another, itself the interpretation of the dream?' It uses imagery of doves and coffee to discuss fear, and ends 'I don't see a shore. / I don't see a dove.' Darwish links the violence of the Lebanese Civil War to the bombing of Hiroshima. Wikipedia

"In Jerusalem"

A poem set 'within the ancient walls' of Jerusalem. The speaker walks 'from one epoch to another without a memory to guide me' and claims 'I don't walk, I fly, I become another, / transfigured.' The speaker describes his hands as 'two doves / on the cross hovering and carrying the earth.' A 'woman soldier' confronts the speaker, shouting 'Didn't I kill you?', and the speaker replies 'You killed me... and I forgot, like you, to die.' The poem also includes imagery of prophets returning from heaven 'less discouraged and melancholy, because love and peace are holy and are coming to town.'

Other Works

Several other works appear in the clues with lower frequency. 'A Soldier Dreams of White Lilies' compares love to a glass of wine. 'The Horse Fell Off the Poem' features the line 'you and I are the absent ones' and an animal that became 'bloodied with my poem.' The collection A River Dies of Thirst is about a river dying. 'The Earth is Closing on Us' ends with Darwish's blood giving birth to olive trees. Victims of a Map is a collaborative volume with Samih al-Qasim and Adonis, containing 'We Fear for a Dream.' The Butterfly's Burden contains 'The Damascene Collar of the Dove.' Leaves of Olives is an early collection. 'Poem for the Land' describes carnations growing where 'stones take flight' and 'dewdrops become weapons.' 'Antithesis' describes meeting Edward Said in New York 'at the edge of an electric chasm sky high.' 'I Come From There' has the speaker learning 'all the words and broke them up / To make a single word: Homeland.' 'Sonnet V' has the line 'touch[es] you as a lonely violin touches the suburbs of the faraway place.' A poem titled for 'writing' includes the line 'a statue the Romans carved and the Arabs forgot.' Darwish wrote about his Jewish lover Rita, as in 'Between Rita and my eyes there is a rifle.' State of Siege is another notable collection. An Imru al-Qays poem about a 'Non-Linguistic Dispute' was written by Darwish.

Summary of Facts

Mahmoud Darwish is the single most frequently asked-about Palestinian poet in quizbowl, consistently identified as a Palestinian who wrote in Arabic and often called the most famous contemporary Arabic-language poet. His poem 'Identity Card' is by far his most clued work, appearing in virtually every question about him. The poem's refrain 'Write down! / I am an Arab' serves as his near-universal giveaway, while its concluding warning 'Beware / Of my hunger / And my anger' and the speaker's mention of having eight children with a ninth on the way are common middle clues. The identity card number 'fifty thousand' also appears regularly.

His second most important work is Memory for Forgetfulness, a long prose poem set on Hiroshima Day (August 6) during the 1982 Israeli siege of Beirut. The narrator's yearning for coffee ('Coffee is geography') and the imagery of doves are distinctive clues for this work. It opens 'Out of one dream, another dream is born.'

The poem 'In Jerusalem' is his third most clued work, recognizable by its setting 'within the ancient walls,' the speaker walking 'from one epoch to another without a memory to guide me,' and the dramatic exchange with a 'woman soldier' who asks 'Didn't I kill you?' to which the speaker replies 'You killed me... and I forgot, like you, to die.'

Beyond these three core works, clues reference a wide range of poems and collections: Mural and State of Siege as notable volumes; 'The Horse Fell Off the Poem' (inadequacy of language causing an animal's death); A River Dies of Thirst; 'The Earth is Closing on Us' (blood giving birth to olive trees); Victims of a Map (co-written with Samih al-Qasim and Adonis); The Butterfly's Burden (containing 'The Damascene Collar of the Dove'); Leaves of Olives; 'A Soldier Dreams of White Lilies' (love compared to wine); poems about his Jewish lover Rita; 'Antithesis' (meeting Edward Said in New York); 'I Come From There' ('all the words broken up to make a single word: Homeland'); and 'Poem for the Land.'

Biographically, Darwish is linked to Yehuda Amichai, whom he admired but called 'a challenge to me, because we write about the same place.' Naomi Shihab Nye called him the 'Essential Breath' of his people. Edward Said wrote critically about his poetry. He also wrote the Palestinian Declaration of Independence. Olive trees recur as a symbol of Palestinian resilience (sumud) in his work.
