Japanese Nobel laureate in Literature (1968). Best known for the novel Snow Country, about Shimamura's affair with the geisha Komako at a hot springs town, ending with the Milky Way image over a burning silk warehouse. Also known for Thousand Cranes (tea ceremony; Kikuji and Mrs. Ota), The Sound of the Mountain (Shingo Ogata), 'The Dancing Girl of Izu' (narrator discovers a drummer-girl is just a child), Beauty and Sadness, The Master of Go, The House of the Sleeping Beauties, and his many Palm-of-the-Hand Stories. Founded the New Sensationalist School (Shinkankakuha). Nobel lecture titled 'Japan, the Beautiful, and Myself.'
Snow Country

Kawabata's best-known novel. Shimamura, a ballet expert/aficionado who has never actually seen a ballet, travels by train to a remote hot springs town and has an affair with the geisha Komako. The novel opens with Shimamura watching the girl Yoko's disembodied reflection in a train window as she cares for a man with tuberculosis (Yukio). Komako practices samisen with sheet music, is enraged at being called a 'good woman,' and drunkenly repeats 'I said I'd come and I've come, haven't I?' The novel ends with Shimamura feeling the Milky Way flow inside him as a fire engulfs a silk/silkworm cocoon warehouse; Komako runs to carry Yoko's body from the ruins. Contains descriptions of Chijimi linens bleaching in the sun. A Basho haiku about the Milky Way arched over a stormy sea inspired the conclusion. Condensed into an eleven-page 'Gleanings from Snow Country.' Wikipedia

Thousand Cranes

A novel structured around the tea ceremony. Kikuji has an affair with Mrs. Ota (his father's old mistress), who commits suicide with sleeping pills. Her daughter Fumiko smashes an ancient Shino bowl/tea bowl stained with her mother's lipstick. The hairy birthmark of Chikako disgusts Kikuji. Yumiko gives Kikuji a handkerchief embroidered with the title pattern (the thousand cranes). The title comes from the pattern on the pink scarf of Yukiko Inamura, the suggested proper bride. Kikuji recalls seeing a woman trim hair from a large birthmark on her breast as a child. Wikipedia

"The Dancing Girl of Izu"

A novella/story about a university student on vacation at the Izu Peninsula who meets a troupe of traveling performers including a drum-carrying/playing girl. The narrator realizes the girl is just a child/adolescent when he glimpses her bathing naked, ending his infatuation. He laughs or cries on a steamship as the story ends, feeling his head 'dripping away drop by drop.' Eikichi and his sister wave goodbye as the narrator's ship leaves. The girl's hairstyle had made her look older. Wikipedia

The Sound of the Mountain

A novel about the elderly businessman Shingo Ogata, who reflects on failing memory and disturbing dreams. He forgives his daughter-in-law Kikuko for aborting her pregnancy in protest of her husband Shuichi's affair with a war widow. The title refers to a disturbing noise Shingo hears in his sleep. Shingo also observes his daughter Fusako's failing marriage. A deceased coworker leaves Shingo a pair of masks. Wikipedia

Beauty and Sadness

A novel about the writer Oki Toshio, who wrote A Girl of Sixteen about his affair with Otoko, a now-famous painter. He visits her many years later; she is now in a lesbian relationship with her artistic protege Keiko. Oki's trip begins with hearing New Year's bells. A photograph that fades when removed from a princess's grave. Keiko (the protege) seduces a shy college student, takes him to a lake on a motorboat, and stages his drowning as an accident. Wikipedia

The Master of Go

A novel based on Kawabata's experience as a journalist, covering a six-month-long retirement game of Go between the old master Honinbo Shusai and his younger opponent Otake (based on Minoru Kitani). The match is broken open by the sealed move on black's 121st play. Shusai is outraged by Otake's forcing move before a scheduled break. Chronicled by journalist Uragami. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Japanese Nobel laureate (1968). Nobel lecture titled 'Japan, the Beautiful, and Myself,' which begins with Dogen's waka about 'fine' seasons; declared 'however alienated one may be from the world, suicide is not a form of enlightenment.' Quoted Akutagawa's suicide note in the lecture. Founded the New Sensationalist School (Shinkankakuha) with other young writers. Decided to become a writer after his grandfather's death ('Diary of a Sixteen Year Old'). Devoted much of his Nobel lecture to explaining poems illustrated with calligraphy. His daughter asks him to take Paul Klee books to a convent. Also wrote The Old Capital (Chieko discovers she is a foundling with twin sister Naeko; Hideo confuses Naeko with Chieko), The Lake (Gimpei Momoi asks Hisako to treat his foot), and The Scarlet Gang of Asakusa.

Summary of Facts

Yasunari Kawabata is a Japanese Nobel laureate in Literature (1968), one of the most frequently clued modern Japanese authors. His best-known work is Snow Country, about the ballet aficionado Shimamura who travels by train to a remote hot springs town and has an affair with the geisha Komako. The novel opens with Shimamura watching the girl Yoko's disembodied reflection in a train window. Komako is a skilled samisen player who drunkenly repeats 'I said I'd come and I've come, haven't I?' The novel ends with Shimamura feeling the Milky Way flow inside him as a fire engulfs a silk warehouse; Komako runs to carry Yoko's body from the ruins.

Thousand Cranes is structured around the tea ceremony. Kikuji has an affair with Mrs. Ota (his father's former mistress), who commits suicide with sleeping pills. Her daughter Fumiko smashes an ancient Shino bowl stained with her mother's lipstick. The hairy birthmark of Chikako disgusts Kikuji. The title comes from the pattern on Yukiko Inamura's scarf.

'The Dancing Girl of Izu' follows a university student who meets a drum-carrying girl and her performing troupe at the Izu Peninsula. The narrator's infatuation ends when he glimpses her bathing naked and realizes she is just an adolescent. He cries on a steamship at the story's end.

The Sound of the Mountain centers on elderly businessman Shingo Ogata, who forgives his daughter-in-law Kikuko for aborting her pregnancy in protest of his son Shuichi's affair with a war widow. The title refers to a disturbing noise Shingo hears in sleep.

Beauty and Sadness follows the writer Oki Toshio, who wrote A Girl of Sixteen about his ex-lover Otoko, now a famous painter in a lesbian relationship with protege Keiko. Keiko seduces a college student and stages his drowning.

The Master of Go chronicles a six-month retirement match between the old master Honinbo Shusai and younger Otake, based on Kawabata's journalism. The House of the Sleeping Beauties has old Eguchi visiting to sleep next to drugged young virgins.

The Palm-of-the-Hand Stories are very short stories named for their brevity. 'The Grasshopper and the Bell Cricket' features children with colored lanterns and a boy's name projected in green light onto a girl's breast. 'Lavatory Buddhahood' humorously depicts a peasant dying on a rival's pay toilet. 'One Arm' has a man taking a talking arm home.

Kawabata's Nobel lecture, 'Japan, the Beautiful, and Myself,' declared 'suicide is not a form of enlightenment.' He founded the New Sensationalist School (Shinkankakuha). The Old Capital features the foundling Chieko discovering her twin sister Naeko.
