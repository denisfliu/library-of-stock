Summary of Facts

Heinrich von Kleist (Bernd Heinrich Wilhelm von Kleist) was a German Romantic playwright and author of novellas and short stories. He died in a murder-suicide with Henriette Vogel in 1811 (some clues incorrectly say 1815). He is a contemporary of Goethe.

"The Marquise of O" is Kleist's most frequently referenced work. In it, the title pregnant widow/noblewoman places a newspaper advertisement seeking The Father of her child to come marry her. A Russian Count had earlier rescued her from soldiers during the Napoleonic Wars, only to assault her himself after she faints -- the rape is omitted from the text and represented with a dash. The groom Leopardo the chasseur is initially put up to feigning responsibility for the central action. When the Count confesses, the Marquise declares she was "ready for some villain of a fellow... but not the devil." In an incestuous moment of reconciliation, her Commandant father "hung over her as if she were his first love and held her mouth and kissed it." The Emperor appends to one law the clause "if it should be the will of God." At the end, she marries Count F.

The Broken Jug is Kleist's best-known comedy. Judge Adam, who is club-footed, tries to hide from Eve the fact that he smashed the title object (a heavy-handed metaphor for Eve's lost virginity). He claims his wig was destroyed because his cat had a litter of kittens in it, and later claims it caught fire while reading cases by candlelight. His baldness and gash on his cheek (which he explains by claiming he fell onto a sharp stove corner) become evidence against him. The secretary Licht discovers Adam's guilt. Brigitte testifies that a wig got caught on a grapevine as Adam fled. Ruprecht's conscription papers are fabricated as part of Adam's seduction attempt. Lebrecht and Ruprecht are initially blamed. Eve testifies against Adam. Inspector General Walter arrives from Utrecht. The play was inspired by a French copper engraving called La Cruche Cassee, according to Heinrich Zschokke. A woman (Marthe) gives a monologue describing the deaths of the jug's previous owners, including a Spanish invader killed by a kettle-mender named Childerich. Adam also blabbers about a noodle dumpling he plans to feed his guinea hen. Kleist also adapted Amphitryon from Moliere.

"The Earthquake in Chile" describes how Josefa Goes into labor during a Corpus Christi procession and is sentenced to death. Her imprisoned lover Jeronimo Rugera prepares to hang himself but is saved by the title earthquake. They wander in a shady grove of pomegranate trees and witness "men and women of every social station" discussing "accounts of extraordinary heroic deeds." A priest compares the city to Sodom and blames its destruction on the two adulterers. The child Philip is adopted after Don Fernando is killed by a religious mob. The story is set in 1647 in Concepcion/Santiago.

Michael Kohlhaas is a novella about a horse-dealer who unrelentingly seeks justice after Junker Wenzel von Tronka mistreats his horses through a fraudulent toll. He leads a criminal army/rebellion and is eventually beheaded. Before dying, he swallows a scroll (paper listing three secrets about the future of Saxony) that predicts the fate of the Elector of Saxony. Coalhouse Walker in E.L. Doctorow's Ragtime is named in homage to Kohlhaas. J.M. Coetzee was inspired by Michael Kohlhaas to write The Life and Times of Michael K.

"On the Marionette Theatre" is an essay framed as a dialogue in which a dancer argues that marionettes can dance without being hampered by gravity. A man tells a story of fencing with a bear owned by a Baltic noble family, which could parry every attack. The essay concludes that grace appears most purely in objects with no consciousness or infinite consciousness (marionettes and God). A graceful young boy loses his grace because of his inability to exactly replicate a movement he had made with his foot. The essay ends by observing that "we would have to eat of the fruit of the tree of knowledge again to return to a state of innocence." It contrasts marionettes' movement with that of dancers.

Prince Friedrich of Homburg is Kleist's final play, not performed during his lifetime. The title character puts on a laurel wreath and receives a glove from Princess Natalie while sleepwalking. He expects to be executed for disobeying orders at the Battle of Fehrbellin but is crowned with a laurel wreath by Natalie. In the play's most controversial scene, the title character sees his open grave and desperately begs for his life. The play ends with the title officer of the Great Elector avoiding execution.

Penthesilea is a tragedy whose title character gnaws on the corpse of Achilles along with her dogs. The title character laments her inability to tell apart the words "Bisse" and "Kusse" before stabbing herself with a dagger. The play's failure contributed to Kleist's decision to commit suicide.

"St. Cecilia, or the Power of Music" describes four brothers who go mad and intone the Gloria in Excelsis every midnight after attempting to ransack a cloister in Aachen, where they were moved by a rendition supposedly directed by Saint Cecilia. "The Beggarwoman of Locarno" describes a nobleman who Burns his castle after it is haunted by the ghost of a beggar woman whose death he caused. "The Betrothal in Santo Domingo" describes Gustav shooting his betrothed Toni after thinking she is a traitor when she ties him up to protect him from Congo Hoango.

Kleist is considered a major predecessor to Kafka. Franz Kafka loved Kleist and wrote that reading him "fills me as if I were an old pig's bladder." Max Brod often compared Kafka to Kleist. Susan Sontag described Robert Walser as the "missing link" between Kleist and Kafka. Walser wrote a story called "Kleist in Thun" in which Kleist "surrenders to the catastrophe of being a poet" and is unnerved by Swiss countryside beauty. Kleist also wrote the unfinished Robert Guiscard and a play based on the Battle of the Teutoburg Forest (Die Hermannsschlacht).
