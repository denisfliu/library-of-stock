Belgian painter (1860–1949) known for grotesque imagery featuring masks and skeletons. His masterpiece Christ's Entry Into Brussels in 1889 — over four meters wide, now at the Getty Museum — shows a Mardi Gras procession under the banner 'Vive La Sociale.' A founding member of Les XX (Les Vingt), organized by Octave Maus after Ensor's The Oyster Eater was rejected by the L'Essor and Antwerp Salons. Associated with Symbolism and proto-Expressionism.

Summary of Facts

James Ensor (1860–1949) was a Belgian painter central to the development of Expressionism and Symbolism, known above all for his grotesque imagery featuring masks and skeletons. His masterpiece, Christ's Entry Into Brussels in 1889, is a monumental canvas over four meters wide — so large that Ensor could not see it all at once in his studio. It shows a Mardi Gras / Carnival procession of mask wearers largely ignoring Christ, who rides a donkey below a giant red banner reading 'Vive La Sociale.' In the foreground, a bishop with a massive red beard caricatures the critic Emile Littre while holding a baton; a sign nearby reads 'Doctrinaire Fanfares Always Succeed.' A roundel depicting the crucifixion hangs above figures with bloody hands, and frogs leap from a black cauldron at lower left; a skull-masked man in a green-banded top hat also appears at bottom left. The painting contains two incongruous vanishing points. Ensor included a self-portrait in a tall conical red hat at left (similar to his Pierrot painting), though some descriptions say he depicted himself as the title figure on the donkey. Stefan Jonsson called the painting 'society degree zero' and linked it to Bosch's Ship of Fools, arguing they share a 'red thread of madness.' The composition may have been inspired by a Verhas realist painting of schoolgirls at a royal wedding. The painting nearly caused Ensor's expulsion from Les XX (who refused to exhibit it) and was kept rolled up for thirty years. It is now housed in a dedicated room at the J. Paul Getty Museum in Los Angeles. Joel Pelletier created a modern version called 'American Fundamentalists.'

Ensor was a founder of Les XX (Les Vingt), the Belgian avant-garde group organized by critic Octave Maus, which also included Fernand Khnopff and Anna Boch (the only female member, who bought Van Gogh's Red Vineyard). Les XX was formed after Ensor's The Oyster Eater — depicting his sister Mitche at a table eating oysters — was rejected by both the L'Essor group and the Antwerp Salon. Ensor later split with Les XX over their adoption of pointillism (the dot-based technique pioneered by Seurat and Signac). His mother Marie owned a curio shop whose oriental vases and dolls he incorporated into paintings. He painted a portrait of his hairdresser's mother and altered it to surround her with grotesque masks — a motif he revisited in Self-Portrait with Masks, where the artist is the only unmasked face.

Other notable works include: Skeletons Fighting Over a Pickled Herring, in which two skeletons (one with a fuzzy black hat) play tug-of-war over a herring representing Ensor mocking his critics; Skeletons Warming Themselves (over a stove with the inscription 'no fire; will you have any tomorrow?'); Skeletons Fighting for the Body of a Hanged Man (man wears a 'civet' sign); Masks Confronting Death (Death in a white cloak and floppy red hat); The Dangerous Cooks (satirizing critics Octave Maus and Edmond Picard); The Vile Vivisectors (depicting a monkey's crucifixion); Christ and the Critics (Ensor as Christ in an Ecce Homo scene); Russian Music (portrait of Anna Boch); Scandalized Masks (grandmother entering a room with his despondent alcoholic father); and a print series including The Seven Deadly Sins Dominated by Death. He also painted a self-portrait imagining himself at age 100, menaced by a spider on a divan. His painting The Man of Sorrows was referenced in the film Oldboy by Park Chan-wook.
