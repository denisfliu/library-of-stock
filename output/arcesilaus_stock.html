Summary of Facts

Arcesilaus was the founder of Academic skepticism and the sixth scholarch of Plato's Academy, where he inaugurated the 'Middle' Academy phase. His central philosophical position was akatalepsia, the idea that nothing can be known with certainty. This attitude toward knowledge may explain why he did not preserve his own writings, since he held all knowledge to be impossible.

One of Arcesilaus's surviving fragments attacks Zeno of Citium's causal theory of perception. He also pointed to a passage in Plato's Theaetetus to illustrate his belief that philosophers could only help people with knowledge, akin to a midwife helping a delivery, drawing on Socrates's comparison of the mind to an aviary.

Arcesilaus was a teacher of Carneades, though scholars disagree over whether Arcesilaus or Carneades first promulgated akatalepsia. The Academic skeptical tradition he founded continued through the 'New Academy' under Carneades and eventually ended during the First Mithridatic War when Philo of Larissa fled to Rome.
