English poet and Poet Laureate, known for his animal poems and his turbulent marriage to Sylvia Plath. His major collections include The Hawk in the Rain, Crow, and Birthday Letters.
Birthday Letters

Hughes's 1998 collection that broke his 35-year silence about his relationship with Sylvia Plath. The collection dramatizes their marriage and includes poems addressed to Plath as well as poems about his other wife Assia Wevill. It is by far the most frequently referenced Hughes work in quizbowl, appearing in virtually every question about him as either a central topic or a giveaway identifier.

The Hawk in the Rain

Hughes's first poetry collection, and the title poem within it. The poem describes a hawk that 'hangs still' at the 'master-fulcrum of violence' while its 'wings hold all creation in a weightless quiet.' At the end, 'the ponderous shires crash on' the hawk and 'mix his heart's blood with the mire of the land.' The collection also contains 'The Thought-Fox' and 'The Jaguar,' establishing Hughes's reputation as a poet of powerful animal imagery.

Crow

A dark, violent 1970 collection subtitled 'From the Life and Songs of the Crow,' written after Sylvia Plath's death. The title figure journeys through the universe in search of his female creator. The collection was illustrated by Leonard Baskin and draws on folklore and Christian imagery. Key poems include 'Examination at the Womb-Door,' 'Crow's First Lesson,' 'Crow's Account of the Battle,' 'Lineage,' and 'The Black Beast.' Max Porter's prose poem Grief is the Thing with Feathers features a fictional Ted Hughes scholar visited by an immature Crow.

Other Works and General

Hughes wrote many animal poems beyond his hawk and crow works, including 'An Otter' (describing otters as 'Four-legged yet water gifted, to outfish fish'), 'The Jaguar' (about an animal whose 'stride is wilderness of freedom'), 'Dick Straightup,' and 'Full Moon and Little Frieda.' He also wrote Tales from Ovid and Lupercal. Hughes was a fellow Yorkshireman of Simon Armitage, who edited an anthology of his poems in 2000. He collaborated with Seamus Heaney on the anthology The Rattle Bag. He translated Frank Wedekind's Spring Awakening from the German in the 1970s. He was from Yorkshire and was a friend of Barrie Cooke, with whom he and Heaney went fishing.

Summary of Facts

Ted Hughes (full name Edward James Hughes) was an English poet who served as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom. He is one of the most frequently clued modern British poets in quizbowl, appearing overwhelmingly in connection with his relationship to Sylvia Plath and his major poetry collections.

Hughes's most commonly referenced work is Birthday Letters (1998), a collection that broke his 35-year silence about his marriage to Plath. The collection includes poems addressed directly to Plath such as 'The Blue Flannel Suit' (set on the day Plath began teaching at Smith College), 'Last Letter,' and 'Red' (in which the speaker tells the addressee 'the jewel you lost was blue'). It also contains 'The Bee God,' in which the speaker says 'When you wanted bees I never dreamed / It meant your Daddy had come up out of the well.' The collection includes a poem about his other wife Assia Wevill, in which he wrote 'the dreamer in her had fallen in love with me.' After Hughes's death, Seamus Heaney wrote a poem titled 'On First Looking Into' Birthday Letters. Janet Malcolm's The Silent Woman chronicled the legacy dispute between advocates for Plath and Hughes.

Hughes's first collection, The Hawk in the Rain, contains the title poem in which a hawk 'hangs still' at the 'master-fulcrum of violence' while its 'wings hold all creation in a weightless quiet.' At the poem's end, 'the ponderous shires crash on' the hawk and 'mix his heart's blood with the mire of the land.' The collection also includes 'The Thought-Fox,' which analogizes the creative process to a fox entering the poet's mind — the fox sets 'neat prints into the snow' and brings a 'sudden sharp hot stink of fox' before entering 'the dark hole of the head.' 'The Thought-Fox' is described as Hughes's earliest poem. The collection also contains 'The Jaguar' (about an animal whose 'stride is wilderness of freedom'), used by J.M. Coetzee's character Elizabeth Costello to argue that poets can embody animals.

The companion hawk poem 'Hawk Roosting' features a hawk declaring 'I kill where I please because it is all mine' and 'It took the whole of Creation to produce my foot... Now I hold Creation in my foot.'

Crow (1970), subtitled 'From the Life and Songs of the Crow,' is a dark, violent collection written after Plath's death. Illustrated by Leonard Baskin, it draws on folklore and Christian imagery to create a mythology around the title figure, who journeys through the universe in search of his female creator. In 'Crow's First Lesson,' God tries to teach Crow to say 'love,' but he instead produces 'Man's bodiless prodigious head' and 'woman's vulva.' In 'Examination at the Womb-Door,' Crow responds to 'who is stronger than death?' with 'me, evidently.' 'Lineage' tells a creation myth replacing God with screaming, which 'begat Blood / Who begat Eye / Who begat Fear / Who begat Wing.' The Crow figure also 'flies the black flag of himself' after 'nailing heaven and earth together.' Max Porter's 2015 prose poem Grief is the Thing with Feathers features a fictional Ted Hughes scholar visited by an immature Crow after his wife's death.

Other notable Hughes works include Lupercal (a collection), Tales from Ovid, 'An Otter' (describing otters as 'Four-legged yet water gifted, to outfish fish'), and 'Dick Straightup' (in which the title man 'survives among hills' while 'the dust of Achilles and Cuchulain itches'). He also wrote 'Full Moon and Little Frieda,' a poem about his daughter in which 'The moon has stepped back like an artist gazing amazed at a work.'

Hughes was from Yorkshire, as was Simon Armitage, who edited an anthology of Hughes's poems in 2000. Hughes collaborated with Seamus Heaney on the poetry anthology The Rattle Bag, and the two went fishing with their mutual friend Barrie Cooke. Hughes translated Frank Wedekind's Spring Awakening from the German in the 1970s. He became Poet Laureate after John Betjeman's death when Philip Larkin refused the title. He was succeeded eventually by Carol Ann Duffy (via Andrew Motion). Hughes made major changes to Plath's collection Ariel, including moving the bee poems to the middle of the collection.
