American film director (1894–1973), a foundational figure of Hollywood and the Western genre. Known for Westerns starring John Wayne: Stagecoach (1939, first Monument Valley film), The Searchers (1956), The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance (1962), The Quiet Man (1952). Also directed The Grapes of Wrath (1940) with cinematographer Gregg Toland. Andrew Sarris placed him in the auteur theory 'pantheon.' Famous line: 'When the legend becomes fact, print the legend.'
The Searchers (1956)

John Wayne plays Ethan Edwards, who obsessively searches for his niece Debbie after she is captured by the Comanche (led by Scar). Jeffrey Hunter plays his companion Martin Pawley. Ethan often says 'That'll be the day' to Martin. The film famously begins and ends with shots of Ethan framed in a cabin doorway — he looks in at his brother's old home, then turns and walks away. Debbie is at one point nearly shot by Ethan when she says she wants to remain with the Comanche. A fistfight involving 'somebody's fiddle' delays Laurie's wedding. The film was shot in Monument Valley. It heavily influenced Lawrence of Arabia's mirage-at-a-well sequence. Zacharias Kunuk made an Inuit adaptation. Considered Ford's masterpiece.

The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance (1962)

James Stewart plays Ranse Stoddard, a senator who returns to the town of Shinbone for the funeral of his old friend Tom Doniphon (John Wayne). In flashback, it emerges that Tom (not Ranse) actually killed Liberty Valance, a whip-cracking villain. Tom addresses Ranse as 'pilgrim.' A black character named Pompey recites the Declaration of Independence in front of a portrait of Lincoln. In the Gold Room, a printing press is destroyed by Liberty Valance. At the end, when the newspaper editor learns the truth, he burns his notes and says: 'When the legend becomes fact, print the legend.' A still photograph from the 1880s party features Jack (Pompey) shooting a can of paint that explodes over Ranse. Stoddard returns to tell the conductor 'nothing's too good for the man who shot Liberty Valance.'

Stagecoach (1939)

John Ford's first film shot in Monument Valley and first collaboration with John Wayne. Apaches chase the title vehicle. The shot briefly goes out of focus as it zooms in to introduce a man who twirls a rifle in one hand and holds a saddle in the other (Wayne's introduction). A Southern gambler aims his last bullet at the woman beside him just before a bugle call sounds in the distance. In a dinner scene, all characters but one choose to sit apart from a certain woman. The negative is lost; extant releases use a positive print discovered by Peter Bogdanovich in Wayne's garage. The film has a major doorway motif like The Searchers.

The Quiet Man (1952)

John Wayne plays Sean Thornton, an Irish-American boxer who returns to Ireland after accidentally killing an opponent. He falls in love with a local woman. The climax is a ridiculously long fistfight between Sean Thornton and Will Danaher (Victor McLaglen) that begins in the countryside and ends miles away in Cohan's Bar. A fistfight in this film inspired a similar long fight in John Carpenter's They Live.

The Grapes of Wrath (1940) and Cinematographer Gregg Toland

Ford partnered with cinematographer Gregg Toland for The Long Voyage Home (1940, adaptation of three Eugene O'Neill plays) and The Grapes of Wrath (1940). The Grapes of Wrath was a massive adaptation of the Steinbeck novel. Toland's work on these Ford films helped develop deep focus cinematography — keeping foreground, middle ground, and background all clearly visible — before he applied it to Citizen Kane with Orson Welles. Andre Bazin admired Toland's deep focus. Ford also shot My Darling Clementine (with Henry Fonda as Wyatt Earp) in Monument Valley.

Style, Legacy, and Monument Valley

John Ford described his style as 'invisible technique,' though he later refused to discuss his work. He frequently used shots of trains, boats, or wagons to begin and end films. Monument Valley (on the Arizona-Utah border) served as the backdrop for Stagecoach, The Searchers, My Darling Clementine, and many other Ford Westerns. In an interview at Monument Valley, Ford angrily cut off Peter Bogdanovich's question about 'the point' of one of his films. Andrew Sarris placed Ford in the auteur theory 'pantheon' in his book American Cinema alongside Orson Welles and twelve other directors. The Cahiers du Cinema editors wrote an analysis of Ford's Young Mr. Lincoln. Ford's career included a transcontinental railroad epic depicting Irish, Italian, Chinese immigrants and African-Americans. An Austrian immigrant dancing in a music hall window is a characteristic early scene. Ford's fistfights are a recurring motif across many films (Donovan's Reef, The Searchers, The Quiet Man).

Summary of Facts

John Ford (1894–1973) was a foundational American film director, known primarily for Westerns starring John Wayne. His films are characterized by Monument Valley (Arizona/Utah) landscapes, a doorway motif, recurring fistfights, and what he called 'invisible technique.' He frequently used shots of trains, boats, or wagons to begin and end films.

The Searchers (1956) is often considered his masterpiece: John Wayne plays Ethan Edwards, who obsessively searches for his niece Debbie (captured by the Comanche leader Scar). Ethan often says 'That'll be the day' to companion Martin Pawley (Jeffrey Hunter). The film famously begins and ends with shots of Ethan framed in a cabin doorway. The Searchers heavily influenced Lawrence of Arabia's mirage-at-a-well sequence; Zacharias Kunuk later made an Inuit adaptation.

The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance (1962) stars James Stewart as senator Ranse Stoddard and John Wayne as rancher Tom Doniphon (who calls Ranse 'pilgrim'). Tom actually killed Liberty Valance, but Ranse got the credit. A black character named Pompey recites the Declaration of Independence before a Lincoln portrait. A printing press is destroyed by Liberty Valance. The climax's newspaper editor, learning the truth, burns his notes and says: 'When the legend becomes fact, print the legend.'

Stagecoach (1939) was Ford's first Monument Valley film and first Wayne collaboration; Apaches chase the title vehicle; the film's negative is lost, and extant copies come from a positive print found by Peter Bogdanovich in Wayne's garage. The Quiet Man (1952) features Wayne as Sean Thornton, a former boxer returning to Ireland, with a climactic long fistfight against Will Danaher (Victor McLaglen).

For The Grapes of Wrath (1940) and The Long Voyage Home (1940), Ford worked with cinematographer Gregg Toland, who pioneered deep focus before bringing it to Citizen Kane with Orson Welles. My Darling Clementine featured Henry Fonda as Wyatt Earp. Ford appears in Andrew Sarris's auteur theory 'pantheon' in American Cinema; Cahiers du Cinema analyzed Young Mr. Lincoln.
