Summary of Facts

Rainer Maria Rilke was a Bohemian-Austrian poet, one of the most significant German-language Poets of the modern era. His most famous work is the Duino Elegies, a cycle of ten poems written while staying at Duino Castle near Trieste as a guest of Princess Marie von Thurn und Taxis. An apparent voice in the wind inspired Rilke to begin the cycle, which he finished eight years later in a creative 'storm' at the Chateau de Muzot. The first elegy opens with the iconic line 'Who, if I cried out, would hear me among the Angelic Orders?' and declares 'beauty is nothing but the beginning of terror' and 'every Angel is terrifying.' William H. Gass wrote that these angels are 'what The Poet would be if he could free himself from human distraction' in his book Reading Rilke. The fifth elegy addresses Picasso's Family of Saltimbanques, asking 'But who are they, tell me, these Travellers?' The fourth elegy features the speaker gazing at a puppet stage and declaring 'finally, a real play!' when an angel brings the puppets to life, drawing on Kleist's 'On the Marionette Theatre.' The tenth elegy describes Lament leading a boy to the 'Mountains of Primal Grief' through a 'landscape of Lamentation' and a 'City of Grief,' with constellations like 'the Burning Book' and 'the Garland of Fruit' and 'the farthest billboard pasted with posters for Deathless.' The first elegy asks 'have you remembered Gaspara Stampa sufficiently yet?' and invokes the 'days of Tobias.' An urn inscribed 'Subrisio Saltat' appears. Friedrich Holderlin's elegies, such as 'Bread and Wine,' heavily inspired those of Rilke, and Rilke addressed Holderlin as 'the most wandering spirit of all.' Robert Musil wrote a memorial speech for Rilke. Ludwig Wittgenstein, via the magazine editor Ludwig von Ficker, gave Rilke 20,000 crowns to support his work.

Sonnets to Orpheus is a cycle of 55 sonnets written during the same creative storm at the Chateau de Muzot and dedicated to Vera Knoop (his daughter's friend). The first sonnet opens 'There rose a Tree. O pure transcendence!' and the collection describes 'new beginnings, intimations, changes' arising from silence. One poem instructs the reader to 'erect no gravestone' for Orpheus. Rilke also wrote the standalone poem 'Orpheus. Eurydice. Hermes,' in which Eurydice is 'already loosened like long hair' and 'already root,' and does not recognize her lover.

'Archaic Torso of Apollo' is one of Rilke's most famous individual poems, ending with the imperative 'You must change your life.' It describes a headless statue whose 'legendary head with eyes like ripening fruit' is lost but whose torso 'glistens like a wild beast's fur' and is 'suffused with brilliance inside,' with a 'dark center where procreation flared.' Stephen Mitchell's translation is particularly well-known. Ocean Vuong responded with 'Torso of Air' ('Suppose you do change your life'), and the line also inspired Mary Oliver's 'The Swan.'

'The Panther' is the most famous of Rilke's 'thing-poems' (Dinggedichte), describing a caged creature to whom 'it seems there are a thousand bars and behind a thousand bars no world.' The panther 'paces in cramped circles' in a 'ritual dance around a center / in which a mighty will stands paralyzed,' and 'the curtain of the pupil soundlessly parts' as 'an image enters in' and 'plunges into the heart and is gone.' Rilke wrote it after visiting a Paris zoo at the instruction of his mentor, Auguste Rodin.

Letters to a Young Poet is a series of ten posthumously published letters to the military cadet Franz Xaver Kappus, advising 'Nobody can advise or help you. Go into yourself' and 'ask yourself in the most silent hour of your night: must I write?' The letters emphasize solitude ('love your solitude'), beauty, and terror. Rilke praised the Danish author J.P. Jacobsen and the Bible as the two 'indispensable' books. Modern audiences often view it as a self-help book.

The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge is Rilke's only novel, a fictional diary of a Danish nobleman who moves to a squalid flat in Paris to begin a career as a writer. The narrator recounts how an iron amulet became embedded in the rotting chest of the mad king Charles VI, and tells the story of the False Dmitri. The novel ends with an interpretation of the prodigal son story claiming he wanted to know what it is like not to be loved. M.D. Herter Norton translated it. It was influenced by J.P. Jacobsen's Niels Lyhne.

The Book of Hours includes sections titled The Book of Monastic Life, The Book of Pilgrimage, and The Book of Poverty and Death. It begins 'I live my life in circles that grow wide / And endlessly unroll' and was drawn from Rilke's experiences of Orthodox Christianity while traveling in Russia. It was dedicated to Lou Andreas-Salome. Rilke wrote the first drafts in a copy of Jacobsen's Novellas.

'The Spanish Dancer' describes a woman who 'fans her dress into passionate flames' and whose 'naked arms' are like 'rattlesnakes.' A dark room 'all at once is completely fire.' She is likened to a 'sulphur match.' Other poems include 'The Carousel' (with 'a white elephant' among animals in a 'blind and breathless game'), 'Autumn Day' ('Lord it is time. Great was the summer feast'; 'He who has not built his house, will not start now'), 'Going Blind' (a woman who 'sat at tea just like the others'), 'Lament' ('I would like to step out of my heart'), 'Portrait of My Father as a Young Man' ('a quickly disappearing photograph in my more slowly disappearing hand'), 'Requiem for a Friend' (dedicated to Paula Modersohn-Becker: 'practice on this: letting each other go'), 'Extinguish Thou My Eyes,' and French poems about roses quoted by Cy Twombly. W.H. Auden called Rilke the 'Santa Claus of loneliness' and Denise Levertov wrote 'Variations on a Theme by Rilke.' John Berryman flatly asserted 'Rilke was a jerk' in his Dream Songs. Rilke served as Rodin's secretary in Paris, and Rachel Corbett wrote the biography You Must Change Your Life about their friendship. In the summer of 1926, Rilke traded letters with Marina Tsvetaeva and Boris Pasternak.
