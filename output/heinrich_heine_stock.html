General / Biographical

Heine is identified as a German Romantic/lyric poet. He left Germany in 1831 for Paris, and Karl Kraus blamed him for infecting German literature with the 'feuilleton' style. He coined the word 'Lisztomania.' He used the term 'mattress-grave' for his deathbed, having been confined for the last eight years of his life with a mysterious paralytic illness. His Travel Pictures opens with a walking Tour through the Harz mountains. Hannah Arendt praised his integration of Yiddish into German literature, identifying him as the beginning of the 'hidden tradition' of the schlemiel. He wrote an anti-censorship text whose only words are 'censors' and 'idiots.' He was a pioneer of the Innerlichkeit style alongside Goethe.

Summary of Facts

Heinrich Heine (Christian Johann Heinrich Heine) is a German Romantic poet best known for his poem 'Die Lorelei' and his poetry collection Buch der Lieder (Book of Songs). He is one of the most frequently clued German-language Poets in quizbowl.

'Die Lorelei' is by far his most clued individual poem, appearing in nearly every question about Heine. It describes a golden-haired maiden sitting on the Rhine who lures sailors to their deaths with her spellbinding singing. The poem opens with lines commonly translated as 'I do not know what it means / That I should feel so sad' as the narrator is haunted by the legend. Gold is a recurring motif in the poem, shared by the siren's jewelry, hair, and comb. The legend first appeared in Clemens Brentano's lay 'Zu Bacharach am Rheine.' During the Nazi period, the poem was officially declared written by an 'unknown author' because of Heine's Jewish heritage. Franz Liszt set it to music, and the Danish poet Sophus Claussen translated it.

Buch der Lieder (Book of Songs) is Heine's most famous collection, containing sections including 'Lyrisches Intermezzo,' 'Youthful Sorrows,' 'Die Heimkehr,' and 'Die Nordsee' (The North Sea). The Lyrisches Intermezzo poems were set to music by Robert Schumann in his song cycle Dichterliebe. The collection ends with 'Die Nordsee,' consisting of two cycles that mock the antiquated Greek gods in 'The Song of the Oceanides' and end with 'In Port.' Heine was a pioneer of the Innerlichkeit style, along with Goethe.

Germany: A Winter's Tale is a long satirical poem about a journey Heine took from Paris to Hamburg. In it, the speaker relives Hermann's victory at the Battle of Teutoburg Forest, imagines explaining Politics to a rewoken Frederick Barbarossa, mocks the construction of Koln Cathedral, and describes being stalked by an axe-carrying 'special devil.' Heine gave it a Shakespearean subtitle.

Atta Troll is a 27-canto mock epic about a dancing bear that escapes and is pursued through the Pyrenees by the witch Uraka and her son Laskaro. The bear is ultimately shot by Laskaro. It ridicules Ferdinand Freiligrath and was also given a Shakespearean subtitle (A Midsummer Night's Dream).

Heine's play Almansor is the source of the famous quote 'Where they burn books, they will ultimately burn people,' set during the burning of Granada during the Spanish Inquisition. This line is frequently clued independently of the rest of the play.

Other notable poems include 'The Grenadiers,' in which a soldier wants to be buried in full uniform to rise from the grave and defend Napoleon; one grenadier says his wife and child should 'go beg' because Napoleon has been taken. 'Du bist wie eine Blume' ('You are like a flower') has the speaker satirically wishing to lay his hands on the addressee's head and pray to keep her 'so comely and beautiful and pure.' 'Morphine' concludes with 'Sleep is good, death is better -- surely the best would be never to have been born.' 'Death, that is the cool night / Life is the sultry day' is the penultimate poem of one of his sets. 'The Silesian Weavers' begins 'Their gloom-enveloped eyes are tearless.' Another poem describes two knights named Crapulinski and Waschlapski fighting the 'tyrant Muscovite' for Polish freedom. His poem 'Wo?' ('Where?'), asking 'Shall I lie in desert reaches, / Buried by a stranger's hand?', was engraved on three sides of his tomb in Paris.

Heine also wrote the Romanzero collection, whose third book 'Hebrew Melodies' includes a poem about Jehuda ben Halevy and describes a 'mysterious stir and whisper' as the steward lights lamps. Hannah Arendt praised Heine's integration of Yiddish into German literature, identifying him as the beginning of the 'hidden tradition' of the schlemiel.

Biographically, Heine coined the word 'Lisztomania.' He left Germany in 1831 for Paris, and his flight to France was blamed by Karl Kraus for infecting German literature with the 'feuilleton' style. He used the term 'mattress-grave' for the bed he was confined to for the last eight years of his life due to a mysterious paralytic illness. His Travel Pictures opens with an account of a walking Tour through the Harz mountains. He also wrote an anti-censorship poem whose only words are 'censors' and 'idiots.' Heine asserted that a Frenchman can 'scarcely get any idea' of the Nibelungenlied.
