Summary of Facts

The Venerable Bede (c. 672-735) is an 8th-century Northumbrian monk who spent nearly his entire life at Wearmouth-Jarrow, called 'the father of English history.' His masterwork, the five-book Ecclesiastical History of the English People (completed around 731 CE), chronicles Christianity's spread in Britain and is dedicated to King Ceolwulf, whom the preface calls 'most glorious king,' while thanking Albinus for assistance. The History preserves Gregory the Great's 'not Angles, but Angels' pun (inspired by seeing Anglo-Saxon slave boys), the Synod of Whitby's Easter controversy between Roman and Irish dating practices, the story of the illiterate cowherd Caedmon who miraculously received the gift of song, and the famous comparison of human life to a sparrow flying through a warm mead hall. It also praises Edwin of Northumbria as the ideal Christian king, details the martyrdom of St. Alban, records the naming of Augustine as the first Archbishop of Canterbury, describes the Dalreudians and a king 'from the female royal race,' and preserves Saint Aidan's founding of Lindisfarne at King Oswald's behest.

Bede's On the Reckoning of Time is a computus work that deals with Easter date calculations, and his writings popularized the BC/AD dating system invented by Dionysius Exiguus. He was the first person to correlate the movement of tides to stages of the moon. He was falsely accused of heresy by Bishop Wilfrid's followers over his Easter calculations, and he explained Easter customs through the Germanic goddess Eostre.

Beyond the Ecclesiastical History, Bede wrote verse and prose hagiographies of Saint Cuthbert (a hermit from Melrose Abbey who became Bishop of Lindisfarne), adopted Paulinus of Nola's hagiography of Saint Felix of Nola, and praised another Saint Felix from Burgundy for building a school in East Anglia. His five-line Death Song ponders 'good and evil within his soul' and states 'before the unavoidable journey, no one is wiser.' He was a teacher of Archbishop Egbert and an intellectual forebear of Alcuin of York. He died on Ascension Day 735 after asking for 'some pepper, and napkins, and some incense.' He was declared a Doctor of the Church by Leo XIII — the only native of Great Britain so honored. Joan of Arc was questioned about prophecies attributed to Bede during her cross-examination at Poitiers, and a medieval writer named Notker the Stammerer fraudulently claimed Bede had written an entire book.
