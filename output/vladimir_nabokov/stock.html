Russian-American author and one of the most frequently clued literary figures. Known for ornate prose, unreliable narrators, and wordplay. His most famous novel is Lolita, about pedophile Humbert Humbert's obsession with 12-year-old Dolores Haze. Pale Fire is structured as a 999-line poem by fictional poet John Shade with commentary by Charles Kinbote. Also known for his literal translation of Pushkin's Eugene Onegin (which sparked a feud with Edmund Wilson), the memoir Speak, Memory, and the academic satire Pnin. A lepidopterist, chess enthusiast, and Cornell professor whose wife Vera managed his academic duties.
Lolita

Nabokov's best-known novel (1955), narrated by the unreliable Humbert Humbert, a pedophile obsessed with the 12-year-old 'nymphet' Dolores Haze (Lolita). Humbert's childhood love Annabel died of typhus, named for Poe's 'Annabel Lee.' The playwright Clare Quilty pursues Lolita and is eventually murdered by Humbert. A poem in the novel calls Lolita 'Wanted, wanted' and ends 'the rest is rust and stardust.' Humbert hands over a death sentence resembling T.S. Eliot's 'Ash Wednesday' before shooting Quilty. Stanley Kubrick adapted it as a 1962 film; Nabokov wrote a 400-page screenplay that Kubrick heavily edited to conform to the Hays Code. The poster depicts heart-shaped sunglasses. Vera Nabokov stopped her husband from burning the manuscript on several occasions. Azar Nafisi's memoir is titled 'Reading Lolita in Tehran.' The poem 'Lilith' is sometimes read as a precursor. Nabokov anagrammed himself as Vivian Darkbloom within the novel. Wikipedia

Pale Fire

A novel structured as a 999-line poem by the fictional poet John Shade, with a foreword and commentary by the unreliable narrator Charles Kinbote, who claims to be the exiled King Charles II of Zembla ('The Beloved'). The poem opens 'I was the shadow of the waxwing slain' (inspired by a Robert Frost image of an owl banking past window glass). Kinbote argues the unwritten thousandth line mirrors the first. Shade is shot by the supposedly insane assassin Jack Grey, who was actually aiming for a former king. Shade's ugly daughter Hazel (daughter of Sybil) drowns herself in an icy lake after an unsuccessful date with Pete Dean. Shade plays chess with his wife after Hazel's drowning. Kinbote and Shade play 'word golf' (going from 'live' to 'dead' in five). The imprisonment of 'The Beloved' is likened to a solus rex chess puzzle. Charles Botkin teaches at Wordsmith College. Eugene Onegin Translation / Notes on Prosody →

Nabokov's extremely literal, unrhymed translation of Pushkin's Eugene Onegin, which sparked a famous feud with his erstwhile friend Edmund Wilson. Wilson criticized the translation for 'a lack of common sense.' Nabokov removed the masculine and feminine rhymes from each of the 14-line stanzas. Accompanied by the essay 'Notes on Prosody,' an analysis of Russian and English verse. In Notes on Prosody, Nabokov writes about elision in English versus Russian. He also harshly criticized Walter Arndt's earlier translation. John Barth compared recomposing Beethoven's Sixth Symphony to Nabokov's translation in 'The Literature of Exhaustion.' Wikipedia

"Signs and Symbols"

One of Nabokov's most anthologized short stories. An elderly couple's institutionalized son suffers from 'referential mania' — he thinks 'everything is a cipher and of everything he is the theme.' The couple tries to bring fruit jellies to their son at a sanatorium. The story ends with an unnamed phone ominously ringing a third time after two misdials from a girl asking for 'Charlie.' The couple reads the labels of jelly jars as the phone rings. Nabokov implied there were 'two plots' in the story. Wikipedia

"The Vane Sisters"

Lectures on Literature / Lectures on Russian Literature

Collections of Nabokov's university lectures. Lectures on Literature includes maps of Leopold Bloom's Dublin and sketches of the beetle Gregor Samsa. Nabokov urged readers to create detailed maps of Mansfield Park and Dr. Jekyll's London. He termed Austen's technique with Fanny Price the 'knight's move' and stressed the 'play theme' in Mansfield Park. He dismissed 'bad readers' who use fiction to learn about 'life in gay Paree or in sad Russia.' He consistently rated Tolstoy as great, Turgenev as 'pleasant,' and Dostoevsky as 'mediocre,' though he praised The Double. In Lectures on Russian Literature, he both praised and criticized Chekhov. He argued Gregor Samsa was 'merely a big beetle,' not a cockroach. He created a count of 'Victories and Defeats' in Don Quixote lectures to refute Krutch's claim. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Nabokov is identified as a Russian-American author. He was a lepidopterist (butterfly collector), chess enthusiast, and synesthete. He taught at Cornell University (where Thomas Pynchon was his student) and at Wellesley College. His wife Vera handled his proctoring and office hours. Roman Jakobson resisted his appointment at Harvard, saying 'are we next to invite an elephant to be Professor of Zoology?' He quizzed students on traits of 'good' vs. 'bad' readers. He published early works under the pen name V. Sirin. His favorite word is 'mauve' (according to Ben Blatt). He lived at the Montreux Palace Hotel in Switzerland. He inspired postmodern writers including Don DeLillo. W.G. Sebald was influenced by him (appears in The Emigrants). He asked John Wayne 'what do you do for a living?' at a party.

Summary of Facts

Vladimir Nabokov is a Russian-American author and one of the most frequently clued literary figures in quizbowl, appearing across difficulties 7 through 10. His two most important works for quiz purposes are Lolita and Pale Fire, which between them account for the majority of giveaway and mid-level clues.

Lolita (1955) is Nabokov's best-known novel, narrated by the unreliable pedophile Humbert Humbert, who is obsessed with the twelve-year-old 'nymphet' Dolores Haze. Humbert's childhood love Annabel died of typhus and is named for Poe's 'Annabel Lee.' The playwright Clare Quilty pursues and kidnaps Lolita; Humbert ultimately murders Quilty after handing him a death sentence resembling T.S. Eliot's 'Ash Wednesday.' A poem within the novel calls Lolita 'Wanted, wanted' and ends with 'the rest is rust and stardust.' Stanley Kubrick adapted it as a 1962 film, for which Nabokov wrote a 400-page screenplay that was heavily edited to conform to the Hays Code. The film poster famously depicts heart-shaped sunglasses. Nabokov anagrammed himself as Vivian Darkbloom within the novel. Vera Nabokov stopped her husband from burning the manuscript on several occasions.

Pale Fire is structured as a 999-line poem by the fictional poet John Shade, with commentary by the unreliable narrator Charles Kinbote, who claims to be the exiled King Charles II of Zembla ('The Beloved'). The poem opens with 'I was the shadow of the waxwing slain,' an image inspired by Robert Frost's poem 'Questioning Faces.' Kinbote argues the unwritten thousandth line would have mirrored the first. Shade is shot by the assassin Jack Grey, who was actually aiming for a former king. Shade's daughter Hazel (daughter of Sybil) drowns in an icy lake after an unsuccessful date with Pete Dean. Shade plays chess with his wife afterward. Kinbote and Shade play 'word golf,' going from 'live' to 'dead' in five steps. The imprisonment of 'The Beloved' is likened to a solus rex chess puzzle. Charles Botkin teaches at Wordsmith College.

Nabokov's extremely literal translation of Pushkin's Eugene Onegin, which removed the masculine and feminine rhymes from each 14-line stanza, sparked a famous feud with his erstwhile friend Edmund Wilson. The accompanying essay 'Notes on Prosody' analyzes Russian and English verse and discusses elision. Nabokov also harshly criticized Walter Arndt's earlier translation.

Speak, Memory is Nabokov's autobiography. It opens with 'The cradle rocks above an abyss' and discusses his governess Mademoiselle O (an old fat swan presages her death), his passion for butterfly-collecting, and his synesthesia. The final chapter is a second-person address to his wife Vera.

Pnin follows the Russian emigre professor Timofey Pnin at fictional Waindell College, baffled by 'unpredictable America.' He boards the wrong train for a lecture, gives a football to the drawing savant Victor (son of his ex-wife), and is confused between football and soccer. Squirrels appear as portents of misfortune throughout, linked to his former lover Mira Belochkin ('squirrel' in Russian) who died at Buchenwald. His colleague Leonard Blorenge reads no French despite being Chair of French Literature.

'Signs and Symbols' is one of Nabokov's most anthologized stories. An elderly couple's institutionalized son suffers from 'referential mania,' thinking everything in the world relates to him. The story ends with a phone ominously ringing a third time, and Nabokov implied the story contains 'two plots.'

'The Vane Sisters' features an acrostic hidden in the final paragraph, revealed by the first letters of each word, which spells a message from the ghosts of Cynthia and Sybil Vane involving icicles and a parking meter. Nabokov had to explain the acrostic to The New Yorker editor after they rejected the story.

'Spring in Fialta' follows narrator Victor's memories of a love affair with Nina at a Mediterranean resort, with circus posters throughout prefiguring Nina's death in a car crash with a circus truck.

The Defense (The Luzhin Defense) follows chess player Luzhin, mentored by the ruthless Valentinov, through a disastrous match against Italian grandmaster Turati, after which Luzhin jumps from a 'frosted window.'

Ada, or Ardor is set on the planet Anti-Terra (Demonia), where siblings Van Veen and Ada fall in love. It opens with a parody of Tolstoy: 'all happy families are more or less dissimilar.' Characters play a Scrabble-like game called 'Flavita,' and endnotes are by Vivian Darkbloom.

In his Lectures on Literature, Nabokov includes maps of Leopold Bloom's Dublin and sketches of Gregor Samsa as a beetle (arguing he is 'merely a big beetle'). He urged students to map Mansfield Park and Dr. Jekyll's London. He rated Tolstoy as great, Turgenev as 'pleasant,' and Dostoevsky as 'mediocre' (praising only The Double). He termed Austen's technique with Fanny Price's emotions the 'knight's move.'

Biographically, Nabokov was a lepidopterist, chess enthusiast, and synesthete. He taught at Cornell (where Thomas Pynchon was his student) and Wellesley. His wife Vera handled academic duties and edited his works. Roman Jakobson resisted his Harvard appointment, saying 'are we next to invite an elephant to be Professor of Zoology?' He published early works under the pen name V. Sirin. His favorite word was 'mauve.' He inspired postmodern writers including Don DeLillo and John Barth, and W.G. Sebald was influenced by him. Phrases from his works appear in the replicant baseline tests in Blade Runner 2049.
