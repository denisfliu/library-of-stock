German philosopher and Young Hegelian who wrote The Essence of Christianity, arguing that 'theology is anthropology' - the concept of God is an anthropomorphic projection of the mind. Known as the bridge between Hegel and Marx, and as the subject of Marx's 11 Theses on Feuerbach.
The Essence of Christianity

Feuerbach's most famous work argues that religious ideals are externalizations of features inherent in humans, and that the reverence we have for God is really reverence for Man the species. It draws on D.F. Strauss's The Life of Jesus Critically Examined and proclaims 'theology is anthropology.' The book identifies humanity itself as the 'essence of Christianity' and claims belief in revelation 'poisons' the innate moral sense. Max Stirner responded to it in The Ego and Its Own, and Bruno Bauer attacked it in an essay on Feuerbach's 'Characteristics.' It strongly influenced Marx's theory of alienation.

Marx's Theses on Feuerbach →

Marx wrote 11 short Theses on Feuerbach, the most famous being the 11th: 'Philosophers have only interpreted the world; the point is to change it.' The Theses criticize Feuerbach for restricting human essence to the individual rather than seeing it as a product of social relations, and for privileging theoretical activity over practice. They served as an outline for The German Ideology. Althusser considered the Theses as the marker of Marx's break from the 'Young' to the 'Mature' period.

General / Biographical

Feuerbach was a Young Hegelian who bridges Hegel and Marx. He argued for a new philosophy grounded in sensation in Principles for the Philosophy of the Future. His late work (On Spiritualism and Materialism; On Moral Philosophy) focused on the drive toward happiness. His review of Moleschott's book The Theory of Food yielded the punning dictum 'Man is what he eats' (a pun only in German: 'ist was er isst'). He also wrote that self-consciousness dependent on nature is the defining characteristic of mankind, declaring the impossibility of a 'religion of the elephants.' Summary of Facts

Ludwig Feuerbach appears in quizbowl as a German philosopher and Young Hegelian who bridges the thought of Hegel and Marx. His most famous work, The Essence of Christianity, argues that religious ideals are externalizations of features inherent in humans, proclaiming 'theology is anthropology.' The concept of God is an anthropomorphic projection of the mind, and reverence for God is really reverence for Man the species. The work draws on D.F. Strauss's The Life of Jesus Critically Examined and claims belief in revelation 'poisons' the innate moral sense. It was attacked by Max Stirner in The Ego and Its Own and by Bruno Bauer.

Feuerbach is perhaps best known through Marx's 11 Theses on Feuerbach, the last of which declares 'Philosophers have only interpreted the world; the point is to change it.' Marx criticized Feuerbach for restricting human essence to the individual and for privileging theoretical activity. The Theses served as an outline for The German Ideology. Althusser considered the Theses as marking Marx's transition from 'Young' to 'Mature' philosophy.

Biographically, Feuerbach coined the punning dictum 'Man is what he eats' (a pun only in German: 'ist was er isst') from a review of Moleschott's book. He argued for a philosophy grounded in sensation in Principles for the Philosophy of the Future, and his late works focused on the drive toward happiness. He declared the impossibility of a 'religion of the elephants,' stating that self-consciousness dependent on nature defines mankind. The Essence of Christianity strongly influenced Marx's theory of alienation.
