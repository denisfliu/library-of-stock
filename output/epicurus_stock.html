Ancient Greek philosopher (341-270 BCE) who founded Epicureanism, teaching at 'the Garden' in Athens. Argued that pleasure (especially the absence of pain) is the highest good. Key doctrines: atomism with the clinamen (swerve), ataraxia and aponia, the tetrapharmakos, the problem of evil, and three criteria of truth. His follower Lucretius wrote De Rerum Natura. Pierre Gassendi revived his thought in the early modern period.
Atomism, the Swerve, and Physics

Epicurus's physics derived mostly from Democritus but introduced key innovations. He formulated the concept of a necessary void making motion possible and argued for the material nature of the soul. His follower Lucretius proposed that atoms spontaneously undergo clinamen (swerve), creating randomness and free will. Epicurus conjectured that gods occupy areas devoid of atoms called metakosmia. He used the example of a square tower appearing round to argue all sense perception is true (though judgment can err). Unlike Democritus, Epicurus believed atoms possess weight. Stephen Greenblatt's The Swerve discusses the rediscovery of Lucretius's poem.

Ataraxia, Aponia, and Ethics

Epicurus argued that the goal of life is eudaimonia, achieved through ataraxia (freedom from distress) and aponia (freedom from pain). He differentiated happiness from mere pleasure. The tetrapharmakos ('fourfold remedy') argues against fearing gods and death, that what is good is easy to get, and what is terrible easy to endure. His argument against fearing death: 'when we are, death is not come, and when death is come, we are not.' He divided pleasure into 'static' and 'kinetic' types. He suggested eating simple foods because those accustomed to luxury are likely to lose it. His school dominated over that of Aristippus of Cyrene.

The Garden, Letters, and Community

Epicurus taught at 'the Garden,' his school in Athens whose gate inscription reportedly read 'Stranger, here you will do well to tarry.' He enjoyed a simple diet of barley bread and water. His surviving letters (to Herodotus, Menoeceus, and Pythocles) are preserved in Book X of Diogenes Laertius's Lives. The Letter to Herodotus summarizes physics including atomism and three criteria of truth (aisthesis, pathe, prolepseis). The Letter to Menoeceus contains ethical teachings. Followers celebrated Eikas on the twentieth of each month. Pierre Gassendi's commentaries led an early modern revival.

Problem of Evil and Reception

Epicurus is credited with the problem of evil (his 'namesake paradox'), asking why God allows suffering if omnipotent and benevolent. David Hume popularized this as a trilemma in Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion. Sextus Empiricus argued against Epicurus's idea that everything perceptible is true. Epicureans were compared to the Indian Carvaka school. Tolstoy discussed Epicureanism in A Confession.

Summary of Facts

Epicurus was an ancient Greek philosopher (341-270 BCE) who founded one of the major Hellenistic philosophical schools. He taught at 'the Garden' in Athens, living simply on barley bread and water. His physics derived from Democritus's atomism but introduced the crucial concept of clinamen (swerve), in which atoms spontaneously deviate, creating randomness and free will. His follower Lucretius expanded on this in the six-book poem De Rerum Natura.

Epicurus's ethical philosophy centered on achieving eudaimonia through ataraxia (freedom from distress) and aponia (freedom from pain). He argued that pleasure is the highest good but distinguished it from raw sensual pleasure, dividing pleasure into 'static' and 'kinetic' types. The tetrapharmakos, or fourfold remedy, instructed followers not to fear gods or death. His famous argument against fearing death was: 'when we are, death is not come, and when death is come, we are not.' He taught that prudence is the most precious virtue, and that 'empty sounds' not referring to real being are the main source of pain.

Epicurus established three criteria of truth: aisthesis (sense-perception), pathe (feelings), and prolepseis (preconceptions). He used the example of a square tower appearing round to argue that all sense perception is true, though judgment can err. He conjectured that gods occupy metakosmia (atom-free spaces) and have no concern for mortals.

His surviving letters to Herodotus, Menoeceus, and Pythocles are preserved in Book X of Diogenes Laertius. He is credited with the problem of evil, popularized by Hume. His school dominated over Aristippus's Cyrenaics. Pierre Gassendi led an early modern revival. Stephen Greenblatt's The Swerve discusses the rediscovery of Lucretius.
