American composer (1896–1981) and long-serving director of the Eastman School of Music (1924–1964). Known for tonal, Romantic-style music. Best known for his Symphony No. 2 ('Romantic'), used in numerous films and as the basis for the Interlochen Arts Camp theme. Also composed the opera Merry Mount and the First Symphony ('Nordic'). Made early contributions to musical set theory in his treatise Harmonic Materials of Modern Music.

General / Biographical

Howard Hanson directed the Eastman School of Music from 1924 to 1964 (forty years). He also wrote a 'Nordic' First Symphony and a seventh (final) symphony setting three ocean-themed Walt Whitman poems. In the 1920s–1930s he hosted an NBC radio series titled Pioneers in Music, highlighting works by American composers. He wrote Chorale and Alleluia for concert band (analyzed by Frederick Fennell). His treatise Harmonic Materials of Modern Music (1960) introduced isomeric (Z-)relations and early musical set theory, preceding Allen Forte's formal development of the field.

Summary of Facts

Howard Hanson (1896–1981) was an American composer who served as director of the Eastman School of Music in Rochester, New York from 1924 to 1964 — a tenure of forty years. He was a champion of American composers, hosting an NBC radio series titled Pioneers in Music in the 1920s and 1930s to spotlight their works. Hanson composed in a tonal, neo-Romantic style that stood apart from the modernist currents of his era.

His best-known work is the Symphony No. 2, nicknamed 'Romantic' (1930), composed in D-flat major and commissioned by Serge Koussevitzky for the 50th anniversary of the Boston Symphony Orchestra. Hanson partly composed it while teaching at the Interlochen Arts Camp in northern Michigan, and proposed a 'lyrical, haunting' theme from the symphony's first movement as the camp's theme — it has ended all Interlochen concerts ever since. The Interlochen theme features strings playing 'long G, triplet G A-flat B-flat, long G' over pulsing woodwinds and an expressive French horn call. The symphony gained wide cinematic fame: it was used without permission in the closing credits of Ridley Scott's Alien (1979), but Hanson decided not to sue 20th Century Fox. John Williams later cited it as an inspiration for his score for E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial. A theme from it also appeared in the animated film Boss Baby.

Hanson wrote seven symphonies in total. The First Symphony is nicknamed 'Nordic.' The seventh and last set three ocean-themed poems by Walt Whitman. His only opera, Merry Mount, spawned a concert suite including a love duet between the characters Marigold and Bradford followed by a 'Maypole Dance.'

Beyond composition, Hanson made early contributions to music theory. His 1960 treatise Harmonic Materials of Modern Music introduced the concept of isomeric (Z-)relations and laid foundational groundwork for musical set theory, predating Allen Forte's formal development of the field. His concert band work Chorale and Alleluia was analyzed by his Eastman colleague Frederick Fennell in A Conductor's Interpretive Analysis of Masterworks for Band.
