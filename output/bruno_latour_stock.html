Actor-Network Theory (ANT)

Latour co-developed actor-network theory (ANT) with Michel Callon and John Law. ANT explains relationships in organizations and society by giving equal emphasis to human and nonhuman actors according to the 'principle of generalized symmetry.' This framework treats both humans and technologies as 'actants' and was further elaborated in Science in Action and Reassembling the Social. Graham Harman has criticized Latour's 'flat ontology' within ANT for not distinguishing between objects. Latour is frequently cited by proponents of the 'ontological turn' in anthropology.

We Have Never Been Modern

Latour's most frequently cited work argues that the 'modern constitution' falsely separates nature and society (or culture and nature). He discusses acts of 'translation' between nature and culture and 'purification' between humans and non-humans as creating the 'Two Great Divides.' The book credits Thomas Hobbes and Robert Boyle with creating the modernist outlook. Latour argues for a 'Parliament of Things' in which natural and social phenomena are studied together. He contends that debates over environmental issues like the ozone layer and climate change are 'hybrid,' bridging culture, technology, and nature. The ontological turn in anthropology often cites this critique of the 'modern constitution.'

Science in Action and Laboratory Life

Laboratory Life, Latour's first major work conducted with Steve Woolgar at the Salk Institute, observed the construction of scientific facts in a laboratory setting. Science in Action (1987) argues that science and technology must be studied 'in the making,' stressing the social aspect over abstract criteria like falsifiability. It introduces the concept of 'blackboxing' — technology becoming invisible through its success. Latour describes the 'mobilization of the world' (making objects available for discourse) as the first of five loops allowing for successful circulation of reference. A Janus diagram shows: 'When things are true they hold' (left) vs. 'When things hold they start becoming true' (right). In Les Microbes, Latour discussed the politics of Louis Pasteur's spread of germ theory.

Reassembling the Social and 'Why Has Critique Run out of Steam?'

In Reassembling the Social, Latour argued that the word 'social' has become loaded with assumptions and tried to return to its original meanings. He re-evaluated the concept to strip away accumulated presuppositions. In the essay 'Why Has Critique Run out of Steam?', Latour reappraised his own work and argued that social criticism has lost legitimacy, advocating a return to the realist empiricism of William James. He cited Donna Haraway's notion that the philosopher of science should 'protect and care' for matters of fact rather than debunk them, drawn from her essay 'Situated Knowledges.' Peter Sloterdijk deeply influenced Latour in this reassessment.

Summary of Facts

Bruno Latour is a French philosopher, sociologist, and anthropologist of science, consistently identified in quizbowl through his development of actor-network theory and his critique of modernity's separation of nature and society.

Latour co-developed actor-network theory (ANT) with Michel Callon and John Law. ANT gives equal emphasis to human and nonhuman actors according to the 'principle of generalized symmetry,' treating technologies and people as equivalent 'actants' in networks. Graham Harman has criticized Latour's 'flat ontology' for not distinguishing between objects.

His most frequently cited work is We Have Never Been Modern, which argues that the 'modern constitution' falsely separates nature and society. He discusses acts of 'translation' between nature and culture and 'purification' between humans and non-humans as creating the 'Two Great Divides.' The book credits Thomas Hobbes and Robert Boyle with establishing this modernist outlook. Latour proposes a 'Parliament of Things' where natural and social phenomena are studied together, and argues that debates over issues like the ozone layer and climate change are 'hybrid.' Proponents of the ontological turn in anthropology, including Eduardo Viveiros de Castro, frequently cite Latour's critique of the 'modern constitution.'

Laboratory Life, Latour's first major work conducted with Steve Woolgar at the Salk Institute, observed the construction of scientific facts in a laboratory. Science in Action (1987) argues that science and technology must be studied 'in the making,' stressing social processes over abstract criteria like falsifiability. It introduces the concept of 'blackboxing' — technology becoming invisible through success — and the 'mobilization of the world' as the first of five loops allowing for circulation of reference. A notable Janus diagram contrasts 'When things are true they hold' with 'When things hold they start becoming true.' An expedition to the Amazon to study whether forest or savanna is encroaching is described in another work. In Les Microbes, Latour discussed the politics of Pasteur's spread of germ theory.

In Reassembling the Social, Latour argued that the word 'social' has become loaded with assumptions and tried to return it to original meanings. In 'Why Has Critique Run out of Steam?', he reappraised his own work, arguing that social criticism has lost legitimacy and advocating a return to William James's realist empiricism. He cited Donna Haraway's notion that the philosopher of science should 'protect and care' for matters of fact rather than debunk them. Peter Sloterdijk deeply influenced this reassessment.

Other works include Aramis, or the Love of Technology (about a failed French rapid transit system), Pandora's Hope, and Paris: Invisible City (an online 'sociological opera' about Paris's 'oligopticons').
