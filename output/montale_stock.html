Summary of Facts

Eugenio Montale was an Italian poet who won the 1975 Nobel Prize in Literature. He is considered a leading figure of Italian Hermeticism alongside Giuseppe Ungaretti and Salvatore Quasimodo, though Montale himself denied being a member of the movement. He was a frequent contributor to the Corriere della Sera.

His most important collection is Cuttlefish Bones (Ossi di Seppia). Its opening poem, 'The Lemon Trees,' contrasts 'laureate poets' who 'wander among plants of unfamiliar name' with ordinary people who know the richness of lemons, and describes how 'golden trumpets of radiance thunder their songs.' 'The Eel' addresses the creature as a 'siren of the freezing oceans,' a 'brief rainbow, iris, twin to the glance mounted within your lashes,' and 'love's arrow on earth,' asking 'Can you not see she is your sister?' The poem 'Don't ask us for the word' ends with 'All that we can tell you today is this: what we aren't, and what we don't wish for.' Other poems include 'Often have I met the ill of living' and 'Salt,' which ends 'once more on our lips we'll taste Greek salt.'

In The Occasions (Le Occasioni), the character Clizia was inspired by Irma Brandeis, with whom Montale shared an interest in Dante. 'The Hitler Spring' describes 'an eternal angel passed in flight' engulfed by 'an eerie emptiness lit and festooned with swastikas.' 'Arsenio' was translated into English by Robert Lowell. 'Dora Markus' features a title figure who owns an ivory mouse charm. Arshi Pipa compared Montale to Dante in a book-length treatise.
