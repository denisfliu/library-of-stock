Reclusive American poet known as the 'Belle of Amherst,' famous for her dashes, non-standard capitalization, and poems about death, nature, and the self. She corresponded with Thomas Wentworth Higginson and wrote the Master Letters to a mysterious addressee. Her poems were published posthumously by Mabel Loomis Todd.
Because I could not stop for Death

Dickinson's most famous poem, in which Death 'kindly stopped for me' in a carriage holding 'just Ourselves - / And Immortality.' The speaker 'passed the Fields of Gazing Grain' and 'the Setting Sun,' and 'first surmised the Horses' Heads / Were toward Eternity.' The speaker puts away 'My labor and my leisure too' for Death's 'Civility.'

My Life had stood - a Loaded Gun

A poem in which the speaker is a loaded gun carried by an 'Owner' through 'Sovereign Woods.' When the speaker fires, 'the Mountains straight reply' with an echo. The speaker has 'the power to kill, / Without - the power to die.' The smile produces 'cordial light' likened to a 'Vesuvian face.' Adrienne Rich wrote about this poem in 'Vesuvius at Home.'

Master Letters

Three passionate, ecstatic letters addressed to a mysterious 'Master' figure, in which Dickinson refers to herself as 'Daisy.' One opens 'If you saw a bullet hit a Bird - and he told you he was'nt shot.' Possible addressees include Charles Wadsworth, Samuel Bowles, and Otis Lord. The first letter suggests walking with the dog Carlo. Lucie Brock-Broido titled a poetry collection after them.

General / Biographical

Dickinson lived reclusively in Amherst, Massachusetts, earning the nickname 'Belle of Amherst.' She corresponded with Thomas Wentworth Higginson after reading his Atlantic Monthly essay 'Letter to a Young Contributor,' sending four poems and asking if her 'Verse is alive.' Her sister Lavinia found over a hundred poems posthumously; Mabel Loomis Todd (who never met Dickinson) edited the first volumes, standardizing her dashes and capitalization. Thomas Johnson's 1955 edition restored the original punctuation. Susan Gilbert was Dickinson's sister-in-law with whom she had a potentially romantic relationship revealed by infrared technology. Dickinson's herbarium contained 424 pressed flower specimens. She allegedly only wore white cotton dresses. Films include A Quiet Passion (with Cynthia Nixon) and Dickinson (Apple TV+, with Hailee Steinfeld). Camille Paglia called her the American Marquis de Sade in Sexual Personae.

Summary of Facts

Emily Dickinson is one of the most heavily clued American poets, known for her reclusive life in Amherst, Massachusetts (the 'Belle of Amherst'), her distinctive dashes and non-standard capitalization, and her posthumous publication history.

'Because I could not stop for Death' is her most frequently giveaway poem: Death 'kindly stopped for me' in a carriage with Immortality, they 'passed the Fields of Gazing Grain' and 'the Setting Sun,' and the speaker 'first surmised the Horses' Heads / Were toward Eternity.' 'I heard a Fly buzz - when I died' ends with 'I could not see to see' after a 'Blue - uncertain - stumbling Buzz,' with 'the Stillness in the Room.' 'I felt a Funeral, in my Brain' features 'Mourners, to and fro,' 'Boots of Lead,' and 'a Plank in Reason, broke.'

'There's a certain Slant of light' inflicts a 'Heavenly Hurt' with the 'Heft of Cathedral Tunes.' 'Hope is the thing with feathers' describes a bird that 'perches in the soul' and 'sings the tune without the words.' 'My Life had stood - a Loaded Gun' declares 'the power to kill, / Without - the power to die,' with a 'Vesuvian face' smile. 'Wild Nights - Wild Nights!' contains 'Rowing in Eden!' and 'Done with the Compass.'

Dickinson's Master Letters -- three passionate letters to a mysterious figure in which she calls herself 'Daisy' -- are frequently clued. Her correspondence with Thomas Wentworth Higginson began when she asked 'are you too deeply occupied to say if my Verse is alive?' Her sister Lavinia found her poems after her death; Mabel Loomis Todd edited the first volumes, standardizing dashes. Thomas Johnson's 1955 edition restored original punctuation. Susan Gilbert was her sister-in-law with a potentially romantic relationship. Infrared technology revealed dedications had been removed. Her herbarium contained 424 pressed flowers. She allegedly only wore white. Adrienne Rich wrote 'Vesuvius at Home' about her. Billy Collins wrote 'Taking Off Emily Dickinson's Clothes.' Camille Paglia called her the American Marquis de Sade in Sexual Personae.
