Summary of Facts

Lu Xun (1881-1936) is considered the father of modern Chinese literature. He abandoned a medical career after watching a lantern slide of a Chinese man about to be executed, deciding to heal China's spirit through literature. He wrote in vernacular Chinese (baihua).

'A Madman's Diary' is the first major work of modern Chinese literature in vernacular. Its narrator believes everyone around him is a cannibal and reads history only to find 'Eat People' written between the lines. It ends with the plea 'Save the children.' 'The True Story of Ah Q' follows a village bully who practices 'spiritual victories,' rationalizing every defeat as a moral win, before being executed during a revolution. 'Medicine' features a father buying a blood-soaked bread roll as a tuberculosis cure, the blood coming from an executed revolutionary. His story collections include Call to Arms and Wandering.
