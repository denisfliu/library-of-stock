The Faerie Queene

Spenser's unfinished allegorical epic poem in six books, written for Queen Elizabeth I (represented as Gloriana, the title character, and as the huntress Belphoebe). Book I features the Redcrosse Knight (embodying Holiness), who is tricked by the sorcerer Archimago into leaving the lady Una. Redcrosse slays Error and meets Fradubio, turned into a tree by the witch Duessa. Book III features the female knight Britomart, who sees 'be bold, be bold' above a door in the house of the sorcerer Busirane. Book IV concerns the friendship of Sir Cambell and Sir Triamond. Book VI features Calidore searching for the Blatant Beast. The Mutabilitie Cantos are an unfinished addition. Spenser introduced the Spenserian stanza (ABABBCBC + alexandrine) for this work. He explained its allegory as a 'darke conceit' in a letter to Sir Walter Raleigh. Milton called Spenser a 'sage and serious' author and 'a better teacher than Scotus or Aquinas' in Areopagitica. Byron's Childe Harold's Pilgrimage and Keats's Eve of St. Agnes use the Spenserian stanza. Spenser originally planned 24 books but fell far short.

Amoretti

Spenser's sonnet cycle of 89 (or 81) sonnets dedicated to Elizabeth Boyle, using his namesake Spenserian sonnet form with interlocking quatrains. Sonnet 75 ('One day I wrote her name upon the strand') is most frequently clued: the speaker writes his lover's name in the sand, the tide washes it away, and he responds 'Our love shall live, and later life renew' and 'let baser things devise / To die in dust, but you shall live by fame.' Sonnet 1 opens 'Happy ye leaves when as those lily hands.' The sonnet about fire and ice states 'My love is like to ice, and I to fire.' Published with Epithalamion. Entries correspond to daily readings from the Book of Common Prayer.

Epithalamion and Other Works

Epithalamion is a marriage ode written for Spenser's wedding to Elizabeth Boyle, published with Amoretti. It has 24 stanzas (the number of books originally planned for The Faerie Queene), each ending with a variation of 'The woods shall to me answer, and my Eccho ring.' It invokes the muses and includes 'Open the temple gates unto my love.' The Shepheardes Calender consists of eclogues tracing 12 months of rural life as described by Colin Clout and fellow shepherds, deliberately using archaic spelling. The character Colin Clout was created by John Skelton and reused by Spenser. The mysterious 'E.K.' narrates/annotates the work and heralds Spenser as a 'new poet.' Spenser published under the pseudonym 'Immerito.' 'Prothalamion' repeats 'Sweet Thames, run softly, till I end my song.' Milton praised Spenser in Areopagitica.

Summary of Facts

Edmund Spenser was an Elizabethan English poet best known for the allegorical epic The Faerie Queene, the sonnet cycle Amoretti, and the marriage ode Epithalamion.

The Faerie Queene is an unfinished six-book epic allegorizing Queen Elizabeth I as Gloriana and Belphoebe. Book I features the Redcrosse Knight (Holiness) who is tricked by the sorcerer Archimago, slays the monster Error, and meets Fradubio, turned into a tree by the witch Duessa. Book III introduces the female knight Britomart, who sees 'be bold, be bold' in Busirane's house. Book IV concerns Sir Cambell and Sir Triamond's friendship. Book VI features Calidore hunting the Blatant Beast. The unfinished Mutabilitie Cantos describe 'the ever-whirling wheel of Change.' Spenser introduced the Spenserian stanza (eight lines of iambic pentameter plus an alexandrine, ABABBCBC C) for this work, later used by Byron in Childe Harold's Pilgrimage and Keats in The Eve of St. Agnes. Milton called Spenser 'a better teacher than Scotus or Aquinas.'

Amoretti is an 89-sonnet cycle dedicated to Elizabeth Boyle, using the Spenserian sonnet with interlocking quatrains. Sonnet 75 ('One day I wrote her name upon the strand') is the most frequently clued: the tide washes away the name, but the speaker declares 'Our love shall live, and later life renew.' Sonnet 1 opens 'Happy ye leaves when as those lily hands.' Published with Epithalamion, a 24-stanza marriage ode ending each stanza with 'The woods shall to me answer, and my Eccho ring.'

The Shepheardes Calender consists of 12 eclogues tracing pastoral life through Colin Clout (a character borrowed from John Skelton), annotated by the mysterious 'E.K.' and using deliberate archaic spelling. 'Prothalamion' repeats 'Sweet Thames, run softly, till I end my song.'
