Aida

Grand opera in four acts by Giuseppe Verdi, with a libretto by Antonio Ghislanzoni. Commissioned for the opening of the Khedivial Opera House in Cairo; premiered in Cairo in 1871. The opera's premiere was delayed because scenery and costumes were stuck in Paris during the Siege of Paris. Set in ancient Egypt, it follows Aida, an Ethiopian princess enslaved at the Egyptian court, and her love for the Egyptian military commander Radames. Their love triangle with Egyptian princess Amneris drives the drama. The opera's title character is represented by an opening leitmotif of 'short A, A-sharp, B, C-sharp, B.' The Triumphal Scene ('Grand March') in Act II celebrates Radames's military victory. In the finale, Radames is entombed alive for treason; Aida chooses to die with him, singing the duet 'La fatal pietra.' Roberto Alagna famously walked offstage during a La Scala performance after being booed by the audience (replaced by Antonello Palombi in street clothes). Angel Blue pulled out of a 2022 Verona Arena performance to protest soprano Anna Netrebko's use of blackface to portray Aida. Leontyne Price's 1985 Met farewell performance was as Aida; she had also been the first African American to sing a leading role at La Scala in this role (1960). Anja Harteros sang the title role in a 2016 Gramophone Award-winning recording conducted by Antonio Pappano. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Summary of Facts

Aida is a grand opera in four acts by Giuseppe Verdi, with a libretto by Antonio Ghislanzoni, premiering in Cairo in 1871 (commissioned for the opening of the Khedivial Opera House). The opera's premiere was famously delayed because scenery and costumes were trapped in Paris during the Siege of Paris (the Franco-Prussian War).

The opera is set in ancient Egypt and centers on Aida, an Ethiopian princess held as a slave at the Egyptian court. She is in love with Radames, a young Egyptian military commander who leads a campaign against Ethiopia. The opera's title character is identified by an opening leitmotif of 'short A, A-sharp, B, C-sharp, B.' A love triangle forms between Aida, Radames, and the Egyptian princess Amneris, who also loves Radames. In Act II, during the spectacular Triumphal Scene (Grand March) celebrating Radames's victory, Amneris tricks Aida into admitting her love for Radames. This scene also features a famous trumpet fanfare melody (beginning short E-flat, long A-flat, triplet B-flat, E-flat, B-flat), and the chorus shouts 'Guerra, guerra, guerra!' A third-act soprano aria, 'O patria mia,' has Aida weeping that she will never see her Ethiopian homeland again; the aria climaxes on a sustained pianissimo high C followed by falling sixteenth-note thirds into the words 'non ti vedrò, non ti vedrò mai più.' Her other key aria is 'Ritorna vincitor,' in which she is torn between wishing Radames to triumph and loyalty to her father Amonasro and Ethiopia; it concludes with the plea 'Numi, pietà.' The third act opens with the first violins playing quiet staccato sixteenth-note Gs sweeping up and down through four octaves.

In the finale, Radames is tried for treason in a Judgement Scene where he refuses to speak in his defense while Amneris sings 'L'aborrita rivale a me sfuggia.' He is entombed alive beneath the Temple of Vulcan. Aida has hidden herself in the tomb to die with him; together they sing the duet 'La fatal pietra.'

The opera carries a rich performance history. Roberto Alagna famously walked off the La Scala stage after being booed by the loggionisti during a performance as Radames, replaced mid-scene by tenor Antonello Palombi in street clothes. During performances in Mexico City, Maria Callas interpolated a high E-flat at the end of the Act II finale 'Gloria all'Egitto.' In 1960, Leontyne Price became the first African American to sing a leading role at La Scala by performing Aida; her 1985 Met farewell was also in this role. Angel Blue withdrew from a 2022 Verona Arena production to protest soprano Anna Netrebko's use of blackface makeup for the role. An acclaimed 2016 Gramophone Award-winning recording featured Anja Harteros as the title character, conducted by Antonio Pappano.
