Summary of Facts

Émile Zola (1840-1902) was the foremost French naturalist novelist, author of the 20-novel Rougon-Macquart cycle set during the Second Empire. The cycle traces hereditary traits through the Rougon and Macquart families, beginning with the matriarch Adelaide Fouque (Tante Dide).

Germinal follows Étienne Lantier's experiences leading a miners' strike at the Montsou coal mines (Le Voreux). The anarchist Souvarine, comforted by his pet rabbit Pologne, destroys Le Voreux. Étienne kills Catherine's abusive lover Chaval while trapped in a flooded pit. The horse Bataille struggles to survive underground.

Thérèse Raquin features Laurent biting Camille on the neck and drowning him; a paralyzed woman witnesses but cannot report the murder. L'Assommoir follows the washerwoman Gervaise Macquart, who marries the roofer Coupeau in a scene involving a visit to the Louvre; both descend into alcoholism. Nana, Gervaise's daughter, becomes a courtesan who dies of smallpox. La Bête Humaine centers on railroad employee Jacques Lantier, who drives the engine La Lison; Roubaud murders Grandmorin on a train, and Flore derails a train.

Zola was a childhood friend of Paul Cézanne and fictionalized him as Claude Lantier in The Masterpiece, ending their friendship. Manet painted his portrait in profile with a Japanese screen and Olympia reproduction. Zola wrote The Experimental Novel applying Claude Bernard's physiology to literature. He defended Alfred Dreyfus in the famous open letter 'J'Accuse...!' published in L'Aurore. He died of carbon monoxide poisoning from a blocked chimney — some suspected foul play.
