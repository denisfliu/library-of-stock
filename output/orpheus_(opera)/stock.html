The mythological figure Orpheus has inspired a remarkable succession of operas across Western history, from Monteverdi's L'Orfeo (1607) — the oldest opera still regularly performed — through Gluck's reform opera Orfeo ed Euridice (1762), Offenbach's satirical Orpheus in the Underworld (1858), and Birtwistle's The Mask of Orpheus (1986). The myth's core — Orpheus descending to Hades to rescue Eurydice, then losing her by looking back — generates the drama in all versions, while each composer uses the material for very different artistic purposes.
L'Orfeo →

Claudio Monteverdi's L'Orfeo (1607) is consistently identified in clues as the oldest opera still regularly performed, making it the standard giveaway for this topic. The opera opens with a trumpet toccata and features the aria 'Rosa del ciel,' sung by Orpheus on his wedding day. A jovial and solemn chorus are repeated in reverse order during those wedding celebrations. Alessandro Striggio wrote the original libretto, and the original ending included a scene in which Orpheus is chased by the Maenads. The opera was influenced by Jacopo Peri's earlier Euridice, which was dedicated to the wedding of Henry IV and Marie de Medici.

Orfeo ed Euridice →

Gluck's Orfeo ed Euridice (1762) is consistently identified as the first of Gluck's 'reform operas.' The opera contains the 'Dance of the Blessed Spirits,' a flute-led interlude played when Eurydice sings in joy after being freed in the Elysian Fields. The aria 'Che faro senza Euridice' ('What shall I do without Eurydice?') is sung by Orpheus after he loses Eurydice again. Gluck published a manifesto with the opera decrying da capo arias and secco recitatives. The 1774 French version (Orphée et Eurydice) added the 'Dance of the Blessed Spirits.' The lead male role was sung by a castrato in the Viennese premiere but not the reworked French premiere. In the trio 'Si les doux accords,' Amore sets conditions for Orpheus to return from the underworld; Amore later prevents his suicide in 'Tendre amour.'

Orpheus in the Underworld →

Jacques Offenbach's Orpheus in the Underworld (Orphée aux enfers, 1858) is a comic operetta satirizing both the myth and Gluck's earlier opera. Its most famous number, the 'Infernal Galop,' closes Act II and became famous as accompaniment to the can-can at the Folies-Bergère. The allegorical figure of Public Opinion drives the plot, pressuring an unwilling Orpheus (here a violin teacher who detests his wife) to rescue Eurydice. A character sings a song about being the former King of Boeotia. In the 'Fly Duet,' a character who has transformed himself into a fly can only buzz instead of sing during a love duet with Eurydice. The opera ends when a lightning bolt from Jupiter startles Orpheus into looking back at Eurydice. Orpheus tries to woo Eurydice by quoting an aria from Gluck's earlier opera about the same character. Carl Binder wrote an overture to the opera. Saint-Saëns parodied the Infernal Galop — playing it at a painstakingly slow tempo in strings — in his Carnival of the Animals, to portray tortoises.

The Mask of Orpheus

Harrison Birtwistle's The Mask of Orpheus premiered at the English National Opera. Its second act sees the title character travel through seventeen arches, beginning with Countryside and ending with Fear. Three 'Passing Clouds' in the opera recount the birth of Dionysus, the myth of Lycurgus, and the myth of Pentheus. A 2019 performance was praised for its IRCAM-composed music by Barry Anderson. The opera has multiple arias entitled 'hysterical' and centers around Orpheus's 'mask.'

Black Orpheus (Orfeu Negro)

Marcel Camus's 1959 film Black Orpheus (Orfeu Negro) transposes the Orpheus and Eurydice myth to Rio de Janeiro during Carnaval. The title character, a Black trolley conductor who is said to make the sun rise, performs a song titled 'Manhã de Carnaval.' Eurydice is chased by a man in a black skeleton costume who identifies himself as Death. The film opens with a bas-relief that explodes to reveal a dancing crowd; in its first scene, the protagonist witnesses a brawl outside a cafe and a motorcycle hit-and-run. Luiz Bonfa's songs — particularly 'Manhã de Carnaval,' known in the US as 'Black Orpheus' — helped popularize bossa nova internationally; Antonio Carlos Jobim co-composed the soundtrack. Vince Guaraldi recorded Jazz Impressions of Black Orpheus, which included 'Cast Your Fate to the Wind.' The film adapts Vinicius de Moraes's play Orpheus of the Conception (Orfeu da Conceição), for which de Moraes and Tom Jobim wrote 'Lamento no Morro.' Jean-Luc Godard complained the film lacked realism in an article titled 'Rising from Caricature.'

General / The Myth

The Orpheus myth underlies all operatic treatments. The core narrative — the musician Orpheus descends to Hades to rescue his wife Eurydice, convinces the gods with his music, but loses her when he looks back — drives all versions. Eurydice's death is typically caused by a snakebite (attributed in Virgil to her fleeing Aristaeus, the keeper of bees). Plato's Symposium explains why the gods favored Alcestis over Orpheus — he 'lacked the spirit to die for the sake of love.' The aria 'Possente spirto' from Monteverdi's L'Orfeo appears in both Monteverdi's and other operas — Orpheus sings it to a ferryman (Charon) to lull him to sleep. Hadestown is a folk musical by Anaïs Mitchell that retells the myth with five named characters including Hermes, Persephone, and Hades. Sarah Ruhl's play Eurydice features an elevator that rains inside and Orpheus unable to read a love letter.

Summary of Facts

The figure of Orpheus — the mythical Thracian musician who descends to Hades to rescue his wife Eurydice, only to lose her again by looking back — has generated more operatic settings than virtually any other mythological subject. In quizbowl, four operas predominate: Monteverdi's L'Orfeo, Gluck's Orfeo ed Euridice, Offenbach's Orpheus in the Underworld, and Birtwistle's The Mask of Orpheus.

Monteverdi's L'Orfeo (1607) is the most consistently clued opera in the canon because it is identified as the oldest opera still regularly performed. The libretto was written by Alessandro Striggio, whose original ending included Orpheus being chased by the Maenads. The opera opens with a trumpet toccata and features the wedding aria 'Rosa del ciel,' during which a jovial and solemn chorus are repeated in reverse order. Orpheus sings the aria 'Possente spirto' (meaning 'powerful spirit') to the ferryman Charon to lull him to sleep. Monteverdi's opera was influenced by Jacopo Peri's earlier Euridice, which was dedicated to the wedding of Henry IV and Marie de Medici. Luigi Rossi also composed an Orfeo commissioned by Cardinal Mazarin, which compared Orpheus's fate to that of France.

Gluck's Orfeo ed Euridice (1762) is the first of Gluck's reform operas, published with a manifesto decrying da capo arias and secco recitatives. The opera contains the famous 'Dance of the Blessed Spirits,' a flute-led interlude associated with the Elysian Fields, and the lament 'Che faro senza Euridice' ('What shall I do without Eurydice?'), sung after Orpheus loses her a second time. Amore sets conditions for Orpheus's return in the trio 'Si les doux accords' and later prevents Orpheus's suicide in the aria 'Tendre amour.' The lead male role was sung by a castrato in the Vienna premiere; the 1774 French revision added the 'Dance of the Blessed Spirits.'

Offenbach's Orpheus in the Underworld (Orphée aux enfers, 1858) is a broad satire of both the myth and Gluck's reverential treatment. The allegorical figure Public Opinion drives the plot, pressuring Orpheus — here a violin teacher who openly detests his wife (claiming she invented hexameter) — into retrieving her. In the famous 'Fly Duet,' Jupiter transforms into a fly to seduce Eurydice and can only buzz rather than sing. A jailer (formerly King of Boeotia) sings of his nostalgia for that role. The opera ends when a lightning bolt from Jupiter startles Orpheus into looking back. The best-known number is the Infernal Galop, which closes Act II and became synonymous with the can-can at the Folies-Bergère. Saint-Saëns later parodied it at a painstakingly slow tempo in the strings of his Carnival of the Animals to depict tortoises.

Harrison Birtwistle's The Mask of Orpheus (1986) takes a highly fragmented, myth-layered approach: its second act follows Orpheus through seventeen arches from Countryside to Fear, while three 'Passing Clouds' narrate adjacent myths (Dionysus, Lycurgus, Pentheus). The opera includes IRCAM electronic music composed by Barry Anderson and features multiple arias labeled 'hysterical,' centering on Orpheus's 'mask.'

Beyond opera, the 1959 Marcel Camus film Black Orpheus (Orfeu Negro) transplants the myth to Rio de Janeiro during Carnaval. The Orpheus figure is a Black trolley conductor said to make the sun rise; Eurydice is chased by a skeletal Death. Luiz Bonfa's score — particularly 'Manhã de Carnaval' — helped introduce bossa nova to international audiences; Antonio Carlos Jobim co-composed the soundtrack. The film adapts Vinicius de Moraes's play Orpheus of the Conception. Vince Guaraldi's Jazz Impressions of Black Orpheus, which included 'Cast Your Fate to the Wind,' was his commercial breakthrough before his Peanuts association. Jean-Paul Sartre's essay 'Black Orpheus' was a preface to Léopold Sédar Senghor's Anthology of New Negro and Malagasy Poetry, defining négritude as 'anti-racist racism'; Ulli Beier edited the African literary journal Black Orpheus associated with Nigeria's Mbari Club.
