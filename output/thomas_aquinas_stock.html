Thomas Aquinas

13th-century Dominican friar and Scholastic philosopher, author of Summa Theologica and Summa Contra Gentiles. Known for the Five Ways (proofs of God's existence), the doctrine of double effect, natural law theory, and his synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology. Student of Albertus Magnus, called 'The Philosopher' by his followers referring to Aristotle and 'The Commentator' referring to Averroes.
The Five Ways (Quinque Viae)

Thomas Aquinas's five proofs of God's existence from the Summa Theologica are among the most frequently clued philosophical arguments in quizbowl. They include the argument from motion (unmoved mover), first cause, contingency, degree (there must be an ultimate goodness), and teleology. Notably, they do not include the ontological argument. Frederick Copleston divided them into in fieri and in esse classes.

Doctrine of Double Effect

Aquinas formulated the principle that an action with harmful but unintended side effects may be morally permissible if the action itself is good, the intention is good, and the good results outweigh the bad. This became enormously influential: Philippa Foot's trolley problem paper popularized it, and G.E.M. Anscombe, H.L.A. Hart, and others engaged with it extensively.

Natural Law and the Treatise on Law

Aquinas revived natural law theory by distinguishing four kinds of law: eternal, natural, human, and divine. He defined law as 'an ordinance of reason for the common good' and argued that the first precept of natural law is that 'good is to be done and pursued, and evil is to be avoided.' His just war criteria (just purpose, proper authority, right intention) built on Augustine, and the Salamanca School later expanded his just price concept.

Summa Theologica (Structure and Reception)

Aquinas's magnum opus, divided into three parts and 512 questions (3,125 articles answering 614 questions in some counts). Written in a question-and-answer format where objections are answered with 'sed contra' ('on the contrary'). It refers to Aristotle as 'The Philosopher' and Averroes as 'The Commentator.' Leo XIII's encyclical Aeterni Patris revived interest in Thomism, and analytical Thomism was later developed by John Haldane.

Summa Contra Gentiles

A treatise likely written for missionaries, intended to convince non-Christians of the value of God. It deliberately refrains from citing the Old or New Testament and is split into discussions of human truths and divine truths. May have been written at the behest of Raymond of Penafort.

Analogy of Being and Metaphysics

Aquinas proposed the Analogia Entis ('Analogy of Being'), arguing that God's similarity with created things can only be affirmed while also affirming dissimilarity - properties like 'goodness' are predicated analogically, not univocally (contra Duns Scotus). He also argued that essence and existence are distinct in all beings except God, building on Avicenna. His quiddity concept is often contrasted with Scotus's haecceity.

Eucharistic Hymns

Pope Urban IV commissioned Aquinas to write five hymns for the feast of Corpus Christi, including 'Adoro te devote,' 'Pange lingua gloriosi corpus mysterium,' and 'O Salutaris Hostia.' Josquin des Prez's last mass was based on Pange lingua. Gerard Manley Hopkins rendered one line as 'Lend this life to me then: feed and feast my mind.'

Summary of Facts

Thomas Aquinas is a 13th-century Dominican friar and the preeminent Scholastic philosopher, most famous for his Summa Theologica. He was a student of Albertus Magnus, who defended him against his early reputation as a 'dumb ox.' He studied at the University of Paris, where he disputed with Siger de Brabant over Averroism and argued against the 'unity of the intellect' proposed by Averroes.

The Five Ways (quinque viae) from the Summa Theologica are Aquinas's five proofs of God's existence: the argument from motion (unmoved mover), first cause, contingency, degree (there must be an ultimate perfection), and teleology. Notably, they do not include the ontological argument. Frederick Copleston divided them into in fieri and in esse classes.

Aquinas formulated the doctrine of double effect, arguing that an action with harmful but unintended side effects may be permissible if the action and intention are good and the good outweighs the bad. He wrote 'nothing hinders one act from having two effects.' Philippa Foot's trolley problem paper popularized this principle, and G.E.M. Anscombe, H.L.A. Hart, and contemporary experimental philosophers like Joshua Knobe have engaged with it.

In natural law theory, Aquinas distinguished four kinds of law (eternal, natural, human, divine) and defined law as 'an ordinance of reason for the common good.' John Finnis argued he derived all norms from the first precept: 'good is to be done and pursued, and evil is to be avoided.' Aquinas also set out criteria for just war (just purpose, proper authority, right intention) building on Augustine.

The Summa Theologica's structure uses a question-and-answer format with 'sed contra' responses. It refers to Aristotle as 'The Philosopher' and Averroes as 'The Commentator.' Leo XIII's encyclical Aeterni Patris revived Thomist studies, and John Haldane later developed analytical Thomism. Roberto Busa's IBM partnership to index the Summa is often credited as the first work of digital humanities. The Summa Contra Gentiles was written for missionaries and deliberately avoids scriptural citations.

Aquinas proposed the Analogia Entis ('Analogy of Being'), arguing that God's properties are predicated analogically rather than univocally (contrasting with Duns Scotus's univocity of being). Karl Barth called this idea 'the antichrist.' Aquinas argued that essence and existence are distinct in all beings except God, building on Avicenna.

Additional clued facts include: he originated the Peripatetic Axiom ('nothing is in the intellect that was not first in the senses'), argued the soul is 'the form of the body' while rejecting plurality of substantial forms, defined beauty in terms of integritas, consonantia, and claritas, and wrote five Corpus Christi hymns including 'Pange lingua' (basis for Josquin's last mass) and 'Adoro te devote.' He reportedly remarked that all his work seemed like 'straw' and stopped writing four years before his death.
