Ancient Greek tragedian (~480-406 BCE). Most commonly identified by Medea, The Bacchae, The Trojan Women, Hippolytus, and his appearances as a character in Aristophanes' comedies (The Frogs, Thesmophoriazusae, The Acharnians). In The Frogs, Dionysus judges Euripides' plays inferior to Aeschylus's. Supposedly died in Macedonia after self-imposed exile; legend says torn apart by Molossian hounds. Also wrote The Cyclops, the only surviving complete satyr play.

Summary of Facts

Euripides (~480-406 BCE) was an ancient Greek tragedian, one of the three great Athenian tragic dramatists alongside Aeschylus and Sophocles. He is most frequently clued through his appearances as a character in Aristophanes' comedies and through his plays Medea, The Bacchae, The Trojan Women, and Hippolytus.

In Aristophanes' The Frogs, Dionysus judges Euripides inferior to Aeschylus in a poetry contest, where a literal scale shows Euripides' descriptions of the Argo and 'iron club' weigh less than Aeschylus's words. Aeschylus interrupts Euripides' prologues by shouting 'Lost his little flask of oil!' (a device called lekythion). Sophocles is given Euripides' chair in Hades. Aeschylus also accuses Euripides of arguing 'life is not life' with his depictions of women giving birth in temples. In The Acharnians, Dicaeopolis borrows a beggar's costume (from the lost play Telephus) from Euripides; a slave says 'he is and he isn't' when asked if Euripides is home. In Thesmophoriazusae, Euripides convinces his relative Mnesilochus to spy on the women-only Thesmophoria festival, where his portrayal of women is debated.

Medea is his most famous play: Medea kills her children after Jason abandons her, has Glauce killed via a poisoned robe and diadem, and escapes on the chariot of Helios pulled by dragons. Unusually, a mortal character appears on the mechane, a position normally reserved for gods. She says she'd 'rather stand three times in battle than give birth once.' She promises to cure King Aegeus's infertility in exchange for asylum in Athens; an oracle tells Aegeus 'not to unstop the wineskin's neck.' The chorus of Corinthian women imagines 'the waters in the sacred rivers are flowing in reverse' and claims 'the mind of man is nothing but a fraud.' Medea predicts Jason will suffer a 'coward's end' from wreckage of his ship. It is the only Greek play where a family murderer goes unpunished. The play opens with a nurse lamenting outside the house. Modern adaptations include Luis Alfaro's Mojada (Medea as seamstress crossing the US-Mexico border), Cherrie Moraga's The Hungry Woman (she kills Chac-Mool), Robinson Jeffers's 1947 version (Judith Anderson won a Tony), and Jean Anouilh's version (Medea lives in a trailer and dies in a fire). Countee Cullen published his translation alongside 'Scottsboro, Too, Is Worth Its Song.'

In The Bacchae, Euripides' final play, Pentheus is convinced to crossdress to spy on women who draw milk and honey from the ground with thyrsi. Dionysus bends a fir tree so Pentheus can sit in it. When captured, Dionysus is chained to a bull but causes an earthquake and fire to escape. Pentheus sees 'two suns' in the sky and horns on Dionysus's head. His mother Agave kills him in a Bacchic frenzy, believing she's ripping apart a mountain lion; she brings the 'lion's head' to her father Cadmus. A messenger describes the maenads performing sparagmos on Cithaeron. The chorus sings 'What is wisdom?' Pentheus's name means 'man of sorrows.' E.R. Dodds authored the still-standard commentary. Tiresias and Cadmus beg Pentheus to sympathize with the Bacchants. Wole Soyinka adapted it as a 'Communion Rite' with a second chorus of slaves set amid Nigerian civil unrest, where blood from Pentheus's head transforms into wine. Joe Orton's Erpingham Camp and Richard Schechner's Dionysus in 69 are also adaptations. It won the prize at the Great Dionysia posthumously.

The Trojan Women describes the fate of Hecuba, Andromache, Cassandra, and Helen after Troy's fall. The herald Talthybius brings in the corpse of baby Astyanax, thrown from the city walls and buried in Hector's shield. It opens with Poseidon and Athena discussing punishment for Ajax the Lesser's rape. Cassandra enters singing a frenzied wedding hymn brandishing flaming torches. It is considered a commentary on the Athenian massacre at Milos. It formed a trilogy with Alexandros and Palamedes; Xenocles bafflingly won first prize over it at the 415 BC City Dionysia. Hecuba is told 'Lift thy head, unhappy lady, from the ground' and unusually prays to a god as 'the fixed law of Nature or man's Mind.' Talthybius vaguely relays that Polyxena will be sacrificed at Achilles' tomb. Jean-Paul Sartre's final stage work was an adaptation. Femi Osofisan adapted it as Women of Owu; Anne Carson created a comic book version with Rosanna Bruno. Plutarch says the tyrant Alexander of Pherae left a performance in shame.

Hippolytus centers on Aphrodite's punishment of the chaste, misogynistic Hippolytus for devotion to Artemis. The play opens with Aphrodite (Cypris) explaining her plan. A nurse pleads on behalf of Phaedra, who is starving herself. Phaedra writes a false suicide note accusing Hippolytus of rape. Theseus uses one of three curses from Poseidon; a monstrous bull from the sea capsizes Hippolytus's chariot and kills him. His famous line 'my tongue swore but my heart remained unpledged' was parodied by Aristophanes. He delivers a misogynistic rant lamenting men can't buy babies at temples. In Roman tradition, he is resurrected by Asclepius and reborn as Virbius. He had a cult where girls about to marry offered locks of hair. Racine's Phedre is based on this play.

Other plays include Orestes (contains the only surviving fragment of ancient Greek music, reconstructed by Armand D'Angour; Apollo arrives as deus ex machina), Alcestis (Heracles ambushes Thanatos and resurrects the title character; adapted as Edufa by Efua Sutherland), Ion (tragicomedy at Delphi), The Phoenician Women (Menoeceus commits suicide; condemns Eteocles), Heracles (adapted by Anne Carson as H of H Playbook), Helen (title character escapes Egyptian king Theoclymenus), Andromache (agon with Hermione), Hecuba (blinds Polymestor), The Children of Herakles, The Cyclops (only surviving complete satyr play), and Iphigenia in Aulis. He supposedly went to live in a cave on Salamis after two bad marriages, died at Archelaus's court in Macedonia, and was torn apart by Molossian hounds according to legend. Plutarch says his verses persuaded Spartans not to destroy Athens. Nietzsche discussed him in The Birth of Tragedy. A HUAC member once asked Hallie Flanagan about what 'Mr Euripides' preached.
