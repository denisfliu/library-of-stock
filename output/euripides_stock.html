Summary of Facts

Euripides (~480–406 BCE) was an ancient Greek tragedian, one of the three great Athenian tragic dramatists alongside Aeschylus and Sophocles. He is most frequently clued through his appearances as a character in Aristophanes' comedies (The Frogs, The Acharnians, Thesmophoriazusae) and through his plays Medea, The Bacchae, The Trojan Women, Hippolytus, and Orestes.

In Aristophanes' The Frogs, Dionysus judges Euripides inferior to Aeschylus in a poetry contest, where a literal scale shows Euripides' descriptions of the Argo and 'iron club' weigh less than Aeschylus's 'two chariots and two corpses.' Aeschylus interrupts Euripides' prologues by shouting 'Lost his little flask of oil!' In The Acharnians, Dicaeopolis borrows a beggar's costume (from the lost play Telephus) from Euripides. In Thesmophoriazusae, Euripides convinces his relative Mnesilochus to spy on the women-only Thesmophoria festival.

Medea is his most famous play: Medea kills her children after Jason abandons her, has Glauce killed via a poisoned robe, and escapes on a golden chariot pulled by dragons. She says she'd 'rather stand three times to face their battles, shield in hand, than bear one child.' It is the only Greek play where a family murderer goes unpunished. Modern adaptations include Luis Alfaro's Mojada and Cherríe Moraga's The Hungry Woman.

In The Bacchae, Pentheus is convinced to crossdress to spy on women who draw milk and honey from the ground; his mother Agave kills him in a Bacchic frenzy, believing she's ripping apart a mountain lion. Wole Soyinka adapted it for Nigeria. The Trojan Women describes the fates of Hecuba, Andromache, Cassandra, and Helen; Astyanax is killed. It comments on Athens' sacking of Milos.

Other plays: Hippolytus (basis for Racine's Phèdre), Orestes (with its surviving musical fragment), Heracles (adapted by Anne Carson as H of H Playbook), Ion (tragicomedy at Delphi), The Phoenician Women, and Alcestis (adapted by Efua Sutherland). He supposedly died in Macedonia at Archelaus's court, torn apart by Molossian hounds. Plutarch says his verses persuaded Spartans not to destroy Athens.
