Nigerian author, the most canonical African novelist. Best known for Things Fall Apart (about Okonkwo's downfall in the village of Umuofia). Also wrote No Longer at Ease (about Okonkwo's grandson Obi), Arrow of God (about the priest Ezeulu), A Man of the People, and Anthills of the Savannah. Famous for the essay 'An Image of Africa,' in which he called Joseph Conrad a 'thoroughgoing racist.' Controversially claimed at a 1962 conference that Nigeria's national literature would be written in English (opposite of Ngugi wa Thiong'o). Served as editor of the Heinemann African Writers Series. Wrote the short stories 'Civil Peace,' 'Dead Men's Path,' 'The Sacrificial Egg,' and 'Girls at War.'
Things Fall Apart

Achebe's most famous novel, set in the Igbo village of Umuofia. Okonkwo is a great wrestler who defeats Amalinze the Cat. He kills his adopted son Ikemefuna after the Oracle of the Hills and Caves decrees it, despite an elder warning 'Bear no hand in his death.' He beats his wife during the Week of Peace. He is exiled for seven years after his gun accidentally kills Ezeudu's son at Ezeudu's funeral; he goes to his mother's village of Mbanta. A fable of Tortoise who grows wings and feasts with the birds is told. A woman loses her first nine children to a curse. Christian missionaries build a church in the 'Evil Forest,' considered taboo. Twins are left to die in a pot. Nwoye converts to Christianity and renames himself Isaac. Okonkwo kills a British messenger with a machete, then hangs himself. The Commissioner considers the suicide will make a good paragraph in his book 'The Pacification of the Primitive Tribes of the Lower Niger.' Inaugural entry of the Heinemann African Writers Series. Feasts honor the goddess Ani before the yam harvest. Wikipedia

No Longer at Ease

Sequel to Things Fall Apart, about Okonkwo's grandson Obi Okonkwo. Title from T.S. Eliot's 'Journey of the Magi.' Obi is sent to England on a scholarship from the Umuofia Progressive Union (UPU) to study law. He falls in love with Clara, an osu (outcast), but his mother begs him not to marry her. When Clara becomes pregnant, Obi arranges an abortion and accepts bribes to pay for it. He is arrested for bribery. His secretary Marie may be spying on him for his boss Mr. Green. He surprises the chairman of the Public Service Commission by criticizing The Heart of the Matter. Discovers an ode to his homeland in a Housman book. Wikipedia

Ezeulu's son Oduche converts to Christianity and tries to kill the sacred royal python. The Feast of the New Yam is turned into a Christian festival. Villagers abandon Ezeulu after a famine and convert to Christianity. Wikipedia

A Man of the People

Uses 'national cake' as a metaphor for corruption. Wikipedia

'An Image of Africa' and Other Essays

Achebe's most famous essay, 'An Image of Africa: Racism in Conrad's Heart of Darkness' (1975 lecture), called Joseph Conrad a 'thoroughgoing racist' for depicting Africa as 'the other world, the antithesis of Europe and therefore of civilization.' Discusses a 'young fellow from Yonkers' who assumed he needed to travel to find 'odd customs and superstitions.' Contrasts Albert Schweitzer's 'junior brother' usage with Conrad's refusal to go beyond 'distant kinship.' Condensed Heart of Darkness to 'the thought of their humanity — like yours... Ugly.' Critiqued by Cedric Watts. Also discussed Marco Polo's omission of the printing press. At a 1962 conference, claimed 'the national literature of Nigeria is, or will be, written in English' (opposite of Ngugi's position). Other essays include 'Colonialist Criticism,' 'The Truth of Fiction' (Picasso's claim that all art is false; Guernica), 'Novelist as Teacher,' 'The African Writer and the English Language.' Collected in Hopes and Impediments.

General / Biographical

Albert Chinualumogu Achebe (1930-2013), Nigerian author. Editor of the Heinemann African Writers Series. Wrote the memoir There Was a Country (2012) about the Biafran/Nigerian Civil War, controversially accusing the government of genocide. Wole Soyinka's poem 'Elegy for the Nation' is addressed to Achebe. Taught at Oba's Merchant of Light school (fictionalized as the 'evil forest'). Achebe's essay collection Hopes and Impediments contains 'An Image of Africa,' 'The Truth of Fiction,' and 'Don't Let Him Die' (commemorating Christopher Okigbo). Children's books include The Flute and Chike and the River. Co-authored How the Leopard Got Its Claws. Car accident left him a paraplegic. Quote: 'Until lions produce their own historian, the story will glorify only the hunter.'

Summary of Facts

Chinua Achebe (1930-2013) is the most canonical African novelist in quizbowl. Things Fall Apart appears in virtually every question: Okonkwo kills his adopted son Ikemefuna despite an elder's warning, beats his wife during the Week of Peace, is exiled after accidentally killing Ezeudu's son, and ultimately hangs himself after killing a British messenger. The village of Umuofia, the fable of Tortoise feasting with the birds, the Evil Forest church, and the Commissioner's planned paragraph about the suicide are all recurring clues. No Longer at Ease follows Okonkwo's grandson Obi, who is sent to England by the Umuofia Progressive Union, falls in love with the osu Clara, arranges her abortion by accepting bribes, and is arrested. Arrow of God features the priest Ezeulu and Captain Winterbottom ('Breaker-of-Guns'). A Man of the People satirizes Chief Nanga's corruption, and its coup anticipated real Nigerian coups. Anthills of the Savannah centers on the dictator Sam in fictional Kangan. Among his short stories, 'Civil Peace' (Jonathan's 'Nothing puzzles God'), 'Dead Men's Path' (Michael Obi's fence), 'The Sacrificial Egg' (Julius steps on an egg; Janet dies), and 'Girls at War' recur frequently. His essay 'An Image of Africa' calls Conrad a 'thoroughgoing racist' and describes a 'young fellow from Yonkers.' At a 1962 conference, he claimed Nigeria's national literature would be written in English, directly opposing Ngugi. He edited the Heinemann African Writers Series and wrote the Biafran War memoir There Was a Country.
