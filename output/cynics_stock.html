Ancient Greek philosophical school founded by Antisthenes (student of Socrates) and most famously associated with Diogenes of Sinope. The name derives from a word meaning 'dog-like.' The Cynics advocated living in accordance with nature rather than convention, practicing asceticism (askesis), and rejecting social norms. Key figures include Crates of Thebes, Hipparchia of Maroneia, and Metrocles.
Diogenes of Sinope →

Diogenes is the most iconic Cynic, appearing in virtually every clue about the school. He lived in a large wine jar (or barrel/ceramic jar), walked around Athens with a lantern looking for an 'honest man,' and told Alexander the Great to 'stand out of my sunlight.' He mocked Plato's definition of man as a 'featherless biped' by plucking a chicken and presenting it at the Academy. He was called a 'dog' and embraced the epithet. Nietzsche compared his books to this school in Ecce Homo.

Antisthenes (Founder) →

Antisthenes, a student of Socrates, is retroactively called the founder of Cynicism by Diogenes Laertius. He argued that virtue, not pleasure, was the end of existence, famously declaring 'I would rather go mad than feel pleasure.' His Physicus asserted there is only one God, unknowable by humans. Diogenes was called his 'faithful hound.' The school identified with the god Heracles.

Crates, Hipparchia, and Other Cynics

Crates of Thebes and Hipparchia of Maroneia were a married Cynic couple who lived on the streets of Athens and famously had sex in public. Hipparchia defended studying philosophy over weaving against Theodorus the Atheist. The Cynic epistles, attributed to figures like Crates, Antisthenes, and Anacharsis, circulated in the Roman Empire. Lucian complained that 'every city was full of upstarts' from the school but wrote an enthusiastic biography of the Cynic Demonax. The Cynic attribute of parrhesia ('candid speech') was discussed by Foucault.

General / School Identity

The Cynics advocated living in accordance with nature rather than convention, practicing askesis (discipline of mind over body), and setting courage over fortune. The name comes from a word meaning 'dog-like.' The school is linked to the later Stoics through Zeno of Citium, who adapted Cynic thought. Nietzsche compared his books to Cynicism in Ecce Homo, calling it 'the highest thing that can be attained on Earth.' Peter Sloterdijk defined cynicism as 'enlightened false consciousness,' and Slavoj Zizek adopted the term 'kynicism.'

Summary of Facts

Cynicism is an ancient Greek philosophical school most closely associated with Diogenes of Sinope and retroactively attributed to Antisthenes, a student of Socrates. The name derives from a Greek word meaning 'dog-like,' which Diogenes embraced, explaining 'I fawn on those who give me anything, I yelp at those who refuse, and I set my teeth in rascals.'

Diogenes is the dominant figure in clues. His iconic behaviors include living in a wine jar, searching for an honest man with a lantern in broad daylight, telling Alexander the Great to step out of his sunlight, and plucking a chicken to refute Plato's definition of man as a 'featherless biped.' He argued for communal property through a syllogism about gods and wise men.

Antisthenes founded the school and argued that virtue, not pleasure, is the goal of existence, declaring 'I would rather go mad than feel pleasure.' His Physicus asserted monotheism. The school identified with Heracles. Crates of Thebes and Hipparchia of Maroneia were a married Cynic couple from the village of Maroneia who famously lived on the streets and had sex in public. Hipparchia debated Theodorus the Atheist on the value of philosophy over weaving.

The Cynics advocated living in accordance with nature, practicing askesis (mental discipline), and rejecting social convention. Their attribute of parrhesia (candid speech) was analyzed by Michel Foucault. Cynic epistles circulated in the Roman Empire. The school directly influenced Stoicism through Zeno of Citium. In modern philosophy, Nietzsche compared his books to Cynicism, Sloterdijk defined cynicism as 'enlightened false consciousness,' and Zizek adopted 'kynicism' to challenge official culture.
