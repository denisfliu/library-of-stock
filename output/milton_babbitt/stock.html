Summary of Facts

Milton Babbitt (1916–2011) was an American serialist composer and music theorist. He is best known for his 1958 essay 'Who Cares if You Listen?' (originally titled 'The Composer as Specialist' — the provocative title was given by the editors of High Fidelity magazine), which defended modern music from public indifference by arguing that dismissing advanced music was as absurd as dismissing advanced mathematics. The article attracted significant controversy.

In twelve-tone theory, Babbitt formulated the six all-combinatorial hexachord sets — complementary series whose aggregates form twelve-tone rows. He also drew comparisons between music and advanced mathematics, and his work on twelve-tone music underpins musical set theory. In 1948 he developed a system of durations for his Composition for Four Instruments. He was frequently contrasted with John Cage — a 1987 New York Times article called them 'two extremes of avant-garde music.' Alex Ross, in The Rest Is Noise, wrote that Babbitt 'produced music so byzantine in construction that one practically needed a security clearance to understand it.'

Babbitt taught at the Juilliard School (whose Juilliard String Quartet won five Grammys for chamber music). He also wrote the never-staged Broadway musical Fabulous Voyage.

His best-known composition is Philomel, an electronic piece combining a synthesizer with both a live soprano and a recorded soprano. Inspired by Ovid's myth of Philomela, the soprano begins by vocalizing the syllable 'eeh' at various pitches over a synthesizer backdrop. Babbitt also wrote the electronic piece Correspondences.
