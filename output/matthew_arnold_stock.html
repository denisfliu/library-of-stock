Victorian English poet and cultural critic. Most commonly identified by "Dover Beach" ('darkling plain where ignorant armies clash by night,' 'Sea of Faith'), Culture and Anarchy (defines culture as 'the best which has been thought and said,' calls middle class 'Philistines'), "The Scholar-Gipsy," "Thyrsis" (elegy for Arthur Hugh Clough), "Empedocles on Etna," and the Marguerite poems. Son of Thomas Arnold, headmaster of Rugby School.

Summary of Facts

Matthew Arnold was a Victorian English poet and cultural critic, the son of Thomas Arnold, the famous headmaster of Rugby School (profiled in Lytton Strachey's Eminent Victorians).

"Dover Beach" is Arnold's most famous poem, written during his honeymoon with Frances Wightman. It opens "The sea is calm tonight" and asks the companion to "come to the window, sweet is the night-air!" The speaker hears "the eternal note of sadness" in the waves, noting that "Sophocles long ago heard it on the Aegean" — the "turbid ebb and flow of human misery." The world "seems to lie before us like a land of dreams, / So various, so beautiful, so new," but the speaker pleads "Ah, love, let us be true / To one another!" The "Sea of Faith" is retreating with a "melancholy, long, withdrawing roar." The poem closes with the image of a "darkling plain... where ignorant armies clash by night." The sea was compared to "the folds of a bright girdle furled." Anthony Hecht wrote a parody, "The Dover Bitch," in which the addressee gets angry at being treated as a "mournful cosmic last resort." Daljit Nagra referenced the poem in "Look We Have Coming to Dover!" In Ian McEwan's Saturday, Daisy recites "Dover Beach" to talk a criminal out of assault. Arnold scribbled the first two stanzas on preparatory notes for Empedocles on Etna.

Culture and Anarchy is Arnold's major critical work, defining culture as "the best which has been thought and said in the world" and urging the "study of perfection" combining "scientific passion" and "moral and social passion for doing good." It divides English society into aristocratic "Barbarians," middle-class "Philistines" (Arnold coined the modern usage), and the "Populace." The first chapter, "Sweetness and Light," borrows the phrase from Jonathan Swift's The Battle of the Books. The work contrasts "Hebraism" (efficiency, Rome) with "Hellenism" (beauty, Greece). T.S. Eliot's Notes Toward a Definition of Culture criticizes Arnold's definition. Terry Eagleton discusses Arnold's influence in Literary Theory. Lionel Trilling wrote a famous biography.

"The Scholar-Gipsy" is based on a story from Joseph Glanvill's The Vanity of Dogmatizing about a scholar who left Oxford to roam with a "wild brotherhood," "tired of knocking at preferment's door." The title character is "free from the sick fatigue" and "languid doubt" that plague modern people. The speaker laments "this strange disease of modern life" and declares "No, no, thou hast not felt the lapse of hours!" The poem opens "Go, for they call you, shepherd, from the hill" and closes with a "grave Tyrian trader" who "undid his corded bales" and "shy traffickers, the dark Iberians."

"Thyrsis" is a pastoral elegy for Arnold's friend Arthur Hugh Clough, named for a shepherd from Virgil's Eclogues. It opens "How changed is here each spot man makes or fills!" and describes Oxford as a "sweet city with her dreaming spires." The poem emphasizes "Time, not Corydon, hath conquered thee" and "The bloom is gone, and with the bloom go I." The speaker recalls "Yes, thou art gone! and round me too the night / In ever nearing circle weaves her shade" and remembers a single elm tree: "still our tree is there!"

"To Marguerite — Continued" is the sequel to "Isolation," noting "in the sea of life enisled" that "we mortal millions live alone," separated by "the unplumb'd, salt, estranging sea." The poem describes "a longing like despair" and "with echoing straits between us thrown." It was collected with Empedocles on Etna. John Fowles's The French Lieutenant's Woman ends by quoting this poem.

"Empedocles on Etna" is a dramatic poem about the Greek philosopher who rejects beauty and leaps into the volcano. Arnold withdrew it in his 1853 preface citing lack of "poetical enjoyment," and reprinted it in 1867 at Robert Browning's urging. To compensate, he added "Sohrab and Rustam" (from the Shahnameh) to his 1853 collection.

"Stanzas from the Grande Chartreuse" is set in a Carthusian monastery where the speaker is "wandering between two worlds, one dead, the other powerless to be born." "Rigorous teachers seized my youth, and purged its faith." A whisper asks "What dost thou in this living tomb?"

Other works include "The Forsaken Merman" (refrain "Children dear, was it yesterday?"), "The Study of Poetry" (claims "religion will be replaced by poetry," Chaucer lacked "high seriousness," originating the "touchstone method"), "The Function of Criticism" (criticism should show "disinterestedness"), Literature and Dogma (religion as "morality touched with emotion"), On Translating Homer (advocates hexameter), and sonnets "To a Friend" (Sophocles "saw life steadily, and saw it whole") and poems inspired by Senancour's Obermann.
