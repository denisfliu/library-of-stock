British artist based in California. Known for A Bigger Splash, Mr and Mrs Clark and Percy, Portrait of an Artist, 'joiners' (photo collages like Pearblossom Highway), and portraits of Gregory Evans and Peter Schlesinger. Summary of Facts

David Hockney (born 1937) is a British artist who has spent much of his career in California, where he taught at UCLA. He is a late member of the Pop Art movement, often mentioned alongside fellow British Pop artist Richard Hamilton.

His most iconic work is A Bigger Splash (1967), an acrylic painting depicting a backyard swimming pool with a large splash, a yellow diving board, two palm trees, a director's chair, and a modern house. The unpainted border frames the scene like a Polaroid. The painting was the subject of a 1974 Jack Hazan biopic and inspired a 2015 Luca Guadagnino film. Portrait of an Artist (Pool with Two Figures) (1972) shows a man in a pink jacket (inspired by Hockney's lover Peter Schlesinger) looking down at a man in white shorts in a swimming pool; it became the most expensive painting by a living artist when it sold in November 2018. The figure was based on a Polaroid of a man against an MG car, with masking tape used for straight lines.

Mr and Mrs Clark and Percy (1970-71) is a double portrait of fashion designer Ossie Clark and textile designer Celia Birtwell. A white cat (Percy) sits on Clark's lap while Birtwell stands in a dark purple and red dress by an open window. We Two Boys Together Clinging, painted after befriending R.B. Kitaj at the Royal College of Art, is a crude, child-like depiction of two boys kissing, titled after a Walt Whitman poem from Leaves of Grass.

Hockney is known for 'joiners' -- photo collages made from multiple Polaroids, including Pearblossom Highway (now at the Getty Center). His Paper Pools series used paper pulp as a medium, with the 30th selling for nearly $12 million in 2018. He also created Beverly Hills Housewife (Betty Freeman in a hot-pink dress by a pool), American Collectors (Fred and Marcia Weisman against bright blue background with totem pole), Portrait of Nick Wilder (1966, subject in swimming pool), and Bigger Trees Near Warter.

His book Secret Knowledge analyzes old masters' use of optical aids such as the camera obscura, and he was interviewed in Teller's documentary Tim's Vermeer. He designed a stained glass window for Westminster Abbey celebrating Elizabeth II's reign using an iPad, featuring a yellow hawthorn. He painted a Yosemite National Park series using an iPad and created a purple-dominated Grand Canyon landscape (A Bigger Grand Canyon) using 21 panels, purchased by the National Gallery of Australia for $4 million. He frequently uses the word 'Bigger' in titles.

Hockney created stage designs for The Rake's Progress opera, depicting a mental institution as a tiered gallery with black-clad men in bizarre masks. He produced homoerotic prints based on Constantine Cavafy's Greek poetry and etchings titled The Blue Guitar (with Aldo Crommelynck) inspired by Wallace Stevens' poem inspired by Picasso's The Old Guitarist. Peter Blake parodied Courbet's The Meeting by replacing Courbet with Hockney, alongside himself and Howard Hodgkin.
