Phedre

Phedre is Racine's most famous tragedy, depicting the title character's incestuous love for her stepson Hippolytus. The play is based on Euripides' Hippolytus but introduces the character Aricie as Hippolytus's love interest. Key plot points include Phedre's confession to nurse Oenone, Theseus invoking Neptune's curse, the sea monster attacking Hippolytus's chariot (described by Theramene), Oenone drowning, and Phedre poisoning herself. The famous line describing Phedre as 'the daughter of Minos and Pasiphae' is repeatedly clued as untranslatable.

Andromaque

Andromaque is Racine's retelling of the Trojan War aftermath. Hermione asks Orestes to kill Pyrrhus, who loves the title character Andromaque. After Pyrrhus dies, Hermione kills herself, and Orestes Goes insane. The role was written for actress Therese du Parc, Racine's lover. The play features nobly-born servants Cleone and Cephisa, and Andromaque marries Pyrrhus to protect her son Astyanax.

Athalie

Athalie is a biblical tragedy that Voltaire called 'the supreme achievement of the human mind.' The high priest Joad and Jehoshebath conspire to dethrone the title queen, who worships Baal, and install Joash as king of the Jews. The title character describes her mother wearing makeup to 'repair the irreparable ravages of time.' Racine was commissioned to write it for Madame de Maintenon's girls' School.

Other Works

Racine's other clued works include Esther (a three-act play about the biblical Esther convincing King Ahasuerus to stop a massacre of Jews), Berenice (about the Roman Emperor Titus renouncing the Queen of Judaea because 'Rome took a hatred to the name of King'), Bajazet, and Mithridates (about Xiphares and Monime inheriting the kingdom). La Thebaide was his first play performed live, premiered by Moliere's troupe in 1664.

General / Biographical

Racine is identified as a 17th-century French neoclassical tragedian, known for his alexandrine verse (twelve-syllable lines with a caesura) and strict adherence to the dramatic unities. He was a contemporary of Moliere and Corneille. Roland Barthes wrote a psychoanalysis-inflected study On Racine, which was attacked by Raymond Picard in 'New Criticism or New Fraud?' -- the resulting controversy helped legitimize literary theory. Lucien Goldmann's The Hidden God analyzed Racine's social class. In Proust's In Search of Lost Time, the actress Berma specializes in Racine's plays. Racine sided with the Ancients in the Quarrel between Ancients and Moderns. He converted to Jansenism late in life.

Summary of Facts

Jean Racine is a 17th-century French neoclassical tragedian best known for his play Phedre, which is by far his most frequently clued work. He is consistently identified alongside Moliere and Pierre Corneille as one of the three great French dramatists of the period, and is particularly associated with his use of alexandrine verse (lines of twelve syllables divided by a caesura into two hemistichs of six).

Phedre, his most famous tragedy, depicts the title character's incestuous love for her stepson Hippolytus (Hippolyte), the son of her husband Theseus (Thesee). Key plot points include Phedre confessing her love to her nurse Oenone, who then concocts a scheme to accuse Hippolytus of raping Phedre. Theseus invokes Neptune's curse against his own son, leading to a climactic scene in which Hippolytus's chariot is attacked by a horned sea monster (described by Theramene as half-bull, half-snake), causing the horses to drag him to his death. Oenone drowns herself, and Phedre poisons herself. The play introduces the character Aricie as Hippolytus's love interest, a notable difference from its source, Euripides' Hippolytus. Theophile Gautier called the line describing Phedre as 'the daughter of Minos and Pasiphae' Racine's finest (or only 'tolerable') verse, and it is frequently described as untranslatable. Ted Hughes spent his last months translating the play.

Andromaque is his second most clued tragedy, adapted from the Trojan War story. In it, Hermione asks her cousin Orestes to kill Pyrrhus, who loves the title character Andromaque. After Pyrrhus dies, Hermione vows to kill herself, which causes Orestes to go insane. The play includes nobly-born servant characters Cleone and Cephisa. The role of Andromaque was written for actress Therese du Parc, Racine's lover, whom he controversially stole from Moliere's company.

Athalie is another major tragedy, which Voltaire called 'the supreme achievement of the human mind.' In this play, the high priest Joad and Jehoshebath conspire to dethrone the title queen, who worships Baal, and install Joash (Joas) as king of the Jews. The play includes extensive musical choruses. Racine was commissioned to write it and Esther as religious pieces for Madame de Maintenon's girls' boarding School.

Britannicus is a tragedy about the Roman Emperor Nero, who abducts Junia (fiancee of the title character) and poisons Britannicus at a feast. At the end, Junia becomes a Vestal Virgin, and Nero's tutor Narcisse is killed by an angry mob.

Esther is a three-act play (unconventional for Racine) in which the title character convinces King Ahasuerus to stop a plotted massacre of Jews, with Hydaspes and Asaph as officers of the king.

Les Plaideurs (The Litigants) is Racine's only comedy, adapted from Aristophanes' The Wasps. It features the mad Judge Dandin, the Porter Petit Jean, and culminates when a judge rules against a dog named Citron for eating a chicken but pardons it after pleas from its puppies. Leander and Isabelle trick Chicaneau and Dandin into allowing their marriage.

Racine was also the subject of a major literary controversy when Roland Barthes published a psychoanalysis-inflected study (On Racine), which was attacked by Raymond Picard in the article 'New Criticism or New Fraud?' Racine's response through Criticism and Truth helped legitimize literary theory as a discipline. Lucien Goldmann's study The Hidden God compared the decline of Racine's social class to how his characters reject the world in which they are compelled to live. In Proust's In Search of Lost Time, the narrator befriends Berma, an actress specializing in Racine's plays, and schoolgirls are assigned to write a letter from Sophocles to Racine from the Underworld. Racine sided with the Ancients in the Quarrel between the Ancients and the Moderns. He converted to Jansenism late in life and spent his final years writing the Spiritual Canticles. He closely adhered to the dramatic unities.
