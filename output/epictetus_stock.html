Discourses (Diatribai)

The Discourses are Epictetus's primary text, compiled by his student Arrian into eight books (only four survive). They discuss prohairesis as the defining faculty of mankind, referring to man's power to choose how he reacts to impressions. The Discourses differentiate between things in one's power (prohairetic) and things not in one's power (aprohairetic). A.A. Long recently translated them as 'How to Be Free.' One passage describes a philosopher choosing calm over anger when his iron lamp is stolen.

Enchiridion (Handbook/Manual)

The Enchiridion, compiled by Arrian from the Discourses, is a shorter manual for practical Stoic living. It opens with the statement that 'some things are up to us and some things are not up to us.' It ends by quoting Cleanthes, Euripides, and Plato's Apology (three maxims 'ready at hand for all occasions'). Notable passages include: comparing life to a banquet, recommending drinking cold water and spitting it out when thirsty, criticizing 'he is a bad father' by distinguishing relationships from qualities, and noting it makes more sense for a horse to say 'I am beautiful' than its owner.

General / Biographical

Epictetus was a late Stoic philosopher born a slave in Hierapolis. His student Arrian compiled his teachings. He heavily influenced Marcus Aurelius, who quoted him frequently in the Meditations. James Stockdale was inspired by Epictetus during his time as a POW in Vietnam. Epictetus identified the 'doctrine of the three topics' (disciplines of desire, action, and assent) and two types of 'other-concern': aidos (modesty) and philanthropia (willingness to act on another's behalf). The central goal of his ethical teaching was eudaimonia (flourishing).

Summary of Facts

Epictetus was a late Stoic philosopher born a slave in Hierapolis. His teachings were recorded by his student Arrian in two works: the Discourses (Diatribai), compiled into eight books of which only four survive, and the Enchiridion (Handbook/Manual), a shorter practical guide derived from the Discourses.

Epictetus's philosophy centers on the distinction between what is 'up to us' (prohairetic) and what is not (aprohairetic). The Enchiridion opens with this fundamental principle: 'some things are up to us and some things are not up to us.' He identified prohairesis (volition/choice) as the defining faculty of mankind, referring to man's power to choose how he reacts to impressions around him. His 'doctrine of the three topics' outlined three disciplines students should study: desire, action, and assent.

The Enchiridion contains several memorable passages: one must behave in life as at a banquet; it makes more sense for a horse to say 'I am beautiful' than its owner; one should drink cold water and spit it out when thirsty to avoid showing off; and the criticism that saying 'he is a bad father' confuses relationships with qualities. It ends by quoting Cleanthes, Euripides, and Plato's Apology as three maxims to keep 'ready at hand for all occasions.'

Epictetus's ethical teaching aimed at eudaimonia (flourishing), achievable through ataraxia, apatheia, and eupatheiai. He identified two types of 'other-concern': aidos (modesty) and philanthropia (willingness to act on another's behalf). He used vivid examples, such as a slave holding a chamberpot to illustrate how what is unbearable differs from person to person.

Epictetus profoundly influenced Marcus Aurelius, who quoted him frequently in the Meditations. The teacher Rusticus introduced Marcus Aurelius to Epictetus's Discourses. In the modern era, James Stockdale was inspired by Epictetus during his time as a POW in Vietnam. A.A. Long recently translated the Discourses as 'How to Be Free.'
