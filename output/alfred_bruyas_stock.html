Alfred Bruyas

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
French art patron and collector (1821–1877) from Montpellier, son of a banker. Best known as the primary patron of Gustave Courbet. Bruyas helped Courbet open the 1855 Pavilion of Realism after Courbet's works were rejected from the Exposition Universelle. Depicted with his distinctive red beard alongside Courbet in the painting The Meeting ('Bonjour, Monsieur Courbet'). Also depicted by Courbet in The Painter's Studio. His collection is housed in the Musée Fabre in Montpellier.
The Meeting / Bonjour Monsieur Courbet (1854)

Painting by Gustave Courbet showing himself (with Assyrian-style beard, backpack, and walking stick) as a 'Wandering Jew' figure being greeted on the road to Montpellier by Bruyas (red-bearded) and his servant Calas, both doffing their caps. Critics dubbed it 'Bonjour, Monsieur Courbet.' Inspired Gauguin's own self-referential version. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Alfred Bruyas (1821–1877) was a French art collector and patron from Montpellier, the son of a wealthy banker, best known for his close relationship with the Realist painter Gustave Courbet. Bruyas is depicted with his distinctive red beard in Courbet's painting The Meeting (1854), also known as 'Bonjour, Monsieur Courbet,' which shows Courbet arriving on foot like a wandering Jew figure while Bruyas and his servant Calas both doff their hats to him on the road outside Montpellier. Critics mocked the painting's apparent reversal of social hierarchy. The composition was later referenced by Paul Gauguin in his own Good Morning, Monsieur Gauguin, and parodied by pop artist Peter Blake in Have a Nice Day, Mr. Hockney, which is set on a Venice Beach rollerskating rink. Bruyas also appears as one of the many figures in Courbet's monumental allegorical work The Painter's Studio (1855), standing among a group of Parisian elites (including Baudelaire and Proudhon) who observe Courbet painting a landscape while ignoring a nude model behind him and a white cat at his feet. When the painting was rejected from the 1855 Exposition Universelle, Bruyas helped Courbet open the independent Pavilion of Realism nearby to exhibit it. Bruyas's art collection is housed in the Musée Fabre in Montpellier.
