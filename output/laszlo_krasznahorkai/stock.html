Hungarian novelist described by Susan Sontag as a 'contemporary master of the apocalypse.' Known for long, labyrinthine sentences and dark, apocalyptic themes. His debut novel Satantango was adapted into a seven-hour film by Bela Tarr. Major works include The Melancholy of Resistance, War and War, Seiobo There Below, The Last Wolf, and The World Goes On. Won the 2015 Man Booker International Prize. Frequently compared to Kafka and Beckett.

Summary of Facts

Laszlo Krasznahorkai (born 1954) is a Hungarian novelist whom Susan Sontag famously described as a 'contemporary master of the apocalypse' — a phrase that appears in virtually every question about him. His best-known work is Satantango (1985), his debut novel about the conman Irimias who convinces inhabitants of a post-communist collective farm to flee to a crumbling mansion. It was adapted by Bela Tarr into an extremely slow, seven-hour film, which is one of the most common clues. The Melancholy of Resistance is equally prominent, centered on a traveling circus that arrives in a small town with the world's largest dead whale; Mr. Eszter's attempt to supplant the 'artificial' tuning system of Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier is a distinctive clue, as is Mrs. Eszter's scheming to head the town committee on moral renewal. The novel allegorizes the rise of fascism and was adapted by Tarr as Werckmeister Harmonies. War and War features Georgy Korin, whose life changes after finding an unknown manuscript about four adventurers; its epigraph states that 'Heaven is sad.' Seiobo There Below is a novel with interconnected chapters on aesthetics numbered according to the Fibonacci sequence, featuring a Kyoto heron, a Louvre guard, and the apprentice Aulista questioning whether Perugino has lost interest in painting. The World Goes On (2014) subverts his apocalyptic reputation with its title, ending with the prose poem 'I Don't Need Anything from Here.' The Last Wolf is a single-sentence novella about reporting on the killing of the title animal in Extremadura; its companion Herman features a game warden who decides to hunt humans. Krasznahorkai won the 2015 Man Booker International Prize and is frequently compared to Kafka and Beckett.
