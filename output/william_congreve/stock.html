The Way of the World

Congreve's best-known comedy, performed in the first year of the 18th century. Mirabell and Millamant are witty lovers who negotiate terms of marriage in the famous 'proviso scene,' in which Millamant demands she be allowed to sleep as long as she wants and never have a female confidante imposed on her, while Mirabell demands she never wear masks made from 'hog's bones, hare's gall, pig water, or the marrow of a roasted cat.' Millamant pins up her hair only with letters written in verse, not prose. Mirabell convinces his servant Waitwell to pose as the rich Sir Rowland to hoodwink Lady Wishfort. Fainall's blackmail scheme is foiled when it is revealed that Mrs. Fainall's fortune already belongs to Mirabell. The play opens with characters playing cards in a chocolate house. In Act Five, two characters break an oath because it was sworn on a poetry collection instead of a bible. Foible reveals Mrs. Marwood's affair. The Prologue, spoken by Mr. Betterton, contrasts 'natural' fools with 'fools of fortune.' Charles Lamb praised it for interesting the audience in characters 'for whom you care absolutely nothing' in his essay 'On the Artificial Comedy of the Last Century.' Millamant tries to avoid being married off to Sir Wilfull Witwoud.

Love for Love

Angelica, the niece of the astrology-loving Mr. Foresight, tests Valentine's devotion by pretending to love his father Sir Sampson. Scandal attempts to cuckold Mr. Foresight. In the opening scene, a character asks if Plato will pay Valentine's bail, or if Epictetus or Seneca taught him to pay debts without money, before they get Trapland drunk on sack. A character weighs the good omen of getting out of bed backwards with the bad omen of encountering a weasel. Coined the phrase 'kiss and tell,' used by the gossip Mr. Tattle.

General / Biographical

Congreve was a major Restoration playwright active during the reign of Charles II, alongside William Wycherley, George Etherege, John Vanbrugh, and George Farquhar. He was a member of the Kit-Cat Club and a friend of Jonathan Swift. He wrote a lengthy response to Jeremy Collier's critique 'A Short View of the Immorality and Profaneness of the English Stage.' He also wrote the libretto for Handel's oratorio Semele, which contains the air 'Where'er you walk.' Sir William Congreve (a different person, the military engineer) based his rocket design on Mysorean rockets from the Kingdom of Mysore, used at the Battle of Pollilur by Tipu Sultan; those Congreve rockets were later used in the War of 1812 at Fort McHenry.

Summary of Facts

William Congreve was a major Restoration playwright, active during the reign of Charles II alongside contemporaries William Wycherley, George Etherege, John Vanbrugh, and George Farquhar. He is best known for The Way of the World, a comedy of manners performed in the first year of the 18th century. Its central characters are the witty lovers Mirabell and Millamant, who negotiate the terms of their marriage in the famous 'proviso scene' -- Millamant demands to sleep as long as she wants and pins up her hair only with letters in verse, while Mirabell demands she never wear masks made from 'hog's bones, hare's gall, pig water, or the marrow of a roasted cat.' Mirabell convinces his servant Waitwell to pose as the rich Sir Rowland to hoodwink Lady Wishfort. Fainall's blackmail scheme is destroyed when it emerges that Mrs. Fainall's fortune already belongs to Mirabell. The play opens in a chocolate house, and its prologue (spoken by Mr. Betterton) contrasts 'natural' fools with 'fools of fortune.' Charles Lamb praised the play in his essay 'On the Artificial Comedy of the Last Century' for interesting the audience in characters 'for whom you care absolutely nothing.'

Love for Love is a comedy about Valentine Legend, who feigns madness and proclaims himself Truth to avoid signing over his inheritance to his sailor brother Ben. Angelica, niece of the astrology-loving Mr. Foresight, tests Valentine's devotion by pretending to love his father Sir Sampson. The play coined the phrase 'kiss and tell,' used by the gossip Mr. Tattle. In its opening scene, a character asks if Plato or Seneca can pay Valentine's debts, before they get the creditor Trapland drunk on sack.

The Mourning Bride is Congreve's only tragedy. It opens with Almeria, princess of Granada, declaring 'Music has charms to soothe a savage breast.' In Act 3, Zara delivers the line paraphrased as 'hell hath no fury like a woman scorned' -- more accurately, 'heav'n has no rage, like love to hatred turn'd, / Nor hell a fury, like a woman scorn'd.' Zara imbibes poison, mistakenly believing she dies beside Osmyn.

The Double Dealer features the nefarious title character Maskwell scheming against Mellefont's courtship of Cynthia, while Brisk cuckolds Lord Froth. In The Old Bachelor, Bellmour cuckolds the banker Fondlewife.

Congreve was a member of the Kit-Cat Club and a friend of Jonathan Swift. He wrote a lengthy response to Jeremy Collier's critique 'A Short View of the Immorality and Profaneness of the English Stage.' He also wrote the libretto for Handel's oratorio Semele, which contains the celebrated air 'Where'er you walk.'
