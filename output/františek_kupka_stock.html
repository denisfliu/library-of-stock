Summary of Facts

František Kupka (1871-1957) was a Czech abstract painter who, along with Robert and Sonia Delaunay, co-founded the Orphism movement. His work inspired Guillaume Apollinaire to coin the term "Orphism." Kupka was a Theosophist, and this spiritual involvement partly motivated his abstraction. He wrote the treatise Creation in the Plastic Arts, arguing that different colors provoke different emotions.

His best-known series is Discs of Newton (Study for Fugue in Two Colors), featuring multicolored circular forms blending into each other based on Newton's discovery of the composition of light. His painting The Cathedral, part of a series titled Vertical and Diagonal Planes, was inspired by blue and red stained glass at Chartres Cathedral, using blue parallelograms to evoke stained-glass windows. Piano Keyboard/Lake, depicting a lake melding with a piano, marks his transition away from representational art. The Yellow Scale is an unusual self-portrait dominated by the title color, in which he slumps back in a chair holding a book and raising a cigarette.

Other works include The Wave (a red-haired girl on a rock facing the sea), The Beginning of Life (giant lily pads with two orbs, one containing a fetus), and Planes By Colors (an exception to his non-representational style, featuring a reclining female nude). He also painted a portrait of his wife "among verticals." He enrolled in the Foreign Legion during WWI, and his wife attempted to accompany him on a march to Picardy but was arrested and sent back to Paris.
