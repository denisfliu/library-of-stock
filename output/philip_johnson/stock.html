Philip Johnson was an American architect who won the first Pritzker Architecture Prize in 1979. Best known for his Glass House in New Canaan, Connecticut, and his collaboration with Ludwig Mies van der Rohe on the Seagram Building, he also designed the AT&T Building (with its Chippendale top), PPG Place in Pittsburgh, the Crystal Cathedral, the Fort Worth Water Gardens, and the Chapel of Thanksgiving. He defected from Modernism to Postmodernism and coined the term 'International Style.' His pro-Nazi sympathies in the 1930s led him to design the Kneses Tifereth Israel Synagogue for free as atonement.
Glass House

The Glass House (1949) in New Canaan, Connecticut was Philip Johnson's personal residence where he lived with partner David Whitney and where he died. Modeled on Mies van der Rohe's Farnsworth House, it has a brick cylinder that conceals the bathroom — the only fully enclosed space. Grounds include the 'Da Monsta' red-and-black gatehouse, a Painting Gallery (three circular rooms inspired by Agamemnon's tomb, with rotating 'poster racks' to view 42 artworks two at a time), a Monument to Lincoln Kirstein (secret inscription atop a 'staircase to nowhere'), landscaping modeled on Poussin's The Burial of Phocion, and a cylindrical Donald Judd sculpture.

AT&T Building (550 Madison Avenue)

The AT&T Building (now 550 Madison Avenue) is crowned by an open pediment that earned it the nickname 'Chippendale Building.' It became a landmark of Postmodernism and represented Johnson's defection from Modernist 'Whites' to Postmodernist 'Grays.'

Biographical

He defected from the Modernist 'Whites' faction to the Postmodernist 'Grays' led by Venturi. Summary of Facts

Philip Johnson (1906–2005) was an American architect who became the first recipient of the Pritzker Architecture Prize in 1979. He coined the term 'International Style' in a book published concurrently with a MoMA exhibition. He later defected from the Modernist 'Whites' faction (led by Peter Eisenman and Richard Meier) to the Postmodernist 'Grays' led by Robert Venturi and Denise Scott Brown — the highest-profile defection in American architecture.

His most famous work is the Glass House in New Canaan, Connecticut (1949), which he shared with partner David Whitney and where he died. The Glass House was based on Mies van der Rohe's Farnsworth House, with a brick cylinder as the only fully enclosed space concealing the bathroom. The surrounding grounds (a key clue set) include the 'Da Monsta' red-and-black visitor's pavilion, a Painting Gallery inspired by Agamemnon's tomb (three circular rooms with rotating 'poster racks' to view 42 artworks two at a time), a Monument to Lincoln Kirstein (with a secret inscription atop a 'staircase to nowhere'), landscaping modeled on Nicolas Poussin's The Burial of Phocion, and a cylindrical sculpture by Donald Judd. The complex also included an Urban Glass House condo project in Hudson Square (his last project before his 2005 death). Johnson was a member of the 'Harvard Five' group of Connecticut architects alongside Eliot Noyes, Marcel Breuer, and others.

Johnson collaborated with Ludwig Mies van der Rohe on the Seagram Building (375 Park Avenue, New York), designing its interiors including the Four Seasons Restaurant. He insisted the blinds could only have three positions. The building scored 3 out of 100 for its Energy Star rating. The Seagram Murals by Mark Rothko (who later withdrew them) were also central to this commission — the play Red by John Logan depicts Rothko calling Philip Johnson after dining badly at the Four Seasons.

The AT&T Building (now 550 Madison Avenue) has a 'Chippendale top' open pediment, an iconic Postmodernist statement. Johnson also designed PPG Place in Pittsburgh (231 glass spires), the Gates of Europe in Madrid (two buildings that mirror each other inclined at 15 degrees), the Crystal Cathedral in Garden Grove, California, the Chapel of Thanksgiving in Dallas's Thanks-Giving Square (inspired by the Malwiya minaret in Samarra, 1976), the JFK Memorial in Dallas (criticized as 'mammoth Lego blocks'), the Fort Worth Water Gardens (pool co-designed with John Burgee), the IDS Tower in Minneapolis, the Amon Carter Museum in Fort Worth, the New York State Pavilion at the 1964 World's Fair, and the Rothko Chapel reflecting pool in Houston (containing Barnett Newman's Broken Obelisk).

He designed the Kneses Tifereth Israel Synagogue in Port Chester, New York for free as atonement for his pro-Nazi sympathies in the 1930s. Robert Venturi in Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture praised Johnson's Wiley House but criticized the Glass House as 'bland architecture.'
