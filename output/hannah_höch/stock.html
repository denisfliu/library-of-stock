Summary of Facts

Hannah Höch (1889-1978) was a German Dada artist and pioneer of photomontage. She was part of the Berlin Dadaists alongside George Grosz, John Heartfield, and Kurt Schwitters, and was the lover and collaborator of Raoul Hausmann (creator of Mechanical Head / The Spirit of Our Time).

Her most famous work is Cut with the Kitchen Knife Dada through the Last Weimar Beer-Belly Cultural Epoch of Germany (1919), a massive photomontage using newspaper cuttings. The top part prominently features Friedrich Ebert and a looming image of Wilhelm II, while Käthe Kollwitz, John Heartfield, Marx, and Lenin appear in the bottom half. John Heartfield can be seen being bathed by Niddy Impekoven in the lower right. It is divided into four sections, one named for being 'anti-Dada,' and includes a map of women's suffrage. Near the center, a headless dancer throws a woman's head toward an elephant. Paul von Hindenburg's face is placed on an exotic dancer's body in the top-right. The 'kitchen knife' in the title alludes to the collage/cutting technique itself.

Her From an Ethnographic Museum series combines magazine cutouts with photographs of ethnographic artifacts. The Sweet One depicts an African statue with a flapper's legs. She overlaid a still of Renée Falconetti with a Cameroonian mask in another entry.

Other works include The Beautiful Girl (a woman with a lightbulb head peering over a pocket watch amid BMW logos), High Finance (a banker with John Herschel's head threatened by a giant gun), Pretty Girl (discussed by Linda Nochlin in 'Women, Art, and Power'), and Watched (a soldier on a hill staring at a giant rose atop a tree). She created a photomontage protesting abortion regulations in Germany depicting a mother wearing a mask. She contributed to Retrato de la Burguesia due to anti-Nazi beliefs, and the Nazis branded her work 'degenerate art.'
