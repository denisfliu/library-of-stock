Centre Pompidou (1977)

Co-designed with Renzo Piano, this Paris museum and cultural center is the defining work of high-tech/inside-out architecture. Its exterior is covered with color-coded pipes and tubes: blue for air/oxygen, green for water, yellow for electricity, red for elevators and escalators. Features a large exterior escalator in a glass tube nicknamed 'the caterpillar.' Houses the Musée National d'Art Moderne, a library, and IRCAM (musical research center). Named for former French President (and Prime Minister) Georges Pompidou. Design was inspired by Cedric Price's Fun Palace and the Archigram movement (specifically the 'Plug-In City'). Gordon Matta-Clark's Conical Intersect critiqued its perceived gentrification. The Stravinsky Fountain outside features sculptures by Niki de Saint-Phalle and Jean Tinguely. Uses 9-ton cast-steel 'gerberettes' to support long trusses. Wikipedia

Lloyd's Building (1986)

London office building for Lloyd's of London insurance market, also known as the 'Inside-Out Building.' Like the Centre Pompidou, it places utilities — pipes, ducts, elevators, staircases — on the exterior to maximize interior space. Has six exterior service towers. Contains the famous Lutine Bell in its underwriting room. Sits across the street from Norman Foster's Willis Building. Described as 'Bowellist' and also as the 'pop' version of Gothic architecture. General / Biographical

British architect (full title: Richard George Rogers, Baron Rogers of Riverside). Pioneer of high-tech architecture alongside Norman Foster. Met Foster at Yale; together with their future spouses Su Brumwell (Rogers) and Wendy Cheesman (Foster) they co-founded the firm Team 4, which designed the Reliance Controls factory. After Team 4, Rogers partnered with Renzo Piano on the Centre Pompidou. King Charles III (as Prince of Wales) compared Rogers's plans to rebuild Paternoster Square, bombed by the Luftwaffe, as being worse than the bombing itself; he also pressured the Qatari royal family to remove Rogers from a project. Other works include the Senedd (Welsh Parliament) in Wales, the Barajas Airport Terminal 4 in Madrid, and participation in the post-9/11 World Trade Center redevelopment (with Fumihiko Maki and others under Daniel Libeskind).

Summary of Facts

Richard Rogers (full title: Richard George Rogers, Baron Rogers of Riverside) is a British architect and one of the preeminent figures of high-tech architecture — a style that places mechanical and structural systems (pipes, ducts, elevators, escalators, staircases) on the exterior of buildings to maximize interior space. This approach is also sometimes called 'Bowellism' or the 'inside-out' style. Rogers met Norman Foster at Yale University, and together with their future spouses — Rogers with Su Brumwell and Foster with Wendy Cheesman — they co-founded the architectural firm Team 4, which designed the Reliance Controls factory before the partnership dissolved.

Rogers's most celebrated work is the Centre Pompidou in Paris (1977), co-designed with Italian architect Renzo Piano. The building is a defining monument of high-tech architecture: its exterior is covered with a color-coded system of pipes and tubes (blue for oxygen/air, green for water, yellow for electricity, red for elevators/escalators). A large exterior escalator runs up the facade within a glass tube nicknamed 'the caterpillar.' The Centre Pompidou houses the Musée National d'Art Moderne, a public library, and the music research center IRCAM. Its design was inspired by Cedric Price's Fun Palace and the Archigram movement's 'Plug-In City' concept. 9-ton cast-steel 'gerberettes' support the building's long trusses. Outside the building stands the Stravinsky Fountain, featuring sculptures by Niki de Saint-Phalle and Jean Tinguely. Gordon Matta-Clark created Conical Intersect to critique the perceived gentrification caused by the building's construction. Sanford Kwinter called it a 'Trojan Horse' in his book Requiem: For the City at the End of the Millennium. The building was named for French President (and former Prime Minister) Georges Pompidou, who commissioned it and died in office in 1974.

Another landmark Rogers work is the Lloyd's Building in London (1986), also known as the 'Inside-Out Building,' designed for Lloyd's of London insurance market. Like the Centre Pompidou, it externalizes utilities — placing service towers, pipes, and staircases on the outside (Rogers moved the stairs to corner towers in the 1978 design). It has six exterior towers. The building contains the famous Lutine Bell in its underwriting room. It has been described as the 'pop Gothic' version of Gothic architecture, and sits across the street from Norman Foster's Willis Building.

Rogers also designed the Millennium Dome in Greenwich (1999), now known as the O2 arena. It resembles a large white marquee with twelve yellow support towers, each representing an hour on a clock face, symbolizing the importance of Greenwich Mean Time. It was considered costly and controversial.

Other works mentioned in the clues include the Senedd (Welsh Parliament), the Barajas Airport Terminal 4 in Madrid, and his participation in the World Trade Center redevelopment team alongside Fumihiko Maki, Norman Foster, and others under master architect Daniel Libeskind, a project that includes the memorial 'Reflecting Absence.'

Rogers attracted criticism from King Charles III (as Prince of Wales), who compared Rogers's plans for the post-Blitz reconstruction of Paternoster Square as worse than the Luftwaffe bombings themselves. Charles also reportedly pressured the Qatari royal family to remove Rogers from a project.
