Mexican poet, essayist, and diplomat. Best known for the 584-line poem 'Sunstone' (based on the Aztec calendar / synodic period of Venus) and the essay collection The Labyrinth of Solitude (about Mexican identity). Wrote a biography of Sor Juana Ines de la Cruz titled The Traps of Faith. His poetry was frequently translated by Eliot Weinberger. Won the 1990 Nobel Prize in Literature and the 1977 Jerusalem Prize. Served as ambassador to India. Collaborated with Jacques Roubaud, Edoardo Sanguineti, and Charles Tomlinson on the quadrilingual renga. Husband of Elena Garro. Mentored Julian Rios. Wrote the prologue to Alejandra Pizarnik's The Tree of Diana.
Sunstone (Piedra de Sol)

A 584-line cyclical poem based on the Aztec calendar (specifically the synodic period of Venus). Opens and closes with the same six lines, beginning with 'a willow of crystal, a poplar of water.' The poem describes 'a river that turns, moves on, doubles back, and comes full circle.' It addresses a feminine figure with the lines 'I travel your body, like the world' and 'I've forgotten your name, Melusina, Laura, Isabel, Persephone, Mary.' Includes the lines 'now you resemble the sharp edge of a sword / and now the executioner's bowl of blood.' The halfway point is the line 'Madrid, 1937.' Translated by Eliot Weinberger. Contains the lines 'resurrection flower, grape of life, lady of the flute and the lightning-flash.' Wikipedia

The Labyrinth of Solitude

A book-length essay collection about Mexican identity and culture. Includes the essays 'The Pachuco and Other Extremes' (about Mexican-American youth subculture), 'The Sons of La Malinche' (describes Mexicans as descendants of a traitorous concubine / a 'violated woman'; defines 'La Chingada' as 'the Mother forcibly opened, violated or deceived'; discusses the macho figure as a 'mask'), 'The Conquest and Colonialism,' and 'The Day of the Dead.' Discusses the idea that 'death was not the natural end of life but one phase of an infinite cycle.' Claims solitude is 'the profoundest fact of the human condition.' Describes attitudes of hermeticism as 'masks' worn by Mexicans. Paz added 'Post Data' after the Tlatelolco student massacre. Also discusses Xavier Villaurrutia's Nostalgia for Death. Wikipedia

The Traps of Faith (Sor Juana biography)

A biography of Sor Juana Ines de la Cruz. Discusses a woman who tried to 'reconcile illuminism and the Baroque' and was 'superior both to her society and her culture.' Describes her world as 'closed to the future.' Analyzes 'First Dream' as an allegory for Sor Juana's desire for knowledge. Returned to themes from 'The Conquest and Colonialism' in The Labyrinth of Solitude. Discusses the challenges that led Sor Juana to become a nun and her defense of women's education. Wikipedia

The Bow and the Lyre / Children of the Mire

The Bow and the Lyre is a book of criticism named after a Heraclitus fragment about the unity of opposites. Claims the poetic experience 'transmutes man into an image,' which Paz defines as 'a space where opposites fuse.' Children of the Mire predicted the end of the avant-garde. Claims the only great modern poet who does not experience inconformity is Walt Whitman. Wikipedia

The Monkey Grammarian

A 1974 hybrid prose work about language, influenced by Paz's time as ambassador to India. Analyzes the origins of language through the figures of Hanuman (the monkey god) and an idealized woman named 'Splendor.' Inspired by a journey to the ruined temples of Galta. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Mexican poet, essayist, and diplomat (1914-1998). Won the 1977 Jerusalem Prize and the 1990 Nobel Prize in Literature. Served as ambassador to India (inspired The Monkey Grammarian and East Slope). His poetry was frequently translated by Eliot Weinberger, who also wrote 'Nineteen Ways of Looking at Wang Wei' (with Paz's afterword). Collaborated with Jacques Roubaud, Edoardo Sanguineti, and Charles Tomlinson on a quadrilingual renga. Collaborated with Samuel Beckett on an anthology of Mexican poetry. Characterized poetry as 'between what I see and what I say' in a letter to Roman Jakobson. Husband of Elena Garro. Wrote the prologue to Pizarnik's The Tree of Diana. Mentored Julian Rios. Wrote an essay 'The Siren and the Seashell' about Ruben Dario. Wrote an adaptation of Hawthorne's 'Rappaccini's Daughter' inspired by Yeats, Noh theatre, and Vishakadatta. Also wrote 'Wind, Water, Stone,' 'San Ildefonso Nocturne,' 'Solo for Two Voices,' 'Return,' 'The Clerk's Vision,' Blanco, East Slope, and the essay 'Here People Talk to God.'

Summary of Facts

Octavio Paz (1914-1998) is the most frequently asked-about Mexican poet in quizbowl. The overwhelming majority of questions reference his 584-line poem 'Sunstone' (Piedra de Sol), a cyclical work based on the Aztec calendar (specifically the synodic period of Venus) that opens and closes with the same six lines beginning 'a willow of crystal, a poplar of water.' This opening line is the single most common giveaway in all Paz questions. Other frequently clued lines include 'I travel your body, like the world' and 'I've forgotten your name, Melusina, Laura, Isabel, Persephone, Mary.' The poem was translated by his close friend Eliot Weinberger. The Labyrinth of Solitude is nearly as dominant, an essay collection analyzing Mexican identity. Its key essays include 'The Sons of La Malinche' (which describes Mexicans as descendants of a 'violated woman' and discusses the concept of 'La Chingada'), 'The Pachuco and Other Extremes' (about Mexican-American youth subculture, the first essay in the collection), 'The Day of the Dead,' and 'The Conquest and Colonialism.' It claims solitude is 'the profoundest fact of the human condition.' The Traps of Faith is his biography of Sor Juana Ines de la Cruz, appearing in about a third of all questions. The Bow and the Lyre, named after a Heraclitus fragment about the unity of opposites, claims the poetic experience 'transmutes man into an image.' Eagle or Sun? is a prose poem collection containing 'My Life with the Wave,' about a narrator who brings an ocean wave home. The Monkey Grammarian, inspired by his time as ambassador to India, analyzes language through Hanuman. Biographically, Paz served as ambassador to India, collaborated on a quadrilingual renga, wrote the prologue to Pizarnik's The Tree of Diana, and was the husband of Elena Garro. He won the Jerusalem Prize in 1977. Weinberger frequently translated his work and co-authored a study of Wang Wei's 'Deer Park' with Paz's afterword.
