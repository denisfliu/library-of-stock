Summary of Facts

Henry Fielding was an 18th-century English novelist and dramatist, one of the founders of the English novel.

Tom Jones (1749) is a comic picaresque novel in 18 books, each opened by a mini-essay, about a foundling discovered as a baby in Squire Allworthy's bed. Tom is raised alongside the duplicitous Master Blifil, educated by the contrasting tutors Square (philosophical, beauty-obsessed) and Thwackum (dogmatic student-beater). Tom impregnates Black George's daughter Molly Seagrim, and Square is later found sleeping with Molly. Tom breaks his arm catching Sophia Western as she falls from a horse. Sophia's father, a drunken boor obsessed with hunting, locks her in her room to force her to marry Blifil; she runs away to London, leaving her muff on Tom's bed (which he kisses 100 times). At the inn at Upton, Tom sleeps with Mrs. Waters in a mock-heroic seduction scene, then fears incest when told she is Jenny Jones. Lady Bellaston and Lord Fellamar plot against Sophia; Fellamar nearly assaults her. Lawyer Mr. Dowling is ordered to execute Tom for a duel with Mr. Fitzpatrick. Mr. Partridge, the barber-surgeon long suspected to be Tom's father, criticizes David Garrick's acting as too realistic. Near the end, Mr. Square recants lies devised by Blifil, and Tom is revealed to be Bridget's son (Allworthy's nephew and Blifil's half-brother). A chapter mimics Homer's style for a graveyard battle where Molly fends off churchgoers with a thighbone. Tom encounters a Roma wedding and accosts the novice highwayman Mr. Anderson, giving him two guineas. Ian Watt's Rise of the Novel discusses the scene where Sophia leaves upon Blifil's arrival. The 1963 film adaptation starring Albert Finney won Best Picture.

Shamela parodies Samuel Richardson's Pamela, with Mrs. Booby exclaiming 'Your virtue? I'll never survive it!' Eliza Haywood also wrote The Anti-Pamela. Joseph Andrews, subtitled 'written in imitation of the manner of' Don Quixote, features the good-natured Parson Abraham Adams, an Aeschylus fan who is chased by hunting dogs in a scene parodying Homeric combat. Joseph, Pamela's 'brother,' resists Lady Booby's advances, feuds with Mrs. Slipslop, and marries Fanny Goodwill. He is recognized as Mr. Wilson's long-lost son by a strawberry birthmark on his chest. Sarah Fielding likely wrote Leonora's letter to Horatio in Joseph Andrews.

Amelia has parallels with the Aeneid and features William Booth unjustly imprisoned in Newgate. Amelia's nose is ruined in a carriage accident, and William is seduced in prison by Miss Matthews.

Fielding's play Tom Thumb (The Tragedy of Tragedies) caricatures Robert Walpole as a tiny character who defeats giants for King Arthur, wins Princess Huncamunca, and is swallowed by a red cow. After Walpole passed the Licensing Act of 1737 (prompted by plays like Eurydice Hiss'd), Fielding was driven from the theater to novels. He wrote The Welsh Opera and The Grub Street Opera under the pseudonym Scriblerus Secundus.

Fielding founded the Bow Street Runners, an early London police force later replaced by the Metropolitan Police under Robert Peel. He wrote An Enquiry Into the Causes of the Late Increase of Robbers and campaigned against gin. He started the 'Paper War' via The Covent-Garden Journal. He called the Odyssey 'the eating-est epic.' His novel Jonathan Wild fictionalizes the life of the 'Thief-Taker General.' His sister Sarah Fielding wrote The Governess (often called the first English children's novel), The Adventures of David Simple, and the epistolary Remarks on Clarissa.
