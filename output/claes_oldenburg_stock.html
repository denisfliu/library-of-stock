Summary of Facts

Claes Oldenburg is a Swedish-born American sculptor associated with the Pop Art movement, known for creating giant versions of everyday objects in both hard and soft varieties. He collaborated extensively with his wife Coosje van Bruggen from the 1970s onward. The majority of his sculptures are made of steel.

His most frequently clued work is Lipstick (Ascending) on Caterpillar Tracks, a giant lipstick on a tank-shaped base at Yale University, originally installed on Beinecke Plaza. The top was initially an inflatable red vinyl balloon, later replaced with a steel version. The sculpture sits in the Morse College courtyard at Yale, a campus that also houses two Louis Kahn art galleries and Robert Venturi's stylized fake trees.

Spoonbridge and Cherry, created with van Bruggen for the Walker Art Center's Minneapolis Sculpture Garden (west of Loring Park), is a curved stainless steel spoon spanning a linden seed-shaped pond. The cherry was designed by van Bruggen and inspired by Versailles. Floor Burger is an enormous hamburger sculpture made of rubber and canvas, part of his broader practice of 'soft' sculptures of household objects including a toilet, shuttlecock, bathtub, and screw. He also made a soft sculpture of a car for the cover of ARTnews, with readers invited to cut out a 'personal Oldenburg souvenir.'

Dropped Cone places an upside-down ice cream cone atop a Cologne shopping center. The Shuttlecock series consists of four giant white feather shuttlecocks at the Nelson-Atkins Museum in Kansas City, inspired by a Native American headdress in a Frederic Remington painting, which must be repainted every year.

Other major sculptures include Clothespin in Philadelphia, Batcolumn (a gray steel lattice baseball bat in Chicago), Free Stamp in Cleveland's Willard Park, and the Binoculars Building (Chiat/Day Building) designed with Frank Gehry featuring giant binoculars at its entrance/parking garage. He modeled a Chrysler car 'profile' in polyurethane inspired by a children's toy and created a weathering steel sculpture for SEPTA's City Hall Station embedding the number '76' into a spring. He proposed replacing Trafalgar Square's Nelson's Column with a 'Colossal Monument' of a 'Gearshift in Motion' in a crayon and watercolor sketch.

Oldenburg operated the Ray Gun Manufacturing Company shopfront, also known as 'The Store,' which served as his studio. He exhibited found objects in the Mouse Museum, a black box shaped like Mickey Mouse displayed with the Ray Gun Wing. He scribbled a crude Mickey Mouse drawing for Forrest Myers' Moon Museum, a ceramic wafer attached to the Apollo 12 Lunar Module. He was involved in Happenings (non-verbal artistic performances), many of which were performed at his Ray Gun Theater. He was taught by Allan Kaprow, the inventor of Happenings, and held his first one-man show at the Judson Memorial Church (which also hosted Carolee Schneemann's Meat Joy). He staged The Course of the Knife at Venice's Arsenale in 1986 and held a collaborative show with Jim Dine featuring his newspaper-and-wire 'Empire' Ray Gun. Brian O'Doherty grouped his recreation of a bedroom with works by Edward Kienholz and George Segal. Additional works include Typewriter Eraser (Scale X), a giant BLT sandwich in the Whitney, the Geometric Mouse, and a sculpture at a horse's grave in Marfa, Texas.
