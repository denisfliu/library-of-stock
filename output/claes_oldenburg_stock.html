Summary of Facts

Claes Oldenburg is a Swedish-born American sculptor associated with the Pop Art movement, known for creating giant versions of everyday objects in both hard and soft varieties. He collaborated extensively with his wife Coosje van Bruggen from the 1970s onward.

His most frequently clued work is Lipstick (Ascending) on Caterpillar Tracks, a giant lipstick on a tank-shaped base at Yale University, originally installed on Beinecke Plaza with an inflatable balloon top. Spoonbridge and Cherry, created with van Bruggen for the Walker Art Center in Minneapolis, is a curved stainless steel spoon spanning a linden seed-shaped pond. Floor Burger is an enormous hamburger sculpture made of rubber and canvas, part of his broader practice of 'soft' sculptures of household objects including a toilet, shuttlecock, bathtub, and screw.

Dropped Cone places an upside-down ice cream cone atop a Cologne shopping center. The Shuttlecock series consists of four giant white feather shuttlecocks at the Nelson-Atkins Museum in Kansas City, inspired by a Native American headdress in a Frederic Remington painting, which must be repainted every year. Other works include Clothespin in Philadelphia, a weathering steel sculpture for SEPTA's City Hall Station embedding the number '76' into a spring, Typewriter Eraser (Scale X), a giant BLT sandwich in the Whitney, and the Geometric Mouse.

Oldenburg operated the Ray Gun Manufacturing Company shopfront and exhibited in the Mouse Museum. He was involved in Happenings (non-verbal artistic performances) and staged The Course of the Knife at Venice's Arsenale in 1986. He held a collaborative show with Jim Dine featuring his newspaper-and-wire 'Empire' Ray Gun. Brian O'Doherty grouped his recreation of a bedroom with works by Edward Kienholz and George Segal in essays about gallery spaces.
