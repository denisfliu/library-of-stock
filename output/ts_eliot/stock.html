The Waste Land

Eliot's landmark modernist poem in five sections, edited by and dedicated to Ezra Pound. It opens with the iconic line 'April is the cruellest month.' It paraphrases Dante to describe a crowd 'flowing' over a bridge: 'I had not thought death had undone so many.' It quotes Baudelaire's 'hypocrite lecteur!-mon semblable,-mon frere.' Section III, 'The Fire Sermon,' repeats 'burning burning burning burning' and features Tiresias as a partial narrator. Phlebas the Phoenician appears 'a fortnight dead,' having first appeared in 'Dans le Restaurant.' Eliot wrote that he 'quite arbitrarily' associated 'The Man with Three Staves' with the Fisher King while explaining the poem's Tarot card references. Section V is titled 'What the Thunder Said.' The poem ends by repeating a Sanskrit word three times. Eliot considered using his poem 'Gerontion' as a preface to it.

The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock

Eliot's early dramatic monologue in which the speaker asks 'Do I dare / Disturb the universe?' and 'how should I presume?' The poem repeats 'there will be time' and mentions 'a hundred indecisions' and 'a hundred visions and revisions.' It adapts from Shakespeare's Sonnet 29 the line 'desiring this man's art and that man's scope.'

Four Quartets

A set of four poems including 'Burnt Norton,' 'East Coker,' 'The Dry Salvages,' and 'Little Gidding.' 'Burnt Norton' opens 'time present and time past / are both perhaps present in time future' and moves from an empty pool in a rose garden to a 'place of disaffection,' the London Underground. 'East Coker' opens 'in my beginning is my end.' Eliot's ashes are held in a village that gives one poem its title, which ends with a personal motto of Mary, Queen of Scots. 'The Dry Salvages' evokes the Mississippi as a 'strong brown god,' drawing on Eliot's St. Louis upbringing. It refers to time as 'destroyer and preserver,' paraphrasing Shelley's 'Ode to the West Wind.' It notes 'time is no healer' in a section imagining a conversation from a Sanskrit book. 'Burnt Norton' claims 'only through time time is conquered' and ends 'ridiculous the sad waste time / stretching before and after.'

Literary Criticism

Eliot's critical essays were foundational for New Criticism. 'Tradition and the Individual Talent' argues for a 'continual extinction of personality' and compares the poet's mind to platinum in a chemical reaction ('Impersonal theory of poetry'). It was collected in The Sacred Wood alongside 'Hamlet and His Problems,' which coined the term 'objective correlative' and called Hamlet an 'artistic failure' compared to the Mona Lisa. 'The Metaphysical Poets' revived interest in poets like Abraham Cowley and Henry Vaughn and introduced the term 'dissociation of sensibility,' praising the 'telescoping of images' in 'A bracelet of bright hair about the bone.' He edited The Criterion and was a director at Faber & Faber. He thought 'Beauty is truth, truth beauty' a 'serious blemish' on Keats's ode.

Other Poems and Plays

Eliot's conversion to Anglicanism informed 'Ash Wednesday,' which opens with variations on 'Because I do not hope to turn again' from Guido Cavalcanti. 'Journey of the Magi' describes travelers 'no longer at ease here, in the old dispensation'; its first lines are taken from a Nativity sermon by Bishop Lancelot Andrewes. 'Gerontion' is a dramatic monologue by an old man 'in a dry month' who was considered as a preface to The Waste Land. The Sweeney poems describe an 'apeneck' character who is 'Erect' and 'Among the Nightingales'; the unfinished Sweeney Agonistes puns on Milton. 'Preludes' begins 'The winter evening settles down... Six o'clock.' Murder in the Cathedral dramatizes the killing of Thomas Becket by four knights loyal to Henry II. The Cocktail Party features the psychiatrist Harcourt-Reilly reuniting Edward and Lavinia Chamberlayne. Eliot suffered a breakdown with his first wife Vivienne Haigh-Wood, inspiring the prose poem 'Hysteria.'

Summary of Facts

T. S. Eliot (Thomas Stearns Eliot) was an American-born British poet and literary critic, one of the most important figures in twentieth-century literature. His works and critical essays are among the most frequently clued topics in quizbowl literature.

The Waste Land is his landmark modernist poem, opening with 'April is the cruellest month.' Edited by and dedicated to Ezra Pound, it is partly narrated by Tiresias and spans five sections including 'The Fire Sermon' (repeating 'burning burning burning burning') and 'What the Thunder Said.' It paraphrases Dante ('I had not thought death had undone so many'), quotes Baudelaire, references Tarot cards including the Fisher King, and ends with a Sanskrit word repeated three times.

'The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock' is his most famous early poem, featuring the speaker's questions 'Do I dare / Disturb the universe?' and 'how should I presume?' with repeated 'there will be time.' Four Quartets includes 'Burnt Norton' ('time present and time past / are both perhaps present in time future'), 'The Dry Salvages' (evoking the Mississippi as a 'strong brown god'), and 'East Coker' ('in my beginning is my end').

Eliot's criticism was foundational for New Criticism. 'Tradition and the Individual Talent' argues for the poet's 'continual extinction of personality,' comparing the mind to platinum. 'Hamlet and His Problems' coined 'objective correlative' and called Hamlet an 'artistic failure.' 'The Metaphysical Poets' introduced 'dissociation of sensibility.' These essays were collected in The Sacred Wood.

His religious poetry includes 'Ash Wednesday' (opening 'Because I do not hope to turn again' from Cavalcanti) and 'Journey of the Magi' (quoting Lancelot Andrewes: travelers 'no longer at ease here, in the old dispensation'). His plays include Murder in the Cathedral (Thomas Becket killed by Henry II's knights) and The Cocktail Party. The recurring character Sweeney appears in several poems as an 'apeneck' figure.
