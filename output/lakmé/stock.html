Lakmé

Léo Delibes's three-act opera, premiered at the Opéra-Comique in Paris in 1883. Set in British-ruled India, the opera follows Lakmé, daughter of the Brahmin priest Nilakantha, who falls in love with the British officer Gérald despite the enmity between Hindus and the British colonizers. Nilakantha forces his daughter to sing in public to draw out Gérald. The opera fell into relative obscurity until a British Airways commercial in the 1980s used the Flower Duet and revived interest in it. Wikipedia

"Flower Duet" ("Sous le dôme épais")

The Act I soprano-mezzo duet from Lakmé, sung by Lakmé and her servant Mallika as they gather flowers near a river. In B major, 6/8 time, featuring extended parallel-thirds melismas. Opens with the melody D-sharp – C-sharp – D-sharp – E over a 'thick dome' of jasmine imagery. Known in English as the 'Flower Duet' or titled for 'Sous le dôme épais' ('Under the Thick Dome'). Used in British Airways advertising since the 1980s. Wikipedia

Lakmé's Act II coloratura aria, in which she tells the story of a 'young Hindu girl' who distracts wild animals by playing bells, and saves the life of a stranger. Summary of Facts

Lakmé is a three-act opera by the French composer Léo Delibes, premiered at the Opéra-Comique in Paris in 1883. It is set in British-ruled India and tells the story of Lakmé, the daughter of the Hindu Brahmin priest Nilakantha, who falls in love with Gérald, a British officer, despite the religious and political antagonism between the two worlds. Nilakantha, incensed that British soldiers have trespassed on sacred Brahmin grounds, forces his daughter to sing publicly in Act II in order to lure and identify the intruder. Lakmé fatally eats a poisonous datura leaf at the end of Act III when she realizes that Gérald's military duty will inevitably pull him away from her.

The opera is famous for two numbers: the Act I 'Flower Duet' ('Sous le dôme épais'), sung by Lakmé and her servant Mallika as they gather flowers by a river, and the Act II 'Bell Song' ('Où va la jeune Hindoue'), a coloratura showpiece in which Lakmé narrates the story of a young girl who saves a stranger from wild animals by charming them with bells — the glockenspiel echoes her voice. The Flower Duet, in B major, 6/8 time, features two voices (soprano and mezzo-soprano) singing in extended parallel thirds over a flowing accompaniment; its melody opens with D-sharp – C-sharp – D-sharp – E. The opera, though popular at its premiere, fell into relative obscurity for much of the 20th century, until a British Airways advertising campaign beginning in the 1980s used an arrangement of the Flower Duet, dramatically reviving the opera's profile.

Delibes is also known for his ballets, particularly Coppélia (in which Swanhilde impersonates the mechanical doll Coppélia to win back Franz) and Sylvia (in which the nymph Sylvia is captured by Orion). The opera premiered at the same Parisian Opéra-Comique that hosted the premieres of Bizet's Carmen and Debussy's Pelléas et Mélisande.
