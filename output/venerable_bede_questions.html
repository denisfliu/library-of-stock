B1 2024 IQBT Undergraduate Championship (2024) Diff 8 · Literature
Much of the earliest surviving literature from the British Isles is in this language or in Latin. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this language used by the poets Caedmon (“KAD-mun”) and Cynewulf (“KOO-nay-wolf”), which was also used to write the epic Beowulf.
ANSWER: Old English [or Anglo-Saxon; prompt on English]
[10] Caedmon is known for an extant nine-line poem of this type written to “honour / heaven- kingdom’s Ward.” The later authors John Newton and William Cowper (“Cooper”) wrote a collection of these poems named for the town of Olney.
ANSWER: hymns [accept “Caedmon’s Hymn”; accept Olney Hymns]
[10] This writer is the namesake of an Old English “Death Song,” which states “before the unavoidable journey,” no one is wiser than he who ponders “good and evil within his soul.”
ANSWER: Bede [or Saint Bede; or the Venerable Bede]
B2 2024 COOT (2024) Diff 8 · Literature
A five-line poem attributed to this author states “none is wiser than the man who considers [...] what judgement his soul will receive after its passing”. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this author who, according to Cuthbert, reportedly asked for “some pepper, and napkins, and some incense” before composing a poem on their deathbed on Ascension Day, 735.
ANSWER: Bede [or Saint Bede or The Venerable Bede or Bede the Venerable or Beda Venerabilis]
[10] Bede’s death song is the poem in this language that survives in the largest number of manuscripts. The Exeter Book contains many poems in this language used to write Beowulf.
ANSWER: Old English [accept Anglo-Saxon; begrudgingly prompt on English]
[10] The Exeter Book contains this 143-line poem which surveys the Heroic Age of Northern Europe. This poem gives a list of tribes and writes of how “Hrothwulf and Hrothgar held longest the kinship together”.
ANSWER: Widsith [accept far-traveller or wide-journey or “The Traveller’s Song”]
B3 2022 ACF Regionals (2022) Diff 8 · Literature
In “The Monsters and the Critics,” J. R. R. Tolkien accepts “without argument” that Beowulf originated in an “age” named for this writer. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this attributed author of a five-line “Death Song.” This writer’s magnum opus relates how the cowherd Caedmon (“CAD-mun”) miraculously received the ability to sing to compose his namesake hymn, which this writer preserved.
ANSWER: the Venerable Bede (“beed”) [or Saint Bede; or Bede the Venerable or Beda Venerabilis]
[10] In Bede’s (“beedʼs”) account, Caedmon (“CAD-mun”) gained the ability to sing during one of these events. One of these events “of the Rood” titles a poem, attributed to Caedmon, in which the cross narrates Jesus’s crucifixion.
ANSWER: dreams [accept The Dream of the Rood; prompt on sleeping]
[10] Bede wrote texts of this type in the Aenigmata section of his Liber epigrammatum. “An onion” is the answer to a suggestive text of this kind in the Exeter Book, which likely inspired a contest of these things in The Hobbit.
ANSWER: riddles [prompt on puzzle or questions or brainteasers or similar]
B4 2018 Cambridge Open (2018) Diff 8 · Literature
Variously attributed to Caedmon and Cynewulf, this poem is primarily preserved in the Vercelli Codex, and is thought to be one of the oldest surviving in the Old English corpus. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this poem. Its first section describes the narrator’s vision of the Cross, while its second part describes Christ’s crucifixion from the perspective of the cross itself.
ANSWER: The Dream of the Rood
[10] Predating any Old English manuscripts, this large eighth-century stone cross, named for the Scottish border town where it still stands, is believed to independently preserve part of The Dream of the Rood.
ANSWER: Ruthwell Cross
[10] The attribution of the poem to Caedmon is based primarily on circumstantial evidence in the Ecclesiastical History of the English People, a work by this “venerable” monk of Jarrow.
ANSWER: The Venerable Bede
B5 2017 NASAT (2017) Diff 8 · Literature
The stories of Leir and Cymbeline were adapted by Shakespeare from this man's Historia Regum Brittaniae, or History of the Kings of Britain. For 10 points each:
[10] Name this historian who began that text with the settling of Britain by Brutus, a great grandson of Aeneas.
ANSWER: Geoffrey of Monmouth
[10] The Synod of Whitby is discussed in the third book of this "venerable" man's An Ecclesiastical History of the British People, which was one of the primary sources for Geoffrey of Monmouth's chronicle.
ANSWER: Venerable Bede
[10] Another source used by Geoffrey of Monmouth was On the Ruin and Conquest of Briton, a description of the battles between the Britons and the Saxons by this 6th-century monk.
ANSWER: Saint Gildas
