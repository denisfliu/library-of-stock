Natsume Soseki

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Meiji-era Japanese author, one of the most important figures in modern Japanese literature. Best known for I Am a Cat, Kokoro, and Botchan. Studied in the United Kingdom and succeeded Lafcadio Hearn as professor of English literature at the University of Tokyo. His works frequently engage with Western literature and the tension between tradition and modernity in Meiji Japan.
I Am a Cat (1905-1906)

A three-volume satirical novel narrated by an unnamed cat belonging to the teacher Mr. Sneaze (Kushami). The cat uses the pretentious first-person pronoun 'wagahai' and observes the lives of characters including the pretentious artist Waverhouse, the student Avalon Coldmoon (who obsessively grinds glass balls to make frog-eye lenses), and Opula Goldfield. The cat narrator gets drunk at a dinner and drowns in a rain barrel/water barrel at the novel's close. Singleman Kidd encourages calmness as children keep intruding to retrieve baseballs. Wikipedia

Kokoro (1914)

Novel about the relationship between a young unnamed narrator and a reclusive older man he calls 'Sensei.' The second half consists of Sensei's autobiographical letter/testament. As a young man, Sensei stole his landlady's daughter (Ojosan/Shizu) from his friend K, a priest's son obsessed with 'the concentration of the mind.' K commits suicide, leaving a note asking 'Why did I wait so long to die?' Sensei himself commits suicide after the death of Emperor Meiji (and General Nogi Maresuke's junshi). Sensei tells the narrator 'loneliness is the price we have to pay for being born in this modern age.' The untranslated title roughly means 'the heart of things.' Wikipedia

Botchan (1906)

A popular comedic novel about a brash math teacher who moves from Tokyo to teach at a rural school. Characters are given derisive nicknames: Red Shirt (the vice principal/head teacher), Porcupine, the Clown, Hanger-on, and Squash. The title character beats up Red Shirt and the Clown for visiting a brothel. The obsequious servant Kiyo gives the protagonist his nickname. He eventually returns to Tokyo to live with Kiyo. Children put grasshoppers in his bed. Wikipedia

Kusamakura / The Three-Cornered World / Grass Pillow (1906)

A lyrical novel about an artist who retreats to remote mountains/a mountain resort and stays at a hotel, where he meets the mysterious hostess O-Nami. He meditates on art, writes poetry, and imagines painting Ophelia drowning. The opening sentence praises the turbidity of one's life. Examines Misao Fujimura's suicide at Kegon Falls in its twelfth chapter. O-Nami reminds the protagonist of Millais's Ophelia. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Natsume Soseki is a Meiji-era Japanese author and one of the most central figures in quizbowl's coverage of Japanese literature. His three most-clued works are I Am a Cat, Kokoro, and Botchan, which appear in virtually every tossup as giveaway identifiers.

I Am a Cat (1905-1906) is his most distinctive work in clue terms: an unnamed cat belonging to the teacher Mr. Sneaze (Kushami) narrates in an unusually high-register style using the pronoun 'wagahai.' The cat observes characters including the pretentious artist Waverhouse, the scientist Avalon Coldmoon (who obsessively grinds glass balls to simulate light through a frog's eye), Opula Goldfield, and Singleman Kidd. The cat gets drunk and drowns in a rain barrel — this is the single most repeated clue for Soseki, appearing in nearly every tossup.

Kokoro (1914) generates the richest variety of clues. The unnamed narrator obsesses over the reclusive Sensei. The second half is Sensei's autobiographical letter/testament, revealing that he stole his landlady's daughter (Ojosan/Shizu) from his friend K, a priest's son obsessed with 'the concentration of the mind.' K commits suicide, leaving a note asking 'Why did I wait so long to die?' Sensei later commits suicide after the death of Emperor Meiji and General Nogi Maresuke. Power clues include Sensei's quote about loneliness being 'the price we have to pay for being born in this modern age,' his insistence that 'there is guilt in love,' and the narrator playing chess with his ill father on a foot warmer.

Botchan (1906) is a comedic novel about a brash math teacher at a rural school, fictionalized from Soseki's own teaching experience. Characters have derisive nicknames: Red Shirt, Porcupine, the Clown, Hanger-on, and Squash. Botchan and Porcupine beat up Red Shirt and the Clown for visiting a brothel. The servant Kiyo gives the protagonist his nickname. Power clues include children filling his bed with grasshoppers and Red Shirt's scheme to steal an attractive teacher from Squash.

Kusamakura / The Three-Cornered World / Grass Pillow (1906) follows an artist retreating to remote mountains, where he meets the mysterious O-Nami (who reminds him of Millais's Ophelia), writes poetry, and meditates on art. The twelfth chapter examines Misao Fujimura's suicide at Kegon Falls.

Sanshiro (1908) features a student fascinated by Thomas Browne's Hydriotaphia and fictionalizes Lafcadio Hearn's departure from the University of Tokyo. Ten Nights of Dreams includes a story about a man waiting a century by a grave until a white lily blossoms.

Biographically, Soseki studied in the U.K. (an unpleasant experience), succeeded Lafcadio Hearn as professor of English literature, and is thematically paired with Machado de Assis in Edwin Frank's Stranger Than Fiction. His novel The Miner appears in Murakami's Kafka on the Shore.
