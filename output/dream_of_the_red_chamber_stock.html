Dream of the Red Chamber

One of the Four Great Classical Novels of Chinese literature, written by Cao Xueqin during the Qing dynasty (18th century). Follows the decline of the wealthy Jia family, centered on Jia Baoyu, who is born with a piece of jade in his mouth. The novel's central love triangle involves Baoyu, his sickly cousin Lin Daiyu (his true love), and Xue Baochai (whom he is tricked into marrying). The frame story involves a stone rejected by the goddess Nuwa that is reincarnated as Baoyu. Notable for its poetry, the Grand View Garden setting, the 12 Beauties of Jinling, and the ongoing scholarly debate ('Redology') over the authenticity of its last 40 chapters. Translated by David Hawkes (with Latinate names for characters) and studied by Eileen Chang and Lu Xun.
Frame Story / The Stone

The novel's frame story centers on a stone rejected by the goddess Nuwa when she was repairing the sky. This stone, along with the Crimson Pearl Flower, are reborn into the human world. The stone becomes a piece of jade found in the mouth of the newborn Jia Baoyu. A priest and a monk carry the stone to the fairy Disenchantment's palace in the Land of Illusion through an arch inscribed 'Truth becomes fiction when the fiction's true.' The alternate title 'The Story of the Stone' references this frame narrative. Wikipedia

Jia Baoyu (Protagonist)

The protagonist of the novel, heir to the declining Jia family. Born with jade in his mouth. His name is a pun on 'Fake Precious Jade.' He loves his cousin Lin Daiyu but is tricked into marrying Xue Baochai. He reads erotica instead of Confucian classics, and at age one chose women's cosmetics from an array of career-symbolizing objects. He has his first sexual experience in a dream in the World of Illusion while resting in Qin Keqing's room. He becomes lethargic when a begonia blooms out of season. Near the end, he is guided through a dream by a Buddhist monk and eventually becomes a monk himself.

Lin Daiyu and the Flower Burial

Lin Daiyu is Baoyu's sickly cousin and true love. She is a reincarnation of the Crimson Pearl Flower. She is famous for burying flower petals and composing elegiac poetry in their honor, in one of the novel's most iconic scenes. She asks 'who will bury me when dead I lie' and composes a poem for dead flowers. She dies of grief after discovering Baoyu has been tricked into marrying Baochai.

Editions, Translations, and Redology

The scholarly study of this novel is called 'Redology.' A major debate concerns whether the last 40 chapters (in the Cheng-Gao editions) were a later addition to the first 80 chapters. The manuscripts contain commentary by Zhiyanzhai ('Inkstone Studio') and Elderly Maimed Tablet. David Hawkes produced the seminal translation, giving characters Latinate names like Sapientia and Adamantina; John Minford completed it. Lu Xun called it both autobiography and confession in A Brief History of Chinese Fiction. Eileen Chang wrote a study of the novel. Hu Shih is the leading 'Reddologist.'

Summary of Facts

Dream of the Red Chamber (also known as The Story of the Stone, Hong Lou Meng, or Shitou Ji) is one of the Four Great Classical Novels of Chinese literature and the most recent of them, written by Cao Xueqin during the Qing dynasty in the 18th century. The scholarly study of this novel is called 'Redology.'

The novel's frame story begins with the goddess Nuwa rejecting a stone when repairing the sky. This stone, along with the Crimson Pearl Flower, are reborn into the mortal world. A priest and a monk carry the stone to the fairy Disenchantment's palace in the Land of Illusion through an arch inscribed with the famous couplet 'Truth becomes fiction when the fiction's true; Real becomes not-real where the unreal's real.' The stone is reincarnated as a piece of jade found in the mouth of the newborn Jia Baoyu. The first section references 36,501 blocks of 'rough stone.'

Jia Baoyu is the protagonist, heir to the wealthy but declining Jia family. His name is a pun on 'Fake Precious Jade.' At age one, he was given an array of career-symbolizing objects and chose women's cosmetics. His father wants him to study Confucian classics, but he reads erotica and the Daoist text Zhuangzi instead. In Chapter 5, he has a dream in the Land of Illusion where he reads allegorical poems about the 12 Beauties of Jinling and has his first sexual experience (euphemistically called 'the Art of Love') with the maid Aroma. He becomes lethargic when a begonia tree blooms inauspiciously out of season. His sister First Spring (Jia Yunchuan) is promoted to imperial consort.

The central love triangle involves Baoyu, his sickly cousin Lin Daiyu (his true love), and Xue Baochai (whom he is tricked into marrying). An inscription on a golden locket, given to Baochai by a Buddhist monk, predestines this marriage. Lin Daiyu is a reincarnation of the Crimson Pearl Flower, and her tears are shed 'during the whole of a mortal lifetime.' She is famous for burying flower petals and composing elegiac poetry for them, asking 'who will bury me when dead I lie.' After Baoyu is beaten for sheltering an actor, a misunderstanding leads Daiyu to symbolically bury flower petals. She dies of grief after discovering Baoyu has been tricked into marrying Baochai. Baoyu eventually runs away to become a Buddhist monk.

Much of the novel takes place in the Grand View Garden (Prospect Garden). The Rongguo and Ningguo branches of the Jia family transform their adjacent compounds into gigantic pleasure grounds. Wang Xifeng ('Splendid Phoenix') manages the household. A purse with an indecent picture causes all maids to come under suspicion. The family eventually declines until the mansion is ransacked by imperial troops. Other notable events include Cassia dying from drinking poison she prepared for her husband's maid, and a loutish character killing a waiter for bringing wine too slowly.

The novel contains extensive poetry: a poetry contest leads friends to write twelve poems about chrysanthemums, and poems about savoring crab meat appear alongside ones about the 'frenzy of poetry.'

A major scholarly debate concerns whether the last 40 chapters (in the Cheng-Gao editions, published by Cheng Weiyuan and Gao E) are authentic or later additions to the first 80. Manuscripts contain commentary by Zhiyanzhai ('Inkstone Studio') and Elderly Maimed Tablet. David Hawkes produced the seminal English translation, giving characters Latinate names like Sapientia and Adamantina; John Minford completed it. Lu Xun called the novel both autobiography and confession in A Brief History of Chinese Fiction. Eileen Chang wrote a study of the novel. Hu Shih is the leading 'Reddologist.' Cao Xueqin himself was also known for making kites and writing a guidebook on kite manufacture.
