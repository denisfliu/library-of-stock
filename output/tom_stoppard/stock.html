Czech-born British playwright (born Tomas Straussler, 1937) best known for Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead and Arcadia. A major figure of twentieth-century British theater, he combines intellectual rigor with wit, drawing on mathematics, philosophy, and history. Other major works include Travesties, The Real Thing, Leopoldstadt, The Invention of Love, The Coast of Utopia, and The Real Inspector Hound. He also wrote screenplays for Shakespeare in Love, Empire of the Sun, Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade, and Brazil.
Arcadia

Stoppard's 1993 play, set in a Derbyshire country house (Sidley Park) in both 1809 and the present day. The 1809 narrative follows the 13-year-old math prodigy Thomasina Coverly and her tutor Septimus Hodge; the present-day narrative follows researchers Hannah Jarvis and Bernard Nightingale. The play opens with Septimus defining 'carnal embrace' as 'the practice of throwing one's arms around a side of beef' after Thomasina asks its meaning. Thomasina challenges the Second Law of Thermodynamics with rice pudding as an analogy and is asked to prove Fermat's Last Theorem—Septimus uses this as a distracting task while he reads the bad poem 'The Couch of Eros' by Ezra Chater. Bernard theorizes that Lord Byron killed Chater in a duel, but Hannah discovers that Chater died of a monkey bite in Martinique. Hannah ultimately reveals that the 'hermit of Sidley Park' was Septimus Hodge himself. The tortoise on stage is named Lightning in the present day and Plautus in 1809. The non-speaking character Gus hands Hannah a drawing of the hermit. The play ends with Thomasina and Septimus waltzing—Thomasina dies in a fire before her 17th birthday. A stage direction admits 'Something needs to be said about' the unusual setting. Lady Croom complains about Richard Noakes's steam engine. Thomasina calls Septimus a 'cheat' after he mockingly translates a speech from Antony and Cleopatra. The play was inspired by James Gleick's 1987 book on chaos theory. Ada Lovelace is generally considered to have inspired Thomasina Coverly.

Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead

The play opens with Rosencrantz flipping a coin that comes up heads 92 consecutive times—Guildenstern theorizes they are 'within un-, sub- or supernatural forces.' Travesties

Stoppard's 1974 comedy set in 1917 Zurich, in which the aged diplomat Henry Carr reminisces about his encounters with Vladimir Lenin, Tristan Tzara, and James Joyce. The play is structured around Henry Carr's real-life role in a production of The Importance of Being Earnest managed by Joyce (Carr played Algernon; in Travesties Tzara and Carr are urged to take up roles of Jack and Algernon). Tzara creates a poem by cutting up the words of Shakespeare's Sonnet 18 and pulling them out of a hat. Lenin defines war as 'capitalism with the gloves off.' In Act 1, Tzara chants 'Dada' 34 times; a scene is spoken entirely in limericks; another scene parodies the catechism chapter of Ulysses. Gwendolen and Cecily mix up folders. The protagonist observes he learned three things during the war but forgets the third. A 'time slip' device repeats scenes as Carr's memory falters. The play's last line refers to Carr not finishing a third exclamation ('vomit on it,' 'I spit on it'—he never says the third). It was World War I Zurich.

Leopoldstadt

General / Biographical

Tom Stoppard was born Tomas Straussler in 1937 in Zlín, Czechoslovakia (now Czech Republic). He is Czech-born and British by nationality. His birth city is Prague (or Zlín); he is associated with Prague (sharing the honor with Václav Havel). He is a non-American author. He admired Margaret Thatcher's policies, causing controversy among contemporaries. He got his professional start in radio dramas (alongside Caryl Churchill). He has won five Tony Awards for Best Play. He received a knighthood (Sir Tom Stoppard). His alternate answerline is Tomas Straussler. He wrote the screenplay for Star Wars: Revenge of the Sith as a script doctor.

Summary of Facts

Tom Stoppard (born Tomas Straussler, 1937) is a Czech-born British playwright widely considered one of the most important dramatists of the twentieth century. He is best known for Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead (1966) and Arcadia (1993), and his work characteristically blends intellectual rigor—mathematics, philosophy, chaos theory, classical scholarship—with sharp wit and theatrical innovation. He received a knighthood and has won five Tony Awards for Best Play.

Arcadia, his most clued work, is set at Sidley Park, a Derbyshire country house, alternating between 1809 and the present day. In 1809, the tutor Septimus Hodge instructs the thirteen-year-old math prodigy Thomasina Coverly, who opens the play by asking the meaning of 'carnal embrace'—Septimus deflects her with the definition 'the practice of throwing one's arms around a side of beef.' Septimus then tasks her with proving Fermat's Last Theorem so he can read the poet Ezra Chater's bad poem 'The Couch of Eros' in peace. Chater challenges Septimus to a duel after a negative review—the real-life poet Lord Byron is implicated but is later cleared when Hannah Jarvis, a present-day researcher, discovers that Chater died of a monkey bite in Martinique. The mystery of the 'hermit of Sidley Park' is resolved when Hannah reveals it was Septimus himself. A tortoise is named Lightning in the present day and Plautus in 1809. The non-speaking Gus hands Hannah a drawing of the hermit and the tortoise. The play ends with Thomasina and Septimus waltzing—Thomasina dies in a fire before her seventeenth birthday. Thomasina illustrates the Second Law of Thermodynamics with rice pudding and models an early fractal; Lady Croom complains about Richard Noakes's noisy steam engine. A stage direction admits 'Something needs to be said about' the setting. Ada Lovelace is generally considered to have inspired the character of Thomasina Coverly. The play was inspired by James Gleick's 1987 book on chaos theory.

Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead retells Hamlet from the perspective of its two doomed minor courtiers. The play opens with Rosencrantz flipping a coin that comes up heads 92 consecutive times, causing Guildenstern to theorize they exist within 'un-, sub- or supernatural forces.' The characters often mix up which of them is which, play a 'Game of Questions' (rhetoricals and statements are fouls), and insert variations into 'Give us this day our daily bread.' The Player and his Tragedians emerge from barrels on the ship to England after playing music. They perform The Murder of Gonzago and The Rape of the Sabine Women (with Alfred forced into a skirt). On the ship, Ros and Guil discover their names substituted in a letter ordering their execution. The Player is stabbed with a retractable knife and stands up to applause; Guil observes 'there is no applause after death.' The experience of seeing a unicorn is 'thinned' into seeing a horse with an arrow in its head. A character muses that death is 'the absence of presence, nothing more' and he would rather be alive in a box forever than dead in one. The play ends with English ambassadors announcing the title fact to Horatio.

Travesties (1974) is set in 1917 Zurich, where the aged diplomat Henry Carr reminisces about his encounters with Vladimir Lenin, Tristan Tzara, and James Joyce. The play is structured around the real-life production of The Importance of Being Earnest that Joyce mounted in Zurich and in which Carr played Algernon—in Travesties, Tzara and Carr are urged to take up the roles of Jack and Algernon. Tzara creates a poem by cutting up Shakespeare's Sonnet 18 and pulling words from a hat; in Act 1, he chants 'Dada' 34 times and defines war as 'capitalism with the gloves off.' One scene is spoken entirely in limericks; another parodies the catechism chapter of Ulysses. A 'time slip' device reflects Carr's faulty memory. Carr labels Wilde a 'Gommorahist' and focuses obsessively on costume changes.

Leopoldstadt (2020), Stoppard's most recent and most personal play, draws on the stories of his own grandparents as Holocaust victims. Five acts span 1899 to 1955, following the prosperous Merz family in the Jewish quarter of Vienna. The Nazis seize the family's property during the Anschluss; Leo Rosenbaum changes his name to Leo Chamberlain and has no memory of his Jewish life; Ludwig Jakobovicz observes a lack of 'rhyme or reason' while playing cat's cradle; the play ends with Rosa responding 'Auschwitz' to a list of family names.

The Real Thing (1982) is about the jealous playwright Henry, who opens the play with a play-within-a-play (House of Cards) in which Max accuses his wife of adultery—later revealed as fictional. Henry leaves his wife Charlotte for the actress Annie, who then has an affair with her co-star Billy while cast in 'Tis Pity She's a Whore. Henry analogizes good writing to a cricket bat and refuses to ghostwrite a play for the imprisoned soldier Brodie, who burned the Cenotaph. The Invention of Love (1997) dramatizes A. E. Housman's unrequited love for Oxford classmate Moses Jackson, opening with the 77-year-old Housman greeting Charon at the River Styx; love is defined as 'a piece of ice held fast in the fist.' The Coast of Utopia (2002) is a nine-hour trilogy (Voyage, Shipwreck, Salvage) about 19th-century Russian intellectuals Alexander Herzen, Ivan Turgenev, and Mikhail Bakunin.

The Real Inspector Hound (1968) features critics Moon and Birdboot watching a murder mystery set at Muldoon Manor—a parody of The Mousetrap—until they become entangled in the fictional plot. Moon is killed when he discovers the dead body is Higgs. Stoppard's Jumpers (1972) features logical-positivist gymnasts and a character who steps on his tortoise and accidentally shoots his hare while using them to disprove Zeno's paradox. Every Good Boy Deserves Favour features two Ivanovs—one a Soviet dissident, one a madman who believes he controls a symphony orchestra. The Hard Problem (2015) opens with Hilary and Spike debating the hard problem of consciousness. Dogg's Hamlet pairs with Cahoot's Macbeth; characters speak Dogg, a Wittgenstein-inspired nonsense language.

As a screenwriter, Stoppard wrote Shakespeare in Love (at the end of which Viola de Lesseps suggests their romance could inspire a play), co-wrote Brazil (creating 'Form 27B/6' alluding to Orwell's flat), and wrote 'almost every dialogue' of Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade, adding the character 'Panama Hat.' He wrote the entirety of Empire of the Sun based on J. G. Ballard's novel. He adapted his play Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead into a film starring Tim Roth and Gary Oldman. He received a script call while showering during the making of Schindler's List and came up with a scene on the spot. He also worked as a script doctor on Revenge of the Sith. Born in Czechoslovakia, he shares his birth city with Václav Havel. He got his professional start writing radio dramas, as did Caryl Churchill. He admired Margaret Thatcher's policies, causing controversy among his contemporaries.
