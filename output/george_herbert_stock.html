English metaphysical poet known for the collection The Temple, which contains shaped/pattern poems like 'Easter Wings' and 'The Altar,' as well as religious lyrics. His work is devoted to Christian themes and was influential on later poets including Richard Crashaw and Zbigniew Herbert.
The Temple

Herbert's major collection of 160 'Sacred Poems and Private Ejaculations,' containing devotional religious poetry. It opens with 'The Church-Porch' (a 'Perirrhanterium') which declares 'A verse may find him, who a sermon flies, / And turn delight into a sacrifice.' Richard Crashaw's collection Steps to the Temple is titled in homage to it. The Polish poet Zbigniew Herbert claimed relation to George Herbert and the English author of The Temple. Samuel Johnson coined the term 'Metaphysical poets' in Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets, grouping Crashaw and Herbert together.

Easter Wings

One of Herbert's most famous pattern/shaped poems, originally printed sideways so as to resemble flying creatures (wings). The lines contract and grow in accordance with the health of the speaker, at their narrowest when he is 'most poor' or 'most thin.' The poem contains the lines 'Thou didst so punish sin, / That I became / Most thin' which grow gradually shorter. It concludes 'if I imp my wing on thine, affliction shall advance the flight in me.'

The Altar

Another of Herbert's shaped/pattern poems, in which the speaker builds the title structure. The poem states 'A heart alone Is such a stone as nothing but Thy power doth cut' and the speaker 'rears' the broken object 'made of a heart, and cemented with tears.' The varied line lengths create a visual image of an altar.

Other Poems

Herbert's other frequently clued poems include 'The Pulley,' in which God pours a 'glass of blessings' onto man but retains only Rest at the bottom. 'The Collar' opens with the speaker striking the board and crying 'No more; I will abroad!' and urging himself to 'forsake thy cage, thy rope of sands.' 'Denial' describes how 'all day long my heart was in my knee' hoping that the addressee's 'favors and my mind may chime, and mend my rhyme.' 'Love (III)' concludes with the lines 'So I did sit and eat' after Love enjoins the speaker to 'taste my meat.' 'Heaven' uses echo verse, beginning 'O Who will show me those delights on high? Echo: I.' Herbert also wrote poems ending 'Then by a sunbeam I will climb to thee' and another calling a sunbeam 'a silk twist let down from heav'n.' 'My tender age in sorrow did begin' is another of his lines.

Summary of Facts

George Herbert was an English metaphysical poet of the early seventeenth century, known almost exclusively in quizbowl for his devotional collection The Temple, described as containing 160 'Sacred Poems and Private Ejaculations.' The Temple opens with 'The Church-Porch,' a 'Perirrhanterium' that declares 'A verse may find him, who a sermon flies, / And turn delight into a sacrifice.' Richard Crashaw titled his own collection Steps to the Temple in homage to Herbert's work.

Herbert's most iconic poems are his shaped or pattern poems. 'Easter Wings' was originally printed sideways to resemble flying creatures; its lines contract and expand to reflect the speaker's spiritual state, growing shortest when he is 'most thin' and concluding 'if I imp my wing on thine, affliction shall advance the flight in me.' 'The Altar' creates the visual shape of its title object, stating 'A heart alone is such a stone as nothing but Thy power doth cut' and describing the structure as 'made of a heart, and cemented with tears.'

Other notable poems include 'The Pulley,' in which God pours a 'glass of blessings' onto man but retains only Rest; 'The Collar,' which opens with the speaker striking the board and crying 'No more; I will abroad!'; 'Denial,' where 'all day long my heart was in my knee'; 'Love (III),' which ends 'So I did sit and eat'; and 'Heaven,' which uses echo verse beginning 'O Who will show me those delights on high? Echo: I.'

Herbert's influence extended to later poets. Richard Crashaw was directly inspired by him. The Polish modernist poet Zbigniew Herbert claimed relation to the English George Herbert. Samuel Johnson coined the term 'Metaphysical poets,' grouping Herbert with Crashaw and others, in his Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets.
