American photographer (1923–1971) known for documentary portraits of marginalized and unconventional people — 'freaks,' transvestites, nudists, giants, and dwarves in New York City. Best known for Child with Toy Hand Grenade in Central Park and Identical Twins, Roselle, New Jersey, 1967 (which inspired the ghost twins in Stanley Kubrick's The Shining). Photographed the 'Jewish Giant' Eddie Carmel and shot in square format after switching to a Rolleiflex. Criticized by Susan Sontag. Committed suicide in 1971.
Child with Toy Hand Grenade in Central Park (1962)

Arbus's most famous photograph: a young boy (Colin Wood) standing in Central Park with one suspender strap hanging off his shoulder, head tilted, making a wide-eyed grimace (rictus smile), clutching a fake MK2 Pineapple hand grenade in his claw-like right hand. Norman Mailer commented on this photo after Arbus photographed him for the New York Times Book Review. Susan Sontag criticized Arbus in part through this image. Wikipedia

Other Works and General / Biographical

American photographer (1923–1971), born Diane Nemerov. Her brother was poet/novelist Howard Nemerov. Committed suicide in 1971, having scribbled 'Last Supper' in her diary beforehand. Switched from a grainy Nikon to a twin-lens Rolleiflex, resulting in her characteristic square format. First major exhibition was the 'New Documents' show (alongside Lee Friedlander and Garry Winogrand). Former student Neil Selkirk compiled a retrospective in 1972 and was the only person authorized to print her work. Published 'A Box of Ten Photographs' (a limited edition of 50 sets, only 4 sold in her lifetime), with a plastic box designed by Marvin Israel, handwritten captions. Marvin Israel also compiled the posthumous Monograph. Last series (posthumously titled 'Untitled') depicted residents of homes for the developmentally disabled, inspired by R.D. Laing. Susan Sontag criticized her in the essay 'Freak Show' (in On Photography). Photographed Anderson Cooper as a baby for Harper's Bazaar. Also photographed a tattooed man at a carnival (Maryland), a naked man tucking his genitals between his legs ('A Naked Man Being a Woman, NYC, 1968'), Lillian and Dorothy Gish in fur coats, and Two Ladies at the Automat. Summary of Facts

Diane Arbus (1923–1971), born Diane Nemerov, was an American photographer famous for documentary portraits of marginalized, unconventional, and taboo subjects — transvestites, giants, dwarves, nudists, mentally disabled people, and circus 'freaks' — primarily in New York City. Her brother was the poet and novelist Howard Nemerov.

Her most famous work is Child with Toy Hand Grenade in Central Park (1962), depicting a boy (Colin Wood) in Central Park with one suspender strap hanging off his shoulder, making a wide-eyed grimace, and clutching a fake MK2 Pineapple hand grenade in his claw-like right hand. Almost equally famous is Identical Twins, Roselle, New Jersey, 1967, showing twin girls Cathleen and Colleen Wade in matching corduroy dresses and headbands with opposite expressions — this photograph directly inspired the ghost twin motif in Stanley Kubrick's The Shining. Other major works include A Jewish Giant at Home with His Parents in the Bronx (depicting the nearly nine-foot-tall Eddie Carmel leaning down to fit under his family's ceiling while his parents look on); Boy with a Straw Hat Waiting to March in a Pro-War Parade (showing a young man in a boater hat with a 'Bomb Hanoi' button); and a portrait of Norman Mailer for the New York Times Book Review (Mailer spread-legged in an armchair — a picture that angered him and that a museumgoer once spat on).

Arbus switched from a grainy Nikon to a twin-lens Rolleiflex camera, producing her characteristic square-format photographs. Her first major exhibition was 'New Documents' (alongside Lee Friedlander and Garry Winogrand). She published A Box of Ten Photographs, a limited edition of 50 sets with plastic boxes designed by Marvin Israel and handwritten captions — only 4 sets sold in her lifetime. Her final, posthumously titled series 'Untitled' depicted residents of homes for the developmentally disabled, inspired by R.D. Laing's writings. Her student Neil Selkirk compiled a 1972 retrospective and was the only person authorized to print her work; Marvin Israel also compiled the Monograph.

Writer Susan Sontag criticized Arbus at length in On Photography, particularly in the essay 'Freak Show' (also known as 'America, Seen Through Photographs, Darkly'). Germaine Greer also accused Arbus of trying to distress her in order to get a better image. Arbus committed suicide in 1971, having written 'Last Supper' in her diary beforehand.
