Japanese Edo Period poet, considered the master of haiku. Best known for his frog haiku ('The old pond / a frog jumps in / sound of water') and the haibun travelogue The Narrow Road to the Deep North, which opens 'The months and days are the travelers of eternity' and recounts a journey with his companion Kawai Sora. Took his pen name from a banana tree planted by disciples outside his hut (previous pen names: Sobo, Tosei/Green Peach). Also wrote The Seashell Game (comparing pairs of poems), Records of a Weather-Exposed Skeleton, and other travel diaries. His death poem reads 'Sick on my journey / only my dreams will wander / these desolate moors.' Influenced by Zen Buddhism and Daoism; advocated for karumi (lightness) in poetry.
The Narrow Road to the Deep North (Oku no Hosomichi)

Basho's most famous travelogue, written in haibun (prose combined with haiku). Opens with 'The months and days are the travelers of eternity.' Recounts a journey of over 1,200 miles through northeastern Japan with his companion Kawai Sora (whose diary, rediscovered in 1943, sometimes contradicts Basho's account). Contains the poem about 'summer grass' being all that remains of warriors' dreams, inspired by a ruin-strewn landscape. At the end, Basho feels 'like a clam ripped from its shell' while boarding a boat as 'autumn was deepening.' Also includes poems about hearing 'the locust's trill stinging into the stones' in stillness, and 'Fleas and lice biting / Awake all night / A horse pissing close to my ear.' Notes that 'the birds all mourn' as spring passes. Describes Castle-on-the-Heights. Translated by Donald Keene. Wikipedia

Frog Haiku

Basho's most famous single poem: 'The old pond / a frog jumps in / sound of water.' Robert Hass rendered it in a 1994 compendium. The last line has been variously translated as 'Kerplunk' and 'sound of water.' Exemplifies haiku format with its 17 morae and seasonal reference. The Japanese 'Furu ike ya' contains a kireji (cutting word) in 'ya.' Wikipedia

Records of a Weather-Exposed Skeleton and Other Travel Diaries

Basho wrote several travel diaries in haibun form before The Narrow Road. Records of a Weather-Exposed Skeleton describes giving food to 'a child crying in the autumn wind' who had been abandoned by parents, while walking along a riverside with servant Chiri. The title object is found 'in windy fields of memory / piercing like a knife.' Also wrote Record of a Travel-Worn Satchel, in which he claimed 'even a thatched hut may change with a new owner.' The Monkey's Raincoat is another collection.

Other Individual Poems

General / Biographical

Basho is the master of haiku and renga. Took his pen name from a banana tree (basho) planted by disciples outside his hut; previously used pen names Sobo and Tosei ('Green Peach'). Born Matsuo Kinsaku. Described a miscanthus bud near the banana tree. The first section of one work describes his old grass hut where the Festival of Dolls takes place; said the new generation would celebrate the festival there. Rejected his samurai upbringing. His students wrote 'The Best Poems of [Basho's] Twenty Disciples.' Robert Hass produced a 1994 compendium with Basho alphabetically first of three poets. Advocated poetic principles including 'awakening to the high, returning to the low,' 'following the Creative,' and 'object and self as one.' Influenced by Zen Buddhism and Daoism. Strove for karumi (lightness). Coined the term haibun. Haiku contain kireji (cutting words), kigo (seasonal words), and 17 morae. Willingness to experiment with form, including 18th syllable.

Summary of Facts

Matsuo Basho is the canonical master of haiku, a Japanese Edo Period poet known equally for his frog haiku and the haibun travelogue The Narrow Road to the Deep North.

His most famous poem is the frog haiku: 'The old pond / a frog jumps in / sound of water.' Robert Hass rendered this in a 1994 compendium (Basho is the first and alphabetically first of three poets in the book). The last line has been variously translated as 'Kerplunk' and 'sound of water.' The Japanese 'Furu ike ya' contains a kireji (cutting word) in 'ya.'

The Narrow Road to the Deep North (Oku no Hosomichi) is a haibun travelogue recounting a journey of over 1,200 miles through northeastern Japan with his companion Kawai Sora (whose diary, rediscovered in 1943, sometimes contradicts Basho's account). It opens with 'The months and days are the travelers of eternity.' It contains the poem about 'summer grass' being all that remains of warriors' dreams. At the end, Basho feels 'like a clam ripped from its shell' as autumn deepens. It also includes poems about the locust's trill 'stinging into the stones' and the comic 'Fleas and lice biting / Awake all night / A horse pissing close to my ear.' Translated by Donald Keene.

The Seashell Game is a critical anthology in which Basho juxtaposes/compares pairs of similar poems by different authors, named for a children's matching game.

Records of a Weather-Exposed Skeleton is another travel diary describing giving food to 'a child crying in the autumn wind.' Its title object is found 'in windy fields of memory / piercing like a knife.' Record of a Travel-Worn Satchel opens with 'even a thatched hut may change with a new owner.'

Other notable poems include his death poem ('Sick on my journey / only my dreams will wander / these desolate moors'), the cicada poem ('gives us no sign / That presently it will die'), the Milky Way poem (which inspired the conclusion of Kawabata's Snow Country), and 'In Kyoto / hearing the cuckoo / I long for Kyoto.'

Biographically, Basho took his pen name from a banana tree (basho) planted by disciples outside his hut; his previous pen names were Sobo and Tosei ('Green Peach'). He described a miscanthus bud growing near the banana tree. He rejected his samurai upbringing to travel. His students wrote 'The Best Poems of Basho's Twenty Disciples.' He advocated poetic principles including 'awakening to the high, returning to the low,' 'following the Creative,' and 'object and self as one.' He was influenced by Zen Buddhism and Daoism, and strove for karumi (lightness). He coined the term haibun. Yosa Buson and Kobayashi Issa are the other two of the three great haiku masters.
