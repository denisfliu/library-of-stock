Norwegian Nobel laureate (1920), born Knud Pedersen. Best known for the psychological novel Hunger, in which an unnamed protagonist wanders the streets of Kristiania (Oslo) suffering from starvation. Also wrote Pan (about the hunter Thomas Glahn and his dog Aesop), Mysteries (Johan Nagel), and The Growth of the Soil (the peasant Isak and his wife Inger). Notoriously a Nazi sympathizer who wrote a controversial 1945 obituary for Hitler calling him 'a warrior for mankind.' Isaac Bashevis Singer called him 'the father of modern literature.'

Summary of Facts

Knut Hamsun (born Knud Pedersen, 1859–1952) is a Norwegian author who won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1920. Isaac Bashevis Singer called him 'the father of modern literature.'

His most frequently clued work is Hunger (1890), a psychological novel about an unnamed young journalist/writer who wanders the streets of Kristiania (now Oslo) suffering from starvation. The protagonist gives the name 'Ylajali' to a woman he meets on the street. He repeatedly writes '1848' on paper, and is turned down for a job after accidentally dating his application with that year. He contemplates selling a blanket, noting 'the consciousness of being honest went to my head.' He imagines his discovery of the word 'Kuboa,' tries to sell a 'character sketch of Correggio,' considers eating his pencil and finger, invents a fairy tale about an octagonal tower with a million blind angels, and gives his name as 'Andreas Tangen' to a guard. At the end, he refuses a free breakfast for the homeless and leaves on a ship bound for England.

Pan is a pastoral novel about the solitary hunter Thomas Glahn, who lives alone with his dog Aesop and falls in love with the trader's daughter Edvarda. Glahn shoots himself in the left foot to match his romantic rival's limp. He murders his dog Aesop and sends the corpse to Edvarda. He begs the narrator of the epilogue to kill him. Hamsun parodied the 'Rousseauian Soul' in this novel.

Mysteries centers on the strange Johan Nagel, who wears a yellow suit and carries a violin case containing dirty laundry (no violin) and a vial of prussic acid. Nagel intrigues a small town, befriends the cripple Miniman, and chases the lieutenant's bride Dagny. He berates his doppelganger, the 'Midget,' for being secretly evil, and drowns after trying to retrieve a mysterious iron ring he threw into the sea.

The Growth of the Soil is a pastoral novel about the peasant Isak and his wife Inger developing their farm at Sellanraa. Inger goes to prison after murdering/strangling her third child, who was born with the same harelip as herself. Their son Eleseus leaves the farm. The entrepreneur Geissler builds an irrigation system to stave off a drought. The novel echoed Nazi ideals of 'blood and soil.'

Hamsun is notoriously remembered for his Nazi sympathies. He wrote a controversial 1945 obituary for Hitler calling him 'a warrior for mankind' and 'a preacher of the gospel of justice for all nations.' Treason charges were dropped due to his supposed mental impairment, and he wrote On Overgrown Paths to prove his faculties were still intact. His essay 'From the Unconscious Life of the Mind' called for novels to attend to 'the whisper of the blood, the pleading of the bone.' Meyer Schapiro compared Hamsun's descriptions of shoes with Van Gogh's depictions in an article criticizing Heidegger. He also wrote Under the Autumn Stars and A Wanderer Plays on Muted Strings about the friendship and enmity of Falkenberg and Grindhausen.
