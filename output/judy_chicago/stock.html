American feminist artist born Judith Sylvia Cohen. Best known for The Dinner Party, a massive triangular installation with 39 place settings dedicated to famous women throughout history, permanently housed at the Brooklyn Museum. Co-organized the Womanhouse installation with Miriam Schapiro at CalArts. Pioneer of feminist art; changed her last name from Cohen to Chicago. Works span installation, needlework, ceramics, welding, and performance.
The Dinner Party (1979)

A massive triangular table installation with 39 place settings dedicated to famous women from history, each with hand-painted ceramic plates and embroidered runners. Permanently housed at the Elizabeth A. Sackler Center for Feminist Art at the Brooklyn Museum. Originally titled "Twenty-Five Women Who Were Eaten Alive." Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Judy Chicago (born Judith Sylvia Cohen) is an American feminist artist whose work spans installation, ceramics, needlework, welding, and performance. She is overwhelmingly asked about in the context of The Dinner Party, her 1979 triangular table installation with 39 place settings dedicated to famous women throughout history. The Dinner Party appears in the vast majority of clues about Chicago, and its key identifiers include: its triangular shape, the 39 place settings with hand-painted ceramic plates featuring vagina/butterfly imagery (described as "central core imagery"), the Heritage Floor inscribed with 999 names, its permanent home at the Elizabeth A. Sackler Center for Feminist Art at the Brooklyn Museum, and the fact that Georgia O'Keeffe is the final guest of honor. The work was originally titled "Twenty-Five Women Who Were Eaten Alive" and includes settings for figures ranging from the Minoan Snake Goddess to Artemisia Gentileschi, Virginia Woolf, and Emily Dickinson. It was criticized by Alice Walker for its treatment of Sojourner Truth (the only setting with faces instead of vaginal imagery) and by feminist art historians for essentialism. Chicago's second most frequently referenced work is Womanhouse (1972), the collaborative installation she co-organized with Miriam Schapiro at CalArts, in which students refitted an abandoned Hollywood mansion with feminist works including a Nurturant Kitchen where eggs morph into breasts. Other works that appear include the Atmospheres performance series (colorful smoke performances countering land art's masculinity), the International Honor Quilt (companion to The Dinner Party), the Holocaust Project (sixteen works inspired by the film Shoah), the Birth Project (needlework about childbirth made with over 100 needleworkers), and the paintings Powerplay and Through the Flower. Biographical clues note that she changed her name from Cohen to Chicago and advertised the change in Artforum, that she and her students at Fresno coined the term "feminist art," and that she posed as a boxer in a 1970 Artforum photograph.
