Victorian novelist known as Mrs. Gaskell. Her most frequently asked-about work is North and South, in which Margaret Hale moves from rural Helstone to the industrial town of Milton and eventually marries mill-owner John Thornton. She also wrote Cranford, an episodic novel about genteel ladies including Miss Matty who goes bankrupt, and the industrial novel Mary Barton, about a title character torn between Jem Wilson and Harry Carson amid the Chartist movement. She wrote The Life of Charlotte Bronte, drawing on letters to Ellen Nussey but omitting Bronte's love for Constantin Heger. Her works were serialized in Dickens's Household Words, and Dickens called her 'dear Scheherazade'. She has a memorial in Poets' Corner at Westminster Abbey.

Summary of Facts

Elizabeth Gaskell was a Victorian novelist whose works were serialized in Charles Dickens's Household Words and All the Year Round. Dickens called her 'dear Scheherazade,' though the two also feuded. She has a memorial in Poets' Corner at Westminster Abbey.

Her most frequently clued work is North and South, a 'condition of England' novel serialized weekly in Household Words from 1854 to 1855. Margaret Hale moves from rural Helstone to the industrial town of Milton (modeled on Manchester) after her father leaves the Church of England. She falls in love with and eventually marries mill-owner John Thornton. In the chapter 'A Blow and its Consequences,' Margaret is hit in the head with a rock while protecting Thornton from striking workers. Nicholas Higgins is the union leader; his daughter Bessie dies of brown lung disease from mill fluff. John Boucher kills himself and Higgins adopts his children. Leonards dies after a fight with Margaret's fugitive brother Frederick at a train station. Margaret inherits Marlborough Mills after Mr. Bell's death and helps Thornton save it. Gaskell considered calling the novel 'Death and Variations' because each character dies in a manner consistent with their personality. The novel's use of free indirect discourse is noted in the scene where Margaret rejects Mr. Lennox's proposal with a 'tinge of contempt.' David Lodge's Nice Work parallels its plot, with Robyn Penrose and Vic Wilcox. It was adapted into a popular 2004 BBC miniseries starring Daniela Denby-Ashe and Richard Armitage.

Cranford is an episodic novel about a small town of genteel ladies, narrated by Mary Smith. Miss Matty Jenkyns goes bankrupt after a bank collapse and sells tea. Spinster sisters Miss Matty and Miss Deborah feature prominently. A famous episode involves a cat swallowing lace, and Betsy Barker dresses a cow in flannel after it falls into a lime-pit. Robberies are blamed on foreigners, and the turban-wearing Signor Brunoni is suspected of being a French spy. Miss Pole is a notable gossip. George du Maurier's illustrations boosted the novel's popularity.

Mary Barton is an earlier industrial novel set in Manchester. The title character is torn between the working-class Jem Wilson and the wealthy Harry Carson. Her father John Barton joins the Chartist movement and murders Harry Carson. Mary chases after Jem's cousin to get testimony in the murder trial. Jem Wilson saved his father and a worker from a fire at the Carson Mill. The preface expresses sympathy with those 'doomed to struggle through their lives in strange alternations between work and want.' The novel is part of the 'Condition of England' genre, along with works by Disraeli and Dickens.

Gaskell wrote The Life of Charlotte Bronte, the first biography of her friend. It drew on correspondence between Charlotte and Ellen Nussey but omitted Bronte's obsession with the married Constantin Heger. It blamed Lydia Robinson for Branwell Bronte's death. William Carus Wilson, who inspired the character Brocklehurst in Jane Eyre, threatened to sue over the biography. The biography noted Charlotte often did not eat, paralleling Jane Eyre's starvation at Lowood School.

Other works include Ruth (about an orphan seduced by Henry Bellingham who becomes a nurse), Wives and Daughters, My Lady Ludlow (in which Margaret Dawson learns to make frumenty at Hanbury Court), and ghost stories including 'The Old Nurse's Story.' She wrote 'Lois the Witch' about the Salem witch trials. She collaborated with Dickens, Adelaide Procter, and Wilkie Collins on 'A House to Let' (1858) and wrote 'The Ghost in the Garden Room' for a collaborative story in the 1859 Christmas edition of All the Year Round.
