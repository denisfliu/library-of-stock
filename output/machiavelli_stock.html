Renaissance-era Florentine political theorist and author of The Prince and Discourses on Livy. Known for his concepts of virtu and fortuna, his claim that it is 'better to be feared than loved,' his condemnation of mercenaries, and his status as the 'first modern philosopher' according to Leo Strauss.
The Prince

Machiavelli's most famous work, a political treatise in the 'mirror for princes' genre, dedicated to Lorenzo de Medici. It argues that a ruler must embody virtu, be feared rather than loved, tame Fortuna (compared to a raging river and a woman that must be 'mastered'), and avoid mercenaries. Cesare Borgia is held up as a model ruler. The work identifies Theseus, Moses, Romulus, and Cyrus as 'outstanding men' who attained power through virtu. Frederick the Great published a critique of it edited by Voltaire.

Discourses on Livy

Machiavelli's commentary on the Roman historian Livy's Ab Urbe Condita. It espouses the virtues of Roman republicanism and discusses the 'origin of cities,' contrasting Athens with Sparta. He cited Rome's ability to switch between strategies of Fabius and Scipio as evidence of republics' superiority to kings. The work aims to 'open a new route' by basing judgments on 'respect for antiquity.'

Leo Strauss and Reception

Leo Strauss considered Machiavelli the 'first modern philosopher' and the origin of the ancients-moderns divide. His Thoughts on Machiavelli analyzes The Prince and Discourses on Livy, arguing that Machiavelli's 'anti-theological ire' was the source of modernity. Harvey Mansfield argued Machiavelli sought to overthrow Christianity. Strauss's students (Allan Bloom, Harry Jaffa) split into East Coast and West Coast schools. James Burnham's The Machiavellians shows how his tradition persists through Robert Michels.

The Mandrake (La Mandragola)

Machiavelli's comedy play in which the marriage-broker Ligurio helps Callimaco trick the infertile Nicia into thinking his wife Lucrezia must take a mandrake fertility potion that will kill the first man who sleeps with her. Callimaco disguises himself as a doctor, sleeps with Lucrezia, and she becomes his lover. Athol Fugard's Serpent Players adapted it as The Cure.

Machiavel in English Renaissance Drama

Machiavelli became a stock character ('Machiavel') in English Renaissance drama, synonymous with ruthless scheming. The ghost of 'Machiavel' delivers the opening monologue of Christopher Marlowe's The Jew of Malta, declaring 'religion but a childish toy.' In Shakespeare's Henry VI Part 3, Richard III boasts he can set 'Machiavel to school.'

Summary of Facts

Niccolo Machiavelli is a Renaissance-era Florentine political theorist, most famous for The Prince and the Discourses on Livy. He is one of the most frequently clued philosophers in quizbowl, appearing across philosophy, history, and literature categories.

The Prince is a political treatise in the 'mirror for princes' genre, dedicated to Lorenzo de Medici (whose initial rejection was supposedly because he was distracted by a gift of dogs). It argues that a ruler must embody virtu, be feared rather than loved, tame Fortuna (compared to a raging river and a woman that must be 'mastered'), avoid mercenaries, and be like both a fox (cunning) and a lion (strength). Cesare Borgia is held up as a model ruler, and Theseus, Moses, Romulus, and Cyrus are identified as 'outstanding men.' Armed prophets succeed while unarmed ones (like Savonarola) fail. Frederick the Great published a critique (Anti-Machiavel) edited by Voltaire.

The Discourses on Livy is a commentary on Livy's Ab Urbe Condita that espouses Roman republicanism. It discusses the 'origin of cities,' contrasts Athens with Sparta, and cites Rome's ability to switch between Fabius and Scipio as evidence of republics' superiority. The work aims to 'open a new route' based on 'respect for antiquity.'

Leo Strauss considered Machiavelli the 'first modern philosopher' and analyzed him in Thoughts on Machiavelli, arguing his 'anti-theological ire' was the source of modernity. Harvey Mansfield argued he sought to overthrow Christianity. James Burnham's The Machiavellians traces his influence through Robert Michels.

Machiavelli wrote The Mandrake (La Mandragola), a comedy in which Callimaco tricks the infertile Nicia into letting him sleep with Lucrezia through a fake mandrake potion scheme. Athol Fugard's Serpent Players adapted it as The Cure. In English Renaissance drama, 'Machiavel' became a stock villain: the ghost of Machiavel opens Marlowe's The Jew of Malta declaring 'religion but a childish toy,' and Richard III in Henry VI Part 3 boasts he can set 'the murderous Machiavel to school.'

Additional biographical facts: Machiavelli was imprisoned after the Sack of Prato, complained of farm life to Francesco Vettori, and J.G.A. Pocock links him with Guicciardini in The Machiavellian Moment. He adapted Terence's Andria, wrote Clizia (based on Plautus), the novella 'Belfagor the Archdemon,' and intermedio songs. Frans de Waal applied his ideas to chimpanzee power politics.
