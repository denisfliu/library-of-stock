American architect and urban planner (1846–1912), associated with the City Beautiful movement. Director of Works for the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago ('White City'). Designed the Flatiron Building in New York, Union Station in Washington D.C., and the 1909 Plan of Chicago. Long partner with John Wellborn Root.
World's Columbian Exposition / White City (1893)

Burnham served as Director of Works for the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago, which featured a complex of white Beaux-Arts buildings nicknamed the 'White City.' The event celebrated the 400th anniversary of Columbus's arrival in America and featured the first Ferris Wheel. Mayor Carter Harrison was assassinated during the exposition. Frederick Law Olmsted designed a Wooded Island for it. The serial killer H.H. Holmes was active during it. Erik Larson chronicled it in The Devil in the White City. The fairgrounds are now near the Museum of Science and Industry. Wikipedia

Flatiron Building

Triangular skyscraper in New York City (1902), originally named the Fuller Building, located between Fifth Avenue, Broadway, and East 22nd Street. Designed with co-architect Frederick Dinkelberg. A classical Greek column inspired the narrow triangular floor plan. Stieglitz and Steichen photographed it. Wikipedia

Plan of Chicago (1909)

Burnham's 1909 City Beautiful urban plan for Chicago, published by the city's Commercial Club. It created an unbroken stretch of parks along Lake Michigan through landfill and shaped Wacker Drive (a double-decker street) and Burnham Park. The plan also preserved a 'string of forests connecting several parks,' called the Emerald Necklace. Wikipedia

Manila / Baguio Plan

William Howard Taft hired Burnham to redesign the city of Manila in the Philippines and plan the summer capital of Baguio in the tropical pine forests of Luzon. Hundreds of workers died building the Kennon Road connecting them. Daniel Immerwahr called him an 'architectural imperialist' who operated without local approval in How to Hide an Empire. Wikipedia

General / Biographical

Daniel Burnham (1846–1912) worked closely with partner John Wellborn Root; together they designed the Masonic Temple Building and the Monadnock Building in Chicago. After the Great Fire of Chicago, Burnham designed the Montauk Block with fire shutters and a fireproof iron skeleton. He was a member of the McMillan Commission that redesigned the National Mall. He also designed Union Station in Washington D.C. and the Union Stock Yard Gate in Chicago. The Baths of Diocletian inspired a design featuring a three-arch loggia in a longer arcade.

Summary of Facts

Daniel Burnham (1846–1912) was an American architect and urban planner closely associated with the City Beautiful movement. He is best known as the Director of Works for the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago, where his plan created a complex of white Beaux-Arts buildings nicknamed the 'White City.' The event debuted the first Ferris Wheel and was the setting for the H.H. Holmes murders chronicled by Erik Larson in The Devil in the White City. The fairgrounds hosted the first Ferris Wheel, the lecture where Frederick Jackson Turner introduced his Frontier Thesis, and the assassination of Mayor Carter Harrison.

Burnham's most iconic single building is the Flatiron Building (1902) in New York City, originally named the Fuller Building, standing at the triangular intersection of Fifth Avenue, Broadway, and East 22nd Street. A classical Greek column inspired its narrow triangular floor plan. It was photographed in pictorialist compositions by Albert Stieglitz and Edward Steichen, and its ground-floor 'cow-catcher' retail space is said to have apocryphally inspired Edward Hopper's Nighthawks.

Burnham's 1909 Plan of Chicago—a City Beautiful initiative published by the Commercial Club—created an unbroken stretch of parks along Lake Michigan through landfill and led to the construction of the double-decked Wacker Drive. The plan also preserved a chain of forests connecting several parks, called the 'Emerald Necklace.'

Hired by William Howard Taft, Burnham designed plans for Manila and for Baguio, the colonial summer capital in the pine forests of the Philippines. Daniel Immerwahr characterized him as an 'architectural imperialist' in How to Hide an Empire. Burnham's motto—'make no little plans'—was said to have inspired George Ferris's design for the original Ferris Wheel.

Burnham worked in long partnership with John Wellborn Root. Together they designed the Monadnock Building and Masonic Temple Building in Chicago. After the Great Fire of Chicago, he designed the Montauk Block with fire shutters and a fireproof iron skeleton. He served on the McMillan Commission that redesigned the National Mall and also designed Washington D.C.'s Union Station and Chicago's Union Stock Yard Gate (a three-archway gatehouse with a terracotta bull's head).
