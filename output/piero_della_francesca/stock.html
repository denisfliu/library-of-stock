15th-century Italian Renaissance painter and mathematician from Sansepolcro. Best known for The Flagellation of Christ, The Resurrection, and the History of the True Cross fresco cycle in Arezzo. Also known for the double portrait of Federico da Montefeltro and Battista Sforza, and the Brera Madonna. Wrote treatises on perspective and mathematics.
The Flagellation of Christ (c. 1455–1460)

Three men in contemporary clothes stand in the right foreground, seemingly unaware of the flagellation happening in the left background. Christ is tied to a pillar topped by a golden statue/swordsman. Unusually low vanishing point near the hem of the flogger's robe. Kenneth Clark called it 'the greatest small painting in the world.' Multiple interpretive theories.

General / Biographical

15th-century Italian Renaissance painter from Sansepolcro. Also a mathematician: wrote the Abacus Treatise, On the Perspective of Painting (first Renaissance treatise dedicated solely to perspective), and treatises on geometric proportions. Worked for the Montefeltro family in Urbino. Better known as a mathematician until the 19th century. Vasari claims he was blind by age 60.

Summary of Facts

Piero della Francesca was a 15th-century Italian Renaissance painter and mathematician from Sansepolcro, who worked primarily for the Montefeltro family in Urbino. Until the 19th century, he was better known as a mathematician for works like the Abacus Treatise and On the Perspective of Painting, the first Renaissance treatise dedicated solely to perspective. Michael Baxandall argued that barrel-gauging and commercial mathematics influenced his use of geometric forms.

His most analyzed painting is The Flagellation of Christ (c. 1455-60), in which three men in contemporary clothes stand in the right foreground seemingly oblivious to the flagellation behind them, where Christ is tied to a pillar topped by a golden swordsman. Kenneth Clark called it 'the greatest small painting in the world.' Carlo Ginzburg argued that Pontius Pilate represents Emperor John VIII Palaiologos and the painting is a symbolic invitation to crusade. Martin Kemp noted the 'geometric control' of the octagonal vs. grid tile patterns. The vanishing point is unusually low, near the flogger's robe hem.

His Resurrection (c. 1463) in Sansepolcro shows a pink-robed Christ carrying a white flag with a red cross, stepping onto a sarcophagus as four guards sleep, flanked by a leafless and a leafy tree. Aldous Huxley called it 'the greatest picture in the world,' which saved it when British officer Tony Clarke defied bombing orders during WWII.

The History of the True Cross fresco cycle in the Basilica of San Francesco in Arezzo depicts the making, use, loss, and recovery of the cross. It opens with the Death of Adam and culminates in the Battle of Heraclius and Chosroes. Constantine's Dream shows soldiers guarding him in a red-coned yellow tent as a backlit angel descends. Puvis de Chavannes was heavily influenced by the cycle.

Other major works include the double portrait of Federico da Montefeltro (who lost his nose bridge in a jousting tournament) and Battista Sforza against a mountainous landscape, which caused Kenneth Clark to fall to his knees; the Brera Madonna sacra conversazione with an egg above the Madonna that inspired Dali's Madonna of Port Lligat; the Baptism of Christ with a man pulling his undershirt over his head; and the Perugia Polyptych. Vasari claimed Piero was blind by age 60, a claim often disputed.
