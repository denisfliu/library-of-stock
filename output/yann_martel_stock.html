Life of Pi

Life of Pi is Yann Martel's most famous novel, telling the story of Piscine 'Pi' Patel, an Indian boy who survives a shipwreck and spends months on a lifeboat in the Pacific Ocean with a Bengal tiger named Richard Parker. The novel explores themes of faith, survival, and storytelling. Pi is the son of the keeper of the Pondicherry Zoo and practices three religions simultaneously. The clues focus on the animals aboard the lifeboat, Pi's religious background, the meerkats and carnivorous algae island, the Poe allusion in Richard Parker's name, and the framing device of the Japanese Ministry of Transport interview.

Summary of Facts

Yann Martel is a Canadian author best known for his novel Life of Pi, which appears as his only work clued in quizbowl at these difficulty levels. The clues overwhelmingly focus on Life of Pi, which tells the story of an Indian boy named after a Parisian swimming pool (the Piscine Molitor) who becomes stranded at sea on a lifeboat with a Bengal tiger named Richard Parker. The narrator, Pi Patel, is the son of the keeper of the Pondicherry Zoo. He practices Christianity, Islam, and Hinduism simultaneously, having received religious instruction from a pandit, Father Martin, and one of two Mr. Kumars. During his ocean voyage, Pi abandons his vegetarianism to eat dorados and encounters an island of carnivorous algae populated by fish-eating meerkats. An orangutan named Orange Juice and a zebra are killed by a hyena earlier in the voyage. The tiger Richard Parker is named after a mutineer who falls victim to cannibalism in Edgar Allan Poe's The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym. A blind Frenchman is killed by Richard Parker, after which the narrator regains his sight. At the end of the novel, the narrator is interviewed in Tomatlan, Mexico by a representative of the Japanese Ministry of Transport. An italicized chapter introduces the narrator's children, Nick and Usha, and reveals that 'this story has a happy ending.' Pi eventually washes up on a beach in Mexico and escapes from Richard Parker. The novel was also adapted into a 2012 film by Ang Lee, notable for its groundbreaking CGI work on the tiger by the VFX company Rhythm & Hues, which went bankrupt shortly after winning an Oscar for the film. The bonus context identifies Martel as a Canadian author, placing him alongside Robertson Davies and Emma Donoghue.
