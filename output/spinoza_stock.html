Baruch Spinoza

← Home · View source questions · Make cards
Dutch-Jewish philosopher of the 17th century, expelled from Amsterdam's Jewish community for his heterodox views. Author of Ethics (demonstrated in geometrical order) and Tractatus Theologico-Politicus. Developed pantheism ('God or Nature'), the doctrine of conatus, and substance monism. Central figure in the 1780s Pantheism Controversy involving Jacobi and Mendelssohn. Called the 'prince of philosophers' by Deleuze.
Ethics and the Geometric Method

Spinoza's masterwork Ethics, Demonstrated in Geometrical Order, is structured after Euclid's Elements with definitions, axioms, and propositions. It argues that God is the only substance, identical with Nature ('Deus sive Natura'), and possesses infinite attributes. The work contains five parts, including 'Of Human Bondage' (on the power of the affects/passions) and concludes that understanding God is the highest virtue. Its first axiom is 'Everything which exists, exists either in itself or in something else.' Roger Scruton called it 'the last indisputable Latin masterpiece.'

Conatus and the Affects

The conatus principle ('each thing strives to preserve its own being') is Spinoza's theory of existential inertia, appearing as Proposition IIIP6 in the Ethics. It drives all human passions and is altered by affects like 'active joy' and 'active desire.' Joy and suffering (pleasure and pain) are feelings of increase and decrease in one's power, a formulation that sparked the 1990s 'affective turn.' Judith Butler treated conatus in 'The Desire to Live.'

Tractatus Theologico-Politicus

Published anonymously in 1670, this work argues for the separation of theology and philosophy, claims that Moses did not write the Torah (Ezra did), denies miracles as violations of natural law, and advocates religious toleration and free speech. It describes the Bible as 'imperfect, corrupt, erroneous, and inconsistent with itself,' argues the Torah was only fit as a state constitution for ancient Israel, and claims the persistence of Jews is largely due to circumcision. Steven Nadler's A Book Forged in Hell examines this work.

The Pantheism Controversy and Reception

In the 1780s, a public dispute between Friedrich Jacobi and Moses Mendelssohn over Gotthold Lessing's deathbed confession of following Spinoza became the 'Pantheism Controversy.' Jacobi accused Spinoza of 'pure materialism' and atheism, while Mendelssohn wrote Morning Hours defending theism. The controversy led to renewed interest in Spinoza and Jacobi's popularization of the term 'nihilism.' Major modern interpreters include Gilles Deleuze (who called Spinoza the 'prince of philosophers'), Antonio Negri (The Savage Anomaly), and Michael Hardt (concept of 'multitude'). Hegel interpreted Spinoza's philosophy as 'immobile.' Leo Strauss examined his 'critique of religion.'

Summary of Facts

Baruch Spinoza (1632-1677) is one of the most frequently clued philosophers in quizbowl, with a deep and varied question set. The two universal giveaways are his pantheism ('God or Nature') and his expulsion from Amsterdam's Jewish community. His Ethics, written in geometric order emulating Euclid, is the central work.

The Ethics argues that God is the only substance, possessing infinite attributes, and is identical with Nature (Deus sive Natura). It distinguishes between natura naturans (active, productive nature/God) and natura naturata (passive nature). The work is structured with definitions, axioms, and propositions; its first axiom is 'Everything which exists, exists either in itself or in something else.' The section 'Of Human Bondage' examines how the affects (emotions) control human life, while the final section praises knowledge of the third kind (scientia intuitiva) as coming from God. Spinoza rejected Cartesian dualism, arguing mind and body are two attributes of one substance. The conatus principle ('each thing strives to preserve its own being') drives all passions and is one of the most distinctive Spinoza clues.

The Tractatus Theologico-Politicus (1670) is Spinoza's political-religious treatise arguing for separation of theology and philosophy, claiming Moses did not write the Torah (Ezra did), denying miracles as violations of natural law, and advocating free speech and religious toleration. It calls the Bible 'imperfect, corrupt, erroneous, and inconsistent with itself' and argues the Torah was only valid as ancient Israel's state constitution.

The 1780s Pantheism Controversy, where Friedrich Jacobi and Moses Mendelssohn disputed over Lessing's Spinozism, is a major reception clue. Jacobi accused Spinoza of materialism and atheism; Mendelssohn wrote Morning Hours in response. Modern reception includes Gilles Deleuze (who called Spinoza the 'prince of philosophers'), Antonio Negri (The Savage Anomaly, concept of 'multitude'), Leo Strauss (Spinoza's 'critique of religion'), and Mark Fisher (K-Punk blog on Spinoza and cybernetics). Spinoza's letters, signed with a rose above the Latin word for 'cautiously,' addressed topics including the infinite (to Lewis Meyer) and the problem of evil (to Willem van Blijenbergh).
