General / Biographical

John Osborne was a British dramatist and a prominent member of the Angry Young Men movement. He is most commonly identified through Look Back in Anger and his association with kitchen sink drama. He also adapted works by other authors and faced censorship from the Lord Chamberlain.

Summary of Facts

John Osborne was a British playwright and a leading member of the Angry Young Men movement. He is best known for Look Back in Anger (1956), a pioneering kitchen sink drama about the working-class Jimmy Porter and his middle-class wife Alison. The play depicts the effects of class pressures on their marriage; Alison Burns her arm on an iron, and the couple eventually reunite playing 'bears and squirrels.' An unseen character named Webster reminds Jimmy of his old love interest Madeline, and Alison loses her baby. The play was championed by the theatre critic Kenneth Tynan, who wrote a glowing review contrasting it with the staid country-house dramas he dismissed as 'Loamshire' plays, and it created a sensation as a founding work of kitchen sink drama. David Sylvester named the kitchen sink cultural movement in reference to John Bratby's painting.

Osborne's second major play, The Entertainer, was written at the request of Laurence Olivier, who premiered the title role. It centers on the music hall performer Archie Rice, who sings numbers such as 'Thank God I'm normal' and 'Why should I care?' and learns that his son Mick has been taken as a prisoner of war. The play ends with Archie walking off stage while music continues to play in the background.

Osborne also wrote A Patriot for Me (1965), based on the story of Alfred Redl, which was banned by the Lord Chamberlain of the Royal Household in an especially egregious use of censorship power. His play Luther deals with the Protestant Reformation. Other works include an English-language version of Lope de Vega's A Bond Honored, an adaptation of Wilde's The Picture of Dorian Gray for the stage, and Inadmissible Evidence, which features the self-hating William Maitland. Until 1968, the Lord Chamberlain retained the legal authority to ban plays from public performance in the UK.
