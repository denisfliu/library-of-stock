Japanese author known for exploring the tension between Western modernity and Japanese tradition, as well as themes of obsession, eroticism, and aesthetics. Best known for Some Prefer Nettles, The Makioka Sisters, Naomi, and In Praise of Shadows. His works frequently involve bunraku (puppet theater), traditional Japanese culture, and complex erotic relationships.
Some Prefer Nettles (1929)

Novel following the waning marriage of Kaname and Misako, who share a disdain for 'Osaka traits.' Kaname is fascinated with the black teeth (ohaguro) of his father-in-law's mistress O-Hisa, whom Misako finds 'filthy and barbarous.' A cultural traditionalist (the father-in-law) invites Kaname to bunraku performances at Awaji Island and in Osaka. Kaname realizes his idea of the 'Eternal Woman' is embodied by the doll of Koharu in a bunraku production of The Love Suicides at Sonezaki. He skims an unexpurgated Arabian Nights for lewd passages and sleeps with a half-Korean, half-Russian prostitute named Louise. Wikipedia

Summary of Facts

Jun'ichiro Tanizaki is a major Japanese author whose works explore the tension between Western modernity and Japanese tradition, as well as themes of eroticism, aesthetics, and obsession. In quizbowl, he is most commonly identified through Some Prefer Nettles and The Makioka Sisters, with Naomi and In Praise of Shadows as secondary identifiers.

Some Prefer Nettles (1929) is his most-clued work, centering on the failing marriage of Kaname and Misako. Kaname is drawn to traditional Japanese culture through his father-in-law, attending bunraku performances at Awaji Island and in Osaka, and becoming fascinated with O-Hisa's archaic teeth-blackening (which Misako finds 'filthy and barbarous'). He realizes the 'Eternal Woman' is embodied by the doll of Koharu in a bunraku production of The Love Suicides at Sonezaki. He also skims the unexpurgated Arabian Nights and sleeps with a half-Korean, half-Russian prostitute named Louise.

The Makioka Sisters (1948), also known as Sasameyuki ('Light Snow'), follows a proud Osaka family whose fortunes have declined. Yukiko resists getting married while Taeko has a scandalous fling with the lower-class Itakura, to the disapproval of her sister Sachiko. Tanizaki's later work The Maids retells this novel from the servants' point of view.

Naomi (1924) features the engineer Joji, who begins an affair with a 15-year-old waitress reminiscent of Mary Pickford. He pays for her English lessons and trains her to be Western, but she ends up dominating him. He also idolizes his dance instructor, Madam Shlemskaya.

The Key (1956) consists of alternating diaries by a wife (Ikiko) and her unnamed husband, who tries to spark an affair between her and their friend Kimura. In Praise of Shadows (1933) is an essay on Japanese aesthetics that famously describes the toilet as a place of 'spiritual repose' and contrasts light and dark elements.

Other works that appear in clues include The Secret History of the Lord of Musashi (protagonist simulates a decapitation), Quicksand (Sonoko Kakiuchi's affair with art student Mitsuko), Diary of a Mad Old Man (Utsugi's attraction to daughter-in-law Satsuko; his last work), and The Devils in Daylight (Sonomura cracks a Gold-Bug-inspired code and uses peepholes to observe a murder).
