Summary of Facts

Cimabue was an early Italian Renaissance painter, most famous as the teacher of Giotto. According to Vasari, Cimabue discovered his most famous student drawing pictures of sheep on a rock. Vasari begins Part 2 of his Lives with Cimabue, and Filippo Baldinucci's dictionary of artists also starts with him. Cimabue's role in the rise of Renaissance art is largely extrapolated from a verse in Dante's Purgatorio. He is credited, alongside Duccio, as one of the first artists to break away from the Byzantine iconlike style known as 'maniera greca.' He was nicknamed 'bull-headed.'

His Santa Trinita Maesta depicts the Virgin and Child enthroned, flanked by two quartets of angels, with liberal use of gold striations on a blue robe to create depth. The throne's base contains three arches displaying portraits of four prophets: Abraham, David, Jeremiah, and Isaiah. Vasari described a famous procession of this Madonna through the streets of Florence, a scene later depicted by the Victorian artist Frederic Leighton. The Maesta is now in the Uffizi Gallery.

Cimabue created multiple crucifixes. One for Santa Croce in Florence, painted in distemper, was badly damaged in the 1966 Arno flood and became a symbol of that disaster. Another crucifixion in the Basilica of St. Francis of Assisi has deteriorated so much that its colors are primarily black, resembling a photographic negative. He also created frescoes for the Basilica, including the Assumption of the Virgin.

His Diptych of Devotion originally had eight panels, of which only three survive. A panel depicting the Mocking of Christ was thought lost until it was discovered in 2019 hanging in an elderly French woman's kitchen. Other works include the Castelfiorentino Madonna (infant in pink-violet wrap caressing mother's bulging cheek), the Virgin Enthroned with Angels (in the Louvre), and a depiction of St. John in Pisa (man in blue robes holding a blue bound box), which is the only work definitively proven as painted by his hand. He created mosaics for the Florence Baptistery with Corsi di Buono.
