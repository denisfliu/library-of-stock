Interpreter of Maladies (title story)

The title story of Lahiri's debut collection, in which the tour guide Mr. Kapasi takes Mr. and Mrs. Das and their children on a tour of the Sun Temple at Konarak. Mrs. Das (Mina) confesses that Bobby is not her husband's son and asks if Mr. Kapasi feels she has pain or guilt. Mr. Kapasi's 'other job' interpreting Gujarati in a doctor's office fascinates Mrs. Das. The story ends with a slip of paper with Mr. Kapasi's address blowing away in the wind after monkeys attack Bobby and Mr. Kapasi saves him. Mr. Kapasi had left his grammar school job after his seven-year-old son died of typhoid.

The Namesake

As an architecture student at Yale, Gogol legally changes his name to Nikhil. He later divorces Moushumi. The novel follows the Ganguli family's immigrant experience.

The Lowland

A novel about two brothers, Subhash and Udayan, who grow up in Calcutta. Udayan joins the Naxalite movement and is shot dead by paramilitary police hiding in the water. Subhash moves to Rhode Island for his PhD and marries Udayan's pregnant widow Gauri, raising the brother's daughter Bela as his own. Gauri later abandons Bela to move to California and write a dissertation on the Naxalites. The brothers once got caught sneaking into the Tollyclub golf course.

Unaccustomed Earth

Lahiri's second story collection, titled from a Nathaniel Hawthorne phrase in 'The Custom-House.' The title story follows Ruma in Seattle, whose widower father visits and she discovers his relationship with Mrs. Bagchi when he loses a postcard. Her son Akash steals a postcard to plant in a garden he makes with his grandfather. The collection includes a trio of linked stories about Hema and Kaushik: in 'Once in a Lifetime,' Kaushik's family moves because his mother is dying of breast cancer; in 'Going Ashore,' Kaushik becomes a photojournalist killed by a tsunami at a Thai resort. 'Hell-Heaven' features the graduate student Pranab following young Usha. 'Only Goodness' has a woman finding her brother passed out drunk beside her son's bath.

General / Biographical

Lahiri is a Bengali-American author who later moved to Rome and began writing exclusively in Italian. Her novel Whereabouts (Dove Mi Trovo) was originally written in Italian, about an unnamed woman wandering an unnamed European city. She translated it herself into English. Her essay 'Teach Yourself Italian' describes her decision to abandon English, comparing the Italian language to 'cradling a newborn' and English to a 'hairy, smelly teenager.' Ann Goldstein translated her essays In Other Words into English. She praised a Michael F. Moore translation of Manzoni's The Betrothed.

Summary of Facts

Jhumpa Lahiri is one of the most frequently clued contemporary American authors in quizbowl, known for fiction exploring the Indian-American immigrant experience. Her debut collection Interpreter of Maladies is extensively clued, especially its title story about the tour guide Mr. Kapasi who saves Bobby Das from monkeys at the Sun Temple at Konarak, whose address blows away in the wind at the end. 'A Temporary Matter' follows the couple Shoba and Shukumar sharing secrets during power outages, culminating in the revelation that their stillborn child was a boy. 'Sexy' features Dev whispering the title word at the Mapparium, with Rohin defining it as 'loving someone you don't know.'

Her novel The Namesake follows Gogol Ganguli, named after the Russian writer because his father Ashoke survived a train crash clutching a page of 'The Overcoat.' Gogol changes his name to Nikhil at Yale and later divorces Moushumi. The Lowland follows the brothers Subhash and Udayan: Udayan joins the Naxalite movement and is killed, while Subhash moves to Rhode Island and raises Udayan's daughter.

Her second collection Unaccustomed Earth (titled from Hawthorne) includes linked stories about Hema and Kaushik, and the title story about Ruma in Seattle. Other notable stories from Interpreter of Maladies include 'Mrs. Sen's' (crashes into pole buying fish), 'This Blessed House' (Twinkle finds Christian objects), and 'The Third and Final Continent' (103-year-old Mrs. Croft exclaims 'Splendid!' about the moon landing).

Lahiri later moved to Rome and began writing exclusively in Italian, publishing Whereabouts (Dove Mi Trovo) in Italian and translating it herself. Her essay 'Teach Yourself Italian' describes abandoning English, comparing Italian to 'cradling a newborn.'
