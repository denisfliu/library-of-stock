American postmodernist author and essayist. Most commonly identified by the novel Infinite Jest (Enfield Tennis Academy, Hal Incandenza, addictive 'Entertainment' film, Quebecois Wheelchair Assassins) and essays 'A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again' (Caribbean cruise on the Nadir), 'Consider the Lobster,' and 'This Is Water' (Kenyon commencement speech). Also wrote The Pale King (unfinished, IRS in Peoria), The Broom of the System, and 'E Unibus Pluram.'
Infinite Jest

Wallace's massive novel set at the Enfield Tennis Academy (ETA) near Boston. Hal Incandenza, a tennis prodigy who memorizes the OED and smokes weed in tunnels, is the son of James Incandenza (founder of ETA and creator of 'the Entertainment,' a film so entertaining viewers lose all interest in anything else). Quebecois Wheelchair Assassins seek the film. Don Gately stays at the Ennet House Drug and Alcohol Recovery House. Characters include Michael Pemulis, John 'No Relation' Wayne, Ortho Stice, and Joelle van Dyne (the Prettiest Girl of All Time). The game Eschaton simulates nuclear warfare. President Johnny Gentle. Hal may have consumed the drug DMZ.

Essays

Wallace's essays are heavily clued. 'A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again' describes a Caribbean cruise on the Zenith (renamed Nadir); mentions 'Professional Smile,' hypnotist Nigel Ellery, waiter Tibor, and a teenage girl's suicide from despair. 'Consider the Lobster' contemplates ethics of boiling animals alive at the Maine Lobster Festival. 'This Is Water' (Kenyon College commencement) begins with parable of two fish; argues 'the real value of a real education' is 'simple awareness.' 'E Unibus Pluram' traces television's influence on U.S. fiction. 'Derivative Sport in Tornado Alley' describes being blown into a fence with Gil Antitoi while playing tennis in Champaign, Illinois. 'Roger Federer As Religious Experience.' 'Big Red Son' about the Adult Video News Awards. 'Good Old Neon': narrator says 'my whole life I've been a fraud'; drives into bridge abutment.

The Pale King

Wallace's unfinished posthumous novel, edited by Michael Pietsch, set largely at an IRS office in Peoria, Illinois. Characters include 'Irrelevant' Chris Fogle (turns pages, signs Memo 20s), David Cusk (fear of sweating causes constant sweating), and the ghost of a mirror inspector who hanged himself. D.T. Max's biography Every Love Story is a Ghost Story chronicles Wallace's life.

The Broom of the System

Wallace's debut novel about Lenore Beadsman, who owns a talking bird named Vlad the Impaler and tries to find her missing grandmother who escaped a nursing home. Title refers to a riddle from Gramma Lenore's encounter with Wittgenstein about whether a broom's handle or bristles are more useful.

Summary of Facts

David Foster Wallace is a defining figure of late-20th-century American literature in quizbowl. Infinite Jest is his primary identifier, appearing in virtually every tossup. The novel is set at the Enfield Tennis Academy near Boston, where Hal Incandenza is a tennis prodigy. His father James created 'the Entertainment,' a film so addictive that viewers lose all interest in anything else, which Quebecois Wheelchair Assassins seek to weaponize. Don Gately stays at the Ennet House Drug and Alcohol Recovery House. The game Eschaton simulates nuclear warfare.

Wallace's essays are equally important to his quizbowl profile. 'A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again' describes a Caribbean cruise (ship renamed Nadir). 'Consider the Lobster' contemplates the ethics of boiling animals at the Maine Lobster Festival. 'This Is Water,' his Kenyon College commencement speech, opens with the two-fish parable and argues for 'simple awareness.' 'E Unibus Pluram' traces television's influence on American fiction. Other essays include 'Derivative Sport in Tornado Alley' (tennis in Champaign with Gil Antitoi) and 'Roger Federer As Religious Experience.'

The Pale King, his unfinished novel edited by Michael Pietsch, is set at an IRS office in Peoria, Illinois, with characters like 'Irrelevant' Chris Fogle. The Broom of the System, his debut, features Lenore Beadsman and a Wittgenstein riddle. 'Good Old Neon' opens with 'my whole life I've been a fraud.' Wallace's essay collection Brief Interviews with Hideous Men uses Q-and-A format. He committed suicide in 2008; frequently described as wearing a bandana.
