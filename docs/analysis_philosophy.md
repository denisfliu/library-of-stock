# Philosophy Analysis Guide

Supplement to `analysis_instructions.md`. Read the core instructions first — this document covers philosophy-specific differences.

## How Philosophy Differs

Philosophers develop **ideas and arguments** that form a coherent intellectual narrative. Unlike literature (discrete works with plots) or visual arts (discrete paintings), philosophy topics are about:

1. **Ideas/concepts** — the categorical imperative, conatus, the thing-in-itself, the veil of ignorance
2. **Arguments and positions** — why Kant says lying to a murderer is wrong, Spinoza's pantheism
3. **Works as vehicles** — the Ethics, Critique of Pure Reason — these matter, but they're containers for ideas
4. **Debates and interlocutors** — Kant vs. Forster on race, Jacobi's critique of Spinoza, neo-Kantian schools
5. **Intellectual lineage** — who influenced whom, who responded to whom

## Sectioning for Philosophers

Instead of organizing purely by work (like literature), organize by **idea clusters that may span multiple works:**

### Primary sections: Key Ideas/Concepts
Each major idea or concept area gets its own section. Name it after the idea, not the work:
- "The Categorical Imperative" (not just "Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals")
- "Conatus and the Affects" (not just "Ethics Part III")
- "The Thing-in-Itself and Transcendental Idealism"

But **include which work(s) the idea comes from** in the section description.

### Secondary sections: Major Works
Works that are frequently clued as answerlines or have specific structural clues still get their own sections:
- "Critique of Pure Reason" — if clues reference specific parts (A/B editions, the Transcendental Aesthetic, synthetic a priori)
- "Ethics (Spinoza)" — if clues reference its geometric structure, specific axioms, specific parts

### Also include:
- **Debates/Critiques** — if clues frequently reference specific interlocutors (Kant's debates with Forster, the Fischer-Trendelenburg debate)
- **Reception and Legacy** — if clues reference how later thinkers used the philosopher (Deleuze on Spinoza, neo-Kantian schools)
- **General / Biographical** — the usual giveaway identifiers

## Card Types for Philosophy

Philosophy cards need more variety than the standard `Work: clue → Work (Creator)` pattern:

### 1. Concept → Philosopher
The most common card type. Tests: "given this description of an idea, who held it?"
- Front: `Philosopher: argued that lying to a murderer at your door is still immoral`
- Back: `Immanuel Kant`

### 2. Concept identification
Tests: "what is this idea called?"
- Front: `Concept: the principle that one should act only according to maxims that are universalizable`
- Back: `Categorical Imperative (Kant)`

### 3. Work → Philosopher (when the work itself is clued)
- Front: `Work: written in geometric order with axioms and definitions, emulating Euclid's Elements`
- Back: `Ethics (Spinoza)`

### 4. Specific passage/argument → Work
For distinctive arguments tied to specific works:
- Front: `Work: proposes that standing armies should be abolished and assassins banned as preliminary articles for peace`
- Back: `Toward Perpetual Peace (Kant)`

### 5. Interlocutor/Debate cards
- Front: `Philosopher: Friedrich Jacobi accused this thinker of "pure materialism" and thus atheism`
- Back: `Baruch Spinoza`
- Front: `Philosopher: Emmanuel Eze ignited the study of this thinker's racism in the 1990s`
- Back: `Immanuel Kant`

### 6. Key term definition
- Front: `Concept: the doctrine that "every man strives to preserve his own being"`
- Back: `Conatus (Spinoza)`

## Indicator Choices

- `Philosopher:` — general facts, identifying the thinker from a described idea
- `Concept:` — identifying a named concept/principle/doctrine
- `Work:` — identifying a specific philosophical text
- `Debate:` — identifying a philosophical dispute or critique (use sparingly)

## What Makes a Good Philosophy Card

- **The front describes an idea or argument** in enough detail that someone who's studied it would recognize it
- **The back is the concept name, work, or philosopher** — whichever is most useful to test
- Avoid cards where the front just says "wrote [book title]" — that's an identifier, not a learnable fact
- **Include the "why"** when possible — "argued lying to a murderer is wrong *because* it violates the universalizability of maxims" is better than just "argued lying to a murderer is wrong"
- **Interlocutors are high-value cards** — knowing that Deleuze wrote a book on Spinoza, or that Constant criticized Kant's lying example, are exactly the kind of cross-references that win quizbowl games

```
  - name: "Transcendental Idealism (Critique of Pure Reason)"

    description: "Common identifiers: German Enlightenment, never left Königsberg, etc."
    clues: [...]
```

## Tags for Philosophy

Use recognized philosophical movements/schools:
- Rationalism, Empiricism, German Idealism, Existentialism, Pragmatism
- Utilitarianism, Phenomenology, Analytic Philosophy, Stoicism, Epicureanism
- Frankfurt School, Vienna Circle, etc.

NOT broad labels like "Western philosophy" or "Ethics."
